# ARTICLE: EBITDA, the Indicator That Creates a False Sense of Security in Companies

In recent years, Brazil has witnessed consistent growth in the number of judicial and extrajudicial restructurings — a phenomenon that goes beyond macroeconomic factors such as high interest rates or economic slowdown. Behind this movement lies a more silent and structural problem: the way many companies evaluate their own financial health.

Public data indicates that requests for judicial restructuring in Brazil have been growing significantly since 2023, with thousands of companies resorting to this instrument annually. In 2025, the volume surpassed recent historical levels, driven primarily by medium and large-sized companies. Emblematic cases have returned to market attention, such as the situations involving GPA and Raízen, which announced restructuring processes amid significant financial pressures.

Although each case has its specificities, there is a common denominator that deserves reflection: the obsession with EBITDA as the primary performance indicator.

EBITDA, the acronym for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortization, is not a formal accounting metric. It does not appear in mandatory financial statements. It is a market construction, widely used to attempt to bring operational results closer to cash generation. The problem is that this approximation is often illusory.

The central point is simple: EBITDA is not cash. And companies do not pay their bills with EBITDA. The indicator starts from accounting profit, which in turn is recognized at the time of sale and not at the time of receipt. In other words, a company can show revenue growth, robust EBITDA margins, and still face serious financial difficulties if that result does not convert into actual cash.

This mismatch manifests itself in critical operational decisions that go unnoticed when focus is exclusively on EBITDA. Longer collection periods, rising default rates, accumulated inventory, and low working capital efficiency are factors that do not appear clearly in this indicator but have a direct impact on company liquidity. It is at this point that deterioration begins silently.

There is a classic passage from Ernest Hemingway's "The Sun Also Rises" that illustrates this process well. When asked how he went broke, a businessman character responds: "Gradually, then suddenly." The logic is the same in the corporate world. Companies do not collapse overnight. They give signs: insufficient cash generation, increased debt, constant need for third-party capital to sustain operations. Meanwhile, EBITDA can continue appearing apparently healthy, creating a false sense of security.

The problem worsens in high-interest environments, like the current one. With rates in the double digits, debt costs consume a significant portion of generated cash — when it exists. In this scenario, indicators such as net debt to EBITDA, widely used by the market, begin to deteriorate rapidly. Historically, levels above 2.5 times have already been considered relevant warning signals. This means, in practice, that the company would need two and a half years of cash generation through operations (with all the problems and limitations of EBITDA), without considering investments and other obligations, just to pay off its debts. It is a scenario of extremely high financial fragility.

What is observed today is that many companies continue operating with an almost exclusive focus on performance metrics, neglecting cash management. They receive from one side and pay from the other, often with mismatched timelines. In more critical situations, they begin using new debt to cover current obligations, feeding a cycle that, at a certain point, becomes unsustainable.

Here, it is worth highlighting the importance of the cash flow statement, a mandatory financial statement in Brazil since 2008, which clearly displays, through three activities (operating, investment, and financing), the sources and uses of company cash in a given period.

This "moment" is the trigger. Hemingway's "suddenly." It can be the loss of a major client, a credit restriction, the review of a banking covenant, or even a regulatory change. When this trigger occurs, the company that was already fragile enters rapid financial collapse, culminating in a judicial restructuring request.

The recent advance of restructurings in Brazil, therefore, should not be interpreted solely as a reflection of an adverse economic environment with stratospheric interest rates. It also exposes a fragility in the way corporate performance is measured and monitored.

This does not mean that EBITDA should be discarded. It is a useful indicator for evaluating operational efficiency and comparing companies within the same sector. The problem lies in its isolated use — or worse, in its elevation to the condition of the main compass for management.

*Marcio Berstecher is an audit partner at RSM in Brazil*
