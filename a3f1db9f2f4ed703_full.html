# Foodtech IIMas Food Opens Plant for Plant-Based Protein Ingredients and Food Production

After eight years of research and development (R&D), in 2020, the Innovation Incubator for Healthier Foods (IIMas Food) was born in Marchigüe, O'Higgins Region. The foodtech company, which develops and produces food solutions based on plant-based raw materials, has just inaugurated a production complex in Marchigüe to process and transform plant proteins—such as legumes and cereals—into ingredients and products for the food industry.

IIMas Food's general manager, Dayhana Digman, stated that the 2,000 square-meter facility required an initial investment of US$8 million—contributed by the private fund Inversiones QueenCorp S.A.—and will allow the integration of R&D, pilot, and industrial scaling capabilities. The complex features five production plants for different processes and four laboratories—including one for molecular analysis focused on R&D of microorganisms for food use—pilot plants, and a gastronomic innovation center. The executive also highlighted that it has storage capacity for silage—raw material preservation—of "more than one thousand tons."

## Five Plants

Digman detailed that the complex includes a biotechnological processing plant for the development of microencapsulated vitamins and minerals, and a fermentation process line, where they developed a sourdough cream for baking processes. It also integrates plants for grinding legumes and cereals; dry fractionation—where proteins, fibers, and starches are physically separated—; and mixing, focused on premixes and natural preservatives with "clean label." This infrastructure is completed by an extrusion plant—a process where ingredients are subjected to pressure, temperature, and humidity to transform them into a new product—which integrates dry and wet technologies, and is oriented toward developing textured proteins, such as "meat analogs" with fibrous structure similar to meat. "It is the first plant with wet extrusion technology in Latin America," Digman affirmed.

## Developments

Among solutions in the commercial phase, Digman highlighted chickpea and bean pearls, which are sold in retail through the Banquete brand by Tucapel, and a dried pea meat analog, sourdough cream, microencapsulated vitamins, and natural preservatives. She advanced that they are developing a product that emulates meat fibers and "requires no hydration," which they seek to bring to market.

"We want to bring these new solutions to scale, make them accessible to consumers, and also allow companies to use the products as ingredients and see us as a technological partner for joint development," she added.
