# Foodtech Opens Plant for Plant-Based Protein Ingredients and Foods Production

After eight years of research and development (R&D), in 2020, the Innovation Incubator for Healthier Foods (IIMas Food) was founded in Marchigüe, O'Higgins Region. The foodtech company, which develops and produces food solutions based on plant-based raw materials, has just inaugurated a production complex in Marchigüe to process and transform plant proteins—such as legumes and cereals—into ingredients and products for the food industry.

IIMas Food's General Manager, Dayhana Digman, stated that the 2,000 square-meter facility required an initial investment of US$ 8 million—provided by private fund Inversiones QueenCorp S.A.—and will allow the integration of R&D, piloting, and industrial scaling capabilities. The complex features five production plants for different processes and four laboratories—including one for molecular analysis focused on R&D in food-use microorganisms—pilot plants, and a gastronomic innovation center. The executive also highlighted that it has silage capacity—for the preservation of raw materials—of "more than one thousand tons."

## Five Plants

Digman detailed that the complex includes a biotechnology processes plant for the development of microencapsulated vitamins and minerals, and a fermentation process line where they developed a sourdough cream for baking processes. It also integrates grinding plants for legumes and cereals; dry fractionation—where proteins, fibers, and starches are physically separated—; and mixing, focused on premixes and natural "clean label" preservatives. This infrastructure is completed by an extrusion plant—a process where ingredients are subjected to pressure, temperature, and humidity to transform them into a new product—that integrates dry and wet technologies, and is aimed at developing textured proteins, such as "meat analogues" with fibrous structure similar to meat. "It is the first plant with wet extrusion technology in Latin America," affirmed Digman.

## Developments

Among the solutions in commercial phase, Digman highlighted chickpea and bean pearls, which are marketed in retail through the Banquete brand by Tucapel, and a dried pea meat analogue, sourdough cream, microencapsulated vitamins, and natural preservatives. She advanced that they are developing a product that emulates meat fibers and "does not require hydration," which they seek to bring to market.

"We want to bring these new solutions to scale, make them accessible to consumers, and also have companies use the products as ingredients and see us as a technology partner for joint developments," she added.
