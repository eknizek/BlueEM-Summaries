# Enel Brasil Raises R$10 Billion One Day Before Credit Rating Downgrade

Enel Americas notified the Central Bank of Chile of the opening of a credit line of US$2 billion (R$10 billion) for its Brazilian subsidiary, with an 18-month term. The notice was made last Thursday (23rd). The resources will be allocated to Enel Brasil operations in São Paulo, Rio de Janeiro, and Ceará. The document, also sent to the Santiago Stock Exchange, the Chilean Electronic Stock Exchange, and the Central Securities Depository, is signed by Giuseppe Turchiarelli, general manager of Enel Americas. It was sent 24 hours before the first of two credit rating downgrades of the company by risk agencies.

According to the material fact disclosed, "the mentioned credit line will strengthen the financial needs of the Subsidiary, which is currently executing and accelerating projects mainly in distribution networks, which are fundamental to strengthen its operational platform, as well as the quality and resilience of service, in order to sustain its competitive positioning in one of the Group's main markets."

On Friday (24th), Fitch reduced the rating of Enel Brasil and assigned a negative outlook to all corporate ratings of the subsidiary. The agency cited uncertainty about the renewal of the energy distribution concession in São Paulo. On Monday (27th), Moody's did the same with Enel Americas, Enel's controlling company in Brazil. The rating fell from Baa2 to Baa3, at the limit of the so-called investment grade. Losing this seal would mean greater risk for investors.

The rating reductions place pressure on the conglomerate, and the credit announced by Enel Americas, already materialized, is an attempt to guarantee liquidity for the group given the current context.

Enel faces a contract termination process in São Paulo for its energy distribution contract, initiated by the federal government due to service delivery failures. Aneel (National Electric Energy Agency) is evaluating whether the company should lose the right to operate distribution in the state, which is the group's largest market in the country. If termination is decreed, a new company could assume the service on a temporary basis, although it has not been decided how this would happen. This places regulatory and financial pressure on Enel.
