# Madero Invests in "Empanada Factory" and Reinforces Delivery Strategy

The Madero Group, led by Junior Durski, plans to increase empanada production from 280,000 to 1.5 million per month starting in June, developing equipment in-house to meet growing demand. Empanadas have become a profitable menu item, with sales of 55,000 per week, and the company seeks to maintain the artisanal production process.

First quarter 2026 gross revenue was R$ 577 million, with net profit of R$ 20.8 million. The hybrid business model, which combines restaurants and delivery, has been crucial for growth, especially with the expansion of "Empório," a convenience store focused on frozen products.

Behind the numbers of Madero Group's first quarter 2026 results, empanadas have proven to be a lucrative addition to the restaurant chain's menu. The company currently produces approximately 280,000 empanadas per month, but demand exceeds this. To handle the production increase, the company is developing in-house equipment that will allow it to increase production more than fivefold starting in the second half of the year.

"By June, we will produce 1.5 million empanadas per month. We are developing some equipment to help. We are selling 55,000 empanadas per week and can't sell more because we have a manufacturing limit," Durski told NeoFeed.

The idea is to increase industrial capacity without losing the artisanal production process. Madero's CEO explains that the "edge" of the dough, considered essential for consumers to feel the crunchiness, will continue to be made manually.

More than a new menu item, the empanada has become a symbol of Madero's numbers. Digital channels already represent 57.1% of restaurant revenue, with delivery alone accounting for 21.8%.

"When we send delivery through iFood, the average order cancellation rate in Brazil is 11%. Ours is 0.5%," he says. "The biggest game today is delivery. But delivery is much harder to deliver good quality to customers."

According to Durski, the difference lies in execution. Each order goes through a double-check system, with photos recorded before and after assembly. Within 24 hours, the company contests cancellations made by delivery companies to try to reverse most of them.

At the same time, Madero strengthened customer service, which more than doubled in size and now responds in real-time via WhatsApp, with immediate refunds or vouchers in case of problems.

These numbers help explain why the company is diversifying its channels. Traditional restaurants, as with much of the food sector, lost traction since the pandemic. What sustains growth is the hybrid model.

Beyond empanadas, now sold in 212 stores served by delivery companies, Madero is preparing the launch of vacuum-sealed ready meals, expanding its portfolio and betting on the growth of "Empório," a convenience store model focused on frozen products. With two stores open in Curitiba, the chain will inaugurate two more in the coming weeks.

"We are building 12, in addition to the two we have already opened and two more that will be inaugurated. We should close this year with 20 Empório Madero," Durski states.

For this strategy to work, he relies on a vertically integrated platform that concentrates production, logistics, and distribution. Today, more than 95% of food served by the chain comes from its own central kitchen in Ponta Grossa, Paraná.

**Quarterly Numbers**

The first quarter numbers show that Madero continues delivering growth and profitability. Gross revenue totaled R$ 577 million in the first quarter, up 5.1% year-over-year. Net revenue was R$ 501.6 million. Net profit reached R$ 20.8 million, maintaining a positive trend since the end of 2024. Adjusted Ebitda reached R$ 141 million, with a margin of 28.1%. Leverage stood at 1.7x, with a projection to fall to around 1.3x by year-end.

Same-store sales (SSS) advanced 1.7% in the quarter. In traditional formats, performance was negative: Madero Steak House and Madero Burger declined 1.5%, while Jerônimo fell 5.8%. What held up SSS was the model combining both brands in the same space. In this format, revenue advanced 32.3% and SSS reached 18.3%. Today, stores with both Madero and Jerônimo together account for 22% of net revenue.

"We continue to have profit quarter after quarter, which helps reduce debt. It's difficult to find another company in the sector with numbers like ours," says Marcelo Aldenucci, the group's investor relations director.

"For the rest of the year we expect to continue investing in empanadas and in our Paraguay store, which we recently opened and already shows excellent results," he adds.

In this report, Durski signs as CEO and CFO, alongside Aldenucci. Executive Bruno Gentil, who held the position before Ariel Szwarc moved to the Council presidency, left Madero.
