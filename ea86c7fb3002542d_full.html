# The Push and Pull of Electricity Bill Increases: What Companies Are Saying

The request from the Lula Government for the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) to postpone electricity bill increases this year has been taken by the regulator to sector companies – which are now beginning to respond, each in their own way.

Energisa stated that it accepts postponing the increases for its distributors in Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul for one week while Brasília studies measures to relieve consumers, provided that the effects on companies are compensated later. CPFL Energia, meanwhile, presented an alternative proposal that would reduce tariff increases now but would recover these amounts between 2027 and 2029. Consumers would, however, be charged the financial costs of raising funds to allow for the postponement.

Equatorial Energia, on the other hand, wants the immediate application of a tariff increase for its rates in Amapá that has been under discussion for months at Aneel.

The agency removed from tomorrow's board meeting agenda the tariff adjustment processes of Energisa's concessionaires, following the company's position. However, CPFL's proposals for the CPFL Paulista rate adjustment and CPFL Santa Cruz tariff review, as well as the tariff increases for Equatorial CEA in Amapá, will still need to be analyzed.

The Ministry had requested that Aneel postpone all tariff decisions while the Government studies alternatives, but sector regulation requires prior agreement from companies for this.

The proposal under study in Brasília would involve a BNDES loan to distributors to compensate for smaller increases, which would require a Provisional Measure to provide legal certainty to the transaction, sector sources told Brazil Journal. Without mitigation measures, the CPFL Paulista tariff adjustment would be approximately 18.6% on average, according to documents available in Aneel's system. Equatorial told the agency that CEA, the concessionaire in Amapá, "will have an imbalance of approximately R$ 20 million for each month of delay" in the tariff adjustment.

Energisa, for its part, agreed to postpone the analysis of its increase to Aneel's April 14 meeting, "with the intention of contributing to the evaluation of possible alternatives aimed at mitigating the impacts of tariff increases on customers." The company added, however, that the postponement must be "compensated and properly adjusted."

Among experts, the measures the Government is studying to hold down electricity bills have been viewed as a "workaround" that would at best postpone impacts on consumers, which would later return to tariffs with interest. The plans, however, are not seen as surprising, following various similar proposals in recent years, also involving loans to distributors.

"Since we're in an election year, the Government wants to avoid the wear and tear of explaining two-digit tariff increases. We've seen this movie before and we know how it ends," said one manager.
