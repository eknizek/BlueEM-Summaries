# The Push-and-Pull of Electricity Bill Increases: What Companies Are Saying

The request from the Lula Government for the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) to postpone electricity bill increases this year has been brought by the regulator to sector companies – which are now beginning to respond, each in their own way.

Energisa said it accepts postponing the rate adjustments of its distributors in Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul for one week while Brasília studies measures to relieve consumers, as long as the effects on companies are compensated later. CPFL Energia, meanwhile, presented an alternative proposal that would reduce rate increases now but recover those values between 2027 and 2029. Consumers would be charged, however, for the financial costs of raising funds to allow the postponement.

Equatorial Energia, on the other hand, wants the immediate application of a rate adjustment for its tariffs in Amapá that has been under discussion at Aneel for months.

The agency removed from tomorrow's board meeting agenda the rate adjustment processes for Energisa's concessionaires, following the company's positioning. However, CPFL's proposals for CPFL Paulista rate adjustment and CPFL Santa Cruz tariff review, as well as Equatorial CEA rate adjustments in Amapá, still need to be analyzed.

The Ministry had asked Aneel to postpone all tariff decisions while the Government studies alternatives, but sector regulation requires prior company agreement for this.

The proposal under study in Brasília would involve a BNDES loan to distributors to compensate for smaller adjustments, which would require a Provisional Measure to provide legal certainty to the transaction, sector sources told Brazil Journal. Without mitigation measures, CPFL Paulista's rate adjustment would be approximately 18.6% on average, according to documents available in Aneel's system. Equatorial told the agency that CEA, the concessionaire in Amapá, "will have a deficit of approximately R$ 20 million for each month of delay" in the rate adjustment.

Energisa, for its part, agreed to postpone analysis of its adjustment to Aneel's meeting on April 14, "in order to contribute to the evaluation of possible alternatives aimed at mitigating the impacts of rate adjustments on customers." The company added, however, that the postponement should be "compensated and properly corrected."

Among experts, the measures the Government is studying to hold down electricity bills have been seen as a "workaround" that at most would postpone impacts on consumers, which would later return to tariffs with interest. The plans, however, are not seen with surprise, following several similar proposals in recent years, also involving loans to distributors.

"Since it's an election year, the Government wants to avoid the wear and tear of explaining high double-digit rate increases. We've seen this movie before and we know how it ends," said one manager.
