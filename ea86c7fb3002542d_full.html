# The Push-and-Pull of Electricity Bill Increases: What Companies Are Saying

The request from the Lula Government for the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) to postpone electricity bill increases this year has been presented by the regulator to sector companies — which are now beginning to respond, each in their own way.

Energisa said it accepts postponing adjustments for its distributors in Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul by one week while Brasília studies measures to ease consumer burden, provided that the effects on companies are compensated later. CPFL Energia, meanwhile, presented an alternative proposal that would reduce tariff increases now but recover these amounts between 2027 and 2029. However, consumers would be charged the financial costs of raising capital to allow for the postponement. Equatorial Energia, on the other hand, wants the immediate application of a tariff adjustment in Amapá that has been under discussion at Aneel for months.

The agency removed from tomorrow's board meeting agenda the tariff adjustment processes for Energisa concessionaires, following the company's position. However, CPFL's proposals for CPFL Paulista rate adjustment and CPFL Santa Cruz tariff review, as well as the rate adjustments for Equatorial CEA in Amapá, still need to be analyzed.

The Ministry had asked Aneel to postpone all tariff decisions while the Government studies alternatives, but sector regulation requires prior company agreement for this. The proposal under study in Brasília would involve a BNDES loan to distributors to compensate for smaller rate increases, which would require a Provisional Measure to provide legal certainty to the transaction, sector sources told Brazil Journal.

Without mitigation measures, CPFL Paulista's tariff adjustment, for example, would be an average of 18.6%, according to documents available in Aneel's system. Equatorial told the agency that CEA, the concession holder in Amapá, "will have an imbalance of approximately R$ 20 million for each month of delay" in the tariff adjustment.

Energisa, in turn, agreed to leave analysis of its adjustment for Aneel's April 14 meeting, "with the purpose of contributing to the evaluation of possible alternatives aimed at mitigating the impacts of tariff adjustments on customers." The company added, however, that the postponement must be "compensated and properly adjusted."

Among experts, the measures the Government is studying to contain electricity bills have been seen as a "workaround" that at best would postpone impacts on consumers, who would later return to tariffs with interest. The plans, however, are not viewed with surprise, following various similar proposals in recent years, also involving loans to distributors.

"Since we're in an election year, the Government wants to avoid the wear and tear of explaining high double-digit tariff increases. We've seen this movie before and we know how it ends," said one manager.
