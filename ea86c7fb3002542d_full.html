# The Push and Pull of Electricity Rate Adjustments: What Companies Are Saying

The request from the Lula Government for the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) to postpone electricity bill increases this year was brought by the regulator to sector companies – which are now beginning to respond, each in their own way.

Energisa said it accepts postponing rate adjustments for its distributors in Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul for one week while Brasília studies measures to ease the burden on consumers, provided that the effects on companies are compensated later. CPFL Energia, meanwhile, presented an alternative proposal that would reduce tariff increases now but would recover these values between 2027 and 2029. Consumers would, however, be charged the financial costs of raising funds to allow the postponement.

Equatorial Energia, on the other hand, wants the immediate application of a rate adjustment for its tariffs in Amapá that has been under discussion at Aneel for months.

The agency removed from the agenda of its board meeting tomorrow the tariff adjustment processes for Energisa concessionaires, following the company's position. However, the proposals from CPFL for the adjustment of CPFL Paulista and tariff review of CPFL Santa Cruz, as well as the rate adjustments for Equatorial CEA in Amapá, should still be analyzed.

The Ministry had asked Aneel to postpone all tariff decisions while the Government studies alternatives, but sector regulation requires prior agreement from companies for this to happen.

The proposal being studied in Brasília would involve a loan from BNDES to distributors to compensate for smaller increases, which would require a Provisional Measure to provide legal certainty to the transaction, sector sources told Brazil Journal.

Without mitigation measures, the tariff adjustment for CPFL Paulista, for example, would be an average of 18.6%, according to documents available in Aneel's system. Equatorial told the agency that CEA, the concessionaire in Amapá, "will face a shortfall of approximately R$ 20 million for each month of delay" in the tariff adjustment.

Energisa, in turn, agreed to postpone the analysis of its adjustment for Aneel's April 14 meeting, "with the aim of contributing to the evaluation of possible alternatives designed to mitigate the impacts of tariff increases on customers." The company added, however, that the postponement must be "compensated and duly corrected."

Among experts, the measures the Government is studying to hold down electricity bills have been seen as a "workaround" that would at most postpone impacts on consumers, which would later return to tariffs with interest. The plans, however, are not viewed with surprise, after various similar proposals in recent years, also involving loans to distributors.

"Since it's an election year, the Government wants to avoid the wear and tear of explaining double-digit rate increases. We've seen this movie before and we know how it ends," said one manager.
