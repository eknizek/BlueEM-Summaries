# The tug-of-war over electricity bill increases: what companies are saying

President Lula's request for the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) to postpone electricity bill increases this year has been relayed by the regulator to sector companies — which are now beginning to respond, each in their own way.

Energisa said it accepts postponing increases for its distributors in Mato Grosso and Mato Grosso do Sul by one week while Brasília studies measures to ease consumer burden, provided that the effects on companies are compensated later. Meanwhile, CPFL Energia presented an alternative proposal that would reduce rate increases now but recover these amounts between 2027 and 2029. However, consumers would be charged the financial costs of raising funds to allow the postponement.

Equatorial Energia, on the other hand, wants the immediate application of a rate adjustment for its operations in Amapá that has been under discussion at Aneel for months.

The agency removed from tomorrow's board meeting agenda the rate adjustment processes for Energisa's concessionaires, following the company's position. However, CPFL's proposals for CPFL Paulista's rate adjustment and CPFL Santa Cruz's tariff review, as well as Equatorial CEA's rate adjustments in Amapá, are still to be analyzed.

The Ministry had asked Aneel to postpone all tariff decisions while the Government studies alternatives, but sector regulation requires prior agreement from companies to do so. The proposal being studied in Brasília would involve a BNDES loan to distributors to compensate for lower increases, which would require a Provisional Measure to provide legal security to the transaction, sector sources told Brazil Journal.

Without mitigation measures, the CPFL Paulista rate adjustment, for example, would be an average of 18.6%, according to documents available in Aneel's system. Equatorial told the agency that CEA, the concessionaire in Amapá, "will have an imbalance of approximately R$ 20 million for each month of delay" in the rate adjustment.

Energisa, for its part, agreed to leave analysis of its adjustment for Aneel's April 14 meeting, "with the aim of contributing to the evaluation of possible alternatives designed to mitigate the impacts of rate adjustments on customers." The company added, however, that the postponement should be "compensated and properly adjusted."

Among experts, the measures the Government is studying to hold down electricity bills have been seen as a "workaround" that at most would delay impacts to consumers, which would later return with interest. The plans, however, are not viewed with surprise, following similar proposals in recent years, also involving loans to distributors.

"Since it's an election year, the Government wants to avoid the wear and tear of explaining high double-digit tariff increases. We've seen this movie before and we know how it ends," said one manager.
