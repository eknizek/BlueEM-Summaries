# Latin America Absorbs 'Relatively Well' Impact of Iran War, UBS Says

Latin America is absorbing "relatively well" the impact of the Iran war thanks to stronger economic structures, external buffers, and greater institutional credibility, according to UBS.

"In our opinion, Latin America is absorbing relatively well, so far, the impact of the Iran war," said Alejo Czerwonko, Chief Investment Officer for Emerging Markets of the Americas at UBS Chief Investment Office.

He explained that the economic fundamentals, external reserves, and institutional credibility built over the past decade "are holding up." Overall, he states, risk rating agencies perceive a panorama of stability in Latin America's classifications.

"It is important to note that, while exposure to oil is a differentiating factor, the quality of domestic policy decisions is equally important, if not more so," observed Czerwonko. In UBS's view, "the climate among policymakers seemed to be one of cautious optimism."

According to UBS, central banks maintain a restrictive and vigilant tone in the face of possible inflationary effects, and Peru stands out for its resilience, while Colombia emerges as the region's most complex case. On the fiscal front, prudence prevails, despite "some exceptions."

Argentina maintains its fiscal discipline, Chile and Peru have room for consolidation, Brazil faces less pressure, Mexico seeks to balance growth and discipline, and Colombia faces greater challenges. "Colombia finds itself at the opposite extreme, with authorities focused on reducing interest costs, given limited room to cut spending before elections," according to the UBS report.

For the Swiss financial institution, politics remains the main factor of uncertainty in the region. "There is less confidence among experts that the region advances uniformly toward conservative-leaning governments."

Financial analyst Gregorio Gandini noted that initially, some countries benefited from rising oil prices, such as Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia, as producers, although he warned that as the conflict drags on, its effects are becoming evident. He explained that Chile, for example—as an importer of oil and gasoline—is already registering price increases. In Brazil's case, the impact could be felt in its agribusiness, given its high dependence on fertilizer imports, some of which transit through the Strait of Hormuz. In any case, "for investors, the region becomes a source of risk diversification," observed Gandini.

## UBS Outlook by Country

Brazil is handling the war's impact through fuel subsidies, maintaining fiscal discipline and monetary caution, though the presidential election could force concessions that affect credibility. "The risk is that October's closely contested presidential race could force fiscal concessions that harm the credibility gained in recent years," he explains. The Finance Ministry maintains fiscal discipline and Brazil's Central Bank shows no rush to cut rates, though the presidential election could cause tension on both fronts.

For now, according to UBS, the war's impact is being well absorbed in the country, with fuel subsidies fully financed by extraordinary oil revenues.

Mexico seeks to boost growth without compromising fiscal discipline, amid uncertainty regarding the T-MEC and doubts about the pace of monetary policy. "In our opinion, it is likely that Moody's will change Mexico's negative outlook on its sovereign rating this year; although recognizing fiscal advances and favorable trade conditions for Pemex, the agency highlighted increased debt and off-balance-sheet risks," UBS states. "We believe Mexico's investment grade is not in question, but its exact position within the category is less clear."

Meanwhile, Argentina's IMF program aims to consolidate reforms by 2026, before electoral volatility expected in 2027.

Chile arrives more strengthened to face external shocks, with controlled inflation and institutional credibility, although it may moderate its fiscal adjustment.

Peru's electoral results generate uncertainty with the entry of a left-wing candidate in the race.

Although Colombia benefits from oil, the country faces greater challenges due to internal tensions, division between Gustavo Petro's government and the country's central bank, and institutional risks. According to UBS analysts, Colombia is heading toward a closely contested election. "Although all signs point to a swing in favor of center-right forces in the upcoming elections, we believe the race will still be tight, due to security concerns and the fact that recent tensions between Petro and the Central Bank have likely diminished Ivan Cepeda's popularity among moderate voters," the report states.

Venezuela considers a political transition possible but faces major challenges regarding debt and attracting investment to the oil sector. "Recovery depends on the oil sector, but the lack of a clear legal framework prevents investment. Neither the government nor the US seem to prioritize debt restructuring for now," the report indicates.
