# Colombia and Venezuela Leaders Agree on Intelligence Sharing and Discuss Electricity

Colombian President Gustavo Petro and Venezuelan President Delcy Rodríguez agreed on Friday (24th) to intelligence-sharing measures to combat crime along their shared border, as well as reinforced trade and bilateral efforts to ensure electricity supply to western Venezuela, which frequently suffers blackouts. The two leaders are under intense pressure from U.S. President Donald Trump — in Rodríguez's case, to open her country's economy to foreign investment, and in Petro's case, to expand drug trafficking enforcement.

The meeting between Petro and Rodríguez at the presidential palace in Caracas was the second for the Venezuelan president with a fellow head of state, following a brief trip to Grenada earlier this month.

"It makes no sense for Colombia or Venezuela to seek elsewhere, in another hemisphere, what we can obtain in our own territories," Rodríguez said in a joint statement with Petro, adding that both governments should increase bilateral trade and seek energy cooperation. "Electrical interconnection is already a step forward, as is gas interconnection, through which we can not only supply gas to Colombia, but also jointly export gas to other countries."

Colombia and Venezuela have deep historical and cultural ties, especially along their shared 2,200-kilometer border, where many families are binational. Approximately 3 million Venezuelan migrants have settled in Colombia in recent years, fleeing the economic crisis in their home country. Although the border region is a center of over $1 billion in annual trade, it also houses drug trafficking, smuggling, and other illegal activities carried out by armed groups, including gangs and Colombian guerrilla organizations.

Human rights groups and previous Colombian governments alleged that armed groups work with the support or complicity of Venezuelan military, an accusation Caracas denies. Petro and Rodríguez's predecessor, Nicolás Maduro, who was ousted in a U.S. operation in January, had already increased the number of troops stationed on the border to combat drug trafficking. The two countries are taking "a very serious and very comprehensive approach" to combat crime along the border, Rodríguez said, and will immediately establish "mechanisms to share information and develop intelligence" to combat drug and fuel smuggling, among other crimes. Petro said the border should belong to the citizens of both countries, not to criminal groups.
