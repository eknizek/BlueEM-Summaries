# The Bottlenecks Suffocating Brazil's Critical Minerals Potential

From Poços de Caldas to Mount Weld, from Washington to Hannover, from Tokyo to Brussels, there is a global movement around critical minerals that has no parallel, in natural resources, since the oil rush of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. Dozens of bilateral agreements. The Pentagon becoming a shareholder in mining companies. Development banks and multilateral organizations participating in the negotiation of price floors, offtakes, and long-term public financing. M&A and IPOs in large volumes. All of this happening before our eyes for years.

I am from Itabira, in Minas Gerais, the land of Vale's first mine. Like my partners here at Ore Investments, my family has worked in mining for generations. I speak, therefore, from the place of someone who lives off the sector.

Brazil is simultaneously a target and a protagonist in this movement. Agreements and memorandums have been signed with South Korea, India, Spain and Germany, and the EU-Mercosur agreement comes into force in May. With continental territory and rich mineral diversity in grades and volumes, Brazil is privileged in critical minerals, as is customary with natural resources. But geology, although it is the first step, is not everything.

To not miss a structuring opportunity for the country, we need to focus on the right questions. Even with some of the world's largest reserves, Brazil today produces about 0.09% of the global volume of critical minerals. In our investment firm, we have mapped more than 500 critical mineral assets in the country, and only about 10% of them are operational. Meanwhile, Brazil has almost 30,000 projects in the mining concession request phase, trying to overcome obstacles to become operational mines.

The question we need to focus on today is: how does Brazil build an ecosystem that (i) takes projects off paper within an acceptable timeframe and (ii) generates verticalization in critical minerals within the country? Without these two fronts succeeding, more mines will be built around the world, and much of what are promising companies today may not become operational in the coming years. Furthermore, without verticalization, we will continue as exporters of raw ore.

A recent Boston Consulting Group study points out that critical minerals are not unlocked by bilateral contracts. At the speed and scale the world needs, they will be unlocked by multilateral coordination that combines governments, investors, miners, refineries and customers, using risk-sharing instruments, traceability, price bands and long-term demand predictability, among others.

Brazil has all the elements to build this ecosystem: reserves, scale, internal and potential global partners' demand, strategic geographic location, clean and cheap energy, world-class track record in some commodities. However, there are three fronts where the bottlenecks are concentrated, and where most of the effort should be: (i) environmental licensing, (ii) access to capital, and (iii) verticalization.

**Environmental licensing.** S&P Global calculates that the global average time between discovery and production of a mine exceeds 17 years for projects that entered operation between 2020 and 2023. Almost double what it was 15 years ago. Brazil's situation is aggravated by the overlapping of competencies between federal, state and municipal authorities and the risk of litigation of licenses. Regarding litigation, the company and the environmental agency spend years in the correct administrative process, and at the time of license issuance, a third party without adequate technical apparatus files a lawsuit requesting an injunction without even hearing the environmental agency and the miner themselves. This is not environmental stewardship. It is legal insecurity.

Law 15.190/2025 created maximum deadlines and a Special Environmental License for strategic projects. It is a step forward. What is missing is practical regulation and—more difficult—an institutional agreement on who decides what, in order to avoid litigation for litigation's sake. We do not want less rigor nor shortcuts. Just predictability. Technical analysis, on schedule, with broad dialogue and transparency.

**Access to capital.** Agriculture has FIAGRO. Infrastructure has FI-Infra. Mining lacks FI-Min. Another angle is incentivized debentures, with tax benefits consistent with the strategic importance of the sector. Mining does not have an incentivized financial instrument designed for its characteristics. Without this, a significant portion of investments will continue to depend on foreign equity, or will not happen on time.

Another complex challenge: project finance, during the construction phase, effectively with banks. Brazil lacks mineral collateral instruments, structured guarantees and risk mitigation mechanisms that enable financing without a balance sheet that guarantees all debt.

**Verticalization.** Downstream does not emerge by decree. It emerges from importing or developing technology, logistics, clean and cheap energy, anchored demand, technical standardization and patient capital. All this with competitiveness. We should pursue the development of two or three regional hubs of critical minerals, to reach refining in Brazil. And the difference this makes is striking.

A Deloitte study with AYA Earth Partners points out that, mining at the current pace, the sector adds R$ 47 billion to GDP by 2050; mining more and processing locally, it rises to R$ 233 billion; mining, processing and refining internally, it reaches R$ 243 billion. The difference between exporting concentrate and refining is five times more added value for Brazil. It is not just mining: it is a vertically integrated, competitive and resilient supply chain.

A good lesson on how to act in critical minerals comes from Japan and South Korea, two countries that do not have such abundant geology. Japan reduced its Chinese rare earth dependency from 90% to less than 50% in two decades—with state venture capital through JOGMEC, equity participation in projects abroad (including Lynas Rare Earths), strategic stockpiles of up to 180 days and a recycling ecosystem that treats urban scrap as a domestic mine. South Korea runs the same playbook at a faster pace. POSCO, LG Chem and Samsung SDI are buying upstream and locking in offtakes on four continents.

Once again, the lesson is about creating an architecture. It is about directing the discussion and effort to the right bottlenecks, and attacking them together, in a coordinated manner. And there is urgency. Brazil is privileged in mineral resources, but we are not alone. There are several projects with significant volumes and grades in various regions of the world, in licensing, development and construction phases, competing for global financing. In critical minerals, global demand volumes are not that large, and markets are not that mature. Price volatility is higher, and cycles, although acute, shorter. Projects that become operational sooner, with quality and competitiveness, will be the long-term winners, while others will fall by the wayside.
