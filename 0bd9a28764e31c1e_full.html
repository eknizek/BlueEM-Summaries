# Andreessen Horowitz and Kaszek invest in Segura, the startup bringing AI to insurance brokers

Operating quietly for two years, the AI startup for the insurance sector is now officially coming out of stealth with a seed round of R$ 45 million.

Segura, a Brazilian artificial intelligence startup for the insurance sector, received a seed round of R$ 45 million, co-led by Andreessen Horowitz and Kaszek. The company, which had been operating in stealth mode for two years, was founded by Luís Alberto Nogueira, Lucca Buffara, and Pedro Nobrega. It is now officially entering the market with the proposition of enhancing the work of insurance brokers, who represent 80% of sector sales. To accomplish this, the startup invests in two AI platforms: Helena, which facilitates data access and relationships with insurance companies, and Conversas, which enables real-time interaction with clients. The startup already has more than 3,000 brokerages and aims to expand to 10,000 by 2026. The funds raised will be used to strengthen the team and improve technological infrastructure, aiming to scale operations in a precise and reliable manner.

One of Silicon Valley's most influential venture capital firms, Andreessen Horowitz (a16z), has been leading, alongside Sequoia Capital, the boom in investments in artificial intelligence startups. In this scenario, a Brazilian newcomer is the recipient of one of the American firm's new checks. Operating for two years in stealth mode, Segura, owner of an AI platform for the insurance sector, is officially emerging from hiding with a seed round of R$ 45 million, co-led by a16z and Kaszek, another protagonist in the venture capital industry—in this case, for Latin America. The round also includes Big Bets and prominent angel investors, such as Assaf Wand, founder of American company Hippo; Anderson Thees, partner at Redpoint Ventures; Fersen Lambranho, board president of GP Investments; Marcelo Blay, founder of Minuto Seguros; and the Almeida Braga family, controllers of Icatu.

"All these names are very respected and quite thorough when deciding on an investment," says Luís Alberto Nogueira, co-founder and CEO of Segura, to NeoFeed. "So, coming to market with these endorsements is a sign that we are on the right track."

The thesis that attracted this lineup was built by Nogueira, Buffara, and Nobrega. Friends from PUC-Rio's engineering program, they began contact with AI in 2018 at Cyberlabs, a company already focused in that area which, in 2021, merged with PSafe, a cybersecurity company. The insurance market, in turn, came onto the trio's radar in 2022, when Nogueira and Buffara moved to Akad Seguros, an operation that originated from Argo Seguros and was rebranded after being purchased that same year by Cyberlabs and GP Investments. The decision to unite these two worlds came in May 2024, when the three founded Segura. The proposal? Keep brokers as the essential link in this chain—the channel accounts for 80% of sector sales. But at the same time, supercharge these professionals' work with AI resources. "AI will bring a new paradigm to this broker because, with it, he can delve into details of unstructured issues," says Nogueira. "But for this to happen, you need to build infrastructure to help how he relates both with insurance companies and with the end client."

To improve these dialogues, Segura works with a universe currently formed by approximately 150,000 brokers operating in the country. And from the premise that these professionals deal daily and manually with an extremely complex dynamic. This routine includes, on one hand, a tangle of systems from different insurance companies, with all the nuances of products, coverage, quotes, and constant changes in conditions. And, on the other end, the challenge of finding the most suitable offerings for each client's budget and needs.

Segura's model is based on two AI platforms. Launched a year ago, Helena is the channel with insurance companies that, among other features, speeds up access to policy data, coverage, and quotes. It also verifies daily changes in the conditions of each offering or sector regulations. A second, more recent tool, launched two weeks ago, is Conversas, through which the broker interacts in real time with end clients, supported by a framework of information about products, contracted policies, and interaction history. The idea is that the two platforms, integrated with WhatsApp, help eliminate bureaucratic tasks and provide support for more efficient and, at the same time, more personalized service. And with considerable autonomy for the broker to define their level of participation in each stage of this process. "The broker configures how these AI agents function," says Nogueira. "He can choose for the assistants to answer everything automatically, without needing his interaction, or set a guard rail so they only answer certain questions."

Among other features, the platforms also allow brokers to access information such as which clients in their database have insurance renewals coming up. Or which ones have auto insurance but not home insurance. In this model, the startup is remunerated by insurance companies, and access to the platforms is free for brokers.

Actually in operation for a year, Segura already has a base of more than 3,000 brokerages, which are mostly formed by two to seven professionals. With an eye on capturing a larger slice of a market that annually moves approximately R$ 250 billion in premiums, the company aims to reach 10,000 brokerages in its portfolio by the end of 2026.

On the path to making this number a reality, Segura already has well-defined the use of raised funds. A first destination will be strengthening its team, currently formed by 25 professionals. More than quantity, the focus will be on further qualifying the team. "The direction is to keep bringing the best talent, but not necessarily expand the team much," states Nogueira. "Our initial plan was to reach 50 people by the end of the year, but with everything that's happening in AI, what we're seeing is that teams will be increasingly lean." The second will be technology infrastructure. "One thing is doing vibe coding and putting a product online. Another is having it at scale, precisely and assertively, which requires very expensive construction," he says. "And this is a trust industry. So, a good portion of resources will be directed to this front."
