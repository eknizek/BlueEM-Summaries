# Oil Companies Win Legal Challenge Against Export Tax; Judge Rules Tax 'Merely Revenue-Raising'

Shell, TotalEnergies, Equinor, and other international oil companies obtained an injunction in court to suspend the taxation of oil exports recently decreed by the Lula Government. The judicial decision accepted the companies' arguments that the tax has a "merely revenue-raising" nature and therefore could not have come into force immediately. The Government itself admitted it wanted to raise R$ 30 billion through the taxation, aimed at financing measures to keep fuel prices down, such as a cut in federal taxes and a diesel subsidy.

"When we implemented the tax, provisionally, it was because we considered that there was exorbitant profit from oil exports in Brazil," Energy and Mines Minister Alexandre Silveira told journalists after the injunction was issued. "With the war, oil prices rose significantly. Why not contribute provisionally to lower the price of diesel and gasoline? We did our part, we removed federal taxes."

Under Brazilian law, new taxes need to be approved with a one-year or 90-day advance notice in some cases – this is the principle of tax temporality. To justify the immediate taxation, the Government argued that in this case it was not creating a new tax – merely raising the rate from zero to 12%. The Government also argued in court that greater advance notice would not be necessary because the taxation of oil companies would have an "extrafiscal nature, aimed at regulating foreign trade and exchange rate policy." It didn't work.

The Government is already appealing. The Attorney General's Office of the Treasury even alleged that the judge's decision cited "an absolutely non-existent normative text." Minister Silveira, speaking to journalists, also used the war in Iran as justification. "In grave moments, grave measures. In moments of normalcy, milder measures. It is the role of the private sector to profit. And it is the role of Government to defend the population and public policy."

Silveira also renewed his defense of Petrobras's repurchase of the Mataripe refinery, as well as the construction of new units in the country. The acquisition of the refinery, privatized during the Bolsonaro Government and acquired by Acelen, of Mubadala Capital, had already been promised by President Lula in a speech at the end of March. "It might take a while, but we will buy it," Lula said after participating in an event alongside Petrobras CEO Magda Chambriard.

In a teleconference at the end of 2025, Magda admitted there were already conversations with Mubadala, but said at that time that she would only do the transaction "at fair prices," and that so far "it made no sense." Minister Silveira said the repurchase will only occur if it is "a reasonable deal" for the state company. "People often think the Government will decide to buy. That's not it. Petrobras is a company listed on the New York stock exchange. It has its shareholders, who must be respected, and we respect their governance."

Despite the minister's comments, the Lula Government has clearly been increasing interference in Petrobras. This week, pressure from Brasília was decisive in the departure of the state company's Logistics director, Claudio Schlosser, removed from his position by the board after negative reactions from Lula to a liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) auction by Petrobras. The Government is also nominating the current secretary of Economic Policy at the Finance Ministry, Guilherme Mello, a licensed professor at Unicamp, to the presidency of Petrobras's board.
