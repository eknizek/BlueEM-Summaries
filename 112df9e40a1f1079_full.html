# War has made the world more expensive, and a more expensive world means insurance will cost more

The world is going through a complicated phase. International relations have lost the peacemaking tools created after World War II, and the "soft peace" that prevailed over the last 80 years has been replaced by the use of brute force, without much discussion. What is startling is that the rupture with the old order was an act of will by the President of the United States, traditionally the great guarantor of an order based on negotiations rather than force.

Today the planet is the scene of wars in Europe, the Middle East, Africa, and Asia. Never since 1945 has the world witnessed so many conflicts at the same time. Some more costly to the global economy, others practically irrelevant, they have changed relations between nations, weakened ties and alliances, created poles of tension, threatening international order and generating a serious economic and financial crisis due to increased oil prices.

The great knot is the Middle East. The war promoted by the United States and Israel against Iran has affected, as was obvious it would, oil traffic through the Strait of Hormuz. Overnight, the price per barrel jumped from $60 to over $100. And what is more serious: it created product shortages, with international aviation canceling thousands of flights due to the new reality of aviation kerosene, and several countries adopting emergency measures to minimize material and economic damages.

International bodies have already priced in the drop in economic growth and the increase in inflation around the world. Including the United States, directly responsible for what is happening, despite being the largest oil producer, already feels the weight of the new scenario with price increases for a large range of products.

The impact on the global economy generates reactions throughout the entire production chain, like an enormous domino where the pieces are nations. Europe already feels the effects of the war. China needs an alternative for oil imported from the Persian Gulf. Brazil will have problems with essential inputs for agribusiness. In short, few nations will not be affected by the consequences of the war.

The economic crisis has already happened. Even if peace is achieved immediately, a return to normal conditions will not happen overnight. Today, the world is more expensive and should remain so for a good while. A more expensive world means insurance will cost more.

Insurance operations are among the most globalized. Risks from the other side of the planet affect the price of policies everywhere. And that is what will happen, firstly because of the increase in insurance prices in the Middle East, and secondly, because of the economic crisis that will increase inflation and the price of products throughout the world.

In simple language, as insurance works through communicating vessels that interconnect companies, Brazilians will pay more for insurance on their automobiles or their companies as a result of events completely outside their control, but that will impact our economy.
