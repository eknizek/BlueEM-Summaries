When Marx and Adam Smith Begin to Agree

# When Marx and Adam Smith Begin to Agree

If there is a useful lens for interpreting the current moment in technology, perhaps it does not come from Silicon Valley, but from Karl Marx. The idea that whoever controls the means of production captures value remains surprisingly current. Over time, these means have changed form, but not logic. They were once land, then physical capital, then industrial infrastructure. In recent decades, they have become software. It was on this basis that the SaaS world was built. Entire companies organized around the premise that code was scarce, difficult to produce, and therefore highly defensible. The playbook became well-known. Scalable products, aggressive sales forces, long contracts and high retention.

But around this, an institutional "shell" also formed. Layers of sales, marketing, customer success and, above all, legal teams, designed to sustain retention more than necessarily delivering value. Frequent updates that often "burden" the user with complexity. And, almost like a Dilbert comic strip, the feeling that the true core of many of these companies is not the software, but the ability to produce contracts without exit clauses.

This model worked because software was, in fact, scarce. Building and maintaining systems required specialized knowledge and complex structures. In large corporations, this translated into squads with hundreds or thousands of developers, operating as internal code factories. But much of this effort is not in innovation, but in maintenance, adaptation and exception handling. Software, which promised to simplify the world, became something requiring a permanent machine to keep functioning.

It is in this context that, in early March, a Sequoia Capital letter gained traction. The thesis, articulated by Julien Chaumond, does not come from a traditional investor. Chaumond was one of the founders of Hugging Face and was at the center of the recent democratization of artificial intelligence, helping transform previously restricted models into accessible infrastructure. He saw, in practice, the marginal cost of intelligence fall. His reading starts from a direct point: despite all the sophistication of software, companies' actual spending remains concentrated in services. For every dollar invested in SaaS, about six are directed toward execution. This is the central point. Software never captured the bulk of value. Only the most efficient layer within a larger system.

Artificial intelligence emerges as the first plausible mechanism to arbitrage this difference. By simultaneously reducing the cost of producing software and the cost of executing tasks, it begins to structurally close that one-to-six gap. What was once inevitably human and cost-intensive becomes partially automatable and scalable. The focus shifts from the tool to the task, the result, the execution. This change is already beginning to appear in the capital markets. "Pure SaaS" companies, especially those more dependent on growth through sales and long contracts, have suffered relevant value compressions. Part of this is macro, but part is structural. The moat based on proprietary code, contractual lock-in and that institutional "shell" begins to be questioned in a world where software becomes more accessible and where execution, increasingly assisted by AI, becomes the true differentiator.

Yet history recommends caution with narratives of total rupture. Means of production do not disappear. They coexist and change position in the hierarchy of value. Mechanization did not eliminate human work, only repositioned it. Electrification did not extinguish previous infrastructures. SaaS itself did not end legacy software. The pattern is consistent. The old remains, but loses centrality. Value shifts.

It is exactly this shift that seems to be underway. Software does not cease to exist, but loses isolated prominence. It becomes infrastructure. And as artificial intelligence advances, value shifts toward execution, precisely where those six dollars historically not captured by software were located.

In this new arrangement, a significant portion of work currently concentrated in large squads tends to be compressed or reconfigured. Not because engineering ceases to be necessary, but because intermediation between demand and execution loses ground. Artificial intelligence shortens this distance and, in doing so, begins to hollow out precisely the additional layers that have accumulated around software over the years.

Even as a gradual transition, its potential effects are clear. Lower costs, less complexity, and progressive erosion of models designed to retain rather than deliver. That almost caricatural scenario, worthy of Dilbert, in which contracts imprison clients, begins to lose sustainability.

There is a rare irony here. Karl Marx and Adam Smith, through different paths, end up describing the same point. For Marx, whoever controls the means of production captures value. For Smith, value emerges from the division of labor and execution. Software organized this production, but never captured its larger part, which has always been in execution. Artificial intelligence, by reducing the cost of producing and executing, begins to close that distance between one dollar of SaaS and six dollars of services, and repositioning where value concentrates. In the limit, it is as if artificial intelligence ended up aligning Marx and Smith.
