# EU-Mercosur Trade Agreement Enters Force May 1; Paraguay Becomes Gateway for Brazilian Exporters

The entry into force of the free trade agreement between the four permanent members of Mercosur (Brazil, Argentina, Paraguay, and Uruguay) and the 27 countries of the European Union, which occurs starting Friday, May 1st, represents a long-awaited opportunity for Brazilian companies focused on exports. Beyond consolidating a market of approximately 718 million consumers from both blocs, representing a Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of more than $22 trillion – equivalent to R$ 110.5 trillion – companies from the four South American and European countries will now be able to export products with reduced or zero tariffs, which is the main attraction of the free trade agreement.

Another predicted effect is the increase in Brazilian commercial reach. Today, the country represents about 9% of global imports. With the agreement, this number could jump to more than 37%.

In this regard, however, a group of more than 200 Brazilian companies has more advantages than other national companies in competing for this rich European market. These are companies that in recent years have transferred production lines to Paraguay, attracted by the Maquila Law – a package of tax benefits offered by the Paraguayan government. Among the advantages are a single export tax of 1% on added value, total exemption from import taxes on inputs, operating costs up to 40% lower, exchange rate stability, plus cheaper labor due to lower labor charges, forming an irresistible package for companies to escape the so-called "Brazil Cost."

"Paraguay, in practice, has become a tax haven for Brazilian industry," summarizes João Eloi Olenike, executive president of the Brazilian Institute for Planning and Taxation (IBPT). According to him, beyond the special export regime, Paraguay adopts the so-called "triple 10" model: 10% VAT, 10% corporate income tax, and 10% personal income tax. "Even outside the Maquila Law, the maximum rate does not exceed 30% in the sum of these taxes," says Olenike. "In Brazil, corporate income tax alone can reach 34%, without considering consumption taxes."

If migration of these companies already brought advantages in competition for the Brazilian market, with the free trade agreement with the European Union the gains are even greater. The sectors most benefited by the agreement are exactly those where Brazilian companies opened the most factories in Paraguay: textiles (Lupo and Lunelli), footwear (Kidy Calçados), lighting (Koumei), light manufacturing (Buddermeyer), plus chemicals and metallurgy. These sectors are among those that will have immediate zero tariffs in the EU. This means larger scale, higher margins, and the ability to compete globally in the European market not only with domestic industries but eventually with Chinese companies that export to the bloc.

For mature companies like Be8 – a Brazilian advanced biofuel company with one of the largest biorefineries in Latin America installed in Paraguay, Omega Green – the EU-Mercosur agreement simply consolidates its entry into the European market. The plant, in the implementation phase in Villeta, Paraguay, will produce HVO, the so-called green diesel, and SAF (sustainable aviation fuel) primarily for Europe. This month, the company presented the new Be8 BeVant biofuel at the Hannover Fair in Germany. Tests conducted with a Mercedes-Benz Actros truck in Germany pointed to a reduction of approximately 99% of CO2 equivalent compared to fossil fuel diesel – which caught the attention of the European truck industry.

**EU Still Distant**

The advantages that most of these companies that migrated to Paraguay have regarding the European market, however, will likely take some time to be realized. Most installed in the neighboring country targeting the Brazilian market, the largest in Mercosur. And, strictly speaking, they did not plan eventual expansion to the European market, perhaps believing that the EU-Mercosur free trade agreement – whose negotiations dragged on for 27 years with many obstacles – paradoxically would not be completed in the short term.

Lupo, a textile group from Araraquara in the interior of São Paulo and one of Brazil's largest clothing brands, is an example. The company invested R$ 30 million in building a plant in Ciudad del Este, on the border with Brazil, already in operation. The unit has the capacity to produce up to 20 million pairs of socks per year – much less than the 70 million from other units distributed across three Brazilian states.

Carlos Mazzeu, superintendent director of Lupo, confirms that Lupo's industrial unit in Paraguay was conceived for the production of basic products, with a focus on cost reduction and efficiency, aiming to maintain competitiveness against imported products. "The project was entirely designed to meet Mercosur demands, with absolute priority to the Brazilian market, which remains the company's main strategic focus," Mazzeu tells NeoFeed, adding that the use of Maquila Law benefits, including the 1% export tax, strengthens the operation's competitiveness and complements the existing industrial structure in Brazil.

In this context, according to him, there is no immediate plan to use the Paraguayan unit as an export platform for the European market, since it was designed to reinforce Lupo's position in Brazil and Mercosur. Mazzeu states that exports still represent a small portion of Lupo's revenue, but expanding this front is part of the company's strategy and the opening of the European market could help this movement. Beyond exports, Lupo is present in the European market with its own store in Portugal, inaugurated in 2024. "Each new market opened reinforces the lever for export growth, contributing to economies of scale and brand recognition abroad," says the executive. "This advance occurs in a planned manner, maintaining the Brazilian market as a priority, but with increasingly relevant international operations."

Other important Brazilian industries installed in Paraguay with potential to target the European market include Buddemeyer, a major producer of textile articles (bed, table, and bath), lamp manufacturer and lighting products company Koumei, and Fiasul – a textile industry from Rio Grande do Sul that came to Paraguay with an initial investment.
