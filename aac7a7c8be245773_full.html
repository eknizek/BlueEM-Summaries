# Fitch Ratings warns next Colombian government will inherit public debt that will condition entire mandate

The public accounts of Colombia are going through a critical moment and the margin of maneuver for the next president is minimal. This is the central conclusion of the most recent sovereign credit analysis for Latin America published by Fitch Ratings, the most influential risk rating agency in the world.

According to the report, the country has consolidated itself as one of the cases of greatest concern in the region due to fiscal deterioration that will not allow shortcuts or immediate solutions.

The assessment of the entity is forceful: regardless of who wins the 2026 elections, the task of fixing the State's finances will occupy the complete agenda of the mandate that begins in August of that year. The challenge is significant, as it requires an adjustment equivalent to 3% of Gross Domestic Product (GDP), which in practice means that the country will have to increase its collection or cut its expenses by three pesos for every hundred that the national economy produces.

"Regardless of who wins, we believe it will be very difficult to have significant adjustment in fiscal policy. It will be a process of several years," warned Richard Francis, Senior Director of Sovereigns at Fitch Ratings, in a webinar by the rating agency.

## A landscape of debt and low growth

Colombia's current situation is the result of a divergence from other countries with BB rating (moderate risk). While a few years ago the Colombian fiscal balance was near the average of this group, today it registers significantly higher deficits. Fitch projects that this trend will continue in the short term, affecting solvency indicators.

"The deficit will increase this year, the interest burden will increase this year and the debt-to-GDP ratio will increase this year," Francis stated.

This increase in the interest burden, which is what the State pays annually only for the money it owes, is already historically higher than comparable nations. To this scenario is added a slowdown in economic growth. Investment has not managed to recover its historical levels prior to the pandemic, which has eroded long-term growth potential. The rating agency warned that what was previously estimated between 3% and 3.5%, has now declined.

"Colombia historically has grown above the average for Latin America. Now we think it is below 3% for sure and there are downside risks," said Richard Francis in the rating agency's information space.

## Inflation and institutional alerts

The economic context is also being pressured by inflation. The 23% increase in the minimum wage during the current administration of Gustavo Petro has driven up prices, forcing the Central Bank to raise its interest rates by 200 basis points between January and March 2026. This increase in credit costs slows household consumption and business investment.

Fitch Ratings also expressed concern about the country's institutional framework. Specifically, it mentioned that the departure of the Finance Minister from the last meeting of the Central Bank's board raises questions about the autonomy of the monetary authority.

"Clearly that suggests that there could possibly be a deterioration in the framework of macroeconomic policy in general," Francis stated when referring to the independence of the entity.

## The challenge for 2026 candidates

Colombia has accumulated three consecutive downgrades in its sovereign rating since 2018, standing today at BB/Stable. The names that today lead political visibility toward the House of Nariño include Iván Cepeda and Abe.
