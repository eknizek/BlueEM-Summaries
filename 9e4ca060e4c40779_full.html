# Lectus: the social network for books developed by Daniel Undurraga

The red envelope with the white logo in the middle arrived punctually at Tomás Recart's mailbox in Boston, United States, where he lived in the mid-2000s. Inside was a Netflix DVD that he had selected the week before from a website. When he returned it—in another envelope of the same color—a few days later the next video arrived. Netflix wasn't yet Netflix, but a movie rental service by mail. "That idea, we said: why not do it with books," Recart recalls. The question took almost 20 years to find an operational answer. On Monday, May 4th, in Llanquihue, the first bibliomobile will be inaugurated to distribute books to single-teacher schools in Los Lagos. It is the first physical chapter of a more ambitious bet: to make Chilean school reading something measurable and networked. For that there is a second parallel leg that launched in September 2025: Lectus, software that functions simultaneously as a library manager and a social network for readers. And it brings together three worlds: that of Recart and his experience in education through Enseña Chile; that of Verónica Abud and her trajectory in libraries through Fundación La Fuente; and that of Daniel Undurraga, co-founder of Cornershop—a startup that Uber bought in 2021—who through Fundación Phaway has built a technological laboratory for education with presence in 165 municipalities across the country.

The three are connected on Tuesday via Meet. Each in their own style: the technology entrepreneur, through a camera that follows and focuses on him as he moves, with electric guitars in the background, and Recart and Abud from their cell phones. They explain how their paths crossed, with one axis: education.

"If education were a pyramid, the base is reading. If a child doesn't understand what they read, they can't solve a math problem. Because if the problem says: 'if you're going 120 kilometers per hour, how long does it take you,' and you don't understand what they're asking, you can't set up the equation," Undurraga reflects. "And what correlates most with cognitive skills, especially at early ages, is reading. So if one had to ensure that children in school learn one thing well, that would have to be reading," he notes.

Books are, then, the starting point of this experiment.

## Step 1: SpaceX

Undurraga and Recart didn't know each other. In 2020, while Sebastián Piñera's government set up online classes amid the pandemic, Enseña Chile raised its hand to say something uncomfortable: the schools where they worked had no internet. The official solution didn't apply. So the network's teachers self-organized to set up an educational radio station with 60 teachers and a daily chapter that reached all of Chile. Recart—who has a master's in public administration from Harvard—was doing this when Benjamín Quiroga called him. They didn't know each other, but he had a proposal: why not bring Starlink, SpaceX's satellite internet network, to the most vulnerable schools?

"Under no circumstances are we going to do that," Recart responded, "because we already saw that internet has positive things, but also very negative things," he recalls. He made a counter-offer: focus the antenna exclusively on education. Quiroga then called Daniel Undurraga. For the Cornershop founder, who was just beginning his philanthropic work, the project hooked him with the technological challenge of connecting a remote school via satellite where there was no cell signal or fiber optic. They started with five antennas. "The pilot was so successful that we went to SpaceX to ask for 100 more antennas," Recart says. The following year they returned to Musk's company and asked for another thousand. No longer just for Chile but for Latin America. From there was born unlimitED, one of Fundación Phaway's programs, which Undurraga launched in 2020 and which today has presence in 165 municipalities and over 50,000 students accompanied.

## Triangle

The triangle of this story is completed by Verónica Abud, who had been waiting 30 years for this moment. A teacher by profession, she first worked at VMA and then at Fundación Educacional Barnechea, founded by Maria Luisa Vial Cox and Gonzalo Vial Correa. There, she recounts, contacted by Anacleto Angelini, they enabled a school in Putú, near one of Arauco's forestry plants, which in four years turned around its scores thanks to reading. They replicated the model in 17 establishments. After 10 years with the Vials, Abud decided to go her own way. Thus was born Fundación La Fuente, 25 years ago, which today has a track record of 645 libraries built in Chile and some $20 million invested. The two iconic projects are the 10 Bibliotecas Vivas that operated for years inside Mall Plaza (which closed due to the pandemic), and the 75 libraries they made for Copec. This in addition to 46 bibliomobiles in rural sectors and four cultural centers. When Biblioteca Viva closed in 2020, she used the donations law to keep 6,200 books. She stored them. "I said: 'At some point I'm going to have to have something to do another project,'" she recounts. That "something" arrived through Recart, who invited her to deliver books to homes in a network of 30 rural schools in Los Lagos. There, explains the founder of Enseña Chile, operates an alliance called El Reto del Aprendizaje, which brings together foundations, companies, and local authorities with the objective of moving the needle on SIMCE results for second-year students in language. The goal is to reverse the current 8% of students from the lowest socioeconomic level who achieve adequate performance, against a national average close to 32%. That's when Fundación La Fuente mentioned it had a container of books waiting for a destination.

## How Lectus Works

Undurraga recounts that a message that repeated often in his philanthropic search is that reading was the most important thing in education matters. "That struck me deeply and makes a lot of sense because I've always been a big reader, so it's something I have a natural interest in," he explains. And adds: "As a child I read a book a day." And that, he comments, translated into his good academic performance. With the triangle complete, the engineer began to analyze the different school library systems existing in Renca, Cerro Navia, Conchalí, La Pincoya, regions. "I realized that the library systems were working really poorly," he notes. That's when the project's vision began to grow, beyond a bibliomobile in the south: "Since we're going to make software, we might as well make one that also works for other schools, an operating system for reading in Chile," Undurraga explains. "Because one reads once a year that 80% of adults leave school without understanding what they read, but nobody does anything. And nobody knows how much children read, what they're reading, which are the books that hook you and convert you into a reader versus the books that make you read in first grade, which make you never want to read a book again," he adds.

Thus Lectus was born, a library management software that also has a reading promotion system that generates real-time data to make decisions about which books to buy for the library and which not to, what is read most in other libraries, etc. It is also, a kind of social network. How does it work? One registers, indicates their age, selects books they like (to instruct the algorithm), and from there can follow authors, works, and people; receive recommendations; form reading clubs...
