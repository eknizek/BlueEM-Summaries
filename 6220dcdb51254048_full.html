# Colombian Foreign Trade Crisis Deepens as Persistent IT System Failures Hit Tax Authority

The Colombian Federation of Logistics Agents in International Trade (Fitac) issued an official statement on April 20, 2026, alerting about the "persistence of serious difficulties" in the technological tools of the National Tax and Customs Authority (DIAN). According to the industry group, these technical irregularities are obstructing the regular flow of the country's foreign trade activities, generating a state of alert among users and the global logistics community.

According to the institutional report, the origin of the technical problems dates back to "December 27," when the government entity conducted a "general maintenance" operation on its digital infrastructure. Although DIAN assured that by January 2, 2026, the situation had normalized and services were operating at "full functionality," Fitac members have reported that the operational reality is different, consistently reporting "intermittencies, blockages, and systemic delays."

These failures, according to the document, have escalated beyond "daily operational incidents," becoming a recurring problem affecting supply chain stability. The federation has proceeded to compile and formally present to the customs authority "representative evidence of the impact" that these errors are causing in import and export processes.

In response to sector complaints, the Customs Authority has stated that it has "full knowledge of the situation" and has proposed as a mitigation measure that each affected operator register a "Single Point of Technological Solutions (PST)" for each individual error that occurs in the system. However, for Fitac, this instruction is "materially insufficient and ineffective," arguing that the problem is not due to "typing errors or user mistakes," but rather a "generalized and structural failure" of the IT platform.

The logistics group emphasizes that requiring operators to complete an administrative procedure for each blocked transaction imposes a "disproportionate administrative burden" that offers no real solution to the "operational intermittency." Fitac maintains that DIAN's measure does not address the core issue and warns that if a "general system failure" occurs in the current timeframe, the consequences for national logistics would have an "exponentially greater impact."

While acknowledging DIAN officials' "willingness to find solutions," the private sector insists that current solutions are not proportional to the magnitude of the "systemic failure" detected. The situation leaves customs agents and exporters in uncertainty, as they depend on the stability of these digital services to comply with their legal obligations and international commercial commitments.
