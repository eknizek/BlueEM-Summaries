Brazil 'Protected' Opens Path to Selic at 12%, Says Morgan Stanley Chief Economist

29 April 2026 · 09:37 PM

# Brazil "Protected" Opens Path to Selic at 12%, Says Morgan Stanley Chief Economist

Ana Madeira, chief economist at the bank, says the country is protected from the oil shock and the risk of inflation increase is greater in the second half. She projects Selic cuts of 0.5 percentage points starting in June.

Ana Madeira, Morgan Stanley's chief economist, predicts a 0.25 pp cut in the Selic rate at the Copom meeting on April 29, reducing it to 14.50% per year. Starting in June, larger cuts of 0.5 pp are expected, with the rate closing at 12% by December. Madeira believes that Brazil is protected from the oil shock, which allows for this forecast, and that the risk of inflation is greater in the second half. She praises the Central Bank's management, which resisted market pressure before beginning the cuts. The economist highlights that fiscal policy is stronger and should not negatively impact economic activity. The expectation for GDP growth is 2% for this year, and the exchange rate is stable, helping to control inflation. Madeira believes that Brazil, as an energy exporter, is relatively protected from geopolitical crises and internal uncertainties.

The consensus among economists for Wednesday's, April 29, meeting of the Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) was a 0.25 percentage point (pp) cut in the Selic rate — which indeed occurred — bringing it to 14.50% per year. American bank Morgan Stanley shared the same view. But, starting from the committee's next meeting in June, chief economist Ana Madeira projects a cycle of 0.5 pp cuts, which will lead the basic interest rate to close this year at 12%. "The alternative of not cutting would require extreme conditions that don't exist," she says in an interview with NeoFeed.

The Portuguese economist, with a long tenure at Bank of America in São Paulo and, more recently, as chief economist at HSBC in the country, believes there is a strong possibility that Copom will announce 0.5 pp cuts starting from the next meeting due to the low impact of the oil shock on the country, especially on inflation. "Brazil is relatively protected, because there is fiscal space to cushion the shock and Petrobras' pricing policy dilutes volatility over time," Madeira states. "The greater risk, in terms of inflation for Brazil, is concentrated in the second half."

Madeira praises the conduct of monetary policy by the Central Bank, resisting market pressure at the end of 2025 to begin the Selic cut cycle, which only occurred in March. Not even the fact that the BC is conducting the interest rate cut cycle amid a complex international scenario and a domestic one with a presidential election in the second half should have any relevant impact. "Fiscal constraints are stronger now," says the economist. "Barring a surprise, our expectation is that the government will indeed be able to deliver something very close to the primary deficit band for this year."

**How do you assess the conduct of monetary policy by the Central Bank since the beginning of the Selic cut cycle?**

The BC acted with serenity and parsimony in initiating the Selic cuts, resisting market pressure at the end of 2025. This stance helped to re-anchor inflation expectations, which were well above the target. After Copom signaled the beginning of the cut cycle for March, came the Iran geopolitical crisis. The BC, then, citing the uncertainty of the future scenario, acknowledged that there was room to begin a calibration cycle.

**What is the difference between a calibration cycle and an interest rate cut cycle?**

The difference is that, at the end of this calibration process, the expectation is that interest rates still remain in contractionary territory. I had this expectation because the Central Bank realized that this time, it would take a little longer for inflation to converge to the target. In other words, it would need an interest rate that remained contractionary for a longer period than normal. In this interim period, then came the beginning of the cuts.

**Did the BC take too long to start the cut cycle?**

No delays occurred, as negative activity surprises only appeared in February 2026, which justified waiting to maximize the effect on expectations. This reading by the BC was in line with our macro scenario. Here at Morgan Stanley, our assessment was that at a moment when there are unanchored inflation expectations, a central bank has more to gain by surprising on the hawkish side, with a stance to fight inflation through interest rate hikes, than on the dovish side (marked by interest rate reductions). Although the initial plan was to cut 50 basis points, geopolitical uncertainty led the BC to adopt a more cautious cut of 25 basis points.

**What was your expectation for today's meeting?**

Our expectation was a 25 basis point cut in the Selic, aligned with the market. The risk of a different decision was very low, given the signal of the calibration cycle in recent communications. The alternative of not cutting would require extreme conditions that don't exist.

**What is needed to increase the pace of cuts?**

"A larger [Selic] cut should come with some improvement in the external scenario, with the oil price sustainably beginning to reach levels below $100 per barrel."

Larger cuts should come with some improvement in the external scenario, with oil prices sustainably beginning to reach levels below $100 per barrel. That's why we see room for the BC to make a 0.25 pp cut by the next meeting in June, so that the external scenario improves and moves toward considering 0.5 pp cuts in the second half. If the external scenario doesn't improve, the risk for the Central Bank is delivering a smaller cut cycle this year, with a continuation of 0.25 pp. We believe the bar for interrupting the cuts remains quite high.

**Does Morgan Stanley also foresee this sequence of cuts?**

Although the risk for today's meeting was low, the June call is higher. Nonetheless, we believe in 0.5 pp Selic cuts starting as early as June through December, which would make the Selic rate close 2026 at 12%.

**What guarantees this more positive expectation from Morgan Stanley in the Selic cut cycle, given that the oil shock caused by the war has the potential to increase inflation and affect the BC's margin of maneuver?**

Brazil is relatively protected, because there is fiscal space to cushion the shock and Petrobras' pricing policy dilutes volatility over time. The greater risk, in terms of inflation for Brazil, is more of a second-order nature, concentrated in the second half. The Central Bank's reaction to this shock, in our view, will be driven more by the behavior of inflation expectations: not so much by the reaction of 2026 inflation expectations, but more by the reaction of 2027 and 2028 expectations, in other words, more long-term.

**Beyond the uncertain external scenario, could the fact that Brazil has presidential elections in the second half interfere with the BC's monetary policy?**

The question is whether the election will impact inflation expectations and economic growth. At this moment we have a presidential race that is well polarized. The expectation of political analysts is that this polarization scenario will remain until practically the ballot box. If this is confirmed, perhaps it won't be a very impactful factor in influencing inflation expectations between now and election day. That said, we don't expect the election to be a determining factor.
