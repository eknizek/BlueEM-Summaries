Brazil 'protected' opens path to Selic at 12%, says Morgan Stanley chief economist

29 April 2026 · 09:37 PM

# Brazil 'Protected' Opens Path to Selic at 12%, Says Morgan Stanley Chief Economist

Ana Madeira, the bank's chief economist, says the country is protected from the oil shock and the risk of inflation increase is greater in the second half. She projects Selic rate cuts of 0.5 percentage points starting in June.

Ana Madeira, Morgan Stanley's chief economist, forecasts a cut of 0.25 pp in the Selic rate at the Copom meeting on April 29, reducing it to 14.50% per year. Starting in June, larger cuts of 0.5 pp are expected, with the rate closing at 12% by December. Madeira believes Brazil is protected from the oil shock, which allows for this forecast, and that inflation risk is greater in the second half. She praises the Central Bank's management, which resisted market pressure before beginning cuts. The economist highlights that fiscal policy is stronger and should not negatively impact economic activity. GDP growth expectations are 2% for this year, and the exchange rate is stable, helping to control inflation. Madeira believes that Brazil, as an energy exporter, is relatively protected from geopolitical crises and internal uncertainties.

The consensus among economists for Wednesday's April 29 Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) meeting was a cut of 0.25 percentage points (pp) in the Selic rate — which indeed occurred — bringing it to 14.50% per year. American bank Morgan Stanley shared the same view. But starting from the Central Bank committee's next meeting in June, chief economist Ana Madeira projects a cycle of 0.5 pp cuts, which will bring the basic interest rate to close the year at 12%. "The alternative of not cutting would require extreme conditions that do not exist," she says in an interview with NeoFeed.

The Portuguese economist, who spent considerable time at Bank of America in São Paulo and more recently as chief economist at HSBC in the country, believes there is a strong possibility that Copom will announce 0.5 pp cuts starting from the next meeting due to the low impact of the oil shock on the country, especially on inflation. "Brazil is relatively protected because there is fiscal space to cushion the shock and Petrobras' price policy dilutes volatility over time," Madeira states. "The greatest risk, in terms of inflation for Brazil, is concentrated in the second half."

Madeira praises the conduct of monetary policy by the Central Bank, resisting market pressure at the end of 2025 to begin the Selic rate cut cycle, which only occurred in March. Not even the fact that the Central Bank is conducting the interest rate cut cycle amid a complex international scenario and a domestic one with a presidential election in the second half should have any significant impact. "Fiscal constraints are stronger now," the economist says. "Barring a surprise, our expectation is that the government will indeed be able to deliver something very close to the primary deficit band for this year."

**How do you assess the Central Bank's conduct of monetary policy since the beginning of the Selic rate cut cycle?**

The Central Bank acted with serenity and prudence in initiating Selic cuts, resisting market pressure at the end of 2025. This stance helped re-anchor inflation expectations, which were well above target. After Copom signaled the start of the cut cycle in March, came the Iran geopolitical crisis. The Central Bank then, citing uncertainty in the future scenario, acknowledged that there was room to begin a calibration cycle.

**What is the difference between a calibration cycle and an interest rate cut cycle?**

The difference is that at the end of this calibration process, the expectation is that interest rates will still remain in contractionary territory. I had this expectation because the Central Bank perceived that this time it would take a little longer for inflation to converge to target. In other words, it would need an interest rate that remained contractionary for a longer period than normal. In the meantime, the beginning of cuts came.

**Did the Central Bank take too long to start the cut cycle?**

There was no delay because negative activity surprises only appeared in February 2026, which justified waiting to maximize the effect on expectations. This reading by the Central Bank was in line with our macro scenario. Here at Morgan Stanley, the assessment was that at a moment when there are unanchored inflation expectations, a central bank that surprises on the hawkish side, with a stance of fighting inflation through interest rate increases, has more to gain than one that surprises dovishly (marked by interest rate cuts). Although the initial plan was to cut 50 basis points, geopolitical uncertainty led the Central Bank to adopt a more cautious cut of 25 basis points.

**What was your expectation for today's meeting?**

Our expectation was a 25 basis point Selic cut, aligned with the market. The risk of a different decision was very low, given the signal of the calibration cycle in recent communications. The alternative of not cutting would require extreme conditions that do not exist.

"A larger [Selic] cut should come with some improvement in the external scenario, with the oil price beginning sustainably to reach levels below $100 per barrel"

**What is needed to increase the pace of cuts?**

A larger cut should come with some improvement in the external scenario, with the oil price beginning sustainably to reach levels below $100 per barrel. Therefore, we see room for the Central Bank to make a 0.25 pp cut until the next meeting in June, so that the external scenario improves and begins to consider 0.5 pp cuts in the second half. If the external scenario does not improve, the risk for the Central Bank is delivering a smaller cut cycle this year, with a continuation of 0.25 pp cuts. We believe the bar for interrupting cuts remains quite high.

**Does Morgan Stanley also foresee this sequence of cuts?**

Although the risk for today's meeting was low, the June call carries higher risk. Even so, we believe in 0.5 pp Selic cuts starting in June through December, which would cause the Selic rate to close 2026 at 12%.

**What guarantees this more positive expectation from Morgan Stanley in the Selic cut cycle, given that the oil shock caused by the war has the potential to increase inflation and affect the Central Bank's room for maneuver?**

Brazil is relatively protected because there is fiscal space to cushion the shock and Petrobras' price policy dilutes volatility over time. The greatest risk, in terms of inflation for Brazil, is more of a second-order issue, concentrated in the second half. The Central Bank's reaction to this shock, in our view, will occur more through the behavior of inflation expectations: not so much through the reaction of 2026 inflation expectations, but more through the reaction of 2027 and 2028 expectations, that is, longer-term.

**Besides the uncertain external scenario, could the fact that Brazil has presidential elections in the second half interfere with the Central Bank's monetary policy?**

The question is whether the election will impact inflation expectations and economic growth. At this moment, we have a presidential race that is quite polarized. The expectation of political analysts is that this polarization scenario will remain until practically election day. If this is confirmed, perhaps it won't be a very impactful factor in influencing inflation expectations between now and election day. That said, we do not expect the election to be a determining factor.
