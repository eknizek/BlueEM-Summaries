# How to Untangle the Investment Knot: The Case of Projects Worth $9-200 Million

These are projects that do not qualify for the RIGI and exceed the RIMI limit—regimes designed by the Government with strong fiscal incentives that encourage investment. The obstacles described by an industrialist who is betting heavily on regional economies. A pistachio plantation, an investment that takes 8 to 9 years to bear fruit.

The government has focused on the key issue of investments. Rightly so: adjusting macroeconomics is not enough. But to clear the path forward, a profound modification of the rules of the game was needed, starting with fiscal problems. "All Argentines know how complicated and costly the entire tax system is," says José Chediack, an agro-industrial entrepreneur with major operations in the provinces of San Juan, La Rioja, and Corrientes. "But this system operates like a parking brake when you think about new investments."

Chediack heads SolFrut, one of the leaders in olive oil production, and is launching pistachio production while also driving an important rice project in northern Corrientes. "The government understands this: that's why the RIGI (Incentive Regime for Large Investments) was created for projects over 200 million dollars, aimed at generating significant foreign exchange flows. And in the second case, the 'RIMI,' companies are not considered SMEs if they bill more than 9 million dollars."

"SMEs have always been major employers," Chediack notes.

Basically, the issue is blocked input VAT. And the accounting depreciation systems that are regulated. He explains the details: "the problem with something that depreciates over 20 years; but suddenly in the first years, the project shows a positive accounting result and we have to start paying income tax when it's still not mature from a financial-economic perspective." The other problem is input VAT withholding: "until there is significant billing and added value, we won't be able to absorb the credit that has been generated."

This is what the RIGI and RIMI aimed to solve. "But many of us are categorized as large companies and therefore don't qualify for RIMI. But our investments don't exceed $200 million to be eligible for RIGI."

This segment, he assures, is generally related to regional companies linked to agro-industry. Export olives. "Well, if you make an investment in an industry that suddenly in the second or third year starts billing, the problem of input VAT that was withheld for two years is a minor problem (I'm not saying it doesn't exist…) because in a short time I'll be able to recover that credit. Now, in projects like rice, where you spend years systematizing, doing hydraulic works, pump stations, etc., the possibility of generating returns is long-term. Not to mention nuts—pistachios, walnuts, pecans, orange trees, or lemon trees. These are projects where first there's a long-term investment process that will break even in the eighth or ninth year. In this case, as the Argentine tax system is designed, obviously that VAT (also without inflation adjustment) over 8 or 9 years hits very hard. The project cost increases by 21% and becomes directly unviable."

To get an idea of what's at stake, the sum of regional economies reaches 10 billion dollars annually. This could be multiplied by 6 or 7 times. Just look at Chile, for example. But beyond volume, what matters is the employment multiplier effect. These are labor-intensive activities. In the Wet Pampas, each million dollars of gross product implies 7 labor days. With processing included, we add one more, going from 7 to 8. But in regional economies, each million dollars of gross product implies 12 to 15 labor days. And if we add the added value of transforming a pistachio into a snack, or an olive into a small bottle of oil, with a label, cap, and brand. Or producing lemon essential oil that goes entirely to export.

But beyond this employment effect, there's another, more important one from the perspective of socioeconomic development of our country: the possibility of reversing the demographic trend we've historically had, reducing the macrocephaly that has developed in the conurbation.

Chediack adds that "beyond what's being developed today in Vaca Muerta or what's being produced in any of the large mining projects, whether silver, gold, lithium, rare earths, etc., which are very localized areas. We have 24 provinces, right? So we also need to think about the development of provinces like Entre Ríos, Corrientes, Chaco, Formosa, Santiago del Estero, La Rioja, Catamarca, San Juan."

There are many alternatives for developing agro-industrial projects related to regional economies that "would give the country a profile of enormously greater social sustainability, where people could be born, grow up, study, and find work in the same province, in the same town where they were born, without having to emigrate to the Buenos Aires conurbation to live in situations known to everyone."

He emphasizes that of all the elements at stake, this last one is the most important "because it has to do with a country project, with strength and much greater diversification of the productive matrix than what a country would have that lives, as I say somewhat humorously, off pipelines—gas pipelines, oil pipelines, mineral pipelines, or grain pipelines. It's the possibility of much more diversified development and with a much more intelligent and broader matrix of social development."
