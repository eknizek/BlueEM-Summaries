# A New Test for Peru's Economic Resilience

By Luis Miguel Castilla

Peru has long been one of the most puzzling cases in Latin America. While its neighbors swung between economic booms and crises, the country quietly drove growth, accumulated reserves, built a solid central bank, and maintained macroeconomic orthodoxy despite governments with starkly divergent ideological positions. Presidents came and went—some removed from office, others fleeing justice, one staging a failed self-coup—and the economy kept growing. Eight presidents in ten years, and yet the currency remained strong, inflation and spreads stayed among the region's lowest, and the mining sector continued producing. This disconnect between economic performance and political dysfunction is, from any regional perspective, remarkable.

However, it is becoming increasingly fragile. While official electoral results remain provisional at the time of writing, and margins continue to be very tight, the first round of the 2026 elections closely resembles that of 2021. Once again, Keiko Fujimori—daughter of former president Alberto Fujimori and three-time presidential candidate—appears headed to face a radical leftist, Roberto Sánchez, former minister in Pedro Castillo's cabinet, in the runoff on June 7. Sánchez represents a serious threat to stability. His intransigent ideological stance and alliance with radical factions (such as nationalist Antauro Humala, with a criminal record) could push Peru toward a governance model that drastically breaks with the institutional continuity that has sustained the economy for two decades.

Indeed, the contest presents itself as a stress test to determine whether the safeguards that protected Peru during past turbulence can withstand a government actively working to dismantle them. The legitimacy of the outcome is already in question. Rafael López Aliaga, the conservative right-wing former mayor of Lima, who placed third, has denounced electoral fraud. Investigations into how electoral authorities handled the count continue. Whoever wins the election will begin governing under a shadow of doubt that will hinder the formation of coalitions necessary to rule. Peru's institutions have been losing credibility for years, and elections that cannot cleanly resolve their own first round do not recover it. A legitimacy deficit is not merely a political inconvenience but a structural limitation on any government's actual capacity.

In the legislative realm, there is some room for cautious optimism. The newly elected Senate has the capacity to contain the most harmful unilateral impulses of any new president. Right-wing parties hold a narrow majority in the 60-member chamber, and a centrist bloc headed by former Defense Minister Jorge Nieto—with eight senators—possesses significant decision-making power. This composition makes it practically impossible to convene a constitutional assembly to establish a statist regime and should protect key appointments at the Central Bank and other autonomous institutions.
