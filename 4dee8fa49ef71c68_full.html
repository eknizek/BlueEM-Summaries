# Traders Bet on AI to Boost Profits, but Results Disappoint Expectations

The AI trading agent of Jake Nesler got a major bet right in its first week. When Nvidia's earnings made stocks surge in late November, the agent—trained based on Nesler's own trading instincts—deliberated with itself about whether it should follow the momentum. Fortunately, the bot decided not to, as chasing the rally would have cost Nesler's portfolio an estimated $10,000 loss that week.

Nesler, a 29-year-old software engineer from Scranton, Pennsylvania, had burned out trying to trade options during his day job. He was inspired by an Anthropic experiment in which the company's Claude model controlled an office vending machine. But what if, instead of dispensing snacks, Claude could be trained to buy and sell stocks?

Nesler spent two and a half weeks teaching the model how he thought about risk, entry signals and building positions, then released it on a simulated brokerage account at Alpaca, an algorithmic trading platform, with $100,000 in fake money to spend.

"I wanted something that could be a substitute for the way I think and do these things while I'm doing other things," he said in an interview.

The rest of the agent's week was less impressive, losing money on a series of speculative trades that didn't pan out. After five days of trading, Nesler was left with one good decision alongside a string of losses.

In stock markets, cryptocurrencies and prediction markets, a growing legion of retail traders is training AI agents to buy and sell assets on their behalf. It's a sign of a new era in this type of investing, where traders believe AI-powered tools can produce better investment results—and that everything still done manually is a process waiting for improvement.

Open-source platforms like OpenClaw allow users to chat with their AI agents through accessible messaging apps like WhatsApp and Telegram, attracting hordes of would-be stock brokers without a tech school resume. All they need to do is connect an AI model to the system and then release it with simple instructions.

On X, claims of extraordinary returns through AI agents have become a genre of their own. One viral post, viewed 4.7 million times, boasted of a 5,860% return in two days on the Polymarket prediction market platform. Its story was later debunked by another account operated by an AI agent, saying the claims were impossible. Similar posts have connected users directly to malware, presenting a security risk for unsuspecting investors.

The tools for setting up these agents have never been more accessible. They are becoming the logical next step for a generation of traders who grew up with apps like Robinhood Markets, adding another layer of automation to speculation. The trading platforms themselves are embracing the trend, with companies like Public Holdings seeking to offer their own AI agents to customers.

But what AI has yet to facilitate is actually making money.

Nesler encountered a recurring problem with his agent. The bot kept adopting responsible behavior, gravitating toward blue-chip stocks and S&P 500 positions—the kind of positions that would barely move in a week. Nesler said he had to override it several times, pushing the model toward riskier trades that suited his own appetite.

The problem is embedded in the technology. Large language models like Claude are trained on large amounts of financial advice, risk management literature and market commentary. Unless prompted otherwise, they absorb the consensus view of what constitutes responsible investing—and behave like the average of all financial advisors' blog posts.

Some traders using agents built on top of these models are struggling against this default conservatism, trying to extract a presumption of risk from a system trained to avoid it.

Once adjusted to Nesler's taste, the agent delivered a return of about 7% over 30 days—beating the S&P 500's gain of approximately 4.5% in the same period. Meanwhile, the agent tested its appetite for volatility, experiencing losses of up to 22%.

Though he published his code online for others to experiment with, Nesler isn't ready to recommend that anyone give it real money.

"It's totally possible to make money with it," he said. "But anyone could do that with a little luck in options. That doesn't mean you won't lose that money too."

Jay Malavia has heard versions of this story before. "The thing about trading is that it's a zero-sum game," said Malavia, co-founder of Chicago-based Kairos, which operates a trading terminal for prediction markets. An edge, by definition, stops being an edge when it's shared with the masses.

"Let's say I'm a company that has a trading agent that works—I wouldn't give it to you," Malavia said. And if you had one that worked, he added, "you definitely wouldn't want to post it online."

What's happening on X, Reddit and Telegram looks to Malavia and others like a familiar cycle. During the 2021 meme stock mania, social media was both a marketplace for trading ideas and an amplifier for invented gains. AI agents added a layer of complexity that makes these claims harder to verify—a dashboard screenshot is harder to debunk than a brokerage statement, and the technology carries enough mystique that people want to believe in its abilities.

Still, rising demand means trading platforms are welcoming the bots. Crypto-driven exchanges like Polymarket, OKX, Bybit and Kraken have launched interfaces in recent months that make it easier for AI agents to execute trades. The incentive is simple: bots trade frequently, and exchanges live off volume.

Annanay Kapila, a former quant trader who now runs the derivatives exchange QFEX, doubts that AI trading bots will work at scale for retail investors. Prediction market contracts are typically low-volume, for example, which makes it difficult for AI agents to deploy capital at speed or scale.
