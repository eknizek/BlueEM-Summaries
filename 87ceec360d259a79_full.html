Sequoia sells mega sorter to Mercado Libre and exits B2C market

Sequoia Logística announced on Wednesday, April 22, the sale of its mega sorter to Mercado Libre for R$ 40 million, in a transaction that consolidates the already-expected exit of the company from the e-commerce (B2C) segment. The warehouse lease contract in São Bernardo where the equipment is installed was also transferred to the marketplace.

The sorter, an automated parcel sorting system using conveyor belts, had been incorporated into operations following the merger with Move3 in 2024, with capacity to process up to 32,000 packages per hour. In practice, however, it operated between 30% and 40% of that volume, making it an idle asset and generator of losses. According to the company, this operation was consuming R$ 2.5 million per month in cash, considering maintenance, warehouse rent, and labor costs.

"We had approximately 260 employees in this operation. We made a severance plan for these people. We recommended some to Mercado Libre. It's a demanding area, with other companies also interested in absorbing this labor," says Leopoldo Bruggen, CEO of Sequoia, to NeoFeed.

The equipment was designed in China and, according to Bruggen, would only make sense if there were sufficient scale. "But, since the pandemic, there has been a rupture in the e-commerce sector, with major marketplaces dominating their own logistics. Independent companies that had been investing in their own infrastructure were caught off guard," he admits.

"Move3 was a company focused on small package delivery and also had a sorter that, at that time, seemed very interesting," recalls Bruno Gomes, partner and head of the Distressed & Special Situations vertical at JiveMauá. The fund has been the main shareholder of Sequoia since 2023, after converting part of the company's debt into equity, with approximately 90% stake.

However, with the verticalization of e-commerce logistics, Gomes states that it was necessary to "pivot" the company's restructuring, which remains under extrajudicial recovery. "As a manager, we have the capacity to adapt and change strategy when the scenario changes significantly."

Without the mega sorter operation, Sequoia plans to maintain an "asset light" operation, focused on segments requiring low fixed infrastructure and predictable revenue. The focus will be on delivery of banking products, such as cards and payment terminals, led by the Flash Courier brand.

"We want to be the preferred logistics for major Brazilian banks and fintechs. I would say that's our mission," says Leopoldo Bruggen. According to the CEO, Flash Courier is already a leader in this market, with approximately 70% market share. Bruggen hopes to increase this share further, now that the company has obtained the negative debt certificate (CND), after concluding negotiations with the Attorney General's Office for payment of federal debts.

"Now, we can participate in bidding processes where CND was required. I would very much like to enter the logistics of public banks, for example. Today, it's dominated by the Post Office. But we are more efficient and cheaper than the Post Office."

According to the CEO, the small package operation — concentrated on delivery of cards and payment terminals — already accounted for the majority of revenue and results, being also the most profitable vertical in the group. In the third quarter balance sheet, the most recent disclosed by the company, Flash Courier was responsible for over 80% of revenue, of R$ 152.4 million. Given the drop in B2C, revenue was 30% lower than recorded in the same period last year.

But Bruggen states this is not the company's main concern. "The market needs to stop asking about revenue, because there are companies billing billions and having cash deficits. I don't want revenue growth, I want to generate cash."

Sequoia did not disclose Flash Courier's exact margins, but one of the debt covenants requires the company to maintain 25% gross margin or higher in the cards unit and only 11% in consolidated results. Despite increased use of digital cards being a risk to the business, the group's assessment is that demand for physical cards should not drop significantly in the short term.

"Physical cards don't compete with digital ones. They're complementary. There's the banking inclusion of classes C and D, which didn't have access to credit cards, and the growth of fintechs that see cards as an important marketing tool," says the CEO.

"We've also had a very large volume of payment terminal deliveries across Brazil, and for us, it's an interesting market because it enters the same delivery network as cards."

In this operation, Sequoia has greater revenue predictability, given the service contracts already signed with financial institutions, without the need for large infrastructure like it maintained in B2C. In practice, cards and terminals are sorted by recipient in sorting centers and, from there, routed for final delivery. This last step is done by outsourced partners, but under Sequoia's own management and monitoring, with real-time tracking and information sharing with clients.

Another front gaining more space with the B2C exit is dedicated deliveries, known as full truck load. In this model, the company transports closed shipments for a single client, with defined origin and destination, without need for consolidation with other orders along the route.

"It's a profitable and low-risk business because, with full load or not, the amount to be received is basically the same," says Bruggen. In this operation, the company also achieves better cost control, since expenses are predicted only after service contracting.

"Unlike B2C, where you anticipated costs to pursue revenue, here revenue and cost are very aligned. So, the B2B operation will rarely be deficit-generating," explains Gomes from JiveMauá.

For Gomes, cash generation will be the only way for Sequoia to recover and gain the necessary attractiveness on the stock exchange to enable a transaction. Since 2023, when JiveMauá bought significant stake in the company, its shares have fallen more than 99%, trading at R$ 0.17, even after stock splits. Currently, Sequoia is valued at R$ 1 billion, less than a third of the approximately R$ 3.5 billion it reached at its peak in 2021.
