# The fintech boom enters its most difficult phase: now it's time to prove who survives and who doesn't

For years, the objective was to grow. Open accounts, add users, launch cards. Today, in the Colombian fintech ecosystem, that narrative is no longer sufficient to stay in the market. The industry is entering a new phase, more demanding and less forgiving: one that must demonstrate it can make money and be a genuinely profitable business.

Colombia, with more than 400 active fintech startups, has established itself as one of the most dynamic markets in Latin America. Digitalization advanced at great speed and millions of people accessed financial services for the first time. But that growth, which for years was the main metric of success, now faces harder scrutiny.

"Financial inclusion only works if it scales," warns Abdul Assal, director of business development for Brazil and Colombia at Galileo Financial Technologies. His assessment reflects the moment in the sector, where growth is no longer sufficient if there is no foundation capable of sustaining that growth over time.

The change is structural. Fintechs no longer compete solely for users, but for efficiency. Cost per active account, the ability to process transactions without friction, and risk management become critical variables. In this context, technological infrastructure ceases to be a technical matter and becomes a strategic decision.

Many companies that prioritized speed in their early stages now face operational tensions, such as higher costs, complexity in reconciliations, and regulatory pressure. What was once an advantage—getting to market quickly—becomes a limitation when volume grows.

At the same time, the industry is experiencing another phase with the integration of artificial intelligence into the financial experience. It is not just about optimizing processes, but about redefining how transactions are executed. A recent case marks this change. For the first time, a purchase was initiated within a conversational environment and executed in real time through a digital agent.

"We are seeing the birth of a new layer in the payments industry," explains Antonio Santiago, Business Development Manager at Pomelo. "Digital agents have evolved from suggesting what to buy to being able to execute the transaction safely and in real time."

The implication is profound, and financial services cease to be a destination and become an invisible layer within the user's digital experience.

This process occurs alongside regional consolidation. Latin America not only adopts financial innovation but also begins to define its own trends. "The energy, ambition, and collaboration reflect the transformative moment that Latin America's financial landscape is experiencing," noted Jessica Blue, Executive Vice President of Money20/20 Americas, during Fintech Americas 2026.

Colombia plays a central role in that map. The country has become a hub for expansion for technology startups, especially from markets like Chile, where companies see the Colombian market as a platform for scaling. "Colombia is a strategic market where companies can scale their businesses both with local investors and abroad," affirms Felipe Opazo Araya, director of ProChile in Colombia.

However, the real impact of the ecosystem is measured by its capacity to solve structural problems. One of the most urgent continues to be access to credit. In Colombia, approximately 12 million people remain outside the formal financial system. "In Colombia, there are millions of people who do not have access to formal credit," notes Juan Pablo Triviño, director of Trego.
