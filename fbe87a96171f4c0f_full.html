# U.S. Futures Rise and Oil Falls on Iran Negotiations Signals

U.S. stock futures are rising and oil is falling, as market sentiment improves on prospects for resuming maritime transport through the Strait of Hormuz and progress toward an agreement between the United States and Iran.

S&P 500 contracts gained 0.2% after the underlying index closed Friday at a record high. Nasdaq 100 futures also advanced. Brent crude fell 2% in early Monday trading, while the dollar retreated against most major currencies. Markets in Japan remain closed for a holiday.

President Donald Trump said the United States will begin escorting ships not involved in the conflict with Iran through the Strait of Hormuz starting Monday, and described talks with Tehran as "very positive" after receiving Washington's response to its latest proposal to end the war.

The measures could pave the way for smoother energy flows and a possible agreement, though uncertainty persists over the conflict. "The devil is always in the details, but it's a positive signal because it shows both sides are willing to find common ground," said Rodrigo Catril, strategist at National Australia Bank in Sydney. Whether the optimism will hold "is hard to say. We've been through this before," he noted.

Iran's proposal called for a complete end to the conflict within 30 days, along with guarantees against new attacks, according to the semi-official Tasnim agency. The plan reaffirms previous Tehran demands, including withdrawal of U.S. forces near Iran, lifting of the naval blockade, end of sanctions, and payment of reparations.

On Sunday, Iran said it received the U.S. response through Pakistan and is evaluating it.

The latest developments add to a month of strong gains in risk assets, as traders largely set aside concerns about the economic impact of hostilities, with signs of corporate resilience driving stocks to their best month since 2020. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 closed April at highs, with around 81% of S&P 500 companies beating first-quarter earnings estimates, according to Bloomberg data. Emerging market stocks hit a new record toward the end of April, while an indicator of Asian stocks has recovered nearly all its war-related losses.

Risk-taking also extended beyond stocks, with high-yield credit spreads near multi-year lows and retailers increasing their participation in prediction markets and daily-expiration options.

The rally held despite the Iran war, oil above $100 per barrel, and a Federal Reserve that has signaled higher rates for longer due to higher energy costs. "The market is being very patient with this level of uncertainty because it's focused on the other side of the conflict, which could be overly optimistic," said Joe Gilbert, portfolio manager at Integrity Asset Management. "The economic damage being generated will be felt more materially in the next month."
