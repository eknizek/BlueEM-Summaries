# Government Strangled by Congress Creates New Economic Impact Alongside Rising Oil Prices

There are abundant indications that the Lula government has ended. And there are still seven months remaining until the government elected in October and November takes office.

President Lula's double defeat in Congress, which rejected the nomination of Jorge Messias to the Supreme Court and overturned the president's veto in the case of sentencing guidelines for the January 8, 2023 convictions, demonstrates the extent to which the president's capacity for political leadership has weakened. It shows that the opposition has gained even more electoral strength. And this means that conditions are increasing for it to win more seats in Congress. The question that buzzes in the ears of those paying attention concerns what kind of Legislative support President Lula could count on to govern if he is re-elected for another term.

The risk now is that desperation takes hold in the leadership of the government and the PT, and that from this point on, Treasury resources are dumped with the objective of winning the voter's favor. It remains to be seen how far the deterioration of public accounts and its perverse effect on the rest of the economy can go.

To this great fragility of the Lula government is added the economic impact produced by the Iran War. On Thursday the 30th, oil quotations reached US$ 126.41 per barrel, the highest level since June 2022. It then retreated and closed at US$ 114, but the blow was strong enough to demonstrate the extent of global insecurity hanging over energy costs.

The conviction is consolidating that, even if the conflicts surrounding the Middle East are resolved, the normalization of the oil market will take time to happen, given the need to replenish already depleted stocks and repair damage done to the region's infrastructure. This means that inflation will have more thrust to rise and, with it, interest rates—which may not rise, but will not have much technical room to fall.

This basic picture is sufficient to increase the perception of worsening economic conditions. Credit costs for an already excessively indebted population will remain high. Beyond the decline in commodity prices, agribusiness now faces problems imposed not only by rising fertilizer prices and other sector inputs but, mainly, by the scarcity of these products. The population, which already suffers from the hardships of INSS queues, high credit costs, and loss of purchasing power from inflation, may feel its future more threatened.

President Lula will also have to deal with the mistrust of app workers and micro-entrepreneurs, with rejection from evangelicals, and with the unease of the middle classes, called upon to pay a larger share of the bill for fiscal deterioration and other distortions.

Time will tell.
