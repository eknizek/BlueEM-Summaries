# U.S. Multinationals Report Harassment by Mexican Tax Authority (SAT)

At least 300 influential U.S. multinational companies have reported being victims of harassment by Mexico's Tax Administration Service (SAT). They have requested urgent action from Finance Secretary Édgar Amador regarding these irregular practices against them, which they claim discourage investment in Mexico.

The complaint filed by the National Foreign Trade Council (NFTC), which includes giants like UPS, Coca Cola, and Hewlett Packard, adds to various statements made in Mexico by domestic companies that have warned of hostility from the Mexican tax authority.

The multinationals submitted a letter to Amador listing practices ranging from aggressive audits and lack of effective access to tax appeals—complaints similar to those made by Mexican corporations. Dated March 16, the letter from foreign companies, which Grupo REFORMA accessed, recalled that since November the NFTC had already denounced various "onerous" fiscal measures contained in the 2026 Economic Package, which is further aggravated by SAT's actions.

"The effect of these measures is aggravated by the irregular enforcement practices carried out by SAT, which creates a tax regime that imposes growing and unprecedented burdens on many U.S. multinational companies that discourage investment and job creation," the letter states.

"At a time when economic cooperation between the United States and Mexico and the rule of law are under stricter scrutiny, these tax administration measures have become a top priority requiring urgent resolution," the letter asserts.

This week, Secretary Amador is visiting Washington DC as part of the spring meetings of the International Monetary Fund and World Bank, but it is unclear whether he will take the opportunity to meet with NFTC leadership to address their complaints against SAT.

As had also been denounced during the open consultations by the U.S. Trade Representative's Office toward the T-MEC review, the NFTC insisted that Mexico's 2024 judicial reform has caused the companies it represents to feel they have fewer resources to appeal SAT abuses.

Without addressing the complaint from large companies, the Finance Ministry reported that SHCP head Édgar Amador held a meeting yesterday with his counterpart from the U.S. Treasury Department, Scott Bessent, in which they addressed efforts to combat drug trafficking and money laundering. The Finance Ministry stated that the meeting aimed to continue strengthening bilateral cooperation in combating drug trafficking and information exchange to halt operations involving illicitly sourced resources, as well as the issue of critical minerals.
