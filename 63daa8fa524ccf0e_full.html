# Renewable energy companies suspend nearly R$40 billion in investments, evaluate leaving Northeast

Renewable energy companies are evaluating migrating from the Northeast, a region with favorable climate conditions for wind and solar generation, to other parts of Brazil, suspending investments close to R$38.8 billion between 2025 and 2026. This scenario is occurring due to a series of factors. Beyond the slow growth in energy demand and "curtailment" (forced cuts in generation), already-known problems, sector representatives are complaining about a recent increase in operating costs, with the loss of tax advantages and increased requirements.

Members of the federal government consider that the tax benefits granted in the past to boost these alternative sources are no longer necessary, since they have already gained space in the national energy matrix. With a consolidated market, they say, it is now necessary to adjust tax policy to prevent advantages to wind and solar energy from generating an imbalance in the national energy system, raising costs for consumers.

Contacted through its press office since March 27, the Ministry of Mines and Energy has not commented until publication of this text.

Most renewable energy projects are in the Northeast, a region abundant in sun and wind. At the request of the reporting, Absolar and Abeólica (associations representing solar and wind energy generators respectively) calculated the impact of the sector crisis. The first entity stated that during 2025, 141 plants returned their operating licenses. They would total R$18.9 billion. Absolar adds that in the comparison between projected and actual results in the last year, there were another R$5.9 billion in frustrated investments. Abeólica said the deceleration in sector growth generated approximately R$14 billion in suspended investments.

"This crisis worsened mainly from 2023, 2024 onwards, and the Northeast, which receives more than 95% of investments, is suffering. We even have factories in the Northeast laying off and closing due to lack of investment, new projects," says Elbia Gannoum, executive director of the association.

Casa dos Ventos, one of the country's main renewable companies, is among companies admitting to reducing investments in the Northeast and recalculating its course to bet on projects in Mato Grosso and Rio Grande do Sul. When contacted, the company stated it invests "both in the Northeast and in other geographies."

Mato Grosso do Sul and Rio Grande do Sul emerge as alternatives because, despite not having climate conditions as favorable as the Northeast's, they are closer to consumer hubs.

The increase in generation costs is explained by two provisions approved in 2025 in Provisional Measure 1304. This text originated from the government's intention to reorganize the electric sector, which ended up frustrated by the strength of lobbies in Congress. The measure created provisions to boost the battery sector, aiming to solve curtailment. This forced cut in generation occurs because the amount produced by renewable sources varies according to wind and sun. During peak times, these plants need to be taken offline to prevent overload that could collapse the national grid.

The measure, however, causes losses to these plants. The country today wastes the equivalent of a Belo Monte plant due to curtailment. One of the most effective ways to solve the problem is using batteries to store excess energy generated during peak hours for consumption when demand increases.

The provisional measure established that only those integrated with storage systems can access the Special Regime of Incentives for Infrastructure Development (Reidi), which provides tax discounts to solar and wind generation plants.

In practice, entities complain this limited the investment power of some companies. "The text caused a setback, creating inequality for the solar source compared to other technologies, by restricting access to Reidi. This type of requirement and limitation does not exist for any other source in Brazil," the entity stated.

Another criticized provision is the transfer of capacity reserve costs to new projects. This reserve is the hiring of plants to be activated during low production but high demand periods. Since renewable energies are intermittent (they don't generate all the time), there is a need to contract more backup generators. The requirement imposed on new projects, according to the entity, increases costs, discourages new investments, and "may economically pressure renewable generation projects."

Furthermore, the entities complain that Aneel approved, in 2022, two resolutions that increase the rate paid by energy generators the farther they are from the country's main consumer hubs, such as the South and Southeast. Absolar evaluates that this "increased network usage costs for plants located far from load centers, especially in the North and Northeast" and "can displace photovoltaic projects," which is already happening.
