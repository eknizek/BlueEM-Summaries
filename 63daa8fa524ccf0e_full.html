# Renewable Energy Companies Suspend Nearly R$ 40 Billion in Investments and Evaluate Leaving Northeast

Renewable energy companies are evaluating migrating from the Northeast, a region with favorable climate conditions for wind and solar generation, to other locations in Brazil, suspending investments close to R$ 38.8 billion between 2025 and 2026. This scenario results from a series of factors. Beyond the slow growth in energy demand and "curtailment" (forced reduction in generation), problems already well-known, sector representatives complain about a recent increase in operating costs, with the loss of tax advantages and increased requirements.

Members of the federal government argue that the tax benefits granted in the past to boost these alternative sources are no longer necessary, since they have already gained space in the national energy matrix. With the market consolidated, they say, it is now necessary to adjust tax policy to prevent advantages for wind and solar energy from creating an imbalance in the national energy system, raising costs for consumers.

Contacted through its press office since March 27, the Ministry of Mines and Energy has not commented until the publication of this text.

Most renewable energy projects are located in the Northeast, a region abundant in sun and wind. At the request of the reporting team, Absolar and Abeólica (associations representing solar and wind energy generators, respectively) calculated the impact of the crisis in the sector. The first entity stated that during 2025, 141 plants returned their operating licenses. They would total R$ 18.9 billion. Absolar adds that, when comparing projected versus actual figures from the last year, there were another R$ 5.9 billion in frustrated investments. Abeólica reported that the deceleration of sector growth generated approximately R$ 14 billion in suspended investments.

"This crisis worsened mainly from 2023, 2024 onwards, and the Northeast, which receives more than 95% of investments, is suffering. We even have factories in the Northeast laying off workers and closing due to lack of investment and new projects," states Elbia Gannoum, executive director of the association.

Casa dos Ventos, one of the country's leading renewable companies, is one of the companies that admits reducing investments in the Northeast and recalculating its strategy to bet on projects in Mato Grosso and Rio Grande do Sul. When contacted, the company reported that it invests "both in the Northeast and in other geographies."

Mato Grosso do Sul and Rio Grande do Sul emerge as alternatives since, despite not having climate conditions as favorable as those in the Northeast, they are closer to consumer hubs.

The increase in generation costs is explained by two provisions approved in 2025 in Provisional Measure 1304. This text originated from the government's intention to reorganize the electrical sector, which ultimately failed due to the strength of lobbies in Congress. The measure created provisions to boost the battery sector, aimed at solving curtailment. This forced reduction in generation occurs because the amount produced by renewable sources varies according to wind and sun. During peak times, it is necessary to take these plants offline to prevent overload that could collapse the national grid.

The measure, however, brings losses to these plants. The country currently wastes the equivalent of a Belo Monte plant due to curtailment. One of the most effective ways to solve the problem is using batteries to store excess energy generated during peak hours for consumption when demand increases.

The provisional measure established that only those integrated with storage systems can access the Special Regime of Incentives for Infrastructure Development (Reidi), which provides tax discounts to solar and wind generation plants. In practice, entities complain that this limited the investment capacity of some companies.

"The text caused a setback, creating lack of equal treatment for solar energy compared to other technologies, by restricting access to Reidi. This type of requirement and limitation does not exist for any other energy source in Brazil," stated the entity.

Another criticized provision is the transfer of capacity reserve costs to new projects. This reserve involves contracting plants to be activated during times of low production but high demand. Since renewable energies are intermittent (they don't generate all the time), there is a need to contract more backup generators. The requirement imposed on new projects, according to the entity, increases costs, discourages new investments, and "may economically pressure renewable generation projects."

Additionally, the entities complain that Aneel approved, in 2022, two resolutions that increase the fee paid by energy generators as they are more distant from the country's main consumer hubs, such as the South and Southeast. Absolar evaluates that this "increased network usage costs for plants located far from load centers, especially in the North and Northeast" and "can displace photovoltaic projects," which is already happening.
