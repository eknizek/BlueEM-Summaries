# Brazil's Financial Constraints Could Be an Advantage in the AI Race, Experts Say

The unbridled enthusiasm surrounding artificial intelligence in the United States is drawing comparisons to the period of optimism and collapse of technology stocks in the early 2000s, known as the "dot-com bubble." This assessment comes from Thiago Kapulskis, partner and manager of the global technology fund at São Pedro Capital.

"As a stock market observer, I am increasingly convinced every day that we are in a bubble like the one in the 2000s," the manager stated during a panel at The AI Economy Brazil event, held by Sólides in collaboration with Bloomberg Línea.

The manager's main thesis is that the business indicators observed to distribute financing checks to AI startups are completely different from what they were in the last "boom" cycle among technology companies. This change could bring real profitability challenges for businesses that are skyrocketing in valuation.

Kapulskis argued that many of these startups do not show the discipline in business models that characterized companies like Google and Amazon in their early stages. "I don't see any debate in this cycle about how these new companies are going to generate revenue within three years," he said.

For Brazil, this could be an opportunity, according to the manager. This is because the scarcity of capital and greater cost challenges can impose on Brazilian startups a discipline that American ones lack.

"The advantage Brazilians have is that we live in a difficult country. A country where capital is expensive, where anyone who invests in a business will want to know how the company is going to make money," said Kapulskis. "Perhaps that is an advantage in this cycle."

## Fierce Competition

Even so, the outlook is challenging. Camila Vieira, partner and Brazil director of global investment firm QED Investors, highlighted that small and medium-sized companies enter the AI cycle weakened by years of high interest rates, fiscal instability, and regulatory changes that have diminished their investment capacity.

The dynamics of excess capital in the U.S. also becomes a fundraising challenge for Brazilian companies seeking global funders and must compete with American startups that go "from $500,000 to $100 million in revenue in months."

Despite the challenges, Vieira sees potential for disruptive businesses to emerge in Brazil as well. "Stripe, for example, launched years later and today it is talked about much more than PayPal. Perhaps a 'Brazilian Stripe' is well positioned in the future to compete with American ones," she said.

For now, the prominence of AI development has been in the hands of the financial sector. Institutions such as Itaú, Nubank, Banco do Brasil, and MercadoLibre are seeking sophisticated internal technological solutions, in contrast to the more cautious stance of major American banks.

"In one weekend, Itaú conducted a test that showed 40% better performance than the original model. In a company that size, that is a gigantic return," she said. "Brazilian financial companies are extremely sophisticated buyers of AI. Many good solutions are being developed, but there is still little talk about it."

Henrique Ferreira, partner at DGF Investimentos—a fund with 25 years of experience in technology—highlights the country's structural advantages in energy. Regulation of the electrical sector and the availability of renewable energy position Brazil as a candidate to host computing infrastructure, one of the main bottlenecks in AI development.

"In the realm of foundational models, we can play quite a strategic role in terms of energy," he said during the panel.
