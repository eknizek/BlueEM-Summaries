# Real estate development in Mendoza targets mining, energy, fintech and logistics companies

Hills, vineyards, fine dining, technology companies and mining and energy service firms—private investment seeks to recreate the California Napa Valley model in western Greater Mendoza. "Sectors such as mining, oil & gas, logistics, health, services, technology and knowledge economy today demand more sophisticated environments, well-connected and capable of expressing a new standard," said Julián Groisman, director of Grupo Presidente.

This is the developer group that owns the Palmares mall (the largest in the interior), with private neighborhoods, automotive dealerships, agricultural investments and a new project in Palmares Valley, aimed at families arriving in the region for energy and mining projects. Grupo Presidente's idea is to consolidate an urban and commercial axis in the foothills. Other competitors, such as Dalvian and Chacras Park, have developed urban and commercial proposals and services also in western Greater Mendoza.

In the case of Grupo Presidente, the project to attract families migrating due to Andean project development is called Valley Boulevard, a building in Palmares Valley, with recreation and networking areas. Groisman assured that they already have several interested parties, including some of the multinational firms that rank among the "Big Four" of auditing.

Mendoza has achieved a high level of services, partly due to wine tourism. "We must care for this opportunity and try to attract those who come to invest in the provinces of the west with mining and in Neuquén with oil and gas," added the businessman, noting that due to quality of life and services, Mendoza can capture all those families of professionals and technicians who settle in San Juan or Neuquén. "As occurs in La Serena and other Chilean cities, which although mining or renewable energy investments are not there, are the headquarters of those investor ecosystems," he argued.

The project aims at a new way of working, a building prepared to receive dynamic operations, qualified personnel and brands that need infrastructure, connectivity, representation and environment in one place. The developers of the district located in La Puntilla, on the border with Chacras de Coria, in the municipality of Luján de Cuyo, say they have a projection of more than 10,000 residents in the coming years.

The Valley Boulevard project seeks to attract professional offices, SMEs, expanding companies, regional headquarters and corporate headquarters. It is the third corporate development by Grupo Presidente in the area; they already have Avatar and Workplace. In a five-story building there will be modular offices from 30 m² to corporate floors of more than 1,000 m², common areas, meeting rooms, terraces, areas for hybrid work, technological infrastructure and an active ground floor with an open-air commercial promenade, designed to enhance daily networking.

"The work environment is migrating to these new spaces where you can socialize, have a coffee, do sports, like play padel, make launches, train or participate in a streaming," said Groisman.

## Agricultural business

In agricultural investments, the Groisman family has 350 hectares of vineyards in Mendoza and San Juan and 300 hectares of walnut groves in the Uco Valley. In the case of viticulture, Grupo Presidente transferred all vineyard operations to the Peñaflor group. They lease them the cultivated fields and audit that the plantations located in high enological value localities such as Los Árboles and El Cepillo in Mendoza, and in Zonda in San Juan, are properly cared for. All vineyard management and grape handling remains with Peñaflor.

"We have decided to concentrate our agricultural business on walnuts in Tupungato," Groisman clarified. They export all of their walnut production, 1,200 tons of Chandler variety, extra light, out of the 20,000 that the country exports. Their main buyers are in the Middle East, Italy, Russia and Brazil. They export 70% of walnuts with shell, because in destination countries, for example India, labor is cheaper and that is where the shell is manually removed. They send 30% shelled, as in Brazil.

The Middle East conflict is a concern for dried fruit exporters. "We expect more inflation and some impact on walnut prices, a commodity that had been stabilized in prices over the last three years," Groisman detailed.

## Electromobility

"The retail business opened the doors to exposure and sale of plug-in hybrid electric technology vehicles," added the Mendocino businessman. During the last quarter of 2025, they opened the Avanim dealership, with branches in Mendoza, San Juan and Neuquén, in partnership with the Antelo group. "Electromobility is here to stay. When people do a test drive, they buy and afterwards there is no turning back. I think this whole ecosystem that is being put together is great for the country," Groisman said, in support of the government's import opening policy by Javier Milei's administration.

They represent GMW (Haval), Changan, JMEV and Mitsubishi in western Argentina, selling between 80 to 100 units per month. "In 2026 we expect to sell 1,000 units a year," said the Presidente executive.

As an investor in different sectors, Groisman considered that urbanizations should gain presence in non-productive areas, such as the foothills of the Mendoza precordillera. And he stressed: "Seeing the growth of cities in the hills, as occurs in Santiago de Chile, we are very optimistic about the possibility of generating an urban and corporate hub, a new ecosystem, based on RIGI projects and other developments in the Andean region."
