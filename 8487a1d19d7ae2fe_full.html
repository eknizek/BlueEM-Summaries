# Mendoza Real Estate Development Targets Mining, Energy, Fintech and Logistics Firms

Grupo Presidente, the developer of Palmares mall and a company with businesses in agro-exports and auto dealerships, launched an office complex with networking spaces, a streaming room and a paddle tennis court.

Hills, vineyards, fine dining, technology companies and mining and energy service firms—private investment seeks to recreate the California Napa Valley model in western Greater Mendoza. "Sectors such as mining, oil & gas, logistics, health, services, technology and knowledge economy today demand more sophisticated, well-connected environments capable of expressing a new standard," stated Julián Groisman, director of Grupo Presidente.

This is the developer group that owns the Palmares mall (the largest in the interior), private neighborhoods, auto dealerships, agricultural investments and a new project in Palmares Valley, aimed at families arriving in the region for energy and mining projects. Grupo Presidente's idea is to consolidate an urban and commercial axis on the foothills. Other competitors, such as Dalvian and Chacras Park, have developed urban, commercial and service proposals also in western Greater Mendoza.

In the case of Grupo Presidente, the project to attract families migrating due to Andean project development is called Valley Boulevard, a building in Palmares Valley with recreation and networking areas. Groisman assured that they already have several interested parties, including some of the multinational firms that rank among the "Big Four" audit companies.

Mendoza has achieved a high level of services, partly due to wine tourism. "We must care for this opportunity and try to attract those who come to invest in the provinces of the west with mining and in Neuquén with oil and gas," added the businessman, noting that due to its quality of life and services, Mendoza can attract all those families of professionals and technicians who settle in San Juan or Neuquén. "As occurs in La Serena and other Chilean cities, which although mining investments or renewable energy projects are not located there, are the headquarters of those investor ecosystems," he argued.

The project aims for a new way of working—a building prepared to receive dynamic operations, qualified personnel and brands that need infrastructure, connectivity, representation and environment all in one place. The developers of the district located in La Puntilla, bordering Chacras de Coria in the municipality of Luján de Cuyo, say they have a projection of more than 10,000 residents in the coming years.

The Valley Boulevard project aims to attract professional firms, SMEs, expanding companies, regional offices and corporate headquarters. It is the third corporate development by Grupo Presidente in the area; they already have Avatar and Workplace. In a five-story building there will be modular offices from 30m² to corporate floors of over 1,000m², common areas, meeting rooms, terraces, areas for hybrid work, technological infrastructure and an active ground floor with open-air commercial space, designed to promote daily networking.

"The work environment is migrating to these new spaces where you can socialize, have a coffee, do sports like play paddle tennis, hold product launches, train or participate in streaming," said Groisman.

**Agricultural Business**

In agricultural investments, the Groisman family has 350 hectares of vineyards in Mendoza and San Juan and 300 hectares of walnut groves in the Uco Valley. In the case of wine production, Grupo Presidente ceded all vineyard operations to Peñaflor group. They rent the cultivated fields and audit that plantations located in high enological value towns such as Los Árboles and El Cepillo in Mendoza, and Zonda in San Juan, are properly maintained. All vineyard management and grape handling remains with Peñaflor.

"We have decided to concentrate our agricultural business on walnuts in Tupungato," Groisman clarified. They export their entire walnut production—1,200 tons of extra-light Chandler variety—out of the 20,000 tons the country exports. Their main buyers are in the Middle East, Italy, Russia and Brazil. They export 70% of walnuts in-shell, because in destination countries like India, labor is cheaper and that's where shells are removed manually. The remaining 30% are sent shelled, as in Brazil. The Middle East conflict is a concern for dried fruit exporters. "We expect more inflation and some impact on walnut prices, a commodity that had been stable in price over the last three years," Groisman detailed.

**Electric Mobility**

"The retail business opened doors for us to the display and sale of plug-in hybrid electric technology vehicles," added the Mendoza businessman. During the last quarter of 2025, they opened the Avanim dealership with branches in Mendoza, San Juan and Neuquén in partnership with Grupo Antelo. "Electromobility is here to stay. When people take a test drive, buy and afterwards there's no turning back. I think this entire ecosystem being built is great for the country," Groisman assured, in support of the import opening policy of Javier Milei's government.

They are representatives in western Argentina for GMW (Haval), Changan, JMEV and Mitsubishi, selling between 80 to 100 units per month. "In 2026 we expect to sell 1,000 units per year," said the Presidente executive.

As an investor in different sectors, Groisman believed that urbanizations should gain presence in non-productive areas, such as the foothills of Mendoza's pre-Andean range. And he stressed: "Seeing how cities are growing on hillsides, as happens in Santiago, Chile, we are very optimistic about the possibility of generating an urban and corporate hub, a new ecosystem, based on RIGI projects and other developments in the Andean region."
