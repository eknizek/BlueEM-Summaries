What Desenrola 1 did for Lula 3 and what Desenrola 2 can do for Lula 4

02 May 2026 · 08:00 PM

# What Desenrola 1 Did for Lula 3 and What Desenrola 2 Can Do for Lula 4

The government announces on Monday (4th) Desenrola 2 ("New Desenrola Brasil"), a program to incentivize debt renegotiation and reduction with Treasury support. Whatever one thinks of this type of measure, in the short term the financial squeeze on some will diminish, of course. Thus Lula 3 intends to gain tenths of a percentage point in approval and votes, so as to avoid Lula 4's defeat. This obvious introduction serves to remind us what effect Desenrola 1 had on default rates and perception of difficulties with debts: little.

Moreover, the Lula government's solution to almost everything—trying to drown problems with money injected directly—has not yielded much results. Desenrola 1 benefited many people, 14.8 million, and renegotiated R$ 53.2 billion in debts, especially for the poor. It lasted from July 2023 to May 2024. An indirect or partial measure of individuals' financial difficulty is loan default rates in banks, according to Central Bank data. Default fell from 4.18% in June 2023 to 3.62% in June 2024 and stayed near there until year's end. Families' spending on interest payments and debt amortization with banks fell from 28.2% in June 2023 to 26.8% in June 2024—then increased again. Total bank loans relative to family income fell from 48.4% to 47.7% in that period—then increased again. This "family income" includes salaries, interest, rent, etc. Interest rates also fell in that period. The Selic rate fell from 13.75% per year to 10.5% per year. The average rate for individuals in banks fell from 36.6% to 32.5%. The ICC (Credit Cost Indicator) for individuals fell through the end of 2023 and remained relatively stable throughout 2024, near the lowest level of the past four years.

In an April report, Bradesco's Economics Department wrote that the ICC "follows Selic variations with some lag, averaging six months." A drop of one percentage point in the Selic reduces the ICC by half a percentage point and spending on debt by 1%.

The National Trade Confederation conducts a monthly survey of perception of "indebtedness" in general. That is, whether people declare themselves indebted, in arrears, or foresee default. The month before Desenrola 1 began, in June 2023, 78.5% of respondents said they were "indebted"; 29.2% had "overdue accounts"; 12% "have no means to pay." In June 2024, "indebted" were 78.8%, in arrears 28.8%, and "have no means to pay" 28.8%. Perceptions changed very little, then.

In March 2026, indebted were 80.4%; in arrears, 29.6%; without means to pay, 12.3%. Default with banks increased. Interest rates increased significantly. Lula thinks this financial squeeze harmed his popularity. It may be. But throughout Desenrola 1 and somewhat after it, Lula's prestige was declining slightly; the biggest drop occurred before the most recent financial squeeze.

Desenrola 1 helped many people, but little changed the overall picture of individuals' credit situation. And it is difficult to attribute variation in Lula's ratings to worsening in interest rates and credit (or it could be that and much more).

Desenrola 2 may increase Lula's voting share by some tenths of a percentage point. But the bulk of recent discontent and deep-rooted aversion to the president comes from somewhere else.
