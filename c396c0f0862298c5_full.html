What Desenrola 1 Did for Lula 3 and What Desenrola 2 Can Do for Lula 4

02 May 2026 · 08:00 PM

# What Desenrola 1 Did for Lula 3 and What Desenrola 2 Can Do for Lula 4

The government announces on Monday (4th) Desenrola 2 ("New Desenrola Brasil"), a program to encourage debt renegotiation and reduction with Treasury support. Whatever one thinks of this type of measure, in the short term the financial squeeze on some will ease, of course. Thus Lula 3 intends to gain tenths of percentage points in approval and votes, in order to avoid the defeat of Lula 4. This obvious introduction serves to recall what effect Desenrola 1 had on default rates and perception of difficulties with debt: little.

Moreover, the Lula government's solution for almost everything—trying to drown problems with injections of money—has not been yielding much results.

President Lula benefited many people with Desenrola 1—14.8 million people—and renegotiated R$ 53.2 billion in debts, especially for the poor. It lasted from July 2023 to May 2024. An indirect or partial measure of financial difficulty for individuals is the default rate on bank loans, according to Central Bank data. It fell from 4.18% in June 2023 to 3.62% in June 2024 and remained close to that until the end of that year. Families' spending on interest payments and debt amortization with banks fell from 28.2% in June 2023 to 26.8% in June 2024—and then increased again. The total of bank loans in relation to family income fell from 48.4% to 47.7% in that period—and then increased again. This "family income" includes salaries, interest, rents, etc. Interest rates also fell during that period. The Selic rate went from 13.75% per year to 10.5% per year. The average rate for individuals in banks fell from 36.6% to 32.5%. The ICC (Credit Cost Indicator) for individuals fell until the end of 2023 and remained relatively stable throughout 2024, close to the lowest level of the past four years.

In an April report, Bradesco's Economics Department wrote that the ICC "tracks Selic variations with some lag, averaging six months". A drop of one percentage point in the Selic decreases the ICC by half a percentage point and by 1% the amount spent on debt.

The National Confederation of Commerce conducts a monthly survey of perception of "indebtedness" in general. That is, whether people declare themselves indebted, in arrears, or foresee default. In the month before Desenrola 1 began, in June 2023, 78.5% of respondents said they were "indebted"; 29.2% had "bills in arrears"; 12% "have no conditions to pay". In June 2024, "indebted" were 78.8%, in arrears 28.8%, and "have no conditions to pay" 28.8%. Perceptions changed very little, then.

In March 2026, indebted were 80.4%; in arrears 29.6%; unable to pay 12.3%. Default with banks rose. Interest rates rose significantly. Lula believes this financial squeeze hurt his popularity. It may be. But throughout Desenrola 1 and shortly after it, Lula's prestige declined somewhat; the biggest drop occurred before the more recent financial squeeze.

Desenrola 1 helped many people, but little changed the overall picture of the credit situation for individuals. And it is difficult to attribute the variation in Lula's approval ratings to worsening interest rates and credit (or it could be that and much more).

Desenrola 2 could increase Lula's votes by a few tenths of a percent. But the bulk of recent discouragement and the entrenched aversion to the president comes from somewhere else.
