Supreme Court begins ruling on oil royalty revenue sharing; producing states defend current framework

06 May 2026 · 11:27 PM

# Supreme Court Begins Ruling on Oil Royalty Revenue Sharing; Producing States Defend Current Framework

The Supreme Federal Court (STF) resumed on Wednesday (May 6) the trial on the model for distributing oil royalties. Representatives of the Union and producing states and municipalities stated that the amounts received constitute compensation for exploitation and defended maintaining current rules. This group includes, among others, the states of Rio de Janeiro and São Paulo.

On the other hand, other parties argued that revising the norms addresses the equitable distribution of national wealth through equal federalism. The plenary heard reports on the cases and all parties and interested parties in the proceedings on Wednesday and is set to begin ruling on the matter Thursday (May 7), starting with the vote of the rapporteur, Justice Carmen Lúcia. The case has been pending for 13 years. The matter had a preliminary injunction from the rapporteur in 2013 and was scheduled for judgment multiple times, including in 2020 and 2023, but was repeatedly postponed—even at the request of producing states like Rio de Janeiro.

On Tuesday (May 5), the Union requested maintenance of current rules. According to the AGU (Attorney General's Office), the measure maintains balance in federal relations and prevents financial collapse of producing states and municipalities. According to the filing, any change to the current understanding would represent a revenue loss of R$9 billion per year for the Union. The document also expressed concern that a ruling could force federal, state, and municipal governments to reimburse amounts already received.

In oral arguments, Andrea Dantas stated that altering the revenue-sharing model affects federative balance by favoring states and municipalities not affected by oil exploitation, distorting the compensatory nature outlined in the Constitution. Rio de Janeiro's attorney general Gustavo Binenbojm spoke on behalf of the state. According to him, Rio is the largest producer: it produces 86% of oil and 76% of natural gas. "The state benefits little from this production because all companies operating in Rio de Janeiro claim ICMS tax credits, and in the end the state cannot charge this ICMS. The logic of the rule was clear: to balance this logic through these participations," he said.

Binenbojm stated the debate concerns essential values, including the autonomy of local communities, as well as the federative pact and legal certainty of acquired rights. According to him, Rio de Janeiro's estimated annual loss is around R$23 billion.

Espírito Santo's attorney general Cláudio Penedo Madureira stated there is a dispute between the majority, formed by non-producing states, and the minority. "There is precedent from this court for solving the problem. In summary, they claim royalties aim to partly compensate for exploitation, and therefore are intended for affected states. These precedents need to be maintained," he said.

São Paulo's attorney general Inês Maria dos Santos Coimbra stated that the law being debated removes revenues historically destined for impacted states. "It shifts revenues from entities bearing the effects of this exploitation to entities not dealing with these externalities."

"The question is whether the legislature can misrepresent the regime expressly written into the Constitution, and the answer is no. The impacts of extractive activity also fall on these entities, which need to strengthen public services, environmental and social demands," she said. According to her, the change has effects that "are severe and structural," with potential annual impact of R$2.5 billion to the state and São Paulo municipalities. Furthermore, numerous coastal municipalities significantly depend on this revenue for essential public services: 120 municipalities receive these amounts, and in at least 6 of them it represents more than 10% of total revenue.

On the other side, representatives from Bahia, Rio Grande do Sul, Paraná, Alagoas, Goiás, and municipalities spoke. For these parties, oil is a national asset and therefore its royalties should be distributed equitably. "We are not dealing with terrestrial oil, and therefore have no justification for this hyperconcentration. The challenge is preventing federalism from becoming distorted and entrenching itself with extreme asymmetries," said Goiás's attorney general Rafael Arruda Oliveira.

For him, countries like the United States and Australia distribute offshore oil revenues among their states, and the choice of geographic criteria to determine who should receive royalties is a Brazilian exception. Bahia's attorney Barbara Camardelli stated the process concerns structuring federalism. "It was said that we have a majority against a majority. What are we talking about? When we take ICMS collection without royalties, RJ, SP and ES hold 37% of ICMS. With royalties it is 39%."

According to her, the rule should not be immutable and the system should reflect the current socioeconomic scenario. Rio Grande do Sul's representative Eduardo Cunha da Costa also added the current international environment. According to him, conflicts and wars impacted the increase in revenues, which should be redistributed as national wealth.
