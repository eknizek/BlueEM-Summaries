# New Desenrola: BTG calculates bank impact; see details

The New Desenrola, a debt renegotiation program announced on Monday by the federal government, introduces a more interventionist framework compared to its first version, according to analysts at BTG Pactual, pointing out that the program brings explicit obligations for participating financial institutions, rather than merely voluntary participation.

They highlight that banks must "automatically clear" (remove negative records) consumers with debts of up to R$100; extend renegotiation offers across main credit lines — including credit cards, overdraft accounts, personal loans and student financing (Fies) — and comply with behavioral and social conditions.

"This design significantly expands the program's reach," stated Luiz Guanais and team in a report sent to clients. According to BTG's team, while the previous Desenrola focused on renegotiated volumes, the new version emphasizes credit reintegration at scale, targeting a broader base of people with negative credit records.

"It is important to highlight that the automatic nature of clearing small debts reduces friction and should accelerate the normalization of credit scores, something crucial in a system in which even small defaults can significantly compromise access to formal credit," they stated.

They emphasized that the New Desenrola occurs at a time when family balance sheets are at historically extreme levels, "which reinforces the urgency of public policy action." At the same time, they added, delinquency indicators remain elevated across main credit lines — credit cards and personal loans — and negative records at credit agencies affect tens of millions of consumers.

In this context, they emphasize, the mandatory policy of "clearing names" for debts of up to R$100, combined with renegotiation mechanisms offering discounts of up to 90% and an interest rate cap of 1.99% per month, directly targets the base of the credit pyramid.

In BTG's team's view, the rules for using FGTS balance to pay off outstanding debts from the New Desenrola reduce misuse of resources and ensure that the liquidity injection is directed directly to deleveraging. The program allows use of up to 20% of the FGTS balance (or R$1,000) to settle debts, with direct transfer to banks.

Guanais and team also drew attention to the prohibition on online betting for those accessing the New Desenrola. "This is particularly relevant given the rapid growth of the betting market in Brazil, with sector estimates pointing to expansion exceeding 200% annually in 2025 and an increasing share of family income being directed to these activities. By restricting access for program participants for a period of 12 months, the government seeks to redirect financial resources toward essential consumption and debt payment."

The New Desenrola, a debt renegotiation program for families, micro and small businesses and family farmers, is expected to use up to R$15 billion in Union guarantees to enable lower interest rates for debtors, with a fiscal impact of up to R$5 billion.

## Credit Cycle

From the perspective of the credit cycle, BTG analysts assessed that the main transmission mechanism lies in rehabilitating access to formal credit. However, they noted that the impact on originating new financings tends to be gradual, since banks remain constrained by high interest rates and a more risk-averse stance.

They estimate a stabilization of volumes, not a sharp acceleration, with more visible effects in financed categories, such as electronics, clothing and e-commerce, where credit penetration can exceed 50% to 70% of transactions.

They also note that quantitative impact should vary between sectors, with food retail, which already absorbs about 35% to 40% of family consumption, potentially benefiting from these measures. Still, they maintained caution regarding the magnitude and duration of this impact.

"Desenrola 2.0 should help stabilize consumption trends in the short term, but sustained recovery will ultimately depend on a more favorable macroeconomic environment — especially from falling interest rates and reduced financial burden on families."
