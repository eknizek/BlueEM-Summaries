# Early Childhood Education as Strategic Evidence-Based Investment

We are experiencing a historic moment for discussion about early childhood education. First, because this topic is gaining increasingly more relevance in the national public debate, moving away from a focus solely on the supply of childcare and preschool slots and concentrating on establishing a concept of what should be understood as quality in this segment. As a practical example, we have the launch of the National Commitment to Quality and Equity in Early Childhood Education (Conaquei), which represents a significant step toward a real collaboration regime between the Federal government, states, and municipalities focused on offering children more than just the guarantee of a place, but a quality space where each child has the opportunity to develop all their potential.

Early childhood education is addressed in the National Common Curricular Base (BNCC), a 2017 document that formalizes the basic principles of this stage and recognizes the rights of children enrolled in school units to socialize, play, participate, explore, express themselves, and get to know themselves in the school space. Despite this, there are still many municipalities—the federal entities responsible for early childhood education—that are poorly structured to ensure that these basic rights recognized by the BNCC form the basis of pedagogical work in each unit. This occurs both due to the absence of adequate training infrastructure and due to pressure from society and oversight bodies focused only on the supply of slots, not necessarily on quality slots that guarantee all these rights to each child.

In reality, municipalities have always received more pressure than support to address the complexity of early childhood education. Conaquei emerges as a collaborative effort to change this scenario, offering support to municipal education departments so that the principles advocated by the BNCC move from paper to reality and there is, in fact, a true sharing of responsibilities.

It is known by much of society, especially those interested in issues related to early childhood, that investments in early childhood education generate enormous returns for socioeconomic development. Many people are familiar with the study conducted by impact evaluation specialist James Heckman on the social return of investment in early childhood. This 2006 study demonstrates that resources applied at this stage of human life generate returns of up to double those invested in later phases; in other words, there has been consensus that investments in early childhood are paramount for at least 20 years. But the question that remains is: why don't we yet have all our attention focused on early childhood education?

Historically, Brazil is a country that began investing late in public school quality. This caused us to accumulate a historical debt to Brazilian society, reflected in the high illiteracy rates we face. Furthermore, before the 1988 Constitution, childcare did not emerge as a demand from the education field, but rather as a social assistance demand, in the sense of guaranteeing a place where children could stay and receive basic care, food, hygiene, and safety so that their guardians could pursue their professional activities.

It was only with the Law of Guidelines and Bases of Education (LDB) in 1996 that early childhood education was officially recognized as the first stage of basic education and thus affirmed the inseparability between caring and educating. Nevertheless, we still face a challenge in prioritizing early childhood education, as the budget allocated to it is far from sufficient to guarantee quality provision, and even organized civil society still has little focused activity and scalable initiatives in this important phase of children's development.

In this context, it is imperative that early childhood education be increasingly strengthened as the first stage of basic education through instruments, indicators, and constant monitoring so that the concept of quality is not something subjective and abstract, but grounded in reliable data and evidence. For this, initiatives like the Program for Improving the Quality of Early Childhood Education, which the Fefig Institute implements in partnership with the Laboratory for Studies and Research in Social Economics at FEA (Lepes) of USP in Ribeirão Preto, prove essential.

Such initiatives bring continuous training, management support, and most importantly, concrete quality indicators, both of the processes occurring in municipal education departments and school units, and of children's development in the dimensions of expression and communication; self-knowledge, self-care and autonomy; imagination, investigation, transformation, and creation; empathy, self-regulation, engagement, and cooperation. All of this so that each municipality can structure itself increasingly better to offer this stage all the attention it deserves, and thus reap the fruits in regional human development.

The more we strengthen early childhood education in each of Brazil's 5,570 municipalities with training of technical teams, technical management support, and evaluation and monitoring of quality based on evidence, the closer we will be to the country we all dream of and that our children deserve: just, prosperous, and less unequal.
