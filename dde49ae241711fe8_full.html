# Early Childhood Education as Evidence-Based Strategic Investment

We are experiencing a historic moment for discussion about early childhood education. First, because this topic is gaining increasing relevance in the national public debate, moving away from a focus solely on the supply of daycare and preschool slots and focusing on formulating a concept of what should be understood as quality in this segment. As a practical example, we have the launch of the National Commitment for Quality and Equity in Early Childhood Education (Conaquei), which represents a significant step toward a real collaborative framework between the federal government, states, and municipalities focused on offering children more than just guaranteed slots, but quality spaces where each child has the opportunity to develop all of their potential.

Early childhood education is covered in the National Common Curricular Base (BNCC), a 2017 document that formalizes the basic principles of this stage and recognizes the rights of children enrolled in school units to socialize, play, participate, explore, express themselves, and know themselves in the school space. Despite this, there are still many municipalities – the federal entities responsible for early childhood education – that are poorly structured to ensure that these basic rights recognized by the BNCC form the basis of pedagogical work in each unit. This occurs both due to the absence of adequate training structures and due to pressure from society and oversight bodies focused only on the supply of slots, not necessarily on quality slots that guarantee all these rights to each child.

In reality, municipalities have always received more scrutiny than support in dealing with the complexity of early childhood education. Conaquei emerges as a collaborative effort to change this scenario, offering support to municipal education departments so that the principles advocated by the BNCC move from paper to practice and there is actual sharing of responsibilities.

It is widely known by much of society, especially those interested in early childhood issues, that investments in early childhood education generate enormous returns for socioeconomic development. Many people are familiar with the study conducted by impact evaluation expert James Heckman on the social return on investment in early childhood. This 2006 study demonstrates that resources applied at this stage of human life generate returns of up to double those invested in later phases; that is, there is consensus that investments in early childhood are paramount, and this has been true for at least 20 years. Yet the question remains: why don't we have everyone's attention focused on early childhood education?

Historically, Brazil is a country that began investing late in the quality of public schools. This caused us to accumulate a historical debt to Brazilian society, reflected in the high illiteracy rates we face. Furthermore, before the 1988 Constitution, care for children did not emerge as a demand from the educational field, but as a demand from social assistance, in the sense of guaranteeing a place where children could stay and receive basic care, food, hygiene, and safety so that their guardians could carry out their professional activities.

It was only with the Law of Guidelines and Bases of Education (LDB) in 1996 that early childhood education became officially recognized as the first stage of basic education and thus affirmed the inseparability of care and education. Still, we face a challenge in prioritizing early childhood education, as the budget allocated to it is far from sufficient to guarantee quality provision, and even organized civil society still has limited action focused on this crucial stage of children's development.

In this context, it is imperative that early childhood education be increasingly strengthened as the first stage of basic education through instruments, indicators, and continuous monitoring so that the concept of quality is not something subjective and abstract, but grounded in reliable data and evidence. To this end, initiatives such as the Program for Improvement of Early Childhood Education Quality, which the Fefig Institute implements in partnership with the Laboratory for Studies and Research in Social Economics at FEA (Lepes) at USP in Ribeirão Preto, are essential.

Such initiatives provide continuing education, management support, and most importantly, concrete quality indicators, both of processes occurring in municipal education departments and school units and of children's development in the dimensions of expression and communication; self-knowledge, self-care and autonomy; imagination, investigation, transformation and creation; empathy, self-regulation, engagement and cooperation. All of this so that each municipality can increasingly better structure itself to offer this stage all the attention it deserves and thus reap the fruits in regional human development.

The more we strengthen early childhood education in each of Brazil's 5,570 municipalities with training of technical teams, technical management support, and quality assessment and monitoring based on evidence, the closer we will be to the country we all dream of and that our children deserve: fair, prosperous, and less unequal.
