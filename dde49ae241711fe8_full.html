# Early Childhood Education as Evidence-Based Strategic Investment

We are experiencing a historic moment for discussion about early childhood education. First, because this topic is gaining increasing relevance in national public debate, moving away from a focus solely on the supply of childcare and preschool slots toward defining what quality means in this segment. As a practical example, we have the launch of the National Commitment to Quality and Equity in Early Childhood Education (Conaquei), which represents a significant step toward true collaborative governance between the Union, states, and municipalities, focused on offering children more than just a guaranteed slot, but a quality space where each child has the opportunity to develop all their potential.

Early childhood education is addressed in the National Common Curricular Base (BNCC), a 2017 document that formalizes the basic principles of this stage and recognizes the rights of children enrolled in school units to socialize, play, participate, explore, express themselves, and know themselves in the school environment. Despite this, there are still many municipalities—the federative entities responsible for early childhood education—that are poorly structured to ensure that these basic rights recognized by the BNCC serve as the foundation for pedagogical work in each unit. This occurs both due to the absence of adequate training structures and due to pressure from society and oversight bodies focused solely on the supply of slots, not necessarily on quality slots that guarantee all these rights for each child.

In reality, municipalities have always received more scrutiny than support in dealing with the complexity of early childhood education. Conaquei emerges as a collaborative effort to change this scenario, offering support to municipal education secretaries so that the principles advocated by the BNCC move from paper to practice, with actual shared responsibility.

It is well known to much of society, especially those interested in early childhood issues, that investments in early childhood education generate enormous returns for socioeconomic development. Many people are familiar with the study conducted by impact evaluation specialist James Heckman on the social return of investment in early childhood. This 2006 study demonstrates that resources applied at this stage of human life generate returns of up to double those invested in later phases. In other words, there has been consensus that investments in early childhood are paramount for at least 20 years, but the question that remains is: why don't we yet have everyone's attention focused on early childhood education?

Historically, Brazil is a country that began investing late in the quality of public education. This caused us to accumulate a historical debt to Brazilian society, reflected in the high rates of illiteracy we face. Furthermore, before the 1988 Constitution, care for children did not emerge as an educational demand but as a social assistance demand, in the sense of guaranteeing a place where children could stay and receive basic care, food, hygiene, and safety so that their guardians could perform their professional activities.

It was only with the Law of Directives and Bases of Education (LDB) of 1996 that early childhood education became officially recognized as the first stage of basic education, thus affirming the inseparability of care and education. Still, we face the challenge of prioritizing early childhood education, as the budget allocated to it is far from sufficient to guarantee quality provision, and even organized civil society still has little focused action with scalable initiatives at this important stage of children's development.

In this context, it is imperative that early childhood education be increasingly strengthened as the first stage of basic education through instruments, indicators, and constant monitoring so that the concept of quality is not something subjective and abstract, but grounded in reliable data and evidence. For this, initiatives such as the Program for Improving the Quality of Early Childhood Education, which the Fefig Institute implements in partnership with the Laboratory for Studies and Research in Social Economics at FEA (Lepes) at USP Ribeirão Preto, prove essential.

Such initiatives bring continuous training, management support, and most importantly, concrete quality indicators—both of the processes that occur in municipal education secretaries and school units and of children's development in the dimensions of expression and communication; self-knowledge, self-care, and autonomy; imagination, investigation, transformation, and creation; empathy, self-regulation, engagement, and cooperation. All this so that each municipality can become increasingly better structured to offer this stage all the attention it deserves, and thus reap the fruits of regional human development.

The more we strengthen early childhood education in each of Brazil's 5,570 municipalities with training of technical teams, technical management support, and quality evaluation and monitoring based on evidence, the closer we will be to the country we all dream of and that our children deserve: fair, prosperous, and less unequal.
