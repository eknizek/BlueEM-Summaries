# Aramis hires more data engineers than fashion designers in technology pivot

Richard Stad, CEO of Aramis Inc., cites the "South Korea project" to explain the movement of the firm he leads: for him, it is possible to transform a company in two or three generations. Son of the founder of the traditional menswear retailer, Henri Stad, the executive's project is to transform the brand into a tech hub in the sector. To do this, he is expanding technology and data teams. Twenty-three interns focused on artificial intelligence were hired, and Stad is searching the market for an AI manager. Today, there are 55 employees focused on developing new systems, which represents about 5% of the boutique's workforce—considering stores, headquarters, and distribution centers. Additionally, the company has set up three innovation teams as a way to decentralize technology-focused functions at the retailer. They are composed of engineers and data analysts who, according to the executive, "have a lot of autonomy."

The company wants to get ahead in what it believes is a race to see who learns to adopt innovations first. In addition to its own app and e-commerce, the group develops programs for internal use to map customer behaviors and trends. Physical stores still represent 93% of the group's sales. Even so, there was 43% growth in sales through the company's digital channels in 2025 compared to the previous year.

Stad uses Aramis's traditional tailoring service as an example of using AI to improve customer experience. Someone who buys a suit, pants, or shirt from the brand can request adjustments from a tailor in the store at no additional cost. If that same customer makes a purchase through the company's digital channels, for example, they may receive the pieces with adjustments already made—since the measurements will already be in the retailer's system. Internally, systems are trained to understand the habits, desires, and problems of customers and suppliers with greater precision and speed.

The investment in changing the company's profile is high, but figures were not disclosed. When asked about the expectation of financial return from the undertaking, the CEO cites a case instead of numbers: one of the AI agents created by the firm helps executives build their business plans—among the ideas, Stad is only interested in those with an ROE (return on earnings) of at least two times. "We have required an expectation of a two-times return, unless it is a project that we understand as structural and will not bring results in the short term," says the CEO.

Traditional retail is experiencing a moment of pressure with the Selic rate at high levels and high consumer debt. Stad states that the company's pivot to the "tech" universe is not related to the challenging scenario. "We are still a fashion retailer, I don't want to escape the sector I'm in," he declares. He doubles down by saying that 2026 should be one of the years of highest investment for the company, even with the economic tightening scenario—the group expects to generate up to R$ 1 billion this year. In 2025, the group opened 16 stores and generated R$ 800 million.

The moment of change appears in changes to the shareholder structure in December 2025. Bradesco exited the business—in which it had invested through 2bCapital—selling a 47% stake, while two new investment funds entered: Concept Investimentos and Fener Capital, each with 32.5%. The founder, Henri Stad, also zeroed out his stake. Today, Richard Stad owns 35% of the company and is the largest individual shareholder.
