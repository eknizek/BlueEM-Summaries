What's behind Caputo's strategy to get dollars

16 April 2026 · 11:22 PM

# What's Behind Caputo's Strategy to Get Dollars

The economic team, headed by Luis Caputo, is negotiating with the World Bank for a loan of around 2 billion dollars, to which another 2 billion would be added from CAF and the Inter-American Development Bank. The disbursement depends on the approval of the Bank's board of directors.

The significance of this information becomes clear when it is noted that Caputo and his collaborators are analyzing taking that credit as a guarantee, in principle, for issuing a bond through which to raise a larger amount: speculation suggests they would take between 8 and 10 billion dollars. The Ministry of Economy moved to cool expectations: official sources denied on Thursday afternoon that a debt issue of that magnitude is on the table.

In recent hours, the economic team has begun studying another option: forgoing a bond and using multilateral loans to back a credit from a group of banks. The Treasury faces 9 billion dollars in maturities for the rest of the year. Next year will be much more demanding: the amount to be paid will be 27 billion dollars.

The operation being negotiated by the economic team, if materialized, would suppose a significant improvement in the prospects of the economic program. Above all because of the fall in the country-risk index. Consequently, Javier Milei's political competitiveness would also rise.

Issuing debt in the conventional market is a strategic issue for Caputo. But it is possible that, once again, it will be ruled out. The last attempt to achieve this objective was vetoed by Milei. It was when the President disauthorized the issuance of a security to raise funds at a rate higher than 9%. The first consequence of that refusal was the resignation of the Secretary of Finance, Alejandro Lew. He was replaced by Federico Furiase. Furiase's almost exclusive purpose, under Caputo's pressure, was to enable access to funds at a lower cost.

The solution being negotiated now is that part of the credit risk be assumed by one or more institutions more trustworthy than the Argentine Republic. Of course, this is an exit that provokes controversy, since it conceals one of the Government's weak points: its economic plan still does not inspire confidence in the markets.

The formula that would count on the guarantee of the World Bank, CAF, and the Inter-American Development Bank would work as follows: hypothetically, starting from a 10 billion dollar operation, 4 billion dollars would be taken at a rate of around 4%, which is what these multilateral institutions charge. The remaining 6 billion would be taken at a rate of 9%. This means that for the total volume of the loan, a rate of 7% would be paid.

The recourse to these multilateral organizations completes a financing initiative being worked on within the economic team for weeks, with the collaboration of a Swiss bank. Resistance to issuing a bond would deprive the Government of the possibility of achieving a very valuable objective: the ability to finance itself in the voluntary debt market. The inability to do so is one of the main shortcomings of the program led by Milei. Officials celebrate the program as the best ever developed, but financiers are not yet willing to bet on the experiment.

It is true that even if a security is issued, the appeal of that paper would not be given by the solidity of economic policy but by the guarantee of multilateral organizations.

The difficulty in taking on debt in the conventional market promises political discussion. From the outset, some opposition economists are developing arguments to object to the operation that the economic team is still studying in a preliminary manner. The main objection is that the Treasury diverts World Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, and CAF resources that could be used to improve infrastructure and thus revive the economy.

"If we could go to the market with a bond, with only the guarantee of the quality of economic policy, we could use multilateral loans to get the economy out of the inertia it is in," explains one of these technicians.

The recourse to external guarantees is a logic that Caputo has been testing with setbacks since mid-last year. Once he received the check for 20 billion dollars from the U.S. Treasury, he sought to obtain a similar sum for debt repurchase. The banks invited to provide the funds requested a Treasury guarantee, which Scott Bessent refused to extend. The project failed. Now it is intended to try the same trick in a more modest way. And with a difference: the money from banks backed by the Treasury did not subtract resources from projects that could boost a reactivation.

Between the failed experiment with the Treasury and various banks and the one now being conducted with the World Bank, a vital factor reappears for Milei: the willingness of the United States, strictly speaking, of Donald Trump, to support the management of his ally. The Treasury's influence over the World Bank is decisive, even though the current president of the entity, Ajay Banga, was proposed by Democrat Joe Biden.

During the week there was an indication of this strategy. It was in the paragraph of the agreement reached with the International Monetary Fund referring to financing. It reads: "An integral strategy is being implemented to refinance currency obligations through the continuous issuance of national debt denominated in dollars, the sale of state assets, repurchase operations with central banks, and external loans, potentially backed by international financial institutions. It is expected that, over time, this strategy will drive timely and sustainable access to international capital markets."

The novelty, although it generates controversy, offers great benefits to the Government. A critical problem for Milei and Caputo is the financing of public spending while persisting in fiscal balance. The decision to put all the weight on spending cuts has not only reached its limit. It has exceeded it. The cuts are producing creaks throughout the administration, as evidenced by the conflict that opened with PAMI suppliers, to cite the most strident case. The authorities will be able to execute the budget in a less dramatic way.

Another very important front is that of Central Bank reserves. Accumulating them is a categorical imperative for Milei's management. But this improvement in the Central Bank's assets would be illusory if the dollars acquired are used to pay the Treasury debt. If the possibility of borrowing at a more reasonable rate is confirmed, the Central Bank's reserve profile will improve in absolute terms. This last front is always very important. But it acquires greater relevance in the current international context.
