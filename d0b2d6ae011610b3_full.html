# Food Inflation Surges in April 2026

By the end of April, the set of 33 essential products for the diet of Mexican families reported an annual increase of 6.32 percent. According to the price survey conducted monthly by Excélsior, the cost of acquiring the basic basket, defined by the defunct Coneval and now used by Inegi, reached 2,360.40 pesos on April 30.

This amount not only represents immediate pressure on family budgets but also raises the four-month increase in essential foods to 3.42 percent. This sum represents not only an immediate blow to purchasing power but also raises the accumulated increase in the first four months of the year to 3.42 percent.

## By Product

The report highlights annual variations that have forced families to make adjustments in their diet. The product with the greatest impact was potatoes, whose price per kilogram soared to 59.00 pesos, representing an annual increase of 79.33 percent. This was followed by tomatoes, with an increase of 69.05%, Lenten chili (47.37%), and limes (42.86%).

The spike in food costs is not an isolated phenomenon. According to data from the National Institute of Statistics and Geography (Inegi), inflation in the first half of April stood at 4.53% annually, exceeding the 3.96% recorded in the same period of the previous year. With this, the indicator reaches four consecutive readings above Mexico's Central Bank (Banxico) tolerance range of 3.0% +/- one percentage point.

Beyond food, external factors such as tensions in the Middle East have begun to filter their impact into the national index. Premium gasoline recently appeared among the items most contributing to inflation, driven by the rise in international crude oil prices following the conflict in Iran.

## Restructuring Spending

Inflationary pressure is forcing a restructuring of personal finances. According to the most recent survey by Research Land, the market research agency of Grupo UPAX, 52% of Mexicans say that their financial situation so far this year has been much more complicated than anticipated.

For most, managing money has become a survival tactic. 45% of respondents stated they have had to prioritize basic expenses above any other plan, while 25% make constant adjustments to stay "afloat."

"The price increase is not an abstract phenomenon; it translates into daily decisions such as cutting spending on food, health, entertainment, not saving and, much less, buying any insurance," noted Pablo Levy, general director of Research Land. He emphasized that the Mexican consumer is acting under a logic of emergency: "What we see is a rational reaction: when inflation pressures the wallet, priorities change."
