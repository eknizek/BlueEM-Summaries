# Brazil systematically loses share in global economy

The "de-Brazilification" of global GDP should be the major theme of our elections

By Pedro Fernando Nery

04/05/2026 | 4:50 PM

Updated: 04/05/2026 | 5:15 PM

Over the last three decades, Brazilian industry has grown by more than 30% according to IBGE. This information might surprise you, because we're always talking about "deindustrialization." In fact, an alien who visited Brazil in the 1990s and returned today would report back to its planet that the country had industrialized.

The alien would look at households. Many had few appliances decades ago. Since then, access to refrigerators and stoves has become universal—even washing machines have become popular—all with enormous consequences for nutrition, hygiene, and time management. More recently, a small machine that does what several large machines once did has also become available, even in the poorest homes: cell phones. The alien would also report the abundance of other industrial products, like clothing and household items.

We speak of deindustrialization not about the reduction of industrial products in our economy, but about the reduction in their production here. The alien would be confused by this idea, and perhaps would even point out that it's good for a country to have hundreds of millions of industrialized products without having to produce them.

But deindustrialization also doesn't mean a reduction in industrial GDP or even industrial production; both have increased. What is deindustrialization, after all? The reduction in the share of industry in total GDP. Industry grew, but other sectors grew more. By this logic, any economy will always experience a "de" of something, since it's impossible for all sectors' relative growth to always increase. Even within industry, some sector will lose relevance.

Because of this, the concept of deindustrialization bothers me, but I'd like to propose another: de-Brazilification. Brazilian GDP has grown in recent decades. But much less than that of other countries. Throughout our history, Brazil gained a share of the global economy. In the 1980s, de-Brazilification began: we had more than 3% of global GDP, and we've been in free fall ever since, now with less than 2%.

De-Brazilification was not foreseen. PwC, in 2008, predicted that by this year Brazil's growth would be surpassing China's. After decades of growing at 4% per year, we would have a GDP 80% larger than today's. It would be another country.

Why did Brazil fall behind countries that had the same income at the end of the dictatorship, like Korea or Poland? We don't need to go that far: why, in Latin America, did we go from the 3rd highest GDP per capita to the 9th?

De-Brazilification should be the theme of these elections.
