# Euphoria and Uncertainty Reinforce Impact of Oil Price Shock on Global Economy

Oil prices collapsed and stock markets rose worldwide on Wednesday, April 8, driven by the immediate relief brought by the ceasefire between the United States and Iran, announced the previous day, and by the partial reopening of the Strait of Hormuz in the Persian Gulf, which had been responsible for interrupting 10% or more of global oil supplies in recent weeks.

Market euphoria, however, contrasts with the certainty that the effects of the war on the global economy are far from being resolved. The resumption of production and transportation of oil tankers, for example, should take weeks or months. Furthermore, the fact that the agreement is limited to a 15-day ceasefire between adversaries who have never adopted a conciliatory tone since the start of the conflict reinforces the degree of uncertainty in the geopolitical scenario in the short term.

Market relief, in practice, confirmed the damage caused by the war—initiated six weeks ago, with effects on the infrastructure of the Persian Gulf region, a major global oil producer.

The Brent oil index opened the day trading between $92.40 and $95.36 per barrel, a decline of 13% to 15% compared to the previous day. The WTI index, from the United States, followed the movement, being sold between $94.98 and $96.82 per barrel, a decline of 14% to 16% compared to the previous day. Both contracts had surpassed $110 during the peak of tensions, but now are undergoing strong correction with geopolitical relief.

In financial markets worldwide, optimism was similar. U.S. futures markets registered gains for the Dow Jones (+2.8%), S&P 500 (+2.9%), and Nasdaq 100 (+3.5%), reflecting the reduction of immediate risk of prolonged disruption in the Strait of Hormuz, driving technology stocks and sectors sensitive to energy costs.

Stocks in Asia, where countries import large quantities of oil and gas, also registered significant gains. Japan's Nikkei 225 index rose 5.4%, while South Korea's Kospi index rose nearly 7%. Markets in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and mainland China also showed significant increases.

In Europe, the Stoxx 600 index, a broad European index, rose 3.5% by midday.

At the same time, the global rally in stocks following the ceasefire announcement is not due solely to geopolitical relief, but also to a possible short squeeze—as the abrupt movement of price increases in a stock or index is called, which forces investors who were short (betting on the decline) to quickly repurchase these assets to limit losses, and this rush to repurchase pushes prices even higher.

Hedge funds had been aggressively expanding short positions in recent weeks—in March, they sold short at a ratio of 7.6 to 1, according to data from Goldman Sachs' brokerage unit. With the stock market surge, these bets against the market began generating losses and may be unwinding, fueling even more of the rally. Defensive strategies based on sales of ETFs and futures, used to protect portfolios during the war, now become an additional risk for those who remain short, increasing the likelihood of abrupt repurchase movements, especially in Europe.

## Suspended Threats

In the ceasefire agreement announced by President Trump on Tuesday evening, Iran agreed to allow ships to pass through the strait without being attacked. Earlier that day, Trump had declared that if the waterway remained closed, "an entire civilization will die tonight, never to be recovered again."

Trump also repeatedly threatened to attack Iranian power plants and other critical infrastructure if Iran does not allow vessels to pass through the strait—acts that could be considered war crimes.

However, experts warn that the return of the oil flow through the Persian Gulf will be slow, as will the recovery of the global economy. U.S. gasoline prices, for example, rose again on Wednesday, April 8, reaching a national average of $4.16 per gallon, according to the AAA auto association. The increase has raised costs for drivers by 40% since the start of the war. Diesel prices increased even more rapidly and reached $5.67 on Wednesday, an increase of 51% in six weeks.

Analysts emphasize that the resumption of oil transport through the Strait of Hormuz depends not only on the security of the waterway, but also on equipment inspection, replacement of critical parts, and reorganization of teams and vessels dispersed during the conflict.

"It's not as if you simply press a button and everything goes back to functioning," said Martin Houston, a veteran executive in the oil and gas sector who now serves as a board member of several energy companies.

Even with the expectation that some wells will return to operation in days or weeks, experts say that complete normalization will take months—and in cases of severe damage, years. Strategic facilities, such as the liquefied natural gas plant at Ras Laffan in Qatar, had part of its capacity destroyed and will require long reconstruction periods due to technical complexity and global shortage of specialized components. Refineries in Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Iran also operate at reduced capacity, while supply chains pressured by demand for energy infrastructure and data centers make it difficult to quickly replenish turbines and cooling systems.

Before the start of the U.S.-Iran conflict, an average of 138 vessels transited through the Strait daily. Since the beginning of the operation, that number fell more than 90%, with daily crossings often dropping to single digits.

"For markets, the flow of oil through the Strait matters more than oil inventory, that is, the energy infrastructure to produce it, because the flow keeps the global economy moving, given that the oil market before the conflict was close to excess inventory, with oversupply," said Ben Emons, founder of Fed Watch Advisors.

"In this sense, the reopening of the Strait functions as the reopening during the pandemic and has characteristics already priced into financial markets, such as being a form of quantitative easing, which was inherently more about flow than about inventory," he adds.
