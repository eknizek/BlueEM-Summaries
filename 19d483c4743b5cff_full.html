# From Colina 1 to the World: Q Studio's Copper Cladding

In a restaurant in France, in a law office in Tel Aviv, and in a hotel in the Middle East, there are copper surfaces that do more than decorate: they tell a story. They don't come from a traditional factory or a large automated industry, but from a workshop installed inside Colina 1 prison in Santiago, Chile. From there, Q Studio has taken Chilean design to the world, combining materiality, innovation, and an unusual commitment: producing with incarcerated people.

The project's origins trace back to 2008, when Codelco launched a contest at the Universidad Diego Portales Design program to develop luminaires using a new electrolytic copper sheet that had begun production. Among all proposals, that of student Estefanía Johnson stood out, as she decided to move away from the obvious. While others designed lamps, she proposed a cladding that incorporated lighting. Years later, Codelco contacted her again to develop the product in a workshop inside the prison. That's when Gonzalo Santana, an industrial engineer, joined the project. The first product—the Lum cladding—quickly went from prototype to exportable piece.

**The Takeoff**

The breakthrough came in 2012. Without a formally established company, they applied to a ProChile program and the Division of Cultures, Arts, Heritage and Public Diplomacy to exhibit in Milan. "We were just starting, building everything from scratch," recalls Gonzalo Santana. They were selected and traveled to Italy. "It was incredible," he remembers. The following year, they began exporting. Today, Q Studio has a presence in more than 20 cities across 11 countries, with projects installed mainly in law offices, architecture studios, hotels, and restaurants.

While Lum was their starting point, the current focus is on custom developments. "Today we live off special projects. People who want to incorporate copper but don't know how, and we design that specific cladding," he explains.

In Chile, their work has been installed in spaces of high symbolic value and daily use. One of the most emblematic is the Palacio Pereira, where they developed the main staircase cladding, combining heritage with contemporary materiality. This is complemented by projects in corporate offices, such as the Teck mining company building, where they intervened on several floors with custom solutions, and recent developments for Codelco, with claddings that incorporate visual identity. Their presence also extends to public infrastructure, such as Santiago Airport, as well as institutional and corporate spaces where copper is used not only as a finishing material but also as a design element.

The versatility of copper allows them to offer a palette of 12 colors. This is no coincidence: it's a "magical material," as founder Estefanía Johnson defines it. "One of its most distinctive qualities is color, peculiarly warm for a metal. The wide range of tones it can acquire according to its oxidation phase—without degrading—allows for multiple finishes: you can practically paint with its own oxidations," she explains.

However, what truly distinguishes Q Studio is not just its aesthetic proposal, but its production model. From the beginning, they decided to maintain manufacturing inside Colina 1 prison, despite the complexities involved. "Many people think it's cheap labor, but it's not. They earn a salary, logistics are complicated, and there are many hidden costs. If you do it, it's not for economic reasons," affirms Santana.

**A Demanding Model**

The system operates through an agreement with Gendarmería, which administers payments to inmates. Currently, the number of workers varies depending on projects, with a core team that can expand during periods of high demand. But beyond production, the challenge lies in reintegration once they are released. "It's not easy. Many companies ask for background certificates and that greatly limits opportunities," Santana acknowledges. Still, the team seeks to generate impact beyond the workshop. Some former inmates have managed to start their own businesses, while others receive support and job recommendations. "We try to contribute as much as possible so that reintegration exists," he says.

The decision to maintain this model stems from a conviction present from the beginning. When Estefanía decided to study Design, she thought of it as a contribution to the world, not just for designing objects. "And that became our value proposition," explains her partner.

Other factors complement this vision: the use of Chilean copper—with lower environmental impact in its processing—and local production. "We fulfill three pillars: social, environmental, and economic," he summarizes. "We are the world's leading copper producer, but we only export it as raw material, without any added value, only to later reimport it as a finished product," Johnson says. Q Studio decided to go in the opposite direction.

The environmental component is added to this: the sheets used allow reducing carbon footprint by up to 80%, as they come from tailings ponds and require fewer resources—especially water—than traditional laminating. **Looking to the Future**

Looking forward, the objective is clear: reaching Asia, particularly China. "One always thinks about what comes from China, but we want to do the opposite: we want to take our product there," affirms Santana. Although they have already worked in markets like Dubai and India, they see China as a natural opportunity for Chilean copper. "Chilean designers must appropriate our copper to transform it into products charged with our identity and offer the world a unique, own, and different value," Johnson concludes.
