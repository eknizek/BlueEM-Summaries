# The clues 'DIAN's eye' followed to dismantle alleged criminal structure in Lili Pink case

A series of alerts detected by the National Tax and Customs Authority (Dian) ended up becoming one of the largest operations against textile smuggling in Colombia in recent times. The investigation, which today involves the Lili Pink chain and the company Fast Moda S.A.S., resulted in raids, asset forfeiture measures, and inquiries into alleged money laundering exceeding 730 billion pesos.

The case began to take shape in 2022, when an internal control system known as 'The Eye of the Dian' detected irregular access to tax databases. According to an investigation by the newspaper 'El Colombiano', some officials allegedly consulted sensitive information without apparent justification, which raised alarms about possible illegal maneuvering linked to textile commerce. The investigations advanced to identify an alleged business network that, according to the Prosecutor's Office, allegedly used fictitious companies, irregular billing operations, and possible fraudulent VAT refunds to move resources and facilitate operations related to imported merchandise.

The magnitude of the case led to the intervention of more than 300 stores and 405 establishments distributed across 59 municipalities and 25 departments of the country. The operation became one of the most relevant recent blows against structures investigated for alleged smuggling and money laundering within the clothing sector. The authorities maintain that the investigated network allegedly laundered more than 730 billion pesos. In addition to this are suspicions of alleged smuggling close to 75 billion pesos and possible illicit enrichment operations that would reach approximately 430 billion pesos.

## Fictitious companies and VAT refunds at the center of investigations

According to the Antioquia newspaper, one of the points that most caught investigators' attention was the alleged use of shell companies to support non-existent commercial operations. According to the investigations, several companies allegedly were used to issue fictitious billing and support irregular requests for tax refunds. The Dian and the Prosecutor's Office are also investigating possible maneuvering related to non-existent VAT, which allegedly allowed reducing tax obligations and moving resources within the scheme under judicial analysis. The authorities believe that part of these operations could have served to hide the origin of money related to smuggling.

The file also mentions possible underinvoiced imports from Asia. According to the authorities, some merchandise allegedly entered the country reporting values lower than actual with the objective of reducing tax payments and customs controls. The routes identified by investigators include corridors considered sensitive for smuggling, including areas such as Maicao, Barranquilla, and Tumaco. Tracking these commercial movements was key to consolidating the investigation that today keeps the company under scrutiny.

Although the process advances on the judicial and patrimonial front, the authorities clarified that the stores will continue operating while investigations are ongoing. The decision seeks to avoid immediate impact on commercial activity and jobs linked to the chain.

## Economic impact and pressure on the Colombian textile sector

The case also brought back to the table the historical concerns of the Colombian textile sector regarding irregular entry of merchandise. Business owners and authorities have warned for years that underinvoicing and smuggling generate strong distortions in prices and competition within the market. The authorities believe that this type of practice can directly affect formal manufacturers and marketers, especially in segments of clothing and women's fashion where price competition has a determining weight in consumption.

The investigation involves Fast Moda S.A.S., the company behind brands such as Lili Pink, Yoi, and Lili Beauty. The Prosecutor's Office seeks to establish whether the company's commercial expansion could have been used to move resources of illicit origin through operations related to textile imports.

After the operations became known, the company assured that it is collaborating with the authorities and asked to protect the continuity of the operation and jobs associated with its stores. For now, the establishments will remain open while judicial and administrative proceedings advance. The case quickly became one of the most relevant recent files for the Dian and the Prosecutor's Office in terms of customs control and money laundering. In addition to the millions of dollars involved, the investigation again focused attention on the structural weaknesses that the country still faces in the fight against textile smuggling.
