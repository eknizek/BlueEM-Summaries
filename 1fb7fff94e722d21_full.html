# Iran conflict pushes up international oil and gas prices, threatening Brazil's electricity bills

The recent rise in international oil and natural gas prices can pressure energy generation costs in Brazil, with effects that tend to reach consumers' pockets. The warning comes from Thymos Energia consulting firm, which released a study forecasting the impact of fuel price increases on two electrical system indicators that are passed on to electricity bills.

One is the increase in the Fuel Consumption Account (CCC), which subsidizes energy generation in isolated systems, especially in the North Region. Another point of concern is the increase in the Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants. In both cases, energy generation depends on fossil fuels, whose price increases end up reaching the electricity tariff.

Another survey by TR Soluções estimates that contracting power for the Brazilian electrical system through the capacity reserve auctions (LRCAPs) in March will generate an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in the next decade, which will also be passed on to electricity bills. By 2032, consumers will face 7.5% increases in tariffs and large industries, 13.5%.

The Thymos study does not calculate how much the electricity tariff will be impacted by the Middle East conflict, especially since oil and natural gas price fluctuations are still ongoing. During the Ukraine war in 2022, for example, Brent – the oil index used as an international reference for prices – exceeded US$ 139 per barrel. In the same period, the CCC recorded a 41% increase compared to the previous year, driven by rising fossil fuel prices.

Now, with the new escalation in the Middle East, Brent has again surpassed the US$ 100 per barrel mark, reaching US$ 109.27 on April 7, which rekindles alerts about pressure on this charge. The bad news is that, unlike other indicators, the CCC has no legally defined ceiling, meaning every increase is entirely passed on to consumers through the Energy Development Account (CDE).

The increase in the Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants also deserves attention. "From the moment fuels become more expensive, these plants become more costly for the electrical system," states Ana Paula Ferme, Head of Utilities and Economic Regulation at Thymos.

Additionally, April marks the beginning of the dry season in the country, with fewer rain incidents. In a more restrictive hydrological scenario, the effect of the CVU increase can be even more intense, since lower water availability tends to increase thermal dispatch precisely in a context of more expensive fuels.

The consequence, according to Ferme, is the increase in the Price of Difference Settlement (PLD), the short-term energy market reference, with potential to further pressure energy costs in the country. That is, electricity purchase contracts of distributors may face adjustments due to increased PLD and thermal costs, further pressuring tariffs.

The effects, however, should not be perceived homogeneously or simultaneously. The increase in the PLD should be felt more immediately. Meanwhile, the impacts on the CCC are moving toward materializing later, being noticed more intensely in subsequent tariff cycles. Should the crisis intensify and there be a review of the CDE budget during 2026, the consequences could affect consumers even this year.

"The consequences may not be limited to the electrical sector," warns the Thymos specialist. "The increase in energy costs tends to pressure inflation and affect the competitiveness of the Brazilian economy as a whole."

Costly Auction

The TR Soluções survey brought more bad news to the electrical sector. According to the consulting firm, the power contracting through the capacity reserve auctions of March 18 and 20 will generate an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in the next decade. Although the greatest impact is expected after 2030, part of the weight will already begin to be felt in 2026.

This is because the consulting firm indicates that the increase in the power charge should represent an average increase of 0.4% in tariffs for captive consumers already this year. In practice, the TR Soluções study reinforced disappointment with the LRCAP modeling, which generated criticism regarding the high power contracted and low auction competition – in which gas and coal thermoelectric plants, as well as hydroelectric and thermoelectric plants powered by fuel oil and biodiesel were contracted.

In total, 18,977 megawatts (MW) of power were contracted, involving R$ 64.5 billion in investments and an average discount of only 5.52%. At the time of the auction, Abrace Energia's calculation – an association of large industrial energy consumers – estimated an annual cost of R$ 40 billion, impacting the average electricity tariff for Brazilians by approximately 10%, versus the 7.5% now estimated by TR Soluções.

The TR Soluções study, however, predicts a larger increase for industrial consumers operating in the free energy market, who will now participate in the allocation of power contracting charges. The reserve charge should increase from the current R$ 7 per megawatt-hour (MWh) to R$ 78 MWh by 2032. Previously, expenses with power in the Brazilian electrical system were paid by captive market consumers, but now free market contracting environment clients will also share these costs, which explains the predicted 13.5% increase.
