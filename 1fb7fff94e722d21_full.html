# Iran War Bill Will Arrive in Brazilian Electricity Bills

Rising international prices for oil and natural gas could increase energy generation costs in Brazil, directly impacting consumers. Thymos Energia consulting firm warns that increases in the Fuel Consumption Account (CCC) and the Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants, which depend on fossil fuels, will be passed on to electricity rates.

During the Ukraine war, Brent crude exceeded $139 per barrel, resulting in a 41% increase in CCC. With the new escalation in the Middle East, Brent has surpassed $100, reigniting concerns about the CCC, which has no legal ceiling. Additionally, lower water availability during the dry season could intensify CVU impact. TR Soluções consulting estimates an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in 2026, with a 7.5% increase in rates through 2032, affecting industries particularly.

Beyond all problems caused in the Brazilian and global productive sectors, the recent rise in international oil and natural gas prices can pressure energy generation costs in the country, with effects that tend to reach consumers' pockets. This is the warning from Thymos Energia consulting, which released a study forecasting the impact of fuel price increases on two electrical system indicators passed on to electricity bills. One is the increase in the Fuel Consumption Account (CCC), which subsidizes energy generation in isolated systems, especially in the North Region. Another point of concern is the increase in the Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants. In both cases, energy generation depends on fossil fuels, whose price increases eventually reach the electricity rate.

Another survey from TR Soluções estimates that contracting power for the Brazilian electrical system through capacity reserve auctions (LRCAPs) in March will generate an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in the next decade, which will also be passed on to electricity bills. By 2032, consumers will face a 7.5% increase in rates and large industries 13.5%.

The Thymos study does not calculate how much the electricity rate will be impacted by the Middle East conflict, especially since oil and natural gas price fluctuations are still ongoing. During the 2022 Ukraine war, for example, Brent – the petroleum index used as an international reference for prices – exceeded $139 per barrel. In the same period, CCC registered a 41% growth compared to the previous year, driven by fossil fuel price increases. Now, with the new escalation in the Middle East, Brent has again surpassed the $100 mark, reaching $109.27 on April 7, which rekindles alerts about pressure on this charge.

The bad news is that, unlike other indicators, the CCC has no ceiling defined by law, meaning every increase is entirely passed on to consumers through the Energy Development Account (CDE). The increase in Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants also deserves attention. "Once fuels become more expensive, these plants become more expensive for the electrical system," states Ana Paula Ferme, Head of Utilities and Economic Regulation at Thymos.

Additionally, April marks the beginning of the dry season in the country, with fewer rainfall incidents. In a more restrictive hydrological scenario, the effect of CVU increase can be even more intense, since lower water availability tends to increase thermal dispatch precisely in a context of more expensive fuels. The consequence, according to Ferme, is the rise of the Price for Settlement of Differences (PLD), a reference for the short-term energy market, with potential to further pressure energy costs in the country. That is, energy purchase contracts of distributors may suffer adjustments due to increased PLD and thermal costs, further pressuring rates.

The effects, however, should not be perceived homogeneously or simultaneously. The PLD increase should be felt more immediately. The impacts on CCC are set to materialize later, being felt more intensely in following tariff cycles. Should the crisis intensify and there be a CDE budget review during 2026, the consequences could affect consumers even this year. "The consequences may not be limited to the electrical sector," warns the Thymos specialist. "Energy cost increases tend to pressure inflation and affect the competitiveness of the Brazilian economy as a whole."

The TR Soluções survey brought more bad news to the electrical sector. According to the consulting firm, contracting power through capacity reserve auctions on March 18 and 20 will generate an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in the next decade. Although the greatest impact is forecast after 2030, part of the burden will already begin to be felt in 2026. This is because the consulting firm indicates that the increase in power charges should represent an average increase of 0.4% in rates for captive consumers already this year.

In practice, the TR Soluções study reinforced disappointment with LRCAP modeling, which generated criticism regarding high contracted power and low auction competition – in which gas and coal thermoelectric plants were contracted, in addition to hydroelectric and thermoelectric plants powered by fuel oil and biodiesel. In total, 18,977 megawatts (MW) of power were contracted, involving investments of R$ 64.5 billion and an average discount of only 5.52%.

At the time of the auction, calculations by Abrace Energia – an association of large industrial energy consumers – estimated an annual cost of R$ 40 billion, impacting the average electricity rate for Brazilians by approximately 10%, against the 7.5% now estimated by TR Soluções. The TR Soluções study, however, predicts a greater increase for industrial consumers operating in the free energy market, who will now participate in sharing power contracting charges. The reserve charge should rise from the current R$ 7 per megawatt-hour (MWh) to R$ 78 MWh by 2032. Previously, power-related expenses in the Brazilian electrical system were paid by captive market consumers, but now free contracting environment clients will also share these values, which explains the predicted 13.5% increase.
