Iran War Costs Will Reach Brazilian Consumers' Electricity Bills

24 April 2026 · 06:09 PM

# Iran War Costs Will Reach Brazilian Consumers' Electricity Bills

The recent spike in international crude oil and natural gas prices could pressure energy generation costs in Brazil, with effects likely to reach consumers' wallets. The warning comes from Thymos Energia consulting firm, which released a study forecasting the impact of rising fuel prices on two electrical system indicators that are passed on to electricity bills.

One of these is the increase in the Fuel Consumption Account (CCC), which subsidizes energy generation in isolated systems, especially in the Northern Region. Another point of concern is the increase in the Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants. In both cases, energy generation depends on fossil fuels, whose price increases ultimately reach the electricity tariff.

Another survey by TR Soluções estimates that the contracting of power for the Brazilian electrical system through capacity reserve auctions (LRCAPs) in March will generate an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in the early next decade, which will also be passed on to electricity bills. By 2032, consumers will face a 7.5% increase in tariffs and large industries, 13.5%.

The Thymos study does not calculate how much the electricity tariff will be impacted by the Middle East conflict, especially since oil and natural gas price fluctuations are still ongoing. During the Ukraine war in 2022, for example, Brent – the crude oil index used as an international reference for prices – exceeded $139 per barrel. During the same period, the CCC recorded a 41% growth compared to the previous year, driven by rising fossil fuel prices.

Now, with the new escalation in the Middle East, Brent has surpassed the $100 per barrel mark again, reaching $109.27 on April 7, which renews concerns about pressure on this charge. The bad news is that, unlike other indicators, the CCC has no ceiling defined by law, meaning any increase is entirely passed on to consumers through the Energy Development Account (CDE).

The increase in the Unit Variable Cost (CVU) of thermoelectric plants also deserves attention. "Once fuels become more expensive, these plants become more costly for the electrical system," says Ana Paula Ferme, Head of Utilities and Economic Regulation at Thymos. Additionally, April marks the beginning of the dry season in Brazil, with less rainfall. In a more restrictive hydrological scenario, the effect of CVU increases could be even more intense, since lower water availability tends to increase thermal dispatch precisely in a context of more expensive fuels.

The consequence, according to Ferme, is the rise in the Settlement Price of Differences (PLD), a reference for the short-term energy market, with potential to further pressure energy costs in the country. In other words, the energy purchase contracts of distributors may suffer adjustments due to increases in the PLD and thermal costs, further pressuring tariffs.

The effects, however, should not be perceived uniformly or simultaneously. The PLD increase should be felt more immediately. Meanwhile, the impacts on the CCC are set to materialize later, being felt more intensely in subsequent tariff cycles. Should the crisis intensify and there be a revision of the CDE budget during 2026, the consequences could affect consumers even this year.

"The developments may not be limited to the electrical sector," warns the Thymos specialist. "Higher energy costs tend to pressure inflation and affect the competitiveness of the Brazilian economy as a whole."

## Costly Auction

TR Soluções' survey brought more bad news to the electrical sector. According to the consulting firm, power contracting through capacity reserve auctions on March 18 and 20 will generate an annual cost of R$ 48 billion in charges starting in the early next decade. Although the largest impact is expected after 2030, part of the burden will already begin to be felt in 2026. This is because the consulting firm indicates that the increase in the power charge should represent an average increase of 0.4% in tariffs for captive consumers already this year.

In practice, the TR Soluções study reinforced disappointment with the LRCAP modeling, which generated criticism regarding the high contracted power and low auction competition – in which thermoelectric plants fueled by gas and coal were contracted, in addition to hydroelectric and thermoelectric plants powered by fuel oil and biodiesel. In total, 18,977 megawatts (MW) of power were contracted, involving investments of R$ 64.5 billion and an average discount of only 5.52%.

At the time of the auction, a calculation by Abrace Energia – an association of large industrial energy consumers – estimated an annual cost of R$ 40 billion, impacting the average electricity tariff for Brazilians by approximately 10%, compared to 7.5% now estimated by TR Soluções.

However, the TR Soluções study predicts a greater increase for industrial consumers operating in the free energy market, who will now participate in the distribution of power contracting charges. The reserve charge should rise from the current R$ 7 per MWh to R$ 78 per MWh by 2032. Previously, expenses with power in the Brazilian electrical system were paid by captive market consumers, but now free contracting environment clients will also share these costs, which explains the predicted 13.5% increase.
