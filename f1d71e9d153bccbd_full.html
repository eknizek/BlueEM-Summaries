# Government Prepares Winter With Rate Hikes, But Risks of Power and Gas Cuts

The Government is preparing for the arrival of winter with new energy rate increases for households and industry, as well as a high risk of power and gas cuts during the coldest days. This emerges from a document circulating among sector companies, accessed by Clarín: the wholesale price of electricity would climb starting in May above $100,000 per megawatt-hour (MWh; equivalent to $100 per kWh, the unit that reaches households), from the current $73,500, an increase of 35%.

The "seasonal price" of energy accounts for approximately 35% to 40% of the total bill amount, so if the Government decided to fully pass this increase on to users, bills would see a rise of around 10% to 15%. The final figure will be impacted by the official subsidy policy, as well as any last-minute changes in the programming of dispatch and costs in the electrical sector.

In consultations with Government offices by Clarín, however, they guarantee that the magnitude of the increases and their transfer to bills will be significantly lower than what appears in that official document.

Similarly, industry will see an even greater increase: it will go from paying $59,200 per megawatt-hour to $111,350, a jump of 88% in the wholesale price of energy.

According to data from RICSA ALyC, until March the wholesale price of energy—considering all types of contracts—was $83,180 per MWh, and users covered 77% of the cost, against 21% in State subsidies.

The reason for the increases lies in greater use of natural gas and liquid fuels (diesel and fuel oil) by the electrical system to generate thermal energy, at prices more expensive than the rest of the year—due to lower availability of gas in the domestic market, given that residential demand multiplies by 5 in winter.

Despite the increases, the risk of energy cuts remains latent and reaches up to 34% in extreme situations, according to simulations conducted by official technicians. "The probability of supplying the maximum demand peak with all reserves is 66% and there is a 34% probability of requiring additional operational measures," the document states.

That is, if electrical sector demand approaches 28,700 megawatts (MW) at the end of June or early July, the Government could be forced to implement a series of measures, given an estimated shortage of 2,000 MW:

- Ask industry and service stations to cut their gas consumption.

- Resort urgently to energy imports—more expensive—from neighboring countries.

- Face problems with electrical supply (cuts and voltage drops) in some regions.

On July 1, 2025, the winter power demand record was reached, with 28,119 MW. At that time, there were also scheduled and unscheduled gas cuts, such as those that complicated supply in Mar del Plata households and some Patagonia localities. The risk of cuts in previous winters was 19%.

However, official sources dismissed the probability of that scenario and assure that the chances of energy cuts are not statistically significant.

## Why Energy is Lacking in Argentina

Possible problems in energy supply have their origin in the lack of sustained investments over time—at least over the last 12 years—in electrical infrastructure, as well as shortcomings in official planning. Underlying this is the need to have dispatch as cheap as possible, to pay fewer subsidies without reinforcing social discontent through new rate increases and temporary jumps in inflation.

"It's the same old story. The system is on the brink of collapse at peak consumption," lamented a former official in conversation with Clarín, on condition of anonymity.

Upon beginning his administration, the Javier Milei government canceled the awarding of a bid to expand the thermoelectric park by 3,000 MW, which would have resolved part of the current problem. A technical report found that it will now be necessary to resort to imports averaging between 1,000 MW and 1,200 MW, mainly from Brazil and Bolivia, at $115 per MWh—$45 more expensive than the prices from that bid awarded in November 2023.

The additional cost would be $240 million this winter—in a total business of $600 million that energy traders from Brazil will take—and will be paid through subsidies or in users' bills.

Precisely to avoid adding more energy costs to the system, the Government canceled on Wednesday the process to privatize liquefied gas imports and will continue managing that operation, possibly with State money.
