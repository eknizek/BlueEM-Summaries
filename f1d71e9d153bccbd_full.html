Argentina's government prepares for winter with tariff increases amid electricity and gas shortage risks

23 April 2026 · 08:43 PM

# Argentina's Government Prepares for Winter with Tariff Increases Amid Electricity and Gas Shortage Risks

The Government is preparing for the arrival of winter with new energy tariff increases for households and industry, as well as a high risk of electricity and gas cuts during the coldest days. This emerges from a document circulating among sector companies, accessed by Clarín: the wholesale price of electricity would climb from May above 100,000 pesos per megawatt-hour (MWh; 100 pesos per kWh, the unit of measurement that reaches households), from the current 73,500 pesos, an increase of 35%.

The "seasonal price" of energy accounts for approximately 35% to 40% of the total bill, so if the Government decided to fully pass this increase to users, bills would rise by around 10% to 15%. The final figure will be impacted by the Government's subsidy policy, as well as any last-minute changes in the programming of dispatch and costs in the electricity sector.

However, Government departments consulted by Clarín guarantee that the magnitude of the increases and their transfer to bills will be substantially less than what appears in that official document.

Industry, meanwhile, will see an even larger increase: it will go from paying 59,200 pesos per megawatt-hour to 111,350 pesos, a jump of 88% in the wholesale price of energy.

According to RICSA ALyC data, until March the wholesale price of energy—considering all types of contracts—was 83,180 pesos per MWh, and users covered 77% of the cost, against 21% in State subsidies.

The reason for the increases is the greater use of natural gas and liquid fuels (diesel and fuel oil) by the electricity system to generate thermal energy, at higher prices than the rest of the year—due to lower availability of gas in the domestic market, given that residential demand multiplies by 5 in winter.

Despite the increases, the risk of energy cuts remains latent and reaches up to 34% in extreme situations, according to simulations conducted by official technicians. "The probability of supplying peak maximum demand with all reserves is 66% and there is a 34% probability of requiring additional operational measures," the document states.

That is, if electricity sector demand approaches 28,700 megawatts (MW) in late June or early July, the Government could be forced to take a series of measures, faced with an estimated shortage of 2,000 MW:

- Ask industry and gas stations to cut their gas consumption.

- Resort urgently to energy imports—more expensive—from neighboring countries.

- Face electricity supply problems (cuts and voltage drops) in some regions.

On July 1, 2025, a record winter power demand was reached with 28,119 MW. At that time, there were also scheduled and unscheduled gas cuts, such as those that complicated supply in homes in Mar del Plata and some Patagonia localities. The risk of cuts in previous winters was 19%.

However, official sources ruled out the probability of this scenario and assure that the possibilities of energy cuts are not statistically relevant.

## Why Argentina Lacks Energy

The eventual problems in energy supply stem from the lack of sustained investments over time—at least over the last 12 years—in electrical infrastructure, as well as shortcomings in official planning. Behind it all is the need to have the cheapest dispatch possible, to pay fewer subsidies without intensifying social unrest over new tariff increases and temporary inflation spikes.

"It's the same old story. The system is on the verge of collapse at peak consumption," lamented a former official speaking with Clarín, under anonymity.

At the start of the administration, Javier Milei's government cancelled the award of a tender to expand the thermoelectric park by 3,000 MW, which would have solved part of the current problem. A technical report revealed that now imports will have to be resorted to averaging between 1,000 MW and 1,200 MW, mainly from Brazil and Bolivia, at US$115 per MWh, US$45 more expensive than the prices of that tender awarded in November 2023.

The additional cost would be US$240 million this winter—in a total business of US$600 million that energy traders from Brazil will take—and will be paid through subsidies or in user tariffs. Precisely to not add more energy costs to the system, the Government cancelled on Wednesday the process to privatize liquefied gas imports and will continue managing it, possibly with State funds.
