# The transactions that became market trends—and will continue

Reverse IPOs, appetitive acquisitions and follow-ons for liquidity remain on the radar

By Maria Luíza Filgueiras — São Paulo, April 19, 2026

More than four years without initial public offerings, combined with high interest rates, leveraged companies and a strong dollar for most of that period, have led to new trends in transaction formats and capitalization options in Brazil. Companies and banks did not stand idle. "The Brazilian market demonstrated remarkable adaptability, developing new tools to maintain activity even in adverse scenarios," says Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

A recent movement that should continue as an alternative if the listing window remains closed for longer is the so-called reverse IPOs. This happens when there is a transaction between a privately held company and an already listed company, resulting in a new company traded on the stock exchange. Often, it is the private company that becomes the controlling shareholder.

This was the path chosen by Bradesco to list its health vertical, which merged with Odontoprev, a dental plan company already listed on B3. It occurred on the same day as another similar transaction: OceanPact, an oil industry services company, incorporated CBO. New combinations between public and private companies are in the works.

These transactions are closed mainly through share exchange relationships, without cash outlay, which has also become the most common currency in M&As in general in the Brazilian market. Two years ago, Arezzo and Soma merged by exchanging shares. Orizon made its largest M&A by paying for Vital to Queiroz Galvão with shares. The BRF and Marfrig merger had a cash component but also share exchanges. 3R and Enauta also became Brava Energia in a combination of shares.

"We will also continue to see small follow-ons, where companies focus on increasing liquidity, and the format known as PIPE, in which funds make a relevant investment in a publicly traded company but with some governance rights," highlights Cristiano Guimarães, head of investment banking at Itaú BBA.

A PIPE announced recently was Advent's investment in Natura.

Companies that wanted to test the receptiveness of their investors, analysts and regulators to new strategies preferred in recent years a cautious path, with a smaller initial check, by purchasing minority stakes. This is what Inpasa did in Vibra, Muffato in Assaí, Ultrapar in Rumo, the Coelho Diniz family and Pereira in Grupo Pão de Açúcar. In the food retail company, for example, the Coelho Diniz family decided to continue advancing in stock purchases and became reference shareholders of GPA. But other appetitive positions have already turned sour—showing that it makes sense to commit less capital initially.

Pharmaceutical company EMS built a position in Hypera to attempt a merger, which was rejected by the target company's board. EMS has already moved on: it just bought Medley from Sanofi's hands.

Despite this divestment by the French pharmaceutical company, Anderson Brito, head of investment banking at UBS-BB, notes a strategic movement by European companies over the last three years. "The flow of Europeans today is greater on the buy side than on the sell side. It's about US$6 billion to US$10 billion on average on the buyer side and US$3 billion to US$6 billion on the seller side," he says.

In energy, for example, while American AES left the country, French EDF made two acquisitions, and Spanish Iberdrola consolidated the purchase of Brazilian Neoenergia. Last year, Norwegian Visma bought Conta Azul—which also went on to make acquisitions under its new owner, acquiring MaisMei this year. In consumption, Europeans also indulged: Swiss Nestlé bought Kopenhagen, Italian Ferrero bought bold snacks from Minas Gerais and Ferrara Candy—which, despite being American, is held by one of Ferrero's heirs—bought Dori Alimentos.

Even private equity funds adapted. With few exit options and investors with less willingness for new contributions, vehicles began to conduct more credit transactions. Research by Condere shows that half of the funds already operate some structured credit product, and 87% intend to increase this exposure. "It's a movement that already happens abroad and is gaining strength in Brazil," says Paulo Cury, founder of Condere.
