# Transactions That Became Market Trends—And Will Continue

A sequence of more than four years without initial public stock offerings, combined with high interest rates, leveraged companies, and a strong dollar for most of that period, has led to new trends in transaction formats and capitalization options in the country. Companies and banks haven't stood still. "The Brazilian market demonstrated remarkable adaptability, developing new tools to maintain activity even in adverse scenarios," says Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

A recent movement that should continue as an alternative if the listing window remains closed for longer is the so-called reverse IPOs. This occurs when there is a transaction between a privately held company and an already listed company, resulting in a new publicly traded company. Often, the private company becomes the controlling shareholder.

This was the path chosen by Bradesco to list its health division, which merged with Odontoprev, a dental insurance company already listed on B3. It happened on the same day as another similar transaction: OceanPact, which provides services to the oil industry, incorporated CBO. New combinations between public and private companies are in the works.

These transactions are closed primarily through stock swap ratios, without cash disbursement, which has also become the most common currency in M&As in general in the Brazilian market. Two years ago, Arezzo and Soma merged by exchanging stock. Orizon made its largest M&A by paying Vital to Queiroz Galvão with stock. The BRF and Marfrig merger had a cash component, but also stock swaps. 3R and Enauta also became Brava Energia through stock combination.

"We will also continue to see small follow-ons, where companies focus on increasing liquidity, and the format known as PIPE, where funds make a relevant investment in a listed company but with some governance rights," highlights Cristiano Guimarães, head of investment banking at Itaú BBA.

A recently announced PIPE was from Advent in Natura. Companies that wanted to test the receptiveness of their investors, analysts, and regulators to new strategies preferred a cautious path in recent years, making smaller initial checks by purchasing minority stakes. This is what Inpasa did in Vibra, Muffato in Assaí, Ultrapar in Rumo, the Coelho Diniz and Pereira families in Grupo Pão de Açúcar. At the food retailer, for example, the Coelho Diniz family decided to continue advancing their share purchases and became reference shareholders at GPA. But other appetitive positions have soured—showing that it makes sense to commit less capital initially.

Pharmaceutical company EMS set up a position in Hypera to attempt a merger, which was rejected by the target company's board. EMS has already moved on: it just bought Medley from Sanofi's hands.

Despite this divestment by the French pharmaceutical company, Anderson Brito, head of investment banking at UBS-BB, notes a strategic movement by European companies over the last three years. "The flow of Europeans today is greater on the buy side than on the sell side. It's about $6 billion to $10 billion on average on the buyer side and $3 billion to $6 billion on the seller side," he says.

In energy, for example, while American AES left the country, French EDF made two acquisitions, and Spanish Iberdrola consolidated the purchase of Brazilian Neoenergia. Last year, Norwegian Visma bought Conta Azul—which has already moved to acquisitions under its new owner, acquiring MaisMei this year. In consumer goods, Europeans also indulged: Swiss Nestlé bought Kopenhagen, Italian Ferrero bought mineiro Bold Snacks, and Ferrara Candy—despite being American, is held by one of Ferrero's heirs—took Dori Alimentos.

Even private equity funds adapted. With few exit options and investors less willing to make new contributions, the vehicles began conducting more credit transactions. A Condere survey shows that half of the funds already operate some structured credit product, and 87% intend to increase this exposure. "It's a movement that already happens abroad and is gaining strength in Brazil," says Paulo Cury, founder of Condere.
