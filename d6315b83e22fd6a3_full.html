# Transactions Trending in the Market—and Likely to Continue

Reverse IPOs, appetizer acquisitions and follow-ons for liquidity remain on the radar

More than four years without initial public offerings, combined with high interest rates, leveraged companies and a strong dollar for most of that period, have led to new trends in transaction formats and capitalization options in the country. Companies and banks have not remained idle. "The Brazilian market demonstrated remarkable adaptability, developing new tools to maintain activity even in adverse scenarios," says Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

A recent movement that should continue as an alternative if the listings window remains closed for longer is the so-called reverse IPOs. This occurs when there is a transaction between a privately held company and an already listed company, resulting in a new company traded on the stock exchange. Often, it is the private company that becomes the controlling shareholder.

This was the path chosen by Bradesco to list its health vertical, which merged with Odontoprev, a dental plans company that was already listed on B3. It happened on the same day as another similar transaction: OceanPact, which provides services to the oil industry, incorporated CBO. New combinations between public and private companies are in the works.

These transactions are closed primarily through stock swap arrangements, with no cash outlay, which has also become the most common currency in M&As in general in the Brazilian market. Two years ago, Arezzo and Soma merged by swapping shares. Orizon conducted its largest M&A by paying for Vital to Queiroz Galvão with shares. The BRF and Marfrig merger had a cash component, but also share swaps. 3R and Enauta also became Brava Energia in a share combination.

"We will also continue to see small follow-ons, where companies focus on increasing liquidity, and the format known as PIPE, where funds make a relevant investment in a public company but with some governance rights," highlights Cristiano Guimarães, head of IB at Itaú BBA. A recently announced PIPE was Advent's investment in Natura.

Companies that wanted to test the receptivity of their investors, analysts and regulators to new strategies have preferred a cautious approach in recent years, making smaller initial checks by purchasing minority stakes. This is what Inpasa did at Vibra, Muffato at Assaí, Ultrapar at Rumo, the Coelho Diniz family and Pereira at Grupo Pão de Açúcar. At the food retailer, for example, the Coelho Diniz family decided to continue advancing in share purchases and became reference shareholders of GPA. But other appetizer positions have already turned sour—showing it makes sense to commit less capital initially.

Pharmaceutical EMS took a position in Hypera to attempt a merger, which was rejected by the target company's board. EMS has since moved on: it just acquired Medley from the hands of Sanofi.

Despite this divestment by the French pharmaceutical company, Anderson Brito, head of IB at UBS-BB, notes a strategic movement by European companies over the past three years. "The flow of Europeans today is larger on the buy side than on the sell side. It's around $6 billion to $10 billion on average on the buyer's side and $3 billion to $6 billion on the seller's side," he says.

In energy, for example, while American AES left the country, French EDF made two acquisitions, and Spanish Iberdrola consolidated its purchase of Brazilian Neoenergia. Last year, Norwegian Visma bought Conta Azul—which has since moved to acquisitions under its new owner, acquiring MaisMei this year. In consumer goods, Europeans also indulged themselves: Swiss Nestlé bought Kopenhagen, Italian Ferrero bought Bold Snacks from Minas Gerais, and Ferrara Candy—which, despite being American, is owned by one of Ferrero's heirs—acquired Dori Alimentos.

Even private equity funds have adapted. With few exit options and investors less willing to make new contributions, vehicles have begun conducting more credit transactions. Research conducted by Condere shows that half of the funds already operate some structured credit product, and 87% intend to increase this exposure. "It's a movement that already happens abroad and is gaining strength in Brazil," says Paulo Cury, founder of Condere.
