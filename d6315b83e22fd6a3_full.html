# Transactions Becoming Trends in the Market—And They Will Continue

Reverse IPOs, appetizer acquisitions and follow-ons for liquidity remain on the radar

By Maria Luíza Filgueiras — São Paulo 19/04/2026 07h10

More than four years without initial public offerings, high interest rates, leveraged companies and a strong dollar for most of the period have led to new trends in transaction formats and capitalization options in the country. In companies and banks, no one remained idle. "The Brazilian market demonstrated remarkable adaptability, developing new tools to maintain activity even in adverse scenarios," considers Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

A recent movement, which should continue as an alternative if the listing window remains closed for longer, is the so-called reverse IPOs. This happens when there is a transaction between a private company and an already listed company, resulting in a new company traded on the stock exchange. Often, the private company becomes the controlling shareholder.

This was the path chosen by Bradesco to list its health vertical, which merged with Odontoprev, a dental insurance company that was already on B3. It was on the same day as another similar transaction: OceanPact, which provides services to the oil industry, incorporated CBO.

New combinations between open and closed companies are in the works. These transactions are closed mainly through stock swap arrangements, without cash outlay, which has also become the most common currency in M&As in general in the Brazilian market. Two years ago, Arezzo and Soma merged by exchanging shares. Orizon made its largest M&A paying for Vital from Queiroz Galvão with shares. The BRF and Marfrig merger had a cash component, but also stock swaps. 3R and Enauta also became Brava Energia through a combination of shares.

"We will also continue to see small follow-ons, in which companies focus on increasing liquidity, and the format known as PIPE, in which funds make a relevant investment in a listed company but with some governance rights," highlights Cristiano Guimarães, head of IB at Itaú BBA. A recent PIPE announced was Advent's in Natura.

Companies that wanted to test the receptiveness of their investors, analysts and regulators to new strategies have preferred a cautious path in recent years, with a smaller initial check, by purchasing minority stakes. This is what Inpasa did in Vibra, Muffato in Assaí, Ultrapar in Rumo, the Coelho Diniz and Pereira in Grupo Pão de Açúcar.

In the food retailer, for example, the Coelho Diniz family decided to continue advancing in the purchase of shares and became reference shareholders of GPA. But other appetizer positions have already soured—showing that it makes sense to commit less capital initially.

Pharmaceutical company EMS built a position in Hypera to attempt a merger, which was rejected by the target company's board. EMS has already moved on: it just bought Medley from Sanofi's hands.

Despite this divestment by the French pharmaceutical company, Anderson Brito, head of IB at UBS-BB, notes a strategic movement by European companies in the last three years. "The flow of Europeans today is greater on the buy side than on the sell side. It's about $6 billion to $10 billion on average on the buyer side and $3 billion to $6 billion on the seller side," he says.

In energy, for example, while American AES left the country, French EDF made two acquisitions, and Spanish Iberdrola consolidated the purchase of Brazilian Neoenergia. Last year, Norwegian Visma bought Conta Azul—which has already moved on to acquisitions under its new owner, acquiring MaisMei this year. In consumer goods, Europeans also indulged: Swiss Nestlé bought Kopenhagen, Italian Ferrero bought Bold Snacks from Minas Gerais, and Ferrara Candy—despite being American, is held by one of Ferrero's heirs—took over Dori Alimentos.

Even private equity funds have adapted. With few exit options and investors with less willingness for new investments, the vehicles have started making more credit transactions. Research conducted by Condere shows that half of the funds already operate some structured credit product, and 87% intend to expand this exposure. "It's a movement that already happens abroad and is gaining strength in Brazil," says Paulo Cury, founder of Condere.
