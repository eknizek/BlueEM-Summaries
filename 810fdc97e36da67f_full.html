# Dollar surges over $10 and closes above $910 after attack on key Middle East industrial zone

The dollar appreciated strongly in the early May trading sessions, as several violent incidents in the Persian Gulf called into question the truce between the United States and Iran, especially an attack by the Persian nation on an important energy complex in the United Arab Emirates.

The dollar-peso parity rose $11.3 to $912.5 at Monday's close, a price that leaves it at near one-month highs, and with an accumulated increase of $35 from its mid-April lows, according to Bloomberg's compiled bid prices.

The dollar index advanced 0.3% to 98.4 points, in line with a sharp increase in U.S. interest rates, as Brent oil rebounded 5.3% to $114 per barrel, after closing lower on Friday. Meanwhile, Comex copper fell 2% to $5.86 per pound.

Stock indices went down. The Chilean peso was the second worst-performing emerging currency of the day, behind the Colombian peso. Early Monday it became known in Chile that the March Imacec registered a decline of 0.1% year-over-year, well below survey forecasts. The sectoral figures from Thursday had already suggested poor economic performance.

**Signals of war**

The weekend brought concerning signals, but investors became decidedly on guard after a new attack on critical infrastructure was reported this Monday. Specifically, an Iranian drone caused a fire in the Emirati port city of Fujairah, a petroleum and refined products hub that had been gaining greater relevance recently, due to its location outside the Strait of Hormuz.

"Reports of a drone attack against petroleum infrastructure in Fujairah, together with new air threat alerts in the UAE, raise fears that an already fragile ceasefire could be put at risk and that key crude routes, facilities, or supplies could be affected. This scenario keeps energy costs high, increases inflationary pressures, and reduces risk appetite in markets," wrote Ignacio Mieres, head of Research at XTB Latam.

"This reflects that the market is not only incorporating the risk of a possible supply shock, but also the possibility that prolonged regional instability could extend a scenario of expensive energy and tighter financial conditions," he observed.

The news arrives as Donald Trump has promised that the U.S. will conduct ships trapped in the strait out of Hormuz, as part of the so-called "Operation Freedom" that the president unveiled this weekend through a post on his Truth Social network, and which is already underway. On Monday, Trump posted: "Iran has launched some attacks against nations outside Operation Freedom, including a South Korean cargo ship. Perhaps it's time for South Korea to join the mission! We have shot down seven small boats or, as they like to call them, 'fast' boats. That's all they have left. Apart from the South Korean ship, there has been no damage so far in passing through the strait."

Secretary of War Pete Hegseth and Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff Dan Caine will hold a press conference early tomorrow Tuesday, according to the publication.

Reports pointing to violent incidents had been generating headlines in recent days. According to Iranian media, a U.S. frigate was attacked near the Gulf of Oman after ignoring alerts from the Persian navy, although Washington later denied this occurred. In any case, previously a tanker ship was attacked by projectiles near Fujairah, less than a day after a cargo ship was besieged by patrol boats, according to the UK maritime authority. Iran warned the U.S. Navy not to enter Hormuz.

The Islamic Republic confirmed this Sunday that, through Pakistan, it received the U.S. response to its latest peace proposal. The 14 Iranian points are "solely focused on ending the war," and there are "no nuclear talks at this stage," according to a statement to state television by a spokesperson for the Ministry of Foreign Affairs.

Analysts have warned that the energy market faces a decisive month, as crude inventories are just weeks away from being depleted, a situation that would lead to price increases at even higher levels.
