# Selfit opens a new gym every 48 hours and explores investor appetite

The Selfit gym chain accelerated its pace at the beginning of this year and opened more than 50 units in less than 90 days – the same number as the entire previous year – totaling investments of R$ 200 million. With these openings, the chain reached 260 branches, mostly in capitals in the Northeast, as well as Manaus and Belém, betting on regional consolidation for growth.

With Ebitda of R$ 250 million in 2025, Selfit is valued at R$ 1.6 billion by Bank of America, which is conducting an international roadshow to test investor appetite for a possible liquidity event. This could mean either the entry of a strategic investor, an IPO, or partial or full exit of the founders or current controller, HIG Capital.

In Rio and São Paulo, the presence is still incipient, concentrated in areas of classes C and D and in the interior, where the chain began using franchises (about 50 today) to gain speed from 2020 onwards. "We became an expansion company and the second largest gym chain with company-owned units. Panobianco has more units, but almost everything is franchise – it's a different game," compares Nelson Lins.

He explains that it took two years to convince the American private equity firm to adopt the licensing model, even if only partially. "The thesis was simple: if I franchise, I lose Ebitda, but gym penetration in Brazil is low, less than 5% of the population goes to a gym. If we don't seize the opportunity to expand and consolidate the brand, others will," he summarizes.

The main reference, SmartFit – which had Pátria as a partner from 2010 until February this year – is worth R$ 12 billion on the market with 1,700 company-owned gyms and 400 franchises, including abroad. According to him, the profitability of units is between 30% and 40%, with breakeven between 12 and 18 months. "Today we are one of the main retail chains in the country," says the executive, who looks to players like Raia and Drogasil for expansion strategy. "There's no point in opening in Fortaleza, São Paulo, Belo Horizonte and Santa Catarina without standardization. The customer needs to perceive equal value."

The current CEO of Selfit is Fernando Duarte Menezes, who has worked at Itaú, Oi, AquaRio and Paineiras Corcovado (Advent portfolio companies), and has acted as an angel investor in various startups. HIG does not operate in the chain's day-to-day operations, but only on the board, he says.

The initial public offering was actually filed in 2022, but the market window remained closed. Capital intensive, Selfit has already raised nearly R$ 300 million in debentures. Last year, the chain generated revenue of R$ 450 million, up 34%. For 2026, it projects revenue between R$ 550 million and R$ 650 million.

Lins' first gym – who is also founder and CEO of the FaceDoctor beauty chain – was opened in 2009 in Recife and was called Vitale Fitness. In 2012, he decided to migrate to the low-cost model and the iFit name, in partnership with Olympikus. "I had state-of-the-art equipment at below-market prices, and I understood that I needed to bring more services, like gymnastics," he recalls.

"SmartFit was riding the wave in the Southeast with a model inspired by the American one, not yet consolidated in the Northeast, but very focused on bodybuilding and the male audience," he remembers. The festive atmosphere of the classes helped popularize Selfit in Salvador and João Pessoa as well, to the point where he couldn't keep up with demand. "It looked like Black Friday."

With his credit limit compromised at the Central Bank and prevented from contracting new debt, the former Bradesco clerk who had become a manager understood that he needed a capitalized equity partner to expand beyond five units. That's when HIG arrived, having invested R$ 300 million since 2015. The other founder is his friend Leonardo Pereira. The current name emerged at the same time, resulting from an international trademark notification from a clothing brand claiming the iFit name. Lins hired the Orb branding consultancy, which combined the wave of selfies with the model of fewer instructors, or "do-it-yourself," a motto that the partners prefer not to follow strictly when the goal is to build corporate muscle mass.

**Update at 7:50 p.m. on 4/24:** If revenue and profit are growing, the company will have to work on its governance, as shareholders are in open conflict. After the publication of the report with Selfit's founder, the communication agency that serves the company, controlled by the HIG fund, contacted the outlet to disavow him as a spokesperson. The company sent the following note: "Selfit informs that it does not recognize the information presented in the article, since the data disclosed were not confirmed by the company. The company reiterates that official statements and positioning are only made through its institutional channels and by its statutory board." It's a topic to heat up the roadshow.
