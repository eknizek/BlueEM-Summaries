# Selfit Opens New Gym Every 48 Hours and Eyes Investor Interest

The Selfit gym chain accelerated its pace at the start of this year and inaugurated more than 50 units in less than 90 days — the same number as the entire previous year — totaling R$ 200 million in investments. With the openings, the chain reached 260 branches, predominantly in Northeast capitals, as well as Manaus and Belém, betting on regional consolidation for growth.

With Ebitda of R$ 250 million in 2025, Selfit is valued at R$ 1.6 billion by Bank of America, which is promoting an international roadshow to test investor appetite for a possible liquidity event. This could mean either the entry of a strategic investor or an IPO or partial or full exit by the founding partners or current controlling shareholder, HIG Capital.

In Rio and São Paulo, the company's presence remains incipient, concentrated in lower-middle-class areas and interior regions, where the chain began using franchises (about 50 today) to gain speed from 2020 onwards.

"We became an expansion company and the second largest gym chain with owned units. Panobianco has more units, but almost all are franchises, it's a different game," compares Nelson Lins, founder. He recounts that it took him two years to convince the American private equity firm to adopt the licensing model, even if partially. "The thesis was simple: if I franchise, I lose Ebitda, but gym penetration in Brazil is low, less than 5% of the population attends a gym. If we don't take advantage to expand and consolidate the brand, others will," he sums up.

The main reference, SmartFit — which had Pátria as a partner from 2010 until February this year — is worth R$ 12 billion on the market with 1,700 owned gyms and 400 franchises, including abroad.

According to him, unit profitability ranges between 30% and 40%, with break-even between 12 and 18 months. "Today we are one of the main retail chains nationally," says the executive, who looks to players like Raia and Drogasil for expansion strategy. "There's no point in opening in Fortaleza, São Paulo, Belo Horizonte and Santa Catarina without standardization. The customer needs to perceive equal value."

The current CEO of Selfit is Fernando Duarte Menezes, with stints at Itaú, Oi, AquaRio and Paineiras Corcovado (Advent portfolio companies), and acting as an angel investor in various startups. HIG does not operate in the chain's day-to-day management, but only on the board, he says.

An initial public offering was actually filed in 2022, but the market window remained closed. Capital-intensive, Selfit has already raised nearly R$ 300 million in debentures. Last year, the chain generated R$ 450 million in revenue, up 34%. For 2026, it projects revenue between R$ 550 million and R$ 650 million.

Lins' first gym — who is also founder and CEO of the FaceDoctor aesthetics chain — was inaugurated in 2009 in Recife and was called Vitale Fitness. In 2012, he decided to migrate to the low-cost model and the iFit name, in partnership with Olympikus. "I had cutting-edge equipment at below-market prices, and I understood that I needed to bring in more services, such as gymnastics," he recounts.

"SmartFit was riding the wave in the Southeast with a model inspired by the American model, not yet consolidated in the Northeast, but very focused on weight training and the male audience," he recalls. The festive atmosphere of classes helped popularize Selfit in Salvador and João Pessoa, to the point where he couldn't meet the demand. "It seemed like Black Friday."

With his credit limit at the Central Bank compromised and unable to contract new debts, the former Bradesco clerk who had become a manager understood that he needed a capitalized partner to expand beyond five units. That's when HIG arrived, having invested R$ 300 million since 2015. The other founder is his friend Leonardo Pereira.

The current name emerged around the same time, resulting from an international notification from a clothing brand claiming the iFit trademark. Lins hired the Orb branding consulting firm, which combined the wave of selfies with the model of fewer instructors, or the "do-it-yourself" motto that the partners prefer not to follow strictly when the goal is to build corporate muscle mass.
