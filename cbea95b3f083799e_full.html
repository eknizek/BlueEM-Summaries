# Milei's Argentine Train Plan Puts US-Backed Key Investor at Risk

Argentine President Javier Milei is seeking billions in private capital to transform the country's deteriorated freight rail network into a key route for transporting grains and minerals to export ports. However, privatizing the state network is a titanic task—one Argentina has already failed at before.

In the 1990s, the government's first attempt to cede railroads to private operators failed because companies invested little in infrastructure and abandoned unprofitable routes. Entire communities were isolated, some became ghost towns, and the system eventually had to be renationalized.

This time, Milei may be making the challenge even greater. The model his administration chose for the auction has generated criticism from sector experts and risks excluding one of the main bidders: Mexican railroad giant Grupo México, which has backing from the Trump administration. Other potential interested parties include an Argentine operator and a grain exporters' consortium that includes Cargill and Bunge Global.

Much is at stake. Without solid participation from major players, the auction could struggle to attract the level of investment needed to modernize the network and turn it into an engine of growth at a time when Argentina's economic recovery is losing momentum.

"If they don't make a radical change in how they plan to carry out the bidding, we will not submit an offer," said Grupo México's CEO Fernando López Guerra in an interview, describing the railroad as the future backbone of the Argentine economy. "This is not just any auction."

The process also highlights the limits of US influence over one of its closest regional allies, even after the Treasury helped Argentina secure a $20 billion rescue during a critical election year.

Currently, the Argentine state company operates approximately 14,000 kilometers of freight rail, but much of it is in such poor condition that only about 7,600 kilometers are operational. Reactivating the system could be transformative, reducing logistics costs, boosting exports, and supporting economic growth. This is where the real controversy begins—how to unlock that potential.

At the core of Milei's plan is a departure from the current vertically integrated system, in which state company Belgrano Cargas controls infrastructure, trains, and operations. This model is widely used across the Americas, including private railroads in the US, Mexico, and Brazil.

Instead, the government plans to divide Belgrano Cargas into nine independent auctions that include tracks, warehouses, and operations across three corridors. Auction winners must allow for "Open Access"—a system in which multiple operators can run trains on the same infrastructure.

Officials, including Deregulation Minister Federico Sturzenegger, argue that this approach not only promotes competition but is also legally grounded in a 2015 law approved during former President Cristina Fernández de Kirchner's administration.

"It is key that Open Access exists for there to be competition in rail operations," said Sturzenegger in a written response. Although Milei has eliminated many regulations from previous governments, this one has been maintained. "The Open Access law was kept because it is totally aligned with this government's principles and values. It is a law that promotes competition," said Alejandro Núñez, director of Belgrano Cargas, who is leading the privatization process.

In a statement, the grain exporters' consortium expressed support for an open access model but noted it cannot confirm whether it will submit an offer until the government publishes final bidding documents. The group represents approximately 85% of current Belgrano Cargas transportation.

"A more efficient open access operation can accelerate the modernization the system has needed for decades, creating a railroad for all Argentines," the consortium said in a written response.

Grupo México strongly disagrees, and its position is shaping up as one of the biggest risks to the auction's success. The conglomerate, controlled by billionaire Germán Larrea, has committed to an initial investment of $3 billion to rebuild Argentina's rail system.

However, it seeks to bid for the entire network, or at least secure an entire corridor, rather than compete for fragmented assets under an open access scheme. CEO López has traveled to Argentina five times since Milei took office to meet with officials and inspect the network, so far without reaching an agreement.

Núñez, from Belgrano, maintains that any company could implement an integrated model as long as it participates in each corridor auction and allows other operators access to the tracks. López argues that open access undermines profitability and warns that "everyone loses money" with that system.

Grupo México highlighted its experience. Following Mexico's rail privatization in 1997, the company helped transform a deteriorated network into a key artery for North American commerce. It now sees similar potential in Argentina, especially for boosting shipments of grains, minerals, and supplies for oil production.
