# 'We Won't Go Public Until the Market Calms Down': Interview with Adrián Neuhauser, President of Abra, Avianca's Controlling Group

The executive points out that the SEC process is almost ready, but the final decision will depend on better conditions. Announces increase in ticket prices.

Adrián Neuhauser, president of Grupo Abra, calls on the next government for clear rules and dialogue to promote the country's air connectivity.

**By Carlos Arturo García Mahecha, Economic Journalist**

**April 12, 2026**

Adrián Neuhauser's ambition as president of Grupo Abra to take the organization public on Wall Street remains intact, but not on the timeline they would have preferred. Global volatility, marked by geopolitical tensions and the persistent impact of oil prices, has cooled investor appetite, particularly toward the airline sector. The goal was to do it in 2026 and, although the technical process before the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)—the U.S. regulator—is practically ready, the decision to go public will now depend on more stable conditions, both in financial markets and in the industry's operating costs.

Adding to this scenario is the increase in jet fuel prices, which has forced airlines to adjust fares. In Colombia, this impact is especially sensitive, not only because of the dependence on air transport for connectivity, but also because of the high tax burden on tickets. Nevertheless, Neuhauser insists that, even with the increases, prices remain competitive compared to pre-pandemic levels, reflecting a profound transformation in Avianca's cost structure.

In an interview with EL TIEMPO, the executive, who participated in the IATA Wings of Change Americas in Santiago, Chile, addressed Abra's regional expansion strategy, regulatory challenges, the logic behind the fare adjustment, and the conditions that will define the right moment to debut on the stock market.

**What is the status of the Sky process?**

Our objective is to build a group that covers the major markets in the region. Looking at the map, Chile and Peru are important gaps. But we don't want to enter by irrationally increasing capacity. The most logical option is to do it through acquisition, and Sky is a company with great cultural and product affinity with us. It has a solid and respected brand. Additionally, there was already a previous relationship through financing that is now in Abra. Our approach has evolved toward owning the entire company to capture synergies. This would allow us to integrate operations, expand connectivity, and cover those strategic gaps. The process is in the regulatory stage in Chile, Peru, and Brazil, and we expect progress toward the end of the year.

**In that plan, there's also interest in Argentina...**

Yes, we have a code-sharing agreement that allows us to connect passengers between networks. It works well and benefits both parties. Argentina is an attractive market, but with a particular dynamic. For now we are comfortable with this collaboration model.

**Are there more moves planned in the region?**

With the incorporation of Sky, we would cover the key markets. Our group model, with independent brands but strategic coordination, gives us agility. We always evaluate opportunities, but there is no critical gap at this moment.

**How does the group face the current situation of rising prices and costs, particularly in Colombia?**

The plan has been in place since 2019, when we redefined Avianca to be more efficient. Today we have a resilient cost structure. However, we cannot avoid passing part of the fuel increase to tickets, which will be done gradually. Even so, even with those adjustments, prices remain lower in real terms than in 2019. It's a basic question: you can't sell below cost.

**Are these increases temporary?**

It depends on market behavior. Competition is efficient: everyone adjusts prices sooner or later. But I am not optimistic about a decline.
