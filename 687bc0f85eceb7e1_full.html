# "We Won't Go Public Until the Market Calms Down": Interview with Adrián Neuhauser, President of Abra, Avianca's Controlling Holding Company

The ambition of Adrián Neuhauser, president of Grupo Abra, to take the organization public on Wall Street remains intact, but not on the timeline they would have preferred. Global volatility, marked by geopolitical tensions and the persistent impact of oil prices, has cooled investor appetite, particularly toward the airline sector. The goal was to do it in 2026, and although the technical process before the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)—the U.S. regulator—is practically ready, the decision to go public will now depend on more stable conditions, both in financial markets and in the industry's operating costs.

To this scenario is added the increase in jet fuel prices, which has forced airlines to adjust fares. In Colombia, this impact is especially acute, not only because of the dependence on air transport for connectivity, but also because of the high tax burden on tickets. Even so, Neuhauser insists that, even with the increases, prices remain competitive compared to pre-pandemic levels, reflecting a profound transformation in Avianca's cost structure.

In an interview with EL TIEMPO, the executive, who participated in the IATA Wings of Change Americas in Santiago, Chile, addressed Abra's regional expansion strategy, regulatory challenges, the logic behind the fare adjustment, and the conditions that will define the right moment to debut on the stock market.

**How is the process with Sky going?**

Our objective is to assemble a group that covers the major markets in the region. Looking at the map, Chile and Peru are important gaps. But we don't want to enter by irrationally increasing capacity. The most logical option is to do it through acquisition, and Sky is a company with great cultural and product affinity with us. It has a solid and respected brand. Additionally, there was already a prior relationship through financing that is now in Abra. Our approach has evolved toward having the totality of the company to capture synergies. This would allow us to integrate operations, expand connectivity, and cover those strategic gaps. The process is in the regulatory stage in Chile, Peru, and Brazil, and we expect progress toward the end of the year.

**In that plan, there's also interest in Argentina...**

Yes, we have a code-sharing agreement that allows us to connect passengers between networks. It works well and benefits both parties. Argentina is an attractive market, but with particular dynamics. For now, we're comfortable with this collaboration model.

In that sense, do you have more movements planned in the region?

With the incorporation of Sky, we would cover the key markets. Our group model, with independent brands but strategic coordination, gives us agility. We always evaluate opportunities, but there's no critical gap at this moment.

**How does the group face the current situation of rising prices and costs, mainly in Colombia?**

The plan has been in place since 2019, when we redefined Avianca to be more efficient. Today we have a resilient cost structure. However, we cannot avoid passing some of the fuel increase on to tickets, which will be done gradually. Even so, even with those adjustments, prices remain lower in real terms than in 2019. It's a basic question: you cannot sell below cost.

**Are these increases temporary?**

It depends on market behavior. Competition is efficient: everyone adjusts prices sooner or later. But I'm not optimistic about a decline...
