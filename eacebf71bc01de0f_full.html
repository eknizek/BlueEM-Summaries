Corporate venture capital market consolidates: fewer players, larger checks

13 April 2026 · 10:57 AM

# The New Map of CVCs: Fewer Companies, Larger Checks, and a Market in Reconstruction

Following the hangover that struck the venture capital universe after the euphoria of the post-pandemic period, Brazilian companies found themselves forced to calibrate their expectations and plans for investments in startups. Some companies that attempted to enter the game by structuring corporate funds to invest in startups, the so-called corporate venture capital (CVCs), dropped out or had to significantly reduce their ambitions and change strategy, with some divesting portfolios. And those that remained are becoming increasingly selective, in line with the venture capital segment trend, resulting in a market with fewer deals in terms of quantity, but with larger checks.

A survey conducted by the Brazilian Association of Private Equity and Venture Capital (ABVCAP) for NeoFeed shows that there are currently 71 active CVCs, a contrast to venture capital's golden days, when the number of proprietary funds exceeded 100 in 2021, according to a study conducted at the time by Apex.

"We see some CVCs announcing strategic changes, because it's not something that works the same for all companies," says Priscila Rodrigues, president of ABVCAP, to NeoFeed. "I think this is part of a maturation process of the ecosystem and this type of fund."

Of the total in operation, 42 made some investment in the 12 months ending in June 2025, while in the previous year there were 48 CVCs, according to a study conducted by the association in partnership with consulting firm EloGroup, ApexBrasil, and Global Corporate Venturing (GCV).

The last few years have been marked by a review of this fund model, with some companies throwing in the towel and transferring their asset portfolios. One that chose to end operations was Braskem. The petrochemical company announced, in early 2025, that it would shut down Oxygea, an innovation hub launched in 2022 with a budget of US$ 150 million for investments. At a time when it faces difficulties in its core business, Braskem justified the decision citing "reassessment and prioritization of its assets."

Americanas also ended its CVC activities. The company closed IF - Inovação e Futuro (IF - Innovation and Future), an innovation arm that created Ame Digital, as part of judicial recovery, in which it decided to focus operations on retail, with asset sales. When contacted by NeoFeed, Americanas reported that the shutdown of IF - Inovação e Futuro resulted from a "business decision, based on Americanas' transformation plan with a focus on physical retail, the company's main activity."

Gerdau, meanwhile, put its CVC initiative under review. Created in 2019 with committed capital of US$ 80 million, Gerdau Next Ventures placed its portfolio under review, evaluating asset sales. When contacted, Gerdau declined to comment.

According to Rodrigues, at the height of optimism, many companies structured CVCs seeking those who could solve business pain points in times of digitalization, as well as riding the optimism surrounding startups, dreaming of high financial returns in the not-too-distant future. Some companies chose to create their own business unit, with an eye not only on attracting synergistic companies but also on obtaining financial returns through the sale of stakes.

"But not all projects went well," states the ABVCAP president. "Sometimes, the expectation created about how quickly they would allocate money, how quickly these companies would grow ended up being disappointed."

The question of lack of patience with startups is fundamental to the success of a CVC and explains why many companies decided to review their operations. If the fund is a cost center for the company, the moment the tide turns, waiting for the business to mature ends up costing money. Successfully selling becomes even more difficult, especially in times of high interest rates, which require focusing attention and resources on core activities.

According to Frederico Wiesel, partner at Spectra Investments, an alternative asset manager, a good number of investments made in recent years were in early-stage companies, when startups are still finding their business models, subject to changes, requiring proximity with investees.

"Sometimes, investing, especially in early stage, seeking a super long-term strategic fit, and then after a year the companies pivot, seeking better, more creative models, with better unit economics, ends up falling outside the strategic objectives the company wanted," he states.

The situation experienced by many CVCs opened an opportunity for Spectra, which is negotiating the purchase of stakes in startups held by these disillusioned funds due to the deflation of multiples and valuations.

"We've seen CVCs wanting to unwind positions. We've done some secondary transactions and are in active conversations with other CVCs to buy their portfolios, because this interest in disinvestment continues," says Wiesel.

## Maturity and Selectivity

With fewer players and higher interest rates, the number of investment rounds with CVCs has fallen in recent years. On the other hand, average checks have increased, a signal that CVCs are more selective, willing to pay more for assets with greater traction and reduced risk.

In the 12 months through June of last year, the total amount of rounds with CVC participation was considerably lower than in the previous 12 months, according to the ABVCAP survey and partners – 66 versus 135, respectively, moving a total of US$ 700 million.

Despite the largest number of rounds occurring at the seed stage – 26 in total – the market observed an increase in the representativeness of Series B and above rounds, which accounted for 27% of total operations, compared to 12% last year.

The average value of a Series B round jumped from US$ 7.4 million to US$ 34.6 million, indicating that Brazilian corporations are participating in larger deals and supporting startups in scaling phases.

The movement reflects the search for greater strategic synergy, since more mature startups have greater capacity to act as business partners for companies.

"The first wave was marked by FOMO (fear of missing out), because there was surplus capital, allowing investment," says Jaime Frenkel, partner at EloGroup. "But when interest rates rose, those who remained were companies where the expected return on investments is aligned with the strategy."
