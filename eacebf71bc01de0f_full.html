The new map of CVCs: fewer companies, larger checks, and a market under reconstruction

13 April 2026 · 10:57 AM

# The new map of CVCs: fewer companies, larger checks, and a market under reconstruction

In the wake of the hangover that hit the venture capital universe after the euphoria of the post-pandemic period, Brazilian companies found themselves forced to recalibrate their expectations and plans for startup investments. Some companies that tried to enter the game by structuring corporate funds to invest in startups, the so-called corporate venture capital (CVCs), dropped out or had to significantly reduce their ambitions and change strategy, with some divesting portfolios. And those that remained are increasingly selective, in line with the trend in the venture capital segment, resulting in a market with fewer deals in terms of quantity, but with larger checks.

A survey conducted by the Brazilian Association of Private Equity and Venture Capital (ABVCAP) for NeoFeed shows that there are currently 71 CVCs active, a contrast to the golden days of venture capital, when the number of proprietary funds exceeded 100 in 2021, according to a study conducted at the time by Apex.

"We see some CVCs announcing strategic changes, because it's not something that works the same for all companies," says Priscila Rodrigues, president of ABVCAP, to NeoFeed. "I think this is part of a maturation process of the ecosystem and this type of fund."

Of the total in operation, 42 made some investment in the 12 months ended in June 2025, while in the previous year there were 48 CVCs, according to a study conducted by the association in partnership with consulting firm EloGroup, ApexBrasil, and Global Corporate Venturing (GCV).

The last few years have been marked by a revision of this fund model, with some companies throwing in the towel and transferring their asset portfolio. One that chose to end operations was Braskem. The petrochemical company announced, at the beginning of 2025, that it would be shutting down Oxygea, an innovation hub launched in 2022 with a budget of US$ 150 million for investments. At a time when it faces difficulties in its core business, Braskem justified the decision citing "reassessment and prioritization of its assets."

Americanas also ended its CVC activities. The company closed IF - Innovation and Future, an innovation arm that created Ame Digital, as part of its judicial restructuring, in which it decided to focus its operations on retail, with asset sales. When contacted by NeoFeed, Americanas reported that the closure of IF - Innovation and Future resulted from a "business decision, based on Americanas' transformation plan with a focus on physical retail, the company's main activity."

Gerdau, meanwhile, placed its CVC initiative under review. Created in 2019 with committed capital of US$ 80 million, Gerdau Next Ventures put its portfolio under review, evaluating asset sales. When contacted, Gerdau stated it would not comment.

According to Rodrigues, at the height of optimism, many companies structured CVCs seeking to solve business pain points in times of digitalization, while also riding the wave of optimism around startups, dreaming of high financial returns in the not-too-distant future. Some companies chose to create their own business unit, aiming not only to attract companies with synergies, but also to obtain financial returns from the sale of stakes.

"But not all projects went well," says the ABVCAP president. "Sometimes, expectations created about how quickly they would allocate money, how quickly these companies would grow ended up being frustrated."

The issue of lack of patience with startups is fundamental to the success of a CVC and explains why many companies decided to review their operations. If the fund is a cost center for the company, when the tide turns, waiting for the business to mature ends up costing money. Getting out becomes even more difficult, especially in times of high interest rates, which require focusing attention and resources on core activities.

According to Frederico Wiesel, partner at Spectra Investments, an alternative asset manager, a good number of investments made in recent years were in early-stage companies, when startups are still finding their business models, subject to changes, requiring close involvement with investments.

"Sometimes, investing, especially in early stage, seeking very long-term strategic fit, and then after a year the companies pivot, seeking better, more creative models, with better unit economics, ends up falling outside the strategic objectives the company wanted," he says.

The situation experienced by many CVCs opened an opportunity for Spectra, which is negotiating the purchase of stakes in startups held by these disappointed funds, due to the deflation of multiples and valuations.

"We have seen CVCs wanting to unwind positions. We've done some secondary transactions and we're in active conversations with other CVCs to buy their portfolios, because this interest in divestment continues," says Wiesel.

## Maturity and selectivity

With fewer players and high interest rates, the number of investment rounds with CVCs has fallen in recent years. On the other hand, average check sizes have increased, signaling that CVCs are more selective, willing to pay more for assets with greater traction and reduced risk.

In the 12 months through June of last year, the total number of rounds with CVC participation was considerably lower than in the previous 12 months, according to the ABVCAP research and partners – 66 versus 135, respectively, moving a total of US$ 700 million.

Despite the largest number of rounds having occurred at the seed stage – 26 in total – the market observed an increase in the representation of Series B and above rounds, which accounted for 27% of total operations, compared to 12% last year.

The average value of a Series B round jumped from US$ 7.4 million to US$ 34.6 million, indicating that Brazilian corporations are participating in larger deals and supporting startups in scaling phases.

The movement reflects the search for greater strategic synergy, since more mature startups have greater capacity to act as business partners for companies.

"The first wave was marked by FOMO [fear of missing out], because there was surplus capital, allowing them to go out investing," says Jaime Frenkel, partner at EloGroup. "But when interest rates went up, what remained were companies in which the expected return on investments is aligned with the strategy."
