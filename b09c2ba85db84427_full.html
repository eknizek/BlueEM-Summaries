# Bank of America Downgrades Suzano on Structural Pulp Price Decline; Stock Falls 6%

Bank of America downgraded Suzano from "buy" to "neutral" and reduced its price target for the stock from R$ 82 to R$ 57, arguing that the global pulp market has entered a new structural cycle of lower prices, which will affect EBITDA and profits for the Feffer family company. The stock plummeted more than 6% following the report, with a 22 million share auction coordinated by BTG in early afternoon. Suzano is now valued at R$ 58.6 billion on the stock exchange. Trading volume for the stock surged to R$ 4 billion, compared to an average of R$ 268 million over the last 30 days.

BofA's downgrade is the first among major investment banks covering the stock. Currently, Suzano has coverage from 15 other banks and brokerages, all with "buy" recommendations.

According to BofA, oversupply—driven by capacity expansion in Latin America and increased Chinese self-sufficiency—should pressure prices for longer, reducing the stock's upside potential. Analyst Caio Ribeiro said the downgrade reflects Suzano's high exposure "to pulp price volatility at a time when we have become structurally more cautious about the commodity's prospects in both the short and long term."

He noted that "the current pulp price rally is approaching its end, sustained by a combination of above-average inventory at Chinese ports, low profitability for papermakers, secondary market and futures prices trading below benchmarks, capacity resumption by Chenming [a Chinese pulp and paper giant], and initial signals that Indonesia's forest restrictions could be partially reversed or relaxed."

According to the analyst, these combined factors point to weaker pricing dynamics in the near term, which should weigh on Suzano's results. "Still, the company continues to trade at a healthy valuation, at approximately 6.4x EV/EBITDA, while offering shareholders a free cash flow yield of roughly 5.4% in 2026," the analyst wrote.

The bank reduced its projections for Suzano's EBITDA and profits, now projecting net income of R$ 5.9 billion for this year and R$ 5.8 billion for 2027, compared to previous projections of R$ 6.9 billion and R$ 6.8 billion. The EBITDA estimate was also reduced: R$ 21.3 billion for this year and R$ 23.8 billion for next year (versus R$ 22 billion and R$ 25.1 billion).

BofA also reduced its long-term pulp price projection from $600/ton to $550/ton, and it could be even lower in stress scenarios.

In the report, the analyst noted that one of the major structural changes in the global pulp market over the past decade has been the rapid expansion of domestic production in China. "Chinese wood pulp production has increased significantly, rising from about 12.6 million tons per year five years ago to approximately 26 million tons currently, driven mainly by greater wood availability in the country," BofA wrote.

As a result, "China has transitioned from being an almost exclusively demand-based market to becoming a relevant player on the supply side, with important implications for price formation."

According to BofA, this trend should continue, as China has a pipeline of projects that should add 6 million tons of capacity. Combined with this, there is a significant pipeline of new projects in Latin America, which should further increase capacity, contributing to supply-demand imbalance.

"Brazil remains at the center of this expansion wave. Large-scale projects represent almost 13 million tons of additional short-fiber capacity that should enter the market in coming years," the analyst said. "These projects should begin operating in a context of weaker demand growth, increasing the risk of persistent oversupply—not just a temporary cyclical tightness."

The report cites Arauco's Sucuriú project, which will begin operating in the fourth quarter of 2027; CMPC's Natureza project, scheduled for 2029; a new Bracell plant in Bataguassu with 2.8 million tons of capacity; and a potential Eldorado expansion that has been in the pipeline for some time.
