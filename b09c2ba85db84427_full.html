# Bank of America Downgrades Suzano to Neutral, Cuts Price Target to R$57 on Structural Pulp Oversupply

Bank of America downgraded Suzano from "buy" to "neutral" and reduced its price target for the stock from R$82 to R$57, arguing that the global pulp market has entered a new structural cycle of lower prices, which will affect the Feffer family company's EBITDA and profits. The stock plummeted more than 6% following the report, with a sale of 22 million shares coordinated by BTG in the early afternoon. Suzano is now valued at R$58.6 billion on the stock exchange. Trading volume for the stock skyrocketed to R$4 billion, compared to an average of R$268 million over the last 30 days.

The BofA downgrade is the first among major investment firms covering the stock. Currently, Suzano is covered by 15 other banks and brokerages, all with "buy" recommendations. According to BofA, the oversupply—driven by capacity expansion in Latin America and increased self-sufficiency in China—should pressure prices for longer, reducing the stock's upside potential.

Analyst Caio Ribeiro said the downgrade reflects Suzano's high exposure "to pulp price volatility at a time when we have become structurally more cautious about the commodity's prospects in the short and long term." For him, "the current pulp price cycle is approaching its end, supported by a combination of above-average inventories in Chinese ports, low profitability for papermakers, secondary market prices and futures trading below benchmarks, the resumption of capacity by Chenming [a Chinese pulp and paper giant] and initial signs that Indonesia's forest restrictions may be partially reversed or relaxed."

According to the analyst, these combined factors point to weaker price dynamics in the short term, which should weigh on Suzano's results. "Still, the company continues at a healthy valuation, trading at approximately 6.4x EV/EBITDA, while offering a free cash flow yield to shareholders of approximately 5.4% in 2026," the analyst wrote.

The bank reduced its projections for Suzano's EBITDA and profit, now projecting a bottom line of R$5.9 billion for this year and R$5.8 billion for 2027, compared to previous projections of R$6.9 billion and R$6.8 billion. The EBITDA estimate was also reduced: R$21.3 billion for this year and R$23.8 billion for next year (versus R$22 billion and R$25.1 billion).

BofA also reduced its long-term pulp price projection from $600 per ton to $550 per ton, potentially even lower in stress scenarios. In the report, the analyst noted that one of the main structural changes in the global pulp market over the last decade was the rapid expansion of domestic production in China. "Chinese wood pulp production increased significantly, rising from approximately 12.6 million tons per year five years ago to approximately 26 million tons currently, driven primarily by greater wood availability in the country," BofA wrote.

As a result, "China stopped being a market based almost exclusively on demand to become a relevant supply-side player, with important implications for price formation." According to BofA, this trend should continue, as China has a pipeline of projects expected to add 6 million tons of capacity. Combined with this is a significant pipeline of new projects in Latin America, which should further increase capacity, contributing to supply and demand imbalance.

"Brazil remains at the center of this expansion wave. Large-scale projects represent almost 13 million tons of additional short-fiber capacity that should enter the market in the coming years," the analyst said. "These projects should begin operating in a context of weaker demand growth, increasing the risk of persistent oversupply—not just a temporary cyclical tightening."

The report cites Arauco's Sucuriú project, which will begin operating in the fourth quarter of 2027; CMPC's Natureza project, scheduled for 2029; a new Bracell plant in Bataguassu with 2.8 million tons of capacity; and a potential Eldorado expansion, which has been in the pipeline for some time.
