# After Rebranding, BOOM Targets International Markets with 'Brazilian Peloton'

The online fitness academy that won over celebrities like Jade Picon and Bruna Griphao now wants to take a step further: export Brazilian "borogodó" to the world. Rebranded as BOOM after nine months of rebranding, the former Academia Foguete now targets the international market with the proposal to be a kind of "Brazilian Peloton."

Like the American company, BOOM bets on live-streamed classes and a digital platform with an engaged community. The difference is that while Peloton was born tied to hardware, with a line of treadmills and spinning bikes, the Brazilian company emerged from content.

"We even have treadmill and bike workouts, but our main offering is bodyweight training to help people break out of inertia. We don't want to compete with big gym chains; our competition is with the couch," says Stelio Belchior, BOOM's CEO, in an interview with Startups.

Currently present in more than 70 countries, BOOM still has a user base that is predominantly Brazilians living abroad. With the help of technology, the company now wants to conquer foreign audiences, riding the wave of popularity of Latin and Brazilian aesthetics abroad. The company projects an investment of R$ 20 million over the next 18 months in these projects.

The name change came to help: Foguete was difficult to pronounce in languages like English. "I want to bring what Americans don't have, which is borogodó. Brazilians have something that is exportable," he says.

In practice, this translates into live classes with charismatic instructors, playlists that mix funk, sertanejo and samba, and a highly interactive dynamic. Students turn on their cameras, chat, create bonds — and often take these connections beyond the screen. "We managed to create a very engaged community, with WhatsApp groups and even in-person meetings," says BOOM's CEO.

## Artificial Intelligence to Scale

To bring this experience to other countries without losing its essence, BOOM is betting on artificial intelligence, especially in dubbing classes into other languages. The decision came after frustrated attempts to adapt the instructors themselves to English. "The instructor couldn't teach in English. It didn't flow and lost the borogodó," says Stelio. "With AI dubbing, it's perfect."

The idea is to maintain the original energy of the classes while the company expands its global reach. The platform is already translated and international expansion should begin this semester, with initial focus on the United States and Latin American countries. The use of technology also includes gamification projects, inspired by Duolingo's model. "Our Head of Growth put together a plan to move us from a virtualized gym to a digital product. This involves changes. For example, we were a web platform, today we're an app. We're accelerating gamification, something Strava does very well, and investing heavily in technology," the executive explains.
