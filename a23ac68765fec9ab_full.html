# OPINION. Berkshire in the Greg Abel era: defensiveness and lower tolerance

OMAHA, Nebraska – Berkshire Hathaway's first annual meeting without Warren Buffett on stage had a noticeably smaller audience than in recent years. For many, it was confirmation of what was imagined: without Buffett, Omaha loses its appeal. But it's worth revisiting an observation from Bob Robotti, a historic Berkshire investor who has run Robotti & Company for over four decades: in a transition of this magnitude, the serious investor should be closer to the company, not further away.

Investors who have known newly appointed CEO Greg Abel for a long time expected him to bring a different style – more disciplined, more process-focused, more oriented toward operational efficiency than Buffett's more charismatic and instinctive management. And that's exactly what appeared on stage. The meeting had fewer parables, fewer digressions about American history, and more let's get down to business. The discussion concentrated on operational performance, execution priorities, and capital allocation. Significant time was dedicated to BNSF Railway, one of the major railroads in the US, and to NetJets. Katie Farmer and Adam Johnson, who now oversee both operations, were presented to shareholders with equal priority. Closing the operational gap against peers is a major challenge for BNSF. (NetJets has already made much of that journey.) Buffett's own remarks during the interview at the event set the tone for this new phase. He said he chose Greg for his exceptional business intelligence, not for being a nice guy. Coming from someone who operated Berkshire for decades also as a network of relationships, the signal goes beyond a compliment. It suggests a shift in emphasis. Tolerance for underperformance in the new management should decrease.

Greg came from Berkshire Hathaway Energy and has a reputation as a meticulous operator. He seems determined to tackle what Buffett, by temperament, typically tolerated: businesses that became comfortable within the conglomerate while delivering below their potential. BNSF is the clearest example of the operational challenge ahead. Operating margins still lag behind those of peers, and this is Greg's explicit priority for the coming years. The turnaround is being driven mainly by technology, and the company is undergoing an important revolution in this regard. It's internalizing technology development instead of relying on external suppliers. The initiative, inspired by what GEICO did four years ago, is the main bet to close the gap.

The Energy group is giving clear signals of a new posture. Berkshire understands that it hasn't been adequately compensated for the risk in the sector, which has increased consistently: aging infrastructure, exploding demand because of AI, and a regulatory environment that isn't keeping pace with the new reality. Without change in this scenario, it prefers not to expand capital allocated to the segment. PacifiCorp itself, controlled by Berkshire, already announced in February the sale of assets in Washington state to Portland General Electric for $1.9 billion. This is the type of operation Buffett almost never did – when he sold, it was reluctantly, generally because he personally knew the previous owners. Greg doesn't seem to have that same attachment.

On the other hand, the new CEO also doesn't have the same network of relationships that Buffett built over 60 years. Family business owners used to call Omaha directly because they wanted to sell to Buffett – for his word, his culture, the autonomy that came after the deal. This advantage still exists, especially because Buffett remains involved as chairman and the group's cultural reference. But it tends to diminish over time, and Berkshire will need to compete for assets like any professional buyer. The company now depends less on one person and more on a structure.

The new structure, however, inherits an old dilemma. With $380 billion in net cash at the end of 2025, Berkshire has gone more than a year without being able to fire its bazooka. The larger the company becomes, the more restricted its field of action. How many mammoths are walking around out there? In 60 years of investing, Buffett observed that there were only five truly juicy years – rare windows in which the market delivered big opportunities at attractive prices. He also admitted to understanding less about industries today than he did ten years ago. The message is clear, even if he didn't state it explicitly: the world is increasingly dominated by technology, an area historically outside Berkshire's circle of competence. It's worth remembering that he resisted until 2020 to trade his flip phone for an iPhone, even while already being Apple's largest shareholder, and to this day, at 95 years old, he uses the device basically to make calls.

Another recurring topic in conversations in Omaha was the fact that for years, diversifying outside US large caps costs returns instead of adding them. This is what some firms call a "bear market in diversification." For 17 years, US large caps have outperformed everything else: American small caps, developed markets outside the US, emerging markets... Diversifying became a drag on returns. For the allocator, this was a period in which the textbook highlighting the advantages of diversification seemed wrong, while American large caps rose in almost a straight line. Looking at the long history, such periods alternate with long windows in which diversification was the superior strategy. Mean reversion, always it. The most famous example is the so-called lost decade between 2000 and 2009, a period in which the S&P 500 delivered negative real returns, having started from a CAPE above 40. A globally diversified portfolio preserved and grew capital in the same period.

Current numbers make the comparison concrete. The Shiller CAPE is now at 37 in the US; at 22 in developed markets outside the US; at 18 in emerging markets; and at 14 among the 25% cheapest in the world. The spread between the US and the rest of the world has never been so large. The discussion doesn't involve zeroing exposure to the US in favor of emerging markets. There are legitimate structural reasons why American margins, especially in technology, should support higher multiples than the rest of the world. The relevant question is different. For the allocator who gave up on everything else and kept concentrating on what worked in recent years, does it still make sense to pay the premium that this quality is charging in the current spread? The long history shows that these cycles reverse. What it doesn't show is when.

There's an additional piece of data from this same thesis that deserves the allocator's attention. American small caps typically trade at a slight P/E premium over large caps, reflecting the premium the market pays for smaller companies in exchange for the higher expected returns they deliver in the long term. Today, this ratio is at record discount levels, even greater than observed in the dot-com era. The cause is the passive concentration of capital in megacaps.
