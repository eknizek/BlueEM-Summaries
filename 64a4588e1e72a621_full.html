Artificial intelligence and productivity: Brazil's SME adoption challenge ahead

03 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Artificial Intelligence and Productivity: The Great Pending Leap for SMEs in Brazil

The debate about artificial intelligence in Brazilian small and medium-sized enterprises has an uncomfortable starting point: Brazil ranks 92nd in the global productivity ranking by hour worked, and has worsened compared to the previous year. This data, cited by Diego Barreto, CEO of iFood, in a panel at The AI Economy Brazil, an event by Bloomberg Línea with Sólides, a Brazilian technology company for HR management, helps explain why the SME segment concentrates today's greatest expectations for gains with technology.

"Argentina has 50% more productivity than us. The United States has four and a half times more," compared the leader of Brazil's largest food delivery platform. For the executives gathered at the event, the size of the gap is also the size of the opportunity, especially because seven out of every ten Brazilians who work do so in small companies, precisely the segment with the least management maturity and greatest resistance to adopting technology.

The first use cases presented by the executives address precisely this conflict. At iFood, the most consolidated example is assistance to small merchants outside business hours, just when the owner closes the cash register and has time to ask for help. "The restaurant owner could only ask for help after 10 p.m., when he closed the cash register. I didn't have a team of 200 people to serve him at that hour," Barreto exemplified. AI agents now cover that 24-hour period, without replacing people, but occupying a space where no one existed before. Internally, the logic is the same: part of traditional consulting has been replaced by agents that send messages on Slack, interview employees, and consolidate the diagnosis.

"A year ago, modifying a company's process required sitting down with people. Today, by sending a message on Slack to 30 or 40 people, you literally enumerate all the processes," said iFood's CEO. What sustains this type of application, according to him, is not the model itself, but the history the company has accumulated: "Anyone can make a SaaS. The great asset is in your own data, and that is built over time."

## Gap, Not Substitution

Barreto's interpretation echoes in the diagnosis of Mónica Hauck, CEO and co-founder of Sólides, who prefers to shift the debate from substitution to filling gaps. The company's potential market is about 7 million small businesses, and most don't even have an HR professional. "There are countless Brazilian companies operating daily and lacking management. We are left without management. It is in that void, covering an absence, where artificial intelligence comes into play and generates an impact," the executive stated. Hauck backed the thesis with two figures: labor disputes moved over R$50 billion (US$10.024 billion) in Brazil the previous year, while Sólides' database registered an average 43% reduction in staff turnover, with a 15-month return on investment. Even so, she made a caveat about adoption pace, little present in current AI discourse. "There are ready technologies that are very advanced, but if you put them in people's hands right now, there will be problems. The customer will reject them. People are not prepared to consume them."

## WhatsApp as an Entry Point

If the problem is distribution, Marcelo Lombardo, CEO of Omie, a Brazilian management software platform for small businesses, offered the most concrete example of how generative AI changes the rules of the game. Around 7,000 new small businesses open in Brazil each month, and 90% of them work, according to him, with notebook and pen. "Our new competition today is Excel," he summarized. By integrating the platform's functionalities with WhatsApp through AI, Omie claims to have moved from a potential market of 1.5 million to 7 million companies, an expansion that, in the executive's view, has more to do with eliminating barriers than with product innovation. "This saved the user a long learning process for technologies."

For Alessandro Garcia, co-founder and co-CEO of Sólides, the debate about AI in Brazilian companies should move away from adoption and into execution. With increasingly accessible technology and similar models available to all, what differentiates one company from another, he said, is the capacity to transform that into operational results. "We have the potential to be ten times more efficient than we are today." The reasoning also applies to people, according to him: increased individual productivity becomes part of the employment contract.

Barreto mentioned the capital angle. Changing the reality of a business in Brazil has always required money, said the executive. Opening a network of stores, starting a bank, or maintaining an operation until it takes off depended on capital that few had. With AI, the barrier becomes different, since the cost of production is reduced to the point that the bottleneck is no longer financial. "The opportunity we have here is to put the tool in the hands of millions of Brazilians and tell them: do it, go ahead," said iFood's CEO.
