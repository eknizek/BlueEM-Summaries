To compete with hotels, platforms like Airbnb must offer much more, says Accor Americas CEO

11 April 2026 · 05:00 PM

# Airbnb Does Not Offer Hotel Experience, Says Accor

Short-term rental platforms like Airbnb will need to guarantee safety, service, and quality like traditional hotels do. That's according to Belgian Thomas Dubaere, CEO of Accor Americas, the network that commands brands such as Ibis, Novotel, Pullman, Mercure, and Faena.

Accor is experiencing a honeymoon period in Brazil. In 2025, for the first time in four years, there was growth in RevPAR (an indicator that measures profit per room) driven by increased occupancy. Demand is growing in the country across segments of guests traveling for business, leisure, and events (shows, festivals, and sports). The company's RevPAR grew 10.2% compared to 2024.

**How is the competition between hotel chains and short-term rental platforms like Airbnb?**

It's a question that emerged ten or fifteen years ago when Airbnb started operating. Short-term rentals are a different offering, not a direct substitute for hotels. Yes, there's a pointed impact, but it's a different business model. Even with the growth of short-term rentals, occupancy in our hotels has continued to grow, as has the average daily rate. If rental platforms want to advance in this market, they'll need to guarantee safety, service, and product quality—and follow the same regulations as traditional hotels.

**What does the guest expect from a hotel?**

Experience, experience, and experience. At Accor, we have more than 45 brands across all hotel segments. Each segment has its service, but we're thinking more about experience now. It's about how we can differentiate ourselves from the competition and differentiate our brands, even in entry-level seals like Ibis.

**What explains the warming of the hotel sector in Brazil?**

Infrastructure is improving, and air connectivity too. The exchange rate is favorable for some countries. The positive point is that foreigners are growing every year, and 2025 was a record of almost 9.2 million. They come not only in the leisure segment, but also in the corporate segment, which is the last one that still hasn't fully recovered when we compare it with 2019, the flow before the pandemic. This is the first year that the increase in average daily rate is not the only reason for RevPAR increase; there was an increase in actual occupancy, which hadn't happened in four years. Demand is growing in all three segments (leisure, business, and events).

**How do you expect Brazil's economic scenario to affect Accor's business in 2026?**

Interest rates are at 15%, which is quite high and always makes attracting new investments more complex. But, of all the hotels we've signed, a large part are greenfield projects (new constructions), concentrated in the Ibis segment, which requires a lower level of investment than a Fairmont or a Sofitel. The Brazilian economy is doing well. This is almost the first year in five or six where demand grows consistently. Instead of 4.5 million international visitors, we've practically doubled that number. When demand grows, the appetite to invest indirectly grows too. Faena will have a format combining residences and a cultural center, reminiscent of the Rosewood model in São Paulo. Is there demand for super luxury? Yes. We already have concrete examples. In terms of volume, the budget, mid-range, and premium segments will always be larger. Luxury doesn't need volume; it needs good location, quality product, and qualified demand. But there's certainly potential in Brazil, and not just in São Paulo.

**Is the tense global scenario a concern for Accor?**

It would be strange if it weren't. We're in many countries. When one region faces difficulties, another does well, which ends up compensating. We're a resilient industry. And the main driver of this growth is the expansion of the middle class worldwide, which travels increasingly more.
