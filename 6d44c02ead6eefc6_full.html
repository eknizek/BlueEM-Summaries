# Following recent gasoline price increases, how much room remains for further hikes this year?

The war in the Middle East and its effects are being felt with increasing force in the global economy and more directly in the pockets of Colombians as the conflict continues with no end in sight. That is why the World Bank issued a warning last week indicating that said conflict is triggering a new inflationary wave driven by rising energy costs. According to its latest report, energy prices will rise around 24 percent this year in a context of severe disruptions in oil supply.

"The war is affecting the global economy in cumulative waves: first with rising energy prices, then with food prices and finally with higher inflation," said Indermit Gill, chief economist of the organization.

Attacks on infrastructure and interruptions in key routes such as the Strait of Hormuz—through which nearly 35 percent of the world's oil passes—have caused one of the worst recent supply crises, with an initial drop of up to 10 million barrels daily, warns the entity. As a result, crude prices have risen more than 50 percent above early year levels, and in scenarios of greater escalation could push the Brent barrel—Colombia's main reference—to an average of $115 in 2026.

But the shock is not limited to oil. The World Bank notes that this situation is having cascading effects on other markets, since a 10 percent increase in crude price can translate to rises of up to 7 percent in natural gas and more than 5 percent in fertilizers and biofuels, pressuring production costs globally.

As a result, inflation in developing economies could climb to 5.8 percent, reflecting how rising energy costs ultimately translate into higher prices for food, transportation, and basic goods.

This global disruption has already begun to be felt in the country, and one of its most visible channels is precisely in fuels, which in the case of gasoline has already completed two consecutive increases of 400 pesos this year, while diesel costs have risen 200 pesos.

But beyond these recent movements, what is beginning to take shape is a more complex scenario, since according to experts, gasoline and diesel prices have not finished rising.

## How much more?

Recent figures help explain why there is room for further increases. Since February 27, a day before the conflict began, the international reference price for gasoline (gallon in the Gulf of Mexico) through May 5 has risen 87.3 percent. In the same period, the dollar has slight downward variation of 1 percent, leaving an increase in pesos of 85.4 percent.

For its part, the Brent barrel remains at elevated levels around $111, although below the maximum of $126 reached last week.

This global context is pressuring domestic prices and forcing the Government to resume adjustments, amid an increasingly narrow margin to contain them. The problem is structural because Colombia imports around 50 percent of the gasoline it consumes and maintains a subsidy system that has served to cushion domestic prices but today strains public finances.
