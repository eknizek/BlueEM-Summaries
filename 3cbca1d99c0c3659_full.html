# Itaú Asset surpasses BlackRock with $10 billion in ETF assets under management in Brazil

Itaú Asset Management achieved an unprecedented milestone in Brazil's exchange-traded fund (ETF) market at the end of last year, becoming the country's largest ETF manager by assets under management, surpassing BlackRock, the historical leader in this segment worldwide. With approximately R$ 30 billion distributed across 31 ETFs, Itaú's asset manager reached roughly 28% market share, according to company data from March.

The shift is in assets under management, not in the number of investors, showing that the manager successfully surfed the recent movement in this market. This is because industry growth has not been accompanied by an explosion in retail, but rather by increased institutional market use. Today, institutional investors account for 56.1% of assets under custody, while retail investors represent 33.7%, according to B3 data.

Evidence of this is that from January 2025 to now, the number of ETF investors increased from 733,000 to 763,000, according to B3. It was precisely this more sophisticated audience where Itaú Asset built its advantage, which corresponds to approximately two-thirds of its total ETF assets under management.

"Our dominance in the institutional market, which is much more selective and discerning, shows that we are more efficient at managing ETFs. We have over 20 years of experience in this market," says Pedro Boainain, director of private credit, fixed income, and indexed investments at Itaú Asset, which launched the first ETF product in Brazil in 2004.

The internal reading is that beyond price, the competition hinges on efficiency. The company closely monitors metrics such as tracking error—the deviation between the fund and its benchmark index—and claims to operate at levels lower than competitors while maintaining competitive administration fees.

"We don't charge cheap prices to 'crush' competitors. We charge the price that our efficiency and size allow. But this market is not just about price," says Renato Eid Tucci, superintendent of indexed strategies and responsible investment at Itaú Asset.

One example is the management of share lending. In Brazil, 100% of this revenue is returned to the shareholder, which can generate relevant performance differences between similar ETFs, depending on the manager's efficiency.

## The fight for retail

If leadership in assets came from institutional investors, the next step is to advance over retail investors, a segment where BlackRock still maintains an advantage in number of shareholders. To achieve this, Itaú Asset has bet on financial education and products more aligned with the Brazilian investor's profile, historically concentrated in fixed income.

Fixed income ETFs became the main entry point. Beyond simplifying access, these products offer relevant advantages: they have no come-cotas (quarterly tax distributions), feature a single 15% income tax rate, and eliminate the need for tax collection via DARF.

Rather than functioning merely as a shortcut to replicate an index, the instrument began to occupy space in portfolio engineering for cost, liquidity, transparency, and tax efficiency. Over the past two years, the manager launched a series of products with this proposal, combining local fixed income and international exposure, such as strategies tied to Treasuries, gold, and inflation.

More recently, it advanced into even more specific territory: ETFs that purchase individual government securities, such as NTN-Bs with defined maturities. The logic is to deliver investors predictability (knowledge of rate and term) with greater tax and operational efficiency. According to Eid, automatic coupon reinvestment without immediate tax incidence can generate a difference of up to 600 basis points compared to direct investment.

The current strategy also reflects lessons from past mistakes. The batch of thematic ETFs launched in 2021 (as did the entire industry), which surfed trends like technology, ESG, and healthcare, had low traction. The only highlight was the technology-focused product, which surpassed R$ 1 billion. The conclusion was that Brazilian investors respond better to "simple done well" than to fashionable theses. This discipline began to guide shelf expansion.

## An industry in expansion

Itaú Asset's growth coincides with an inflection point for Brazil's ETF industry. From January 2025 onward, ETFs have been the fastest-growing fund class in the country (with the exception of Fiagros), doubling in size and surpassing the R$ 100 billion mark in assets under management. Still, they remain small and represent about 1% of the local fund market.

The contrast with the United States is enormous. There, ETFs have already surpassed traditional funds, with over US$ 19 trillion under management.

"We have always believed in the product, its efficiency and simplicity. But Brazil has always been a high-interest country," says Boainain. "ETFs were seen as 'stock market,' not as an investment vehicle. This is changing."

The shift began with institutional investors. Asset managers, pension funds, and insurance companies began using ETFs not just as passive exposure but as a portfolio construction tool—including in more complex strategies. Within Itaú Asset's own solutions division, which builds customized portfolios for private and institutional clients, average ETF participation already hovers around 15%.

Itaú Asset's leadership occurs in a market tending toward consolidation. In the United States, three firms (BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street) concentrate more than 80% of ETF assets. The logic is that scale, liquidity, and operational efficiency are determinant in a business with compressed margins. And in Brazil, this race has already begun. Managers like XP, Bradesco, and BTG Pactual have accelerated recent launches, while new firms are entering the segment. According to B3, 62 new ETFs were listed in 2025 and more than 20 in 2026 alone.

"We view the entry of new competitors favorably. But we are not comfortable with our leadership. The game is just beginning," says Eid. Boainain projects that the Brazilian market could reach R$ 1 trillion within a decade, which would imply annual growth below the current pace. If confirmed, the competition tends to follow the same global trajectory.

"In the end, few large players should remain," says Boainain. "And we want to be one of them, with more than 30% market share."
