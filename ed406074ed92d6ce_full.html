# AI will reshape corporate hierarchy and eliminate 'middle management'

April 7, 2026

Most companies are using artificial intelligence as if it were a new computer or a new machine – something that helps improve productivity, but without promoting structural changes. But for Jack Dorsey, co-founder and chairman of fintech Block, AI will revolutionize the century-old corporate hierarchy. In an article written with Roelof Botha, a partner at Sequoia Capital, Dorsey argues that the organizational structure of modern companies dates back to the organization of Roman legions 2,000 years ago. It was a structure created to coordinate thousands of people distributed across large distances with limited access to communication. In the American corporate world, this military hierarchy was established in the earliest railroads, in the 1840s and 1850s. Communication failures were causing accidents – and deaths. There have been innovations and attempts at change over time, but the hierarchical structure remains dominant. The limitations are the same ones the Romans faced: reducing the span of control means adding layers of command, but more layers mean slower information flow. "Two thousand years of organizational innovation have been an attempt to work around this dilemma without breaking it," write Dorsey and Botha in the article From hierarchy to intelligence.

According to Dorsey, with the advent of AI for the first time we have a system that can keep all company activities and decisions continuously updated – and use that information to coordinate work that previously required multiple layers of management. The executive – who also founded Twitter – is already employing the ideas detailed in the article. At the end of February, Block laid off approximately 4,000 employees, equivalent to 40% of its payroll. Block wants to position itself as a remote-first company – in which all discussions and information are recorded and used as raw material for an AI model. "At Sequoia, we perceive that speed is the best indicator of success for startups," they state in the text. "Most companies are focused on AI as a means to increase productivity. Few are focused on the potential of AI to change the way we work."

In a traditional company, the manager's function is to know what is happening in their team and convey that context to higher hierarchical levels – and vice versa. With AI-based management, it will be possible to have an uninterrupted view of the whole, with no loss of information. The authors call this system a company world model, which can be integrated with a customer world model – that is, all information about your customers and users. In this way, the need for a permanent layer of middle management, such as product managers, disappears. Everything that the old hierarchy did, the AI-based system can coordinate – and potentially in a way much more integrated with customer demands. "How quickly or slowly companies move depends on information flow. Hierarchies and middle management hinder this flow," say Dorsey and Botha. "The question was never whether we needed hierarchical layers. The question was whether humans were the only option for what these layers did. They are not anymore."
