# 'No one will think about technology if they're hungry,' says Rio Grande do Sul innovation secretary

The economic and social revolution promised by artificial intelligence runs into a Brazil of contrasts. For Lisiane Lemos, secretary of Innovation, Science and Technology for Rio Grande do Sul, technology has the potential to include underrepresented groups in the labor market, but can be a factor of exclusion if algorithms ignore human context. "When we feed databases into artificial intelligence, we must be attentive to adjustments and interpretations. If it were just something predictive, we wouldn't be talking here. Statistically, the chance of me being a young, Black woman from Rio Grande do Sul in a science and technology chair borders on zero because we don't have historical data. No one like me has sat in this chair," says Lisiane Lemos.

The secretary leads a governmental ecosystem ranging from teacher qualification projects to digital inclusion initiatives for the elderly, passing through climate resilience policies and engineer training programs. Leading the innovation department in a state with almost 11 million inhabitants, the executive has previously worked at Google and Microsoft, was Forbes Under 30 and is a LinkedIn Top Voice. From these experiences, she points out Brazil's paradoxes — a country that develops pioneering technologies in research centers but still struggles against digital illiteracy and lack of basic connectivity.

In conversation with Estadão, Lisiane cites the importance of technology for the emancipation of women victims of tragedies affecting the gaucho state and domestic violence, explains the role of diversity in companies and argues that "every company will be a technology company." She is one of the confirmed speakers at São Paulo Innovation Week.

**Can AI both include and exclude the labor market?**

It can be both, depending on how we insert ourselves and what we do with it. Speaking from my trajectory, technology transformed where I came from, where I'm going and what I want for the future. I'm a woman born and raised in Rio Grande do Sul, I built my career between Microsoft, Google and the State Department. I lived on the African continent. None of this would be possible without the internet. Technology includes by providing tools for people from underrepresented or vulnerable groups to transform their lives. On the other hand, it excludes when decisions about people's lives — such as access to education, credit, transportation and communication — are made exclusively by algorithms and AI. To question or advance, we need to understand how these tools work. Since my work in the Digital Inclusion Department, I'm in favor of technology for the issue of scale and access to knowledge. In my view, AI comes primarily to include, but has potential for exclusion that will only be mitigated if each of us dedicates time to studying its effects on daily life.

**How does Brazil participate in this market? Are we advanced or behind in our relationship with technology and AI?**

It depends. In terms of academic research and vision for the future, we're very far ahead. We have CPQD in Campinas developing 5G, 6G, electric cars and fiber optic use, plus technology parks in Rio Grande do Sul that surpass centers in Europe and the United States. Brazil is indeed a country of the future when we talk about artificial intelligence, knowledge production and research incentive, although it needs more investment and visibility. At the same time, we have a population that is illiterate and without basic access to internet, sanitation, health and education. How do we bring these people to the center of progress? And how do we encourage foreign countries to see Brazil as a partner in developing new technologies and research?

**What are the main obstacles to this journey?**

There are several obstacles. First, infrastructure, especially in a state like Rio Grande do Sul, which faced severe climate disasters that devastated all infrastructure. There is a process of reconstruction and cabling to be developed. The rest of Brazil faces similar challenges. There is an urbanized Brazil on the Rio-São Paulo axis, there is the Brazil of agriculture and the countryside. How do I provide internet of things and sensors in all these places? Infrastructure, energy and transport are necessary. It's not just AI for AI's sake. Additionally, there's the obstacle of technical access and knowledge. Who is going to operate these new machines? This is a major challenge in Rio Grande do Sul, which is Brazil's oldest state. When we talk about technology it's always about young people, but what about elderly people? How will someone who is 50, 60 years old retrain themselves? We think there is cultural transformation because more than 60% of the population says they have already used artificial intelligence, like ChatGPT. Now they're anxious that ChatGPT will take their jobs and end everything. I've learned that technology comes to leverage what we do best and save time for what we enjoy. How do I make this transformation a driver of human potential? You built a career in big tech and now work in the public sector, which has a reputation for being slower. How do you apply your previous experiences to this new reality?

In the public sector, there is a lack of awareness among the population about the influence of laws on daily life. We are the first state to have internet as a fundamental right. However, we have legislators who don't know the legal framework of the internet. Today, I have a sense of the magnitude and influence of government in the creation and use of technology. And these people don't have the technical knowledge to make these legal frameworks. The public sector has a reputation for being more bureaucratic and slow because people don't have a sense of the order of magnitude of the impact we have and the consequences of our actions. One thing is to be a manager at Google. In recruitment, my impact was 5,000 to at most 10,000 people. Now I indirectly impact 11 million. The decision I make today can reflect in the next government, the next decade or the next century.
