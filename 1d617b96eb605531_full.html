# Real Estate Development in Mendoza Targets Mining, Energy, Fintech and Logistics Companies

Grupo Presidente, the developer of Palmares mall and a company with businesses in agroexport and automotive dealerships, launched an office complex with networking spaces, streaming room and paddleball court.

Hills, vineyards, haute cuisine, technology companies and mining and energy services — private investment seeks to replicate the California Napa Valley model in western Greater Mendoza.

"Sectors such as mining, oil & gas, logistics, health, services, technology and knowledge economy today demand more sophisticated environments, well-connected and capable of expressing a new standard," said Julián Groisman, director of Grupo Presidente.

This is the developer group that owns Palmares mall (the largest in the interior), with private neighborhoods, automotive dealerships, agricultural investments and a new project in Palmares Valley, aimed at families arriving in the region for energy and mining projects. Grupo Presidente's idea is to consolidate an urban and commercial axis in the foothills.

Other competitors, such as Dalvian and Chacras Park, have developed urban, commercial and service proposals also in western Greater Mendoza.

In the case of Grupo Presidente, the project to attract families migrating due to Andean project development is called Valley Boulevard, a building in Palmares Valley with recreation and networking areas.

Groisman assured that they already have several interested parties, including some multinationals that rank among the "Big Four" audit firms.

Mendoza has achieved a great level of services, in part due to wine tourism. "We must care for this opportunity and try to attract those who come to invest in the provinces of the west with mining and in Neuquén with oil and gas," added the businessman, noting that due to its quality of life and services, Mendoza can attract all those families of professionals and technicians who settle in San Juan or Neuquén.

"As occurs in La Serena and other Chilean cities, which although mining investments or renewable energy are not located there, they are the headquarters of those investor ecosystems," he argued.

The project aims at a new way of working — a building prepared to receive dynamic operations, qualified personnel and brands that need infrastructure, connectivity, representation and environment in one place.

The developers of the district located in La Puntilla, on the border with Chacras de Coria, in the municipality of Luján de Cuyo, say they have a projection of more than 10,000 residents in the coming years.

The Valley Boulevard project aims to attract professional offices, SMEs, expanding companies, regional offices and corporate headquarters. It is the third corporate development by Grupo Presidente in the area — they already have Avatar and Workplace.

In a five-story building there will be modular offices from 30m² to corporate floors of over 1,000m², common areas, meeting rooms, terraces, areas for hybrid work, technological infrastructure and an active ground floor with open-air shopping promenade, designed to encourage daily networking.

"The work environment is migrating to these new spaces where you can socialize, have a coffee, do sports like paddleball, make launches, train or participate in a streaming," said Groisman.

**Agricultural Business**

In agricultural investments, the Groisman family has 350 hectares of vineyards in Mendoza and San Juan and 300 hectares of walnut trees in Valle de Uco.

In the case of winemaking, Grupo Presidente ceded all vineyard operations to Peñaflor group. They rent the cultivated fields and audit that plantations located in high enological value localities such as Los Árboles and El Cepillo in Mendoza, and in Zonda in San Juan are cared for. All vineyard and grape management remains with Peñaflor.

"We have decided to concentrate our agricultural business on walnut trees in Tupungato," Groisman clarified.

They export all their walnut production — 1,200 tons of Chandler variety, extra light — from the 20,000 that the country exports. Their main buyers are in the Middle East, Italy, Russia and Brazil.

70% of walnuts are exported in-shell because in destination countries, for example India, labor is cheaper and that is where the shell is removed by hand. 30% is sent shelled, as in Brazil.

The Middle East war is a concern for dried fruit exporters. "We expect more inflation and some impact on walnut prices, a commodity that was stabilized in its prices over the last three years," Groisman detailed.

**Electromobility**

"The retail business opened the doors to us for the display and sale of plug-in hybrid electric technology vehicles," added the Mendoza businessman.

During the last quarter of 2025, they opened the Avanim dealership, with branches in Mendoza, San Juan and Neuquén, in partnership with Grupo Antelo.

"Electromobility is here to stay. When people take the test drive, they buy and then there's no turning back. I think this whole ecosystem that is being built is great for the country," Groisman assured, in support of the government's import opening policy.

They represent GMW (Haval), Changan, JMEV and Mitsubishi in western Argentina, selling between 80 to 100 units per month. "In 2026 we expect to sell 1,000 units per year," said the Presidente executive.

As an investor in different sectors, Groisman considered that urban developments should gain presence in non-productive areas, such as the foothills of the Mendoza pre-Andean range. And he emphasized: "Seeing the growth of cities on the hills, as happens in Santiago Chile, we are very optimistic about the possibility of generating an urban and corporate hub, a new ecosystem, based on RIGI projects and other developments in the Andean region."
