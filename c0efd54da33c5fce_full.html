# Former Minister Bonilla Questions Central Bank Rates; Villar Defends Strategy Amid Growing Economic Tension

Former Finance Minister Ricardo Bonilla's public reappearance came amid a tense political and economic environment. After regaining his freedom due to the expiration of legal proceedings by the General Prosecutor's Office in the corruption scandal at the Risk Management Unit, the former official focused his intervention on a direct critique of recent decisions by the Central Bank of Colombia.

During an academic forum held at the National University, Bonilla questioned the current level of interest rates and their impact on the economy, pointing out that monetary policy is generating excessive credit costs. His diagnosis stems from what he considers a problematic gap: inflation near 5.5 percent against an intervention rate of 11.25 percent, which results—according to his calculations—in a high real rate that directly affects the cost of money.

For the former minister, this differential is significant. In his view, it translates into financing restrictions, less economic dynamism, and slower recovery of productive activity. However, this vision contrasts with the defense made by Leonardo Villar, manager of the Central Bank, during the recent political control debate in Congress. There he insisted that maintaining high rates responds to the need to prevent greater deterioration of purchasing power and contain still-latent inflationary pressures.

## The Initial Adjustment Question

One of the former minister's most critical points addressed the initial handling of the inflationary cycle. Bonilla maintains that the Central Bank reacted late to the price increase following the pandemic, which then forced more aggressive rate increases. According to his analysis, the delay in beginning the monetary tightening cycle contributed to inflation rising from levels near 1.5 percent in 2021 to a peak of 13.34 percent in March 2023, which took the intervention rate to 13.25 percent.

From the Central Bank's perspective, however, Villar defended that the monetary policy scheme has been consistent with international standards and that its countercyclical nature first allowed stimulating the economy after the pandemic and then reacting to the inflationary surge.

## Current Debate

Today, the disagreement centers on interpreting the present moment. Bonilla believes that monetary policy has not accompanied the inflation decline with sufficient speed, maintaining restrictive financial conditions in a context that would require greater economic growth stimulus. The Central Bank manager, in contrast, argued before Congress that the environment remains complex and that rapidly reducing rates could reignite price pressures.

According to his explanation, factors such as the fiscal deficit, growing demand, and recent decisions like the minimum wage increase have elevated inflation expectations. In that sense, he defended the Central Bank's caution and stressed that the effects of monetary policy materialize with lags of several months.

## Credibility

The differences also extend to the conceptual level. Bonilla questioned the Central Bank's emphasis on the 3 percent inflation target and institutional credibility, noting that this approach does not necessarily translate into better economic results.
