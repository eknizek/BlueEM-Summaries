# Bemobi Accelerates Pivot to Payments After Paytime Acquisition

At the end of 2025, Bemobi (BMOB3) announced the acquisition of control of Paytime, a no-code white label fintech and one of the country's leading Payment as a Service (PaaS) platforms. More than a simple acquisition, the transaction marks a new moment for the company, which was once known as the "Netflix of Apps." Led by Pedro Ripper, also co-founder, the company went public in early 2021 and has undergone a major transformation in its business model. The company closed last year with net revenue of R$ 728.8 million, up 20.0% compared to the previous year.

In the fourth quarter, the vertical payments division represented 43.2% of net revenue, followed by SaaS (Software as a Service) products. Subscriptions, the company's original business, accounted for 25.2% and microfinance 10.1%. Together, payments and software revenues totaled 65% in the last quarter of the year, after advancing 50% and 23% respectively in annual comparison.

The numbers reflect a successful strategy to migrate the company's business model, which bet on recurring payments in certain sectors of the economy such as telecommunications, sanitation and energy, education, and more recently, healthcare, as a compass.

"We focused on specialization, understanding what is most important for them, because industries are not all the same and it is necessary to understand the peculiarities of each one to actually add value for companies and their consumers," Ripper states.

The strategy, which began over the last four years, includes clients such as Vivo, Tim, Sabesp, Copel, and Hapvida.

"On this front, we will have natural expansions in recurring and essential service industries where we do not currently operate, such as condominiums and insurance categories," the executive says.

The acquisition of Paytime reinforces this bet while opening a new front for the company, with entry into B2B2B focused on vertical payments for ecosystems and marketplaces. This new frontier, which Bemobi intends to drive over the next two years, goes beyond recurrence and should be capitalized from volumes and new possibilities for interactions.

"We believe in this division as a partnership model, where I sell to a business that combines its knowledge with my solution to deliver a product to a company at the end," says the CEO.

The opportunities with this customized format, according to Ripper, are in markets such as franchises. Franchisors can benefit from better-developed payment flows that offer split (division of value among the various parts of the chain), avoid double taxation, and advance to credit and banking solutions.

"There is a lot of value in integrating payments into these ecosystems or productive chains," Ripper states, citing other markets such as fuel distribution and manufacturers in chains like auto parts that finance their clients' operations such as gas stations and mechanic shops.

According to an Itaú BBA report released in early March, the transaction will allow "the PaaS (Platform as a Service) model to be implemented in larger customers, potentially improving take rates in the long term — which we consider, for now, a positive risk," states the content signed by Maria Clara Infantozzi. "Additionally, it will contribute to marginal TPV growth going forward."

More Acquisitions Ahead

With R$ 350 million in cash, Bemobi should continue exploring M&A opportunities to advance in the market, a strategy it has pursued over time. Last year, in addition to its stake in Paytime, the company acquired fintech Celer, from Casas Bahia. The moves accompany both the idea of opportunity for entry into a new market and technical capacity.

"In the case of Paytime, it has software that manages payments for ecosystems. We were starting to develop it and realized theirs was already mature," says the CEO.

According to Ripper, the company should not change the pace of acquisitions, a path it has learned to develop in recent years. Since the 2021 IPO, there have been seven deals, counting the two most recent. "But transactions need to be very surgical and check several boxes, such as price, characteristics, and delivery. We look at many companies. Last year, it was about 100 and we closed two. And we will continue looking," he adds.

In a moment of accelerated transformation in the technology world, driven by artificial intelligence investments, the last thing the CEO wants is to play a supporting role in his own market. That's why, in parallel with M&As, Ripper says the company is super active regarding AI and its impacts on the future of the business, which he projects will be completely different in a five-year horizon.

"It's not hard to imagine a not-too-distant future where you'll have an agent that, among other things, will organize all your accounts and pay for you, knowing which ones it can delay, which ones it cannot delay, or which can be installments. Part of our role is how we envision this possible world and how we are not spectators," Ripper concludes.
