# Bemobi Accelerates Pivot to Payments Following Paytime Acquisition

At the end of 2025, Bemobi (BMOB3) announced the acquisition of control of Paytime, a no-code white label fintech and one of the country's leading Payment as a Service (PaaS) platforms. More than a simple acquisition, the transaction marks a new moment for the company, which was once known as the "Netflix of Apps." Led by Pedro Ripper, also co-founder, the company went public in early 2021 and has undergone a major transformation in its business model. The company closed last year with net revenue of R$ 728.8 million, up 20.0% compared to the previous year.

In the fourth quarter, the vertical payments division represented 43.2% of net revenue, followed by SaaS (Software as a Service) products. Subscriptions, the company's original business, accounted for 25.2% and microfinance, 10.1%. Together, payments and software revenues totaled 65% in the last quarter of the year, after advancing 50% and 23%, respectively, in year-over-year comparison.

The numbers reflect a successful strategy of migrating the company's business model, which bet on recurring payments in specific economic sectors such as telecommunications, sanitation and energy, education, and more recently, healthcare.

"We focus on specialization, understanding what is most important for them, because industries are not all the same and it is necessary to understand the peculiarities of each one to actually add value for companies and their consumers," says Ripper.

The strategy, which began to be implemented over the last four years, includes clients such as Vivo, Tim, Sabesp, Copel, and Hapvida.

"On this front, we will have natural expansions in recurring and essential service industries where we don't currently operate, such as condominiums and insurance categories," says the executive.

The acquisition of Paytime reinforces this bet while opening a new front for the company, with entry into B2B2B vertical payments for ecosystems and marketplaces. This new frontier, which Bemobi intends to drive over the next two years, goes beyond recurrence and should be capitalized based on volumes and new possibilities for interactions.

"We believe in this division as a partnership model, in which I sell to a business that combines its knowledge with my solution to deliver a product to a company at the end," says the CEO.

According to Ripper, opportunities with this customized format exist in markets such as franchising. Franchisers can benefit from better-developed payment flows that offer split (division of value among various parts of the chain), avoid double taxation, and advance to credit and banking solutions.

"There is a lot of value in integrating payments into these ecosystems or production chains," says Ripper, citing other markets such as fuel distribution and manufacturers in chains such as auto parts that finance the operations of their clients like gas stations and repair shops, respectively.

According to a report from Itaú BBA released in early March, the transaction will allow "the PaaS model (Platform as a Service) to be implemented with larger clients, potentially improving take rates (commission fees) in the long term — which we consider, for now, a positive risk," states the content signed by Maria Clara Infantozzi. "Additionally, it will contribute to marginal TPV growth going forward."

**More Acquisitions Ahead**

With R$ 350 million in cash, Bemobi should continue exploring M&A opportunities to advance in the market, a strategy it has pursued over time. Last year, in addition to its stake in Paytime, the company acquired fintech Celer, from Casas Bahia. The moves align with both the idea of opportunity for entering a new market and technical capacity.

"In the case of Paytime, they have software that manages payments for ecosystems. We were just starting to develop this and realized theirs was already mature," says the CEO.

According to Ripper, the company should not change the pace of acquisitions, a path it has learned to develop in recent years. Since the 2021 IPO, there have been seven deals, counting the two most recent. "But transactions need to be very surgical and check several boxes, such as price, characteristics, and delivery. We look at many companies. Last year, around 100, and we closed two. And we will continue looking," he adds.

At a moment of accelerated transformation in the technology world, driven by investments in artificial intelligence, the last thing the CEO wants is to play a supporting role in his own market. For this reason, in parallel with M&As, Ripper says the company is very active regarding AI and its impacts on the future of the business, which he projects will be completely different within a five-year horizon.

"It's not difficult to imagine a not-so-distant future in which you will have an agent that, among other things, will organize all your accounts and pay for you, knowing which one it can delay, which one it can't delay, or which one can be installment paid. Part of our role is how we envision this possible world and how we are not just spectators," says Ripper.
