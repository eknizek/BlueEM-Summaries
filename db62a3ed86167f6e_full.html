# Nine in Ten Consumers in Latin America Use Digital Payments, Mastercard Says

Nine out of ten consumers in Latin America and the Caribbean already identify themselves as users of digital payments. This is what a survey commissioned by Mastercard and released on Tuesday (21st) shows. The data reveals, however, that there is pent-up demand for digitalization, which is hindered by the challenge of improving consumer access to digital payment options.

The study, conducted by independent consulting firm Many Minds Group with 3,558 adults in ten Latin American and Caribbean countries in March 2026, shows that despite significant progress, physical money persists as an alternative in contexts where digital acceptance is still precarious. Nearly half of respondents (47%) used cash in the last six months, and 36% still resort to it on buses. Among street vendors, dependence on cash reaches 32%.

"Inclusion is no longer just about incorporating people into the financial system, but rather ensuring that the system works for them in their daily lives," said Andrea Scerch, Mastercard's president for Latin America and the Caribbean, in a statement.

The research covered markets in Argentina, Colombia, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, Guatemala, Jamaica, Mexico, Panama, Peru, and Puerto Rico.

It is worth noting that in Brazil, digitalization is much more advanced due to the widespread adoption of Pix. The instant payment system, created by the Central Bank in November 2020, has already surpassed cash usage in market share since the first quarter of 2021. In March this year, the total number of users who had already used Pix, according to Central Bank data, amounted to 81% of the Brazilian population.

## Rising Appetite

In the rest of the region, Mastercard's survey points to growing demand for digital payments. Eight out of ten consumers point to security and convenience as reasons to prefer digital, and 87% want more establishments and people to accept this type of payment.

About 59% report that at least once a month they need to withdraw cash when they would like to use their mobile phone or card. Consumers who opt for digital payment prefer debit cards: six out of ten use this option for daily transactions – more than digital wallets (42%) and transfers (39%).

The categories where debit cards most appear among preferences are supermarkets (34%), restaurants and cafeterias (33%), mobile phone bills (27%), and ride-sharing apps (26%).

The research reinforces that the expansion of digital payments depends on both infrastructure and perception of security. Among digital users, 95% consider protection a relevant factor when choosing how to pay, and 94% cite system reliability. Among consumers still resistant to digital payments, 43% say that security improvements would change their stance, followed by clearer protection in case of problems (38%) and more efficient customer service (36%).

Among non-users, 68% of them say it is "somewhat or very likely" that they will adopt digital payments in the future. The conversion window exists — what is missing is building the trust and infrastructure to make it real.
