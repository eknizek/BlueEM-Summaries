The R$37 billion 'blackout' in the FIDC market

20 April 2026 · 12:10 PM

# The R$37 billion "blackout" in the FIDC market

A class of funds that has grown the most in Brazil in recent years, Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) are known for their complex structures involving different types of receivables. But at this moment, complexity has combined with a kind of "blackout."

The delays in releasing monthly FIDC reports have become recurring in recent months and have demanded a more active stance from the Securities and Exchange Commission (CVM). The document, which contains information such as assets, credit volume, and default levels, is the main reference for those who regularly monitor these portfolios.

According to a Uqbar survey conducted at NeoFeed's request, 76 FIDCs had not yet reported February data as of April 10 — the deadline for submission was March 15. The gap is R$37 billion, considering the last net asset value reported by the funds. The volume corresponds to 5% of the entire FIDC industry and 50% of all net fundraising from last year.

The study considered only active FIDCs that had reported monthly statements in November, when delays began to worsen, according to Alfredo Marrucho, a partner at Uqbar. Marrucho has been following the FIDC market for over 10 years and states that non-compliance in delivering reports is unprecedented, especially at these proportions.

"Some administrators claimed that the year-end transition would have caused problems. But this argument doesn't hold up, given that at the beginning of 2025 there wasn't the same volume of delays. It was around 1% of the industry," he says.

Reag, currently in liquidation by the Central Bank, is the administrator with the largest number of FIDCs that failed to deliver February data, with 37. Planner comes next, with 11 FIDCs with delayed reports. Of the 76 FIDCs that did not publish February balances, 30 had at least two delayed reports. Another 39 FIDCs failed to present reports from December and January.

These gaps, according to Marrucho, have become increasingly frequent and hindered industry monitoring. "Even with FIDCs raising capital, a survey we conducted pointed to a decline in assets, due to delays in disclosures. We no longer have a sense of the actual size of this market."

Among FIDCs that have not yet presented February's balance sheet, the largest is ANNA FIC FIDC Não Padronizado. The fund, administered by Reag and managed by CBSF Trust, reported a net asset value of R$15.7 billion in December. Created at the end of 2023, the fund invested primarily in shares of other investment funds and has never had an audited financial statement.

Of the R$37 billion that are in the dark, funds administered by Reag account for R$23.66 billion. "Reag is having operational problems today. The Central Bank, in liquidation, is watching these portfolios, many of them don't have adequate backing," says one fund administrator.

These delays, according to this source, may be creating a cascading effect. "If one FIDC doesn't send information, another fund that invests in it ends up not having enough data to account for its portfolio."

One of Reag's funds with reports is Rover FIDC, which once had a relevant portion of credits transferred by EntrePay, according to a NeoFeed report, before the institution entered liquidation by the Central Bank at the end of last month. As a result of EntrePay's liquidation, the manager Redwood suspended one of its funds for redemption at the beginning of this month. Two other funds from the manager, which together had more than R$130 million in assets in November, have had delayed payments for months.

The scenario is similar to Fictor FIDC, which, as NeoFeed showed, also closed for redemptions amid liquidity problems on the operational front. The fund, which until January declared R$201 million, also did not publish its February report.

## CVM on Alert

The recurring delays in delivering monthly reports also caught the attention of the CVM. Faced with the problem, the agency published a letter at the beginning of this year, reinforcing that delays are subject to automatic fines of up to R$60,000 per fund. In the letter, the CVM stated that "it has received a significant volume of appeals filed by administrators" due to fines applied for delays or non-delivery of information.

"At times administrators argue in their appeals that they were surprised by the volume of fines applied and that this will impact their assets. However, such allegations reinforce that internal controls are not adequate, including to identify delays that have occurred and to timely account for provisions and expenses for future fine payments," says the CVM letter.

For a businessman in the FIDC sector, the lack of transparency in publishing reports has generated an "immense" reputational risk for the industry. "There is enormous fear, especially among major fintech market entrepreneurs. It is a class that has become increasingly institutional and a poor administrator disrupts the entire operation," he says.

This source, who spoke with NeoFeed on condition of anonymity, also stated that even published data may be inaccurate due to the difficulty in processing more complex portfolios. "I feel that many times what is being reported is not exactly what is happening in real time within the portfolio. FIDCs are becoming a class of assets that institutional investors have allocated increasingly — and institutional investors don't tolerate this level of little information, of wrong information."

In his assessment, it is only a matter of time before the CVM toughens the rules for the FIDC market, especially now that it will have the Central Bank's help to supervise this class of funds. "The Central Bank, in November of last year, raised the bar for DTVMs. It is a movement where the Central Bank has already been very categorical and I think the CVM should certainly do the same soon. So many administrators will be left behind."

When asked about the delay in delivering reports, Planner stated that the FIDCs identified in the survey "are funds recently transferred from other administrators, in different stages of regularization." According to Planner, "most of them present pending issues that already existed with the previous administrator at the date of transfer" and, in two of them, the delay stems from "operational issues in implementation in the internal system."

The company also stated that "there is no cascading effect" on others.
