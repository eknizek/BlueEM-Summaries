R$37 Billion FIDC Market Faces Reporting Crisis as Monthly Disclosures Lag

20 April 2026 · 12:10 PM

# The R$37 Billion Blackout in the FIDC Market

A class of funds that has grown the most in Brazil in recent years, Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) are known for their complex structures involving different types of receivables. But at this moment, complexity has combined with a kind of "blackout."

Delays in the disclosure of monthly FIDC reports have become recurring in recent months and have demanded a more active stance from the Securities Commission (CVM). The document, which contains information such as assets, credit volume, and default levels, is the primary reference for those who regularly monitor these portfolios.

According to a survey by Uqbar conducted at the request of NeoFeed, 76 FIDCs had not yet reported February data by April 10—the deadline for delivery was March 15. The gap amounts to R$37 billion, considering the last net assets reported by the funds. The volume corresponds to 5% of the entire FIDC industry and 50% of all net fundraising from last year.

The study considered only active FIDCs that had reported monthly statements in November, when delays began to worsen, according to Alfredo Marrucho, a partner at Uqbar. Marrucho has followed the FIDC market for over 10 years and states that the non-compliance in delivering reports is unprecedented, especially in these proportions.

"Some administrators claimed that the year-end transition caused problems. But the argument doesn't hold up, given that at the beginning of 2025 there wasn't the same volume of delays. It was about 1% of the industry," he says.

Reag, currently in liquidation by the Central Bank, is the administrator with the most FIDCs that failed to deliver February data, with 37. Planner follows, with 11 FIDCs with delayed reports. Of the 76 FIDCs that did not publish February balances, 30 had at least two delayed reports. Another 39 FIDCs failed to present December and January reports.

These gaps, according to Marrucho, have become increasingly frequent and hampered industry monitoring. "Even with FIDCs raising capital, a survey we conducted showed a decline in assets due to delays in disclosures. We no longer have a notion of the actual size of this market."

Of the FIDCs that had not yet presented the February balance, the largest is ANNA FIC FIDC Non-Standard. The fund, administered by Reag and managed by CBSF Trust, reported net assets of R$15.7 billion in December. Created at the end of 2023, the fund primarily invested in shares of other investment funds and has never had an audited financial statement.

Of the R$37 billion in the dark, funds administered by Reag account for R$23.66 billion. "Reag is having operational problems today. The Central Bank, in the liquidation process, is watching these portfolios—many of them don't have adequate backing," says a fund administrator.

These delays, according to this source, may be generating a cascading effect. "If a FIDC doesn't send information, another fund that invests in it ends up not having sufficient data to account for its portfolio."

One of Reag's funds with reports is Rover FIDC, which had a significant portion in credits ceded by EntrePay, according to NeoFeed reporting, before the institution entered liquidation by the Central Bank at the end of last month. As a result of EntrePay's liquidation, the Redwood manager suspended one of its funds for redemptions at the beginning of this month. Two other funds from the manager, which totaled over R$130 million in assets in November, have had payments delayed for months.

The scenario is similar to Fictor FIDC, which, as NeoFeed showed, also closed for redemptions amid liquidity problems on the operational front. The fund, which until January reported R$201 million, also did not publish its February report.

## CVM on Alert

The recurring delays in delivering monthly reports have also caught the attention of the CVM. Facing the problem, the agency published a letter at the beginning of this year, reinforcing that delays are subject to automatic fines of up to R$60 thousand per fund. In the letter, the CVM stated that it "has received a significant volume of appeals filed by administrators" due to fines applied for delayed or non-delivery of information.

"At times, administrators allege in their appeals that they were surprised by the volume of fines applied and that this will impact their assets. However, such allegations reinforce that internal controls are not adequate, including to identify delays and account for provisions and expenses for future fine payments in a timely manner," says the CVM letter.

For a FIDC industry entrepreneur, the lack of transparency in publishing reports has generated an "immense" reputational risk for the industry. "There is enormous fear, including among major fintech market players. It's a class that has become increasingly institutional, and a poor administrator disrupts the entire operation," he says.

This source, who spoke with NeoFeed on condition of anonymity, also stated that even published data may be inaccurate due to difficulties in processing more complex portfolios. "I feel that often what is being reported is not exactly what is happening in real time within the portfolio. FIDCs are becoming a class of assets that institutional investors are allocating increasingly to—and institutional investors do not tolerate this level of poor or incorrect information."

In his assessment, it is only a matter of time before the CVM tightens rules for the FIDC market, especially now that it will have the help of the BC to supervise this class of funds. "The Central Bank, in November of last year, raised the bar for DTVMs. It's a move in which the Central Bank was already very categorical, and I think the CVM should certainly do the same soon. So many administrators will be left behind."

When questioned about the delay in delivering reports, Planner stated that the FIDCs identified in the survey "are funds recently transferred from other administrators, in different stages of regularization." According to Planner, "most have pending issues that already existed with the previous administrator at the time of transfer" and, in two of them, the delay stems from "operational issues in implementation in the internal system."
