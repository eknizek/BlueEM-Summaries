# Cepeda Proposes Business Pact: Rules Out Nationalization, Focuses on Tax Evasion

The business sector in Colombia is a key and decisive factor for the country. Iván Cepeda knows this, and he addressed issues that would impact them in an interview with Andrés Mompotes, director of EL TIEMPO. The presidential candidate from the Historic Pact outlined a roadmap to repair the relationship between the State and the private sector, amid an environment marked by tensions and distrust. His central message: there will be no nationalization of the economy and, on the contrary, he will seek broad coordination with business owners, entrepreneurs, and different productive actors.

"I have never spoken of nationalizing the economy, that is not in any of my public statements nor has it been my conviction," the candidate affirmed, responding to one of the main fears expressed by business sectors. In his vision, the economic model he proposes is a "social economy" where multiple forms of production coexist. Cepeda went further and detailed this approach: "I believe in a social economy with participation, of course, from the private sector, entrepreneurship, large companies, but also small and medium enterprises, of course, the popular economy, the development of the peasant economy."

## Equity as an Engine for Growth

One of the central axes of his proposal is equity. Cepeda defended the idea that reducing inequality is not an obstacle, but a condition for economic growth.

"When there is greater equity, there is more development," he assured, contrary to what he characterized as a dominant narrative in recent decades. "Not as we were tried to be convinced during so many years of neoliberalism, that the greater the equity, the more everyone loses. No, the greater the equity, we all win."

To support his thesis, he even cited international experiences: "In Mexico they say it very clearly: 'For the good of all, the poor first.' And it's going well for them." In his view, a society with less poverty and inequality generates collective benefits, even for the wealthiest sectors. "If there is less poverty, there is less backwardness, there is less inequality, and society as a whole, even the wealthiest sectors, have greater benefits," he added, insisting that this approach should be part of the economic debate in Colombia.

## Relationship with Business: From Tension to Dialogue

The candidate acknowledged that the relationship between the current Government and some business sectors has been complex. "There have been heated discussions with the financial sector especially," he admitted. However, he also launched a message seeking to change the tone of the debate. "The wealthiest sectors, the largest companies in this country can only have words of recognition and gratitude toward President Petro's government and the Historic Pact, since the profits they have obtained are enormous," he maintained.

Facing a potential government of his own, Cepeda promised a different relationship: "I would say cordial, respectful, and sincere, around clear theses and around clear rules." He emphasized that his style would be to establish clear rules and build trust based on transparency.

## Dialogue with Business Groups

Along the same lines, he announced his intention to approach organized business directly. "When I meet, as will happen soon, with the National Business Council, I will make them a cordial invitation to think about this thesis," he said, referring to his proposal for equity as the basis for development.

For Cepeda, this dialogue will be key to rebuilding trust and opening spaces for agreement. His bet is that the private sector will participate actively in building economic and social agreements.

## Public Finances and Fiscal Responsibility

The candidate also addressed one of the issues that most concerns markets: the management of public finances. He acknowledged that the next government will face a complex fiscal scenario, but proposed solutions based on fulfilling obligations. "There are ways to solve this, but they involve evident, I would not say sacrifices, but fulfillment of duties," he explained.

In that sense, he focused on the need to strengthen tax collection without necessarily resorting to new tax burdens. "Everyone must pay taxes. It is a constitutional duty. Tax evasion is a crime," he emphasized. Cepeda was especially critical of evasion in the higher income sectors: "Those who have the largest assets are the biggest evaders and there are many ways to evade."

For the candidate, combating evasion is a priority that could even prevent new tax reforms. "Tax evasion is corruption," he stated, proposing that this phenomenon should be treated as a structural problem.

## Regarding Private Property

Regarding ET's director's question about private property, Cepeda stated that "it is not about violating the Constitution, but what this Government has shown, this government that is criticized so much, is that it is possible to do it. Did we not buy land from ranchers? Something that seemed absolutely implausible a few years ago. And I would like, pardon my immodesty, that they also attributed some responsibility to me, so to speak, not only for terrible things that have supposedly occurred in the country, but also for the good things that have happened." Additionally, he indicated there was an agreement with Fedegán "that allowed us to buy land, and of the land that has been bought and distributed, which is 750,000 hectares."
