# BPool Opens Base in Europe and Already Derives Half of Revenue from Outside Brazil

Growing outside Brazil continues to be more of an exception than a rule for most Brazilian startups. Tax issues, legal structure, exchange rates, and access to global clients usually limit international expansion. Few manage to break through these barriers — although the number of those who venture out has grown.

This is the case with BPool, a platform that connects major advertisers with creative companies and has clients such as Kenvue, Dell, Unilever, Telefónica, Danone, L'Oréal, Reckitt, XP, Novartis, and Casas Bahia, founded by Beto Sirotsky and Daniel Prianti. The startup, which counts Chromo Invest and Quartz Investimentos among its investors, has just opened an office in Ireland to advance into Europe, plans to strengthen its operations in the United States, and consolidate its presence in Latin America.

"Today practically 50% of our revenue comes from outside Brazil," says Beto Sirotsky, co-founder and co-CEO of BPool, to NeoFeed. In March, the estimate is that annualized revenue represents R$ 300 million.

Unlike startups that try to internationalize through commercial offices or local sales channels, BPool followed a different path. The company began operating outside Brazil through multinational clients who already used the platform in Brazil and decided to replicate the model in other geographies.

"Our clients themselves took us to other markets," says Sirotsky. "The platform starts being used in a country and, when the model works, that multinational tends to expand its use to other regions where it operates."

This movement led to BPool's presence in countries such as Mexico, Argentina, Chile, Colombia, and Ecuador, in addition to Uruguay, which functioned as a regional base for Latin America. Today, according to the company, Mexico is BPool's second-largest market, behind only Brazil. In the United States, operations began to scale throughout 2024 and 2025, with teams dedicated to serving local clients. In Europe, the process is more recent, with contracts still in the initial phase.

"The structure in Ireland was created with the objective of serving the European market, functioning as a starting point for contracts on the continent," says Sirotsky.

The decision to operate in a new country follows three main criteria. The first involves legislation, tax structure, and regulatory viability. The second is the formation of a local supplier base. The third is the existence of clients willing to concentrate hiring through the platform.

"When we start a new market, we first look at legislation, then at the supplier network, and finally at the client," says Sirotsky.

Part of the supplier base is formed with support from local partners. Another part comes from the clients themselves, who migrate already-contracted suppliers into the platform. The company states that expansion occurs without the need for large physical structures.

"There is no need to have a traditional office in every country. It's a growth model that is lighter from an operational perspective," says the co-founder.

When business volume increases, BPool begins to open local companies, controlled by the Brazilian holding company, to make operations viable.

So far, BPool's international growth has been bottom-up, starting with local operations of multinationals. According to the founders, the company is now entering a new phase, with a focus on negotiating regional and global contracts directly with the headquarters of these companies.

"With six years of operation and a consolidated customer base, we are now also acting in a top-down manner, talking with global headquarters to structure broader contracts," says Daniel Prianti, co-founder and co-CEO of the company.

BPool's proposal is to centralize the hiring of marketing and events services — a category that, according to the company, tends to be fragmented in large organizations, with multiple suppliers, contracts, and payments distributed across different areas and countries.

## BPay Gains Weight in Revenue

Beyond geographic expansion, BPool has registered growth in a new revenue stream linked to the financial sector. BPay, a module created to centralize payments, compliance, and financial advances for suppliers, accounted for approximately 20% of the company's transactions in the first quarter of 2026, according to data disclosed by the company.

The product was developed to serve both clients and suppliers, standardizing financial flows and reducing the need for individual approvals in each country.

"The fiscal, financial, and contractual differences between countries have always been a relevant challenge for internationalization. BPay is consolidated in Brazil and in the internationalization process, it is a solution to deal with this complexity," says Prianti.
