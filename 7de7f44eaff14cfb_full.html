# Techint and Mindlin lose: key gas pipeline awarded to Víctor Contreras and Italian SICIM

The Southern Energy consortium, in charge of Argentina's first large-scale Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) export project, has awarded the construction of the dedicated gas pipeline to Argentine company Víctor Contreras and Italian firm SICIM. Additionally, it has contracted Oilfield Production Services (OPS) for the installation of a compressor plant.

In this way, Techint has lost one of the year's most important contracts, following its elimination in early 2026 from pipe manufacturing to Indian company Welspun. The pipes for the Southern Energy (SESA) gas pipeline will be from Welspun, with construction assigned to Víctor Contreras and SICIM.

According to Clarín, the construction companies Víctor Contreras and SICIM won the 3 main sections covering 471 kilometers with an offer close to $530 million, while OPS secured the compressor plant for approximately $95 million. The constructors from Paolo Rocca and the Mindlin family were left out, as was the consortium of American company Pumpco, Italian Bonatti, and local Contreras Hermanos.

**The year's major project: the "dedicated" pipeline**

Along with other works not included in this tender, the complete gas pipeline project for LNG exports has a cost close to $1.3 billion. The pipeline, tendered by the San Matías Pipeline investment vehicle, will run from Tratayén in Neuquén—in the "heart" of Vaca Muerta—to Golfo San Matías in Río Negro, with a natural gas transport capacity of 27 million m³ per day (MMm³/d).

The proposal from the Temporary Joint Venture (UTE) of Víctor Contreras and SICIM was 15% cheaper—with about $80 million difference—than the Techint-Sacde consortium offer.

Rocca and Mindlin's construction companies built the Vaca Muerta gas pipeline in 2023, expanded Oleoductos del Valle (Oldelval) in 2024, and are finishing the Vaca Muerta Oil Sur (VMOS) pipeline this year, but now they have lost this continuation of business. Meanwhile, Pumpco, owned by the Mas brothers—owners of Inter Miami FC—added another defeat in oil tenders and continues unable to enter Argentina. It had previously lost bids for the Northern gas pipeline and the VMOS.

OPS—owned by businessman Carlos Pérez, owner of Río Negro newspaper and with growing business ventures in Neuquén—beat Sacde itself, BTU, Pecom (of the Perez Companc family), and constructor Contreras Hermanos for the compressor plant. The compressor plant will be installed at kilometer 80 of the pipeline with 46,000 HP of power.

**Southern Energy (SESA) consortium structure**

SESA is a consortium formed by Pan American Energy (PAE, of the Bulgheroni family) at 30%; YPF at 25%; Pampa Energía (of the Mindlin family and partners) at 20%; British Harbour Energy (with German capital) at 15%; and Norwegian Golar LNG, owner of the vessels, at 10%. The company has legal protection under the Large Investment Incentive Regime (RIGI), with disbursements reaching $15 billion over the coming decades. Of these, $2.441 billion will be allocated through April 2027.

Construction will begin mid-year and must be completed shortly before mid-2028, when the MK II liquefaction ship—currently being converted in China—arrives at Río Negro's shores. OPS will build the compressor plant to "push" the gas through the pipeline.

Previously, starting spring 2027, SESA will sell liquefied gas equivalent to 2.45 million metric tons per year (MTPA) or about 11.5 million m³ daily of natural gas through the Hilli Episeyo vessel, which is completing a contract in Cameroon this year before heading to a Singapore shipyard for preparation.

With both ships in operation, liquefaction capacity will reach 6 MTPA—about 27 MMm³/d, equivalent to 20% of current average national production. Thus, the foreign sales business will add around $2.5 billion annually.

The PAE-led consortium already secured an LNG export contract with German public company SEFE for 2 MTPA over 8 years. It continues negotiating with potential clients including Brazil, China, India, South Korea, Japan, and Argentina itself during winter demand peaks.

**Techint wins lawsuit against the State**

Not all news was bad for Techint. This week, the courts sided with Tecpetrol, the group's oil company, in its lawsuit against the national state over gas subsidies committed in Resolution 46/2017 of the Energy Secretariat and unfulfilled even during Mauricio Macri's government. Judge Enrique Lavié Pico ordered the State to pay approximately $2.5 billion plus interest from 2018 for gas from Fortín de Piedra, a Vaca Muerta area where Rocca's group made a rapid $1.7 billion investment, leveraged on promises of differential prices between market rates and a cap of $7.50 per million BTU. As internal gas market prices dropped significantly due to oversupply, the Macri government soon realized it would lack funds and changed conditions. Seven years later, the courts upheld Techint's claim, though the State can appeal in higher instances.
