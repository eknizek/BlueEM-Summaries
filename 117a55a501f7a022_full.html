# Exxon sees 'positive' scenario and reconsiders investing in Venezuela, according to CEO

Exxon Mobil is seriously considering the possibility of returning to invest in Venezuela just a few months after CEO Darren Woods told President Donald Trump that the South American country's oil sector was "impossible to invest in." Recently, Venezuela altered energy regulations and contract terms to make them more attractive to foreign companies after the Trump administration captured former leader Nicolas Maduro in January and took control of the country's crude oil exports.

"Venezuela is a huge resource that is now opening up more freely to the world," Woods said in a conference call with analysts on Friday. "I feel positive about what is happening, about the opportunity that exists there."

Exxon has a long history in Venezuela, but had its operations nationalized twice—first in the 1970s and then under President Hugo Chávez in the mid-2000s.

In a meeting at the White House earlier this year, Woods told Trump that the country was "impossible to invest in," which led the president to characterize Exxon as "too cute." The recent changes in Venezuelan leadership could create "attractive investment opportunities," Woods said. He emphasized Exxon's long history in producing heavy oil, which makes up the majority of Venezuela's reserves. The company sent a team to the country in recent weeks to assess the situation.

Beyond Venezuela hosting the world's largest oil reserves, U.S. Gulf Coast refineries highly value its crude oil.

"There is more work to be done, but I think we will be in a unique position and play an important role in bringing those barrels to market," Woods said.

Rival Chevron is the largest foreign oil producer in Venezuela after deciding to remain in the country despite Chávez's nationalizations.

Venezuela owed Chevron approximately $1.5 billion at the end of 2025. Higher oil prices mean this should be fully paid by 2027, said CEO Mike Wirth on Friday. At that point, Chevron would consider further investments in the country, where it currently produces about 250,000 barrels per day, he said.

"I think, in any scenario, we continue to be the incumbent with an advantage," Wirth said.
