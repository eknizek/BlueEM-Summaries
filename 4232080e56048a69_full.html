# Small Caps vs. Ibovespa: Foreign Flows Create "Two Stock Markets" in Brazil

It is common for a liquidity premium to exist in the market, but the external flow that has flooded the Brazilian stock exchange in recent months has widened the gap between Ibovespa stocks and small caps — both in terms of performance and valuation. This difference began to increase in 2021. The rise in the Selic rate in the aftermath of the pandemic penalized the stocks of retailers and other companies sensitive to interest rates — many of them small caps. But this year, the movement has intensified, with B3 receiving R$ 65 billion in external investments — resources that were directed basically to the most liquid stocks, either directly or via ETFs. Meanwhile, domestic investors are selling. Local institutional investors withdrew approximately R$ 50 billion from B3 this year.

"The combination of foreigners buying and locals selling causes this distortion," an equity manager told Brazil Journal. "Those who buy small caps are dedicated funds and local investors, and these investors are waiting for a clearer scenario regarding the economy and interest rates to invest."

Furthermore, the appreciation of oil stocks — due to price increases from the war — also helped the Ibovespa. Petrobras and PRIO shares, which together account for about 15% of the index, rose around 50% since January.

The conclusion: while the Ibovespa is at historic highs, SMAL11, the ETF that tracks B3's small caps index, is 20% below the peak reached in mid-2021. The Ibovespa has risen 20% since the beginning of the year, 46% in 12 months, and 60% since 2021. Meanwhile, SMAL11 appreciated only 9% in 2026, 22% in one year — and fell 14% over five years.

There are differences even among companies within the Ibovespa itself, with the most liquid stocks rising more than other stocks, despite no change in fundamentals that would justify superior performance of the index's main weights. The Ibovespa multiple stands at 10.6x earnings for the next 12 months, slightly above the 10.3x average of the last ten years, according to XP. Small caps, meanwhile, trade at a 10% discount to the average, at 9.3x earnings.

It is possible that small caps will return to historical valuation levels, but it is still early to bet on this convergence trade, said Fernando Ferreira, chief strategist at XP. "This should happen at some point. The question is when," Ferreira said. "The catch-up of small caps depends on interest rates falling, because many of these companies depend on the domestic economy."

Furthermore, the recovery — when and if it happens — will not necessarily lift all discounted companies back to the average. "Many retailers, for example, should not grow as they did in the past, with competition from e-commerce and sales on social media. The P/E could rise around 40%, but I find it difficult to double to return to the historical average," says a manager who has invested in the sector for years.

Regarding the small caps index, the recovery may be more complicated due to the "horrible methodology" of the indicator, said Christian Keleti, founder of AlphaKey. The index brings together companies with low market capitalization and thinly traded stocks — which means that companies going through a difficult time become part of the indicator (this happened recently with Hapvida and Natura). "At the same time, when they grow above a certain level, they need to leave the portfolio, which prevents an investor from fully taking advantage of a company's recovery cycle," Keleti said.
