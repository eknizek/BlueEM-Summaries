04 May 2026 · 10:45 PM

# Colombian Labor Ministry Uncovers 200+ Cases of Sexual Harassment in Media Sector

Inspections carried out by the Colombian Labor Ministry at several media outlets identified at least 200 cases of workplace and sexual harassment, amid recent allegations that have shaken the country's journalism and television sector. The findings were disclosed by the "Yo Te Creo Colega" (I Believe You, Colleague) movement and correspond to visits made between March and April to different newsrooms, including those of Caracol Television, Canal RCN, RTVC Sistema de Medios Públicos, Canal Capital, Canal 13, and Red+ Noticias.

According to the report, the inspections documented 142 cases related to workplace harassment and another 58 associated with sexual harassment situations within different workspaces in national media organizations. The report comes amid growing discussion about working conditions in newsrooms and allegations of abuse of power, gender violence, and institutional silence in the communications sector.

## Caracol and RCN concentrate the most harassment complaints

According to the released assessment, Caracol Television was the media outlet with the highest number of reports registered during inspections carried out by the Labor Ministry. In total, the channel accumulated 78 cases, of which 64 corresponded to workplace harassment situations and another 14 to alleged sexual harassment incidents within the organization.

Canal RCN appeared in second place with 52 cases reported, distributed among 39 workplace harassment situations and 13 of a sexual nature. Behind these were Red+ Noticias with 24 cases, RTVC Sistema de Medios Públicos with 20 reports, Canal Capital with 16, and Canal 13 with a total of 10 situations identified during official visits.

The Labor Ministry warned in its conclusions about the existence of a "repeated pattern of violence or harassment" within the inspected organizations, despite several companies formally having internal response protocols. However, the report stated that these mechanisms "do not function" adequately to prevent, process, or sanction cases reported within the newsrooms and work environments evaluated by authorities.

## Ministry warns of failures in harassment complaint protocols

As part of the inspection process, the Ministry installed QR codes at media outlets' headquarters to facilitate complaints and allow workers to report situations more confidentially. This tool made it possible to identify cases that, according to the document, remained hidden or had never been formally processed through channels established by media companies.

Among the main failures detected, the report noted that some cases of sexual violence ended up being treated institutionally as "coexistence conflicts" or simple psychosocial problems within work teams. Additionally, the Ministry warned about processes closed through apologies, internal agreements, workshops, or commitments between parties, despite these mechanisms "not substituting investigation by competent authorities."

The labor agency also identified problems of "lack of traceability" in complaints, which makes it difficult to follow up on cases and establish clear responsibilities within inspected organizations.

"Cases are evident in which situations with sexual connotations, gender violence, or physical aggression can be processed as simple coexistence conflicts, psychosocial matters, or reconcilable scenarios," the report stated. The document added that this situation "can lead to insufficient or inadequate institutional responses," especially regarding complaints related to gender violence and sexual harassment within journalistic work spaces.

## Yo Te Creo Colega reveals over 260 testimonies of abuse in newsrooms

The Ministry's report coincided with another report recently released by the "Yo Te Creo Colega" movement, promoted by journalists Juanita Gómez, Paula Bolívar, Laura Palomino, Catalina Botero, and Mónica Rodríguez. That document compiled more than 260 testimonies from workers and journalists describing alleged systematic patterns of abuse of power, institutional silence, and normalization of various forms of harassment in Colombian media.

The investigation also warned about possible underreporting of cases, as many situations never reach formal channels due to fear of workplace retaliation or distrust of institutions responsible for investigating. The report further stated that in some scenarios, adopted measures end up shifting the problem to victims, while labor and hierarchical structures within newsrooms remain without substantial changes.
