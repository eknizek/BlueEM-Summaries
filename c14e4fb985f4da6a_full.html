# Cimed hits R$1 billion quarterly revenue: 'we want to be Brazil's P&G,' says CEO

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "Revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold triple the number of items. This figure is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years — investments and brands. In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and managed to grow by reducing production volume for the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

However, it is in consumer goods where the company sees greater diversification of audience, from low to high income, and of margins — it has already entered the segments of children's hygiene, oral hygiene, deodorants, and will now launch a supplement line called Urso. "We want to be Brazil's P&G," affirms Cimed's CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand — such as hyaluronic acid — which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and targets R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the end of the year, launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted through marketing on reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed completed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing Super items but with intermediary costs — owning production saves 25% in costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$600 million. The consumer performance helped Cimed achieve a record-breaking first quarter, with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and a 26% increase in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharmaceuticals. Marques calls attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition — gaining market share even at the expense of part of profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with product trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if you get an allergy? Nobody will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty through quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

The entry into products with recurring use did not come without challenges. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorants and toothpaste rotate every two days in some sales points. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock shortages than sales," recounts Marques, who adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory. The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "They are completely different businesses. We're discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," says the CEO.

The company projects revenue of R$4.8 billion in 2026 and targets reaching R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then — those with potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Cimed Generics, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth could be Urso or Milimetric.
