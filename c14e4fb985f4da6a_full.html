# Cimed Posts R$1 Billion in Quarterly Sales: 'We Want to Be the Brazilian P&G,' Says CEO

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, showing Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "The revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold three times more items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years, gaining momentum through investments and brands.

In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing production volume for the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

It is in the consumer segment, however, that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and margins. It has already entered the segment of children's hygiene, oral hygiene, deodorants, and will now launch a line of supplements called Urso.

"We want to be the Brazilian P&G," affirms Cimed's CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of its Carmed line, which became a sales phenomenon with its lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand featuring hyaluronic acid, which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and is targeting R$300 million this year.

The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the turn of the year, launched the Super brand of deodorant and oral hygiene products, which was boosted by marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super products but was charging as an intermediary. Owning production saves 25% in costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$600 million.

The performance in consumer products helped Cimed achieve record first-quarter growth, with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and 26% growth in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer products represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharmaceuticals. Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because it's a relevant factor—gaining market share even at the cost of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with product trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if you get an allergy? Nobody will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty through quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

The entry into recurrent-use products did not come without challenges. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some retail locations. "We had higher demand than our capacity, with more stock ruptures than sales," Marques recounts. He adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "They are completely different businesses. We are discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," the CEO notes.

The company projects revenue of R$4.8 billion in 2026 and has set a target of reaching R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Generic Cimed, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth could be Urso or Milimetric.
