# Cimed reports R$1 billion quarterly revenue; CEO states ambition to become 'Brazilian P&G'

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "Revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold triple the number of items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years and which has begun to gain momentum—investments and brands.

In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has grown by reducing production volume aimed at the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

It is in the consumer segment, however, that the company sees greater diversification of customers, from low to high income, and margins. It has already entered the infant hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorant segments and will now launch a supplements line called Urso.

"We want to be the Brazilian P&G," states Cimed's CEO.

The company was encouraged by the performance of Carmed product lines, which became a sales phenomenon with lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand—such as hyaluronic acid—which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and targets R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the beginning of the year, launched the Super brand for deodorant and oral hygiene, which was boosted by marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing Super items but charging intermediary fees—owning production saves 25% in costs, the CEO states. Super generated R$150 million in the period, which projects annual revenue of R$600 million.

Consumer performance helped Cimed achieve record first-quarter growth for the period, with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and 26% growth in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer accounted for 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharmaceuticals.

Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition—gaining market share even at the expense of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the efficacy, if you get an allergy? No one will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty through quality," he says. "I don't win on the first sale. I win on repurchase."

The entry into products with recurring use did not come without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some sales points. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock ruptures than sales," Marques recounts, adding that he adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "These are completely different businesses. We are discussing whether a spin-off would make sense," the CEO says.

The company projects R$4.8 billion in revenue for 2026 and aims to reach R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Cimed Generic, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth, perhaps, could be Urso or Milimetric.
