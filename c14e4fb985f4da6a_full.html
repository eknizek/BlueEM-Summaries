# Cimed makes R$ 1 billion in the quarter: 'we want to be the Brazilian P&G', says CEO

João and Maria, Super and Milimetric fill the pharmaceutical company's consumer portfolio

By Maria Luíza Filgueiras — São Paulo, April 10, 2026

CEO João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "The revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$ 400 million, but Cimed sold three times as many items. This figure is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the last two years and which has begun to gain momentum — investments and brands.

In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing the volume of production aimed at the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

But it is in consumer goods that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and margins — it has already entered the infant hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorant segments, and will now launch a supplement line called Urso. "We want to be the Brazilian P&G," says Cimed's CEO.

The company was encouraged by the performance of the Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand — such as hyaluronic acid — which made R$ 140 million in revenue last year and aims for R$ 300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the turn of the year, launched the Super brand of deodorant and oral hygiene, which was boosted with marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but with an intermediary fee — owning production saves 25% in costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$ 150 million in the period, which projects annual revenue of R$ 600 million.

Performance in consumer goods helped Cimed achieve record first-quarter growth, with R$ 1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and 26% growth in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer goods represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharmaceuticals.

Marques calls attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition — gaining market share even at the cost of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if you get an allergy? Nobody will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we gain loyalty through quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

The entry into recurring-use products did not come without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy pace, where restocking happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some retail locations. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock shortages than sales," says Marques, who adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The expansion of consumer goods has made Cimed discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "They are completely different businesses. We are discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," says the CEO.

The company projects revenue of R$ 4.8 billion in 2026 and has the goal of reaching R$ 10 billion by 2030. For this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then — those with the potential to generate more than R$ 1 billion each. Five are already defined: Generic Cimed, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. A sixth could possibly be Urso or Milimetric.
