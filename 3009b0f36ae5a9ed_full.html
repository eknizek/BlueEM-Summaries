Lula should prioritize economic agenda with electoral impact after Messias veto; Central Bank to remain understaffed

# Lula Should Prioritize Economic Agenda with Electoral Impact After Messias Veto; Central Bank to Remain Understaffed

BRASÍLIA - The rejection of Jorge Messias's nomination for a Supreme Court (STF) vacancy on Thursday, April 29, by the Federal Senate should lead the Lula government to screen the economic agenda that can still be approved this year, before October elections.

According to PT parliamentarians heard by Estadão, the priority now is to secure approval of proposals that have positive electoral impact, such as the project aimed at mitigating the effects of the Iran war on fuel prices, in addition to the proposal that eliminates the 6x1 work shift and reduces working hours.

With this in mind, the nomination of names for the two open Central Bank positions that need Senate approval may be postponed until after the elections. Another project with little appeal for the government is the Constitutional Amendment (PEC) on the Central Bank's financial autonomy and the proposal that gives more tools to the monetary authority to prevent cases like the Master situation. The understanding among PT leadership is that this project places the Treasury as the last guarantor for bank failures, and this needs to be corrected.

On the other hand, senators heard by Estadão understand there is no connection between the Messias veto and the nominations for Central Bank directors.

## Central Bank Will Remain "Understaffed"

Within the government, there was already an alert and suggestions that President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva not send the Central Bank names until May. The perception was that it was necessary to monitor how the Messias nomination would unfold, which went wrong for the government. Therefore, the issue ceased to be a priority in the view of federal government political coordinators.

Now, the directive is that there should be no rush, even though the Central Bank leadership is understaffed, with two directors accumulating positions. In yesterday's decision on interest rate cuts to 14.50% per year, only six of the nine members of the Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) participated in the process—director Rodrigo Teixeira was absent due to bereavement.

A government member who spoke to Estadão under anonymity described the relationship between the Senate and the Executive as "the Strait of Hormuz."

At the end of 2025, Central Bank directors Diogo Guillen, of Economic Policy, and Renato Gomes Brito, of Financial System Organization, ended their terms. As a result, their positions were accumulated by directors Paulo Picchetti, of International Affairs (who also took on Economic Policy), and Gilneu Vivan, of Regulation (who took on Organization).

In the Senate, however, even opposition parliamentarians attempt to separate the two agendas. The understanding is that the economic agenda operates in parallel to the STF nomination, and that the reasons for rejecting Messias have no correlation with the Central Bank vacancies.

According to Senator Efraim Filho (PL-PB), one of the names closest to Senate President Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP), the Senate still awaits the submission of Central Bank names so that proceedings can occur on technical grounds.

"These are completely different situations. The Senate will have the capacity to differentiate what is the economic agenda from what was the vote for this STF vacancy. I don't see one thing contaminating the other," he explained.

For Efraim, the rejection of Messias stems from the disarticulation of the government base, and also from the perception that this STF vacancy belongs to the president elected in October, not to President Lula's current term.

"Lula has already nominated two names from his government for the STF and this vacancy opened because of Minister Barroso's early retirement. Therefore, the understanding is that it falls to the next elected president to nominate this name," he stated.

The view is similar to that of Senator Alessandro Vieira (MDB-SE), who sees the agendas as "different." He views Messias's defeat as a majority of occasion, resulting from a combination of interests involving Alcolumbre, but also Justices Alexandre de Moraes and Flávio Dino from the STF itself.

"These are different agendas. Yesterday's defeat does not signal the construction of a solid and empowered opposition. It signals a majority of occasion enabled by the alignment of interests among such distinct figures as Alcolumbre, Moraes, and Dino. The opposition enters in the third position on this list of relevant actors for the government's defeat."

Senator Sérgio Moro (PL-PR) also views Messias's rejection as an event with its own circumstances, with no current relation to Central Bank nominations.

"I saw no conversation about this regarding the Central Bank. In my understanding, there would be no reason to disapprove. What happened with Minister Messias was a Senate response to political nominations for the STF that have caused the court to distance itself from its constitutional functions," Moro explained.

In the STF case, nominations go through the Constitutional and Justice Committee (CCJ), but for the Central Bank, the Economic Affairs Committee (CAE) is responsible for the hearing before the plenary vote.

## PEC on Autonomy

On the Central Bank side, the most likely scenario is that the institution's leadership will concentrate its efforts in the coming days to put the Constitutional Amendment that grants the agency administrative, budgetary, and financial autonomy (PEC 65) to a vote.

The text still has government members opposed to some points, but the expectation is that there will be sufficient votes for approval after Central Bank President Gabriel Galípolo requested help from parliamentarians. PEC 65 is in the CCJ and committee senators received a message from the Central Bank this week requesting the matter be scheduled and approved.

On Thursday morning, there was optimism that Messias would be well-received by senators. Parliamentarians spoke of 45 or 46 favorable votes, while government members bet on 48. However, after lunch, the mood began to "sour," and a recount indicated that the Attorney General would have only 35 votes—it was 34.

What the government does not want is to go through such a stressful moment in a Central Bank vote, with little room for maneuver and time to reverse the situation.

"We had betrayal, we had surprise and we cannot go through this again without having everything well-oiled," commented another source. This interlocutor stressed that the government's map was poorly made. This is especially because, as the saying goes within the Senate—a very different picture from the Chamber—the result of every vote in the Lower House is calculated before it occurs. What surprised yesterday is that the government didn't know this.
