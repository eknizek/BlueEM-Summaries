The future of restructurings is in extrajudicial recovery?

30 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# The Future of Restructurings is in Extrajudicial Recovery?

The Brazilian corporate restructuring market is undergoing a profound transformation. The era of judicial recovery as the sole exit from financial crisis appears to be giving way to movements that offer a broader range of possibilities. The market has matured, and with it, the perception that the judiciary should be a last resort, not the first.

At negotiating tables where the fate of debts and assets is discussed with creditors and debtors, the premise is clear: judicial recovery can be very costly for everyone, depending on the case, and extrajudicial recovery emerges as a strategy to be discussed, even as a substitute for the well-known preparatory injunction.

The initial urgency of any debtor is to obtain a stay period that allows the company to negotiate with its creditors without suffering restrictive measures and attacks on cash and assets. On the creditors' side, there is often willingness to sign a standstill agreement, but the premise is that creditors in similar conditions remain pari passu—that is, there is only effective granting of negotiation time while other creditors are equally willing or obligated to make the same concessions.

It is precisely in this scenario that the preparatory injunction established by Law No. 14,112 of 2020 had been widely used as a way to obtain, even if forcefully, a temporary suspension that would allow negotiations in an environment protecting the debtor's assets. However, the preparatory injunction brought with it a series of uncertainties: from the documentation necessary to support the request, to the scope of the measure, to the need for confidentiality (to avoid any situation of fragility between the filing date and the granting of the measure), and even regarding creditors subject to it.

Furthermore, once the measure is triggered and a mediation procedure is instituted, the debtor obtains temporary protection for a period of 60 days. If it is not possible to reach an agreement with its main creditors during this interval, judicial recovery becomes the next step, since there is no consensus on the possibility of extending the preparatory injunction deadline.

Extrajudicial recovery, meanwhile, while requiring prior adhesion of at least one-third of the credits covered by a previously negotiated plan, already allows obtaining an automatic stay period that applies from the filing of the recovery request—not only for signatories, but for all those covered by the plan. Thus, once this minimum quorum of one-third is obtained, the debtor will already have 90 days, not just the 60 of the preparatory injunction, to complete the quorum of adhesion from more than half of the credits.

Other advantages of extrajudicial recovery include the speed of the procedure, though this aspect can be impacted by the presentation of an updated version of the plan, greater flexibility in organizing creditors and preparing payment proposals, and the absence of credit verification procedures. There is also no provision for the appointment of a judicial administrator, although in cases of greater relevance, such appointment has been frequent. All these aspects contribute to a less costly and more predictable procedure, at least compared to judicial recovery.

Nonetheless, extrajudicial recovery also brings certain limitations, such as the absence of DIP financing provisions and specific consolidation rules (which govern recovery requests by more than one company in the same economic group).

Furthermore, asset sales in the form of isolated productive units do not have the same succession exemption provisions found in judicial recovery rules, although there is clarity regarding the future preservation of operations conducted based on approved and homologated plans. Another relevant aspect is the tax issue, with debate already underway on the possibility of extending the judicial recovery tax settlement rules to extrajudicial recovery.

Recent cases, such as GPA and Raízen, demonstrate that extrajudicial recovery is an institute in frank development, which could be useful for obtaining an initial 90-day stay period among all creditors covered, allowing for a properly structured plan with payment terms and conditions and greater detail of recovery measures to be approved and homologated as negotiations evolve.

Brazil is in a clear movement toward maturation in the use of innovative strategies to restructure companies. The country ended 2025 with 5,680 companies in judicial recovery, a growth of 24.3% compared to the end of 2024. Agribusiness was one of the highlights of this movement: the number of sector companies in recovery grew 67%, reaching 493 companies, which represents about 8.7% of the total number of companies undergoing restructuring in the country. The data comes from the RGF Judicial Recovery Monitor and were published by Valor.

This increase is not merely a reflection of the challenging business moment (an environment of prolonged high interest rates and more restricted credit), but of a quality migration. Today's entrepreneur has viable alternatives for renegotiating their debts. He now anticipates the solution. The strategy of using extrajudicial recovery to obtain a suspension of executions while adjusting the definitive plan should be celebrated.
