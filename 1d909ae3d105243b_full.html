Is the future of restructurings in extrajudicial recovery?

04 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Is the future of restructurings in extrajudicial recovery?

The Brazilian corporate restructuring market is undergoing a profound metamorphosis. The era of judicial recovery as the sole solution for financial crisis appears to be giving way to movements that offer a broader range of possibilities. The market has matured, and with it, the perception that the Judiciary should be the last resort, not the first.

At the negotiating tables where the fate of debts and assets is discussed between creditors and debtors, the premise is clear: judicial recovery can be very costly for everyone, depending on the case, and extrajudicial recovery emerges as a strategy to be discussed, even as a substitute for the famous preparatory injunction.

The initial urgency of any debtor is to obtain a stay period that allows the company to negotiate with its creditors without suffering restrictive measures and attacks on cash and assets. On the creditors' side, there is often willingness to sign a standstill agreement, but the premise is that creditors in similar conditions remain pari passu, meaning there is effective grant of negotiation time only while other creditors are equally willing or obligated to make the same concessions.

It is precisely in this scenario that the preparatory injunction established by Law No. 14,112 of 2020 had been widely used as a way to obtain, albeit forcibly, a temporary suspension that would allow negotiations in an environment protecting the debtor's assets. However, the preparatory injunction brought with it a series of uncertainties: from the documentation necessary to support the request, to the scope of the measure, the need for confidentiality (to avoid any weakness between the filing date and the granting of the measure), and even the creditors subject to it.

Furthermore, once the measure is triggered and a mediation procedure is initiated, the debtor obtains temporary protection for a period of 60 days. If it is not possible to reach an agreement with its main creditors within this timeframe, judicial recovery becomes the next step, since there is no consensus on the possibility of extending the preparatory injunction period.

Extrajudicial recovery, while requiring prior adhesion of at least one-third of the credits covered by a previously negotiated plan, already allows for obtaining an automatic stay period that runs from the filing of the recovery request – not only for signatories, but for all those covered by the plan. Thus, once this minimum quorum of one-third is obtained, the debtor will have 90 days, not just the 60 of the preparatory injunction, to complete the adhesion quorum of more than half of the credits.

Other advantages of extrajudicial recovery are the speed of the procedure, although this aspect may be impacted by the presentation of an updated version of the Plan, greater flexibility in organizing creditors and preparing payment proposals, and the absence of credit verification procedures. There is also no provision for appointing a judicial administrator, although in cases of greater relevance, this appointment has been frequent. All these aspects contribute to a less costly and more predictable procedure, at least in comparison with judicial recovery.

In any case, extrajudicial recovery also has limitations, such as the absence of DIP financing provisions and specific consolidation rules (which govern recovery requests by more than one company in the same economic group). Additionally, sales of assets in the form of isolated productive units do not have the same succession-free provisions found in judicial recovery regulations, although there is clarity regarding future preservation of operations concluded based on approved and homologated plans.

Another relevant aspect is tax treatment, already subject to debate is the possibility of extending judicial recovery tax settlement rules to extrajudicial recovery. Recent cases, such as GPA and Raízen, demonstrate that extrajudicial recovery is an institute in frank development, which could be useful for obtaining an initial 90-day stay period among all creditors covered, allowing that, as negotiations evolve, a proper plan, with terms and conditions of payments and greater detail of recovery measures, be approved and homologated.

Brazil is in a clear movement of maturation in the use of innovative strategies to restructure companies. The country ended 2025 with 5,680 companies in judicial recovery, a 24.3% increase compared to the end of 2024. Agribusiness was one of the highlights of this movement: the number of companies in the sector in recovery grew 67%, reaching 493 companies, which represents approximately 8.7% of the total number of companies in restructuring processes in the country.

This increase is not merely a reflection of the challenging business moment (environment of prolonged high interest rates and more restricted credit), but of quality migration. The businessman today has viable alternatives for renegotiating his debts. He now anticipates the solution. The strategy of using extrajudicial recovery to obtain a suspension of executions while adjusting the definitive plan should be celebrated.
