Government Retreats on App Regulation Support, Congressional Vote at Risk

# Government Retreats on App Regulation Support, Congressional Vote at Risk

The government of Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) has withdrawn its support for the bill regulating deliveries and rides through applications like iFood and Uber, jeopardizing a vote in the Chamber of Deputies originally scheduled for Tuesday and Wednesday (14th and 15th) of next week.

The new report by congressman Augusto Coutinho (Republicanos-PE) eliminates the minimum fare per trip for drivers and creates two models for delivery workers: one with basic compensation of R$ 8.50 and another with hourly payment. At the Palácio do Planalto, the diagnosis is that this version has attracted rejection on an agenda considered a priority in an election year to bring Lula closer to delivery workers—seen as predominantly Bolsonaro supporters—and has further disfigured the previous version of the text, leaving out the government's original demands on the topic.

However, the rejection is not unanimous. One faction of the government argues that the base should change position, understanding that Chamber president Hugo Motta (Republicanos-PB) has embraced the issue and intends to schedule it even without Planalto support. Opposition leaders, sensing that the agenda could attract rejection for Lula's government, are now evaluating supporting it. In the centrist bloc, the assessment is that some regulation should be voted on.

The rapporteur, Augusto Coutinho, has told interlocutors that if PT and the government continue wavering, he will defend that the project not be voted or go to the plenary as is, forcing the government base to attempt amendments through voting. On Wednesday (8th), the PT leader in the Chamber, Pedro Uczai (SC), indicated that the tendency of the caucus was to support the project and present amendments in the plenary. According to government sources, the assessment changed due to the poor reception of the proposal.

Even within the PT caucus, opposition to Coutinho's text is not unanimous. For some, Minister Guilherme Boulos, from the Secretary-General of the Presidency, created wear and tear for the government by insisting on a minimum rate of R$ 10 for deliveries and rides, handing the opposition a discourse that their delivery orders and rides would become more expensive.

The centrist assessment is that the government pulled the discussion, came to accept the central points of the proposal, and now retreated due to poor reception. The group considers that if the proposal is scheduled, the government will need to defend the R$ 10 minimum rate alone and suffer isolated potential wear and tear. For these parliamentarians, R$ 10 only makes sense in the city of São Paulo, Boulos's electoral base, where income levels and order values are higher. In other cities and even other capitals, the minimum freight could make orders unfeasible.

Boulos did say in early March, after a meeting with Coutinho and Chamber president Hugo Motta, that the government would insist on R$ 10 through a highlight in the plenary vote. The statement displeased the rapporteur and other deputies who had been participating in negotiations because this issue was considered settled by that point.

Last week, after Coutinho presented the new report, Boulos used his social networks to say the government opposes the latest version of the text. In justifying the opposition, the minister cites, besides the minimum rate, the removal of additional payments such as night shift and a benefit equivalent to a 13th monthly salary. The removal of these additions, however, was expected, as it had been announced in March after the meeting with Motta.

A technical note from the PT caucus also criticizes what parliamentarians consider to have been a change in the conceptual framework of the proposal. While the first version dealt with "labor relations," the new one refers to "intermediated autonomous work." For PT, the project insists on keeping away "any possibility of recognizing subordination and eventual formal employment relationship."

The exclusion of formal employment relationship provisions was a request from company representatives during negotiations but also accommodates delivery workers. A first regulation attempt faced difficulties at the Ministry of Labor and Employment because the working group was initially created only with union representatives, who demanded recognition of employment relationships with platforms. Leaders questioned union legitimacy and managed to be included in the discussion, as is the case with Anea (National Alliance of App Delivery Workers), a collective that defends maintaining the autonomous nature of work.

Despite the government's retreat, the main companies affected by regulation plan to defend the proposal's approval. The assessment is that the project, as is, will still cost companies dearly but is more reasonable than the previous version. According to the calendar set in recent days, the report will be analyzed in the special commission that discussed the subject on Tuesday and will go to the plenary on Wednesday.
