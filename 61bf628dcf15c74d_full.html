# In Germany, Lula criticizes 'false narratives' about Brazilian agribusiness

In Germany, where he opened the Hannover Messe, the world's leading industrial fair, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) stated this Sunday (19th) that the European Union insists on "false narratives" about Brazilian agribusiness, a sector that largely does not politically support the president.

Lula spoke at the event's opening ceremony, which practically begins on Monday (20th). Brazil is the honored country at the fair for the second time. The first time was in 1980, when Lula was imprisoned by the military regime for leading strikes in the ABC region, a movement that affected several German companies and catapulted the then-union leader to public prominence. "Exactly on April 19th," the president recalled, drawing applause from an audience of industrialists and executives.

The reference to agribusiness occurred when Lula complained about trade barriers imposed by the EU on Brazilian biofuels. "Brazil can help the European Union reduce energy costs and decarbonize things. For this, it is essential that the bloc's rules take into account the clean energy matrix used in our production processes," he declared.

"It is also necessary to combat false narratives about the sustainability of our agriculture," he emphasized. "Creating additional barriers to a biofuels agreement is counterproductive, both from an environmental and energy perspective," the president stated, referring to still nascent negotiations on the subject.

Received with military honors by Prime Minister Friedrich Merz, Lula recalled the oil shocks of the 1970s, which led Brazil to develop the Pro-Alcohol program, unprecedented in the scale it achieved at that time. "In 1980, at this same fair, in this same city, Volkswagen and Mercedes presented ethanol-powered engines," he recalled. The reference to the German automakers drew more applause.

Biofuels and Brazil's energy matrix, dominated by clean energy, led the ceremony's mentions alongside the EU-Mercosur agreement, which enters into force in May. "We have already adopted a 30% ethanol blend in gasoline and 15% in biodiesel. We produce biofuels sustainably, without compromising food cultivation or deforesting forests," the president said, touching on the historical root of European differences with the energy modality that Brazil sees as sustainable.

Biofuel imports in general are restricted in the bloc for environmental reasons. Similarly, the Soy Moratorium, now abandoned by traders operating in Brazil, was seen as a fundamental mechanism for protecting Brazilian biomes. Lula addressed the subject. "We have a government commitment that by 2030 we will reach zero deforestation. In the last three years, we reduced Amazon deforestation by 50% and cerrado deforestation by 32%," he enumerated.

On Monday (20th), Lula makes his inaugural fair visit alongside Merz. In the afternoon, after high-level meetings between the two countries, the Brazilian president travels to a private visit to Volkswagen headquarters in Wolfsburg, accompanied by three union leaders. The European visit, which began in Barcelona last week, ends on Tuesday with a stop in Lisbon.
