# Record Minimum Wage Does Not Break Employment in Colombia, But Raises Some Alerts

Against many predictions, the sharp increase in Colombia's minimum wage has not—at least so far—caused immediate deterioration in employment. This is according to a recent report from J.P. Morgan, which describes a surprisingly solid labor market, though with early signs of moderation.

"Job creation remains positive despite the unprecedented minimum wage increase, which underscores the resilience of the labor market," the bank states in its analysis.

The institution's assessment indicates that the Colombian labor market remains "tight" and resilient even after a historic wage increase. The unemployment rate, for example, remains near recent lows. In February, it fell to 8.4 percent, very close to the floor of 8.3 percent observed in November 2025.

However, the report introduces some alerts that cannot be overlooked: while employment continues to grow, it is doing so at a slower pace. "Job creation remains positive, although it is decelerating at the margin," the document warns.

This moderation is also reflected in recent dynamics: employment growth lost momentum compared to late 2025, suggesting that the adjustment could be just beginning.

Another key point is informality. Despite fears that a higher minimum wage would drive informal employment, there is no clear evidence of this so far. "The informality rate remains around 55 percent, with no clear signs of an increase in informal employment thus far," J.P. Morgan notes. That "thus far," however, leaves open the possibility of lagged effects.

Similarly, the report notes a direct impact on incomes. "Real wage growth has accelerated, with early transmission—though below average—of the minimum wage increase," the report explains. In fact, real wages grew around 5.5 percent annually in January, which could become a significant boost to consumption in the coming months.

Moreover, employment growth is not concentrated solely in the public sector. Sectors such as manufacturing, construction, entertainment, and communications are also generating jobs, suggesting a relatively broad base.

Still, structural weaknesses persist. Labor force participation remains below historical levels, which could limit medium-term economic growth. According to J.P. Morgan, the message is one of cautious optimism: "The Colombian labor market remains notably tight, showing resilience despite the unprecedented minimum wage increase."

The question now is whether that resilience will be maintained or if the effects of the wage increase will ultimately be reflected more strongly in the coming months, analysts at the institution indicated.
