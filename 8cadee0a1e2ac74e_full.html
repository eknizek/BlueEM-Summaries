Brazil's CVM Portrait: More Investigations, Fewer Judgments, and a Paralyzed Regulator

09 April 2026 · 09:44 PM

# Brazil's CVM Portrait: More Investigations, Fewer Judgments, and a Paralyzed Regulator

The volume of judgments at the Brazilian Securities Commission (CVM) has not kept pace with the growth in investigations opened by the authority responsible for overseeing and developing the capital markets. In 2025, the CVM initiated 95 administrative sanctioning proceedings (PAS), a 25% increase compared to the previous year. However, the board judged only 49 cases, a 48% decline compared to 2024.

Administrative sanctioning proceedings are the final stage of CVM investigations, initiated when the technical area believes there are sufficient elements to formally accuse market participants, ranging from asset managers and funds to individuals and consultants.

Data released in the CVM's annual report on Thursday, April 9, also indicate a decline in revenue from settlement agreements—a mechanism whereby the accused propose payment of a sum to end the process before judgment. In the year, revenue from settlement agreements was R$ 33.4 million, compared to R$ 64.9 million in 2024. In 2025, the board approved 42 settlement agreements, 9 fewer than in the previous year.

With fewer cases judged, the volume of sanctioned agents—individuals or institutions that committed irregularities in the Brazilian capital market and were punished after administrative proceedings—plummeted from 176 to 65. The largest decline occurred in fines, which fell from 153 to 45, while prohibitions of market agents remained stable at 12. The total number of sanctioned parties is the lowest since 2021, when 111 were recorded. The highest number in the last six years occurred in 2022, with 222 sanctioned.

Although the data may suggest a possible prioritization of more serious cases in the face of the authority's resource scarcity, with fewer proceedings, the volume received by the CVM in fines dropped by half, from approximately R$ 1 billion in 2024 to R$ 511 million last year. Although lower than the previous year, the volumes collected by the CVM through fines and settlement agreements still far exceed the amounts allocated for the authority's operations.

## Fewer Resources and Directors

Although it had a budgeted R$ 938.44 million in 2025, only R$ 268.06 million were committed and R$ 222.87 million were actually paid, according to data from the Transparency Portal. The figures are even smaller than those realized in 2024, when CVM expenses paid amounted to R$ 274.06 million and committed expenses to R$ 314.76 million.

The reduction in resources allocated to the CVM occurs even amid a record number of regulated participants. At the end of 2025, there were 92,818 participants under the authority's supervision, 3.4% more than in the previous year. Securities emissions, also supervised by the CVM, reached R$ 980.9 billion. Last year's highlight was the jump from R$ 12.6 billion to R$ 48.6 billion in the volume of Real Estate Receivables Certificates (CRIs) emissions—a category that, by allowing more complex structures with different types of guarantees and specific rules, tends to demand a higher level of supervision.

Beyond budgetary limitations, the CVM also faced institutional instability throughout the period. In 2025, months before the Master case came to light, the then-president of the authority, João Pedro Nascimento, left office two years before the end of his term. Since then, CVM leadership has been exercised on an interim basis, in a scenario of board depletion. Since the beginning of the year, only two of five seats have been occupied. Although President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva nominated Otto Lobo to the board, his nomination, which requires Senate approval, remains blocked by the House president, Davi Alcolumbre.

Amid this scenario, market participants have criticized the CVM's performance in recent cases involving operations linked to Banco Master.
