# Ultrapar weighs increasing Rumo stake amid portfolio overhaul

Ultrapar, one of Brazil's largest industrial groups, is reviewing its business portfolio and holding discussions with potential partners for at least two of its divisions: Ipiranga, its largest unit, and Ultracargo, according to sources familiar with the matter. Under the leadership of Marcos Lutz, who has chaired the board for a year, the group has increasingly positioned Ultrapar as a holding company focused on active portfolio management, following a strategy similar to that of Votorantim, which is also undergoing a broad business review.

Another key, though less visible, move by Ultrapar is to increase its stake in Rumo, the Cosan-owned railway infrastructure company. Lutz, who previously played a central role in structuring Rumo during his tenure at Cosan, has driven Ultrapar to build a roughly 5% position in the company through various investment vehicles. Sources familiar with the matter said Lutz is interested in expanding exposure to Rumo and has approached Cosan to discuss the possibility. However, there is uncertainty over whether Ultrapar would be willing to remain only a significant minority investor in a company with a market value of around R$31 billion. Cosan is not willing to relinquish control and would consider selling between 10% and 15% of the business, Valor has learned. Discussions are ongoing, but no short-term decision is expected.

Cosan is currently divesting assets and has attracted interest from competitors, foreign companies, and financial investors. In logistics, Ultrapar already controls Hidrovias do Brasil, having quietly built a minority position in the company since 2023. This is a sector Lutz knows well, having helped establish Cosan's infrastructure division. Beyond management familiarity, Rumo's strong exposure to agribusiness—one of Brazil's key drivers of economic growth—adds to its strategic appeal.

Energy, railways, infrastructure, and other sectors linked to agribusiness are now central to Ultrapar's strategy, following its unsuccessful foray into pharmaceutical retail with the acquisition of Extrafarma in 2013. The chain was sold nearly a decade later after failing to deliver expected results.

With Cosan facing financial pressure and Rumo emerging as a potential asset sale candidate to support deleveraging, and with shares trading at discounted levels, the railway operator has drawn interest from potential investors. In this context, Ultrapar took an initial step by acquiring about 5% of Rumo through a derivative instrument known as a total return swap. Its entry into Hidrovias do Brasil followed a similarly discreet approach.

A key challenge in increasing its stake in Rumo, which is significantly larger than Hidrovias, would be the size of the investment required. With a market capitalization of around R$32 billion, Ultrapar does not currently have the capacity to take on a large acquisition. At the same time, the group does not intend to act merely as a financial investor.

According to a source, Ultrapar aims to apply its operational management capabilities to new assets, as it has done with Hidrovias do Brasil. Following governance simplification and a full management overhaul, Hidrovias nearly doubled its earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA) last year, with further gains expected, according to people familiar with the matter.

In Rumo's case, the same source noted that more than two dozen senior Ultrapar executives have prior experience in railway businesses, highlighting the group's ability to manage assets in the sector.

Funding a potential acquisition would likely involve asset sales by Ultrapar. While no formal divestment process has been launched, talks with interested parties are underway. Pricing—both for acquisitions and divestitures—will be decisive, and the group is not under pressure to act quickly, according to sources.

Ipiranga, the group's core business, is no longer considered as strategically central as in the past, despite remaining its largest revenue contributor. Discussions about reducing its stake date back five years, when the company faced high leverage. The high level of informality in Brazil's fuel distribution market has also weighed on the business.

While Ultrapar has not formally hired banks to sell a stake or exit the segment, it has held talks with potential buyers. Still, the distribution sector is not currently in a favorable phase, even with some reduction in informality following enforcement actions. At the same time, competitor Raízen—a joint venture between Shell and Cosan—has faced financial challenges after a period of heavy investment and high leverage amid rising interest rates, potentially opening the door to new investors.

Brazil's fuel distribution market is highly concentrated, dominated by three main players, including Vibra (formerly BR Distribuidora). The conflict in the Middle East could benefit distributors by reducing opportunistic diesel imports, tightening supply, and favoring established suppliers. However, the conflict may also introduce new challenges, such as compliance with government-set pricing if distributors participate in subsidy programs aimed at lowering fuel prices.

Since taking leadership of Ultrapar, Lutz—also a minority shareholder—has driven a strategic shift. He joined in 2021 with the backing of private equity firm Pátria, another key shareholder. Rodrigo Pizzinatto has served as CEO since April last year.

As part of its restructuring, Ultrapar divested Extrafarma and Oxiteno, the chemicals business that originally gave rise to the conglomerate. On the financial side, Ultrapar maintains a comfortable leverage position, providing room for new acquisitions. At the end of the fourth quarter, net debt stood at R$12.14 billion, equivalent to 1.7 times EBITDA over the previous 12 months.

Revenue totaled R$142.5 billion last year, up 7%, while net income reached R$2.5 billion, stable year over year. Recurring EBITDA rose 36% in the fourth quarter of 2025 to R$1.75 billion and increased 15% for the full year to R$6.7 billion.
