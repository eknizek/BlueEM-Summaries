The IMF said there are 'reasons' for March inflation increase in Argentina and estimated prices will decelerate 'in coming months'

17 April 2026 · 06:37 PM

# Article Text

The International Monetary Fund said on Friday that "there are reasons why we saw an increase in inflation in the month of March," although they project that "it will decelerate in the coming months." It stated that the technical agreement reached on Wednesday with Argentina is "very positive" and also that it is "essential" for the Government's strategy to obtain guarantees from international organizations to manage credits for financing.

At a press conference focused on the Western Hemisphere, within the framework of the organization's Spring Assembly in Washington, the head of the Department Nigel Chalk, together with his deputy Luis Cubeddu, answered questions from Argentine journalists, including from Clarín.

"We consider the technical agreement as a very positive result, both for Argentina and for the Fund. We have had very productive discussions with the Government and we continue to collaborate very closely," said Chalk regarding the Staff Level Agreement on the second review of the program announced on Wednesday after several months of negotiations, which must be approved in the coming weeks by the Executive Board.

"Our plan is to be able to go to the board at some point in early or mid-May, and we are preparing the documents for that," Cubeddu specified.

Chalk praised the management of Javier Milei's government: "What has occurred during this year is a very positive political push for the country. A very important budget was approved. There was a significant labor reform. Reserves are being accumulated thanks to the constant action of the Central Bank in the markets to purchase reserves." He also referred to technical improvements in its monetary operations and a "continuous effort to eliminate distortions in the economy, open it up, and increase productivity."

And he noted that these results are already being seen in the energy sector. "Argentina is now an energy exporter. This is enormously valuable in the current circumstances, when energy prices are rising."

When asked whether the country would be granted a waiver or dispensation for not meeting year-end reserve targets, Chalk noted that this is a matter to be analyzed by the Executive Board. "They will decide," he said. "But we feel that now we have the entire framework in place. There is a solid microeconomic framework consistently. We believe that it will be truly important and will continue to improve. Argentina is at stake and I think that is also reflected in the markets."

Cubeddu, the Fund's lead negotiator on Argentina, answered a question from Clarín about inflation, which last month stood at 3.4%, the highest in a year. The president said those numbers were "bad."

"The 3.4% increase in March reflects a series of factors. Clearly, the rise in energy prices globally had an impact during March; there were basically increases also in education and seasonal prices and regulated prices, as the authorities begin, I suppose, to address some of the relative price misalignments that exist," said Cubeddu.

"So there are reasons why we saw an increase in inflation in March. We project that the disinflation process will take place in the coming months. High-frequency indicators from April suggest that this is the case. This will be supported by prudent policies in the fiscal realm, with a very strong fiscal anchor," he said, "and also improvements in the monetary policy framework, which will help sustain the process of remonetization of the economy."

"If you look at inflation expectations for 12 months, they remain relatively well anchored, and it has been around 25%, which is also relatively consistent with our forecast for this year, regarding the financing strategy and views on alternative financing, as we mentioned in our press statement," he explained.

Regarding recent announcements of guarantees from the World Bank and the IDB to obtain loans from private banks (guarantees of $2.55 billion were announced), he noted that for debt financing "the authorities are following a multifaceted strategy with different components."

"The first is to try to mobilize financing in US dollars from local domestic markets. The second has to do with the sale of state assets or privatization. The third is through central bank REPOs and, finally, through commercial bank loans that are being guaranteed by multilateral development banks such as the World Bank and the IDB. And we see that this aspect is also essential, as well as important, for the overall financial strategy this year."

"Looking ahead, our view is that, through the mobilization of this financing, through the implementation of the program, through increased accumulation of reserves, we will see a reduction in spreads and, over time, timely and more sustainable access to international capital markets," he added.

Regarding reserves, Cubeddu noted that "what we project is that the authorities will accumulate net international reserves of at least $8 billion this year. Part of this aims to offset some of the decreases that occurred last year."

"This, obviously, will be supported by efforts to mobilize external financing and also by continued purchases by the central bank. The central bank is moving in that direction. They have already purchased $6 billion this year. As for waivers, these are requested by the authorities and approved by the executive board. Our plan is to be able to go to the board at some point in early or mid-May, and we are preparing the documents for that."

At the end of the conference, Cubeddu was asked whether it was necessary for the Government to update the way it measures inflation for the board to approve the review. "This is not a condition for us to move forward to the board. These are matters that we are in conversations about. The authorities have explained that they will update the way they report inflation once the disinflation process has been initiated and consolidated."
