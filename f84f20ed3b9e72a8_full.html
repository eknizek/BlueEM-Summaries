# Kast's Flagship Project Advances; Codelco Tensions Dominate Government Agenda Amid New Middle East Hostilities

After intensive negotiations in the preceding days to pave the way for parliamentary proceedings, the government finally sent forward the national reactivation and reconstruction project, the flagship initiative of the start of its administration. Along with the expected submission of the plan, the milestone accomplished early Wednesday at La Moneda was accompanied by the landing of the main effects of the mega-project, in terms of spending, lower revenues, and tax collection for the Treasury. In calculations that do not include the spending cuts planned by the Executive for this year—approximately US$ 3 billion—nor do they incorporate the impact of economic incentive measures, the financial report established a negative balance of US$ 944 million in the first year. The actions with the greatest impact are the tax credit for employment (US$ 752 million) and VAT exemption for housing (US$ 476 million). Likewise, when considering the effect of growth-stimulating actions, it foresees that the burden moderates until reaching a net positive—greater tax collection than reduction in revenues—starting in 2035. In that context, the director of Dipres, José Pablo Gómez, reported that the spending cut planned by the Executive for this year amounts to US$ 1.762 billion, out of a total of US$ 3 billion anticipated for 2025.

## Harsh Clashes Over Codelco

Codelco's 2026 shareholders' meeting was marked by the tense exchange between board president Máximo Pacheco and Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz and Mining Minister Daniel Mas, in an atmosphere of high pressure. While Pacheco defended his management, asserting that the state-owned company "is not in crisis," the government questioned his tone and lack of self-criticism. The most tense moment occurred when Quiroz directly challenged Pacheco: "Is there no criticism? Doesn't the administration engage in any self-criticism about anything?" The person in question responded by acknowledging errors: "There are many things we should have done differently (...) We have learned to be humble."

Beyond the exchange, the session revealed the structural challenges facing the state company: falling production, rising costs, aging deposits, and a significant increase in debt to finance its investment portfolio.

## Fire in Hormuz

The ceasefire continues to stretch and the second round of peace negotiations between Iran and the United States has yet to materialize. U.S. Vice President JD Vance canceled his trip, Iran did not confirm its attendance, and conversations remain suspended, while uncertainty grows about a diplomatic solution. The Strait of Hormuz continues under maximum tension and is practically restricted. Markets continue operating on a real roller coaster. Each signal of de-escalation pushes Wall Street forward. The S&P 500 touched highs above 7,100 points when Iran announced a temporary opening, but each new escalation hits investors again. In that scenario, Brent oil has remained above US$ 100 per barrel this week, well above levels prior to the conflict.

## Warsh's Defense

Kevin Warsh, nominated by Donald Trump to lead the Federal Reserve (Fed), appeared before the Senate in a hearing marked by doubts about his independence, his wealth, and the direction of monetary policy. In the instance, he argued that the entity requires a profound transformation, warning that it carries inflation problems from the pandemic that it has failed to correct. Under pressure from lawmakers, the economist defended his autonomy and assured that he will not be "a puppet" of the president. Along those lines, he proposed a "regime change" at the Fed, with modifications in the conduct of monetary policy and its communication.

## Critical Minerals and Security Agreements

Chile and the U.S. signed an amendment to the Letter of Agreement on drug control and law enforcement, aimed at strengthening Chilean State capacities against transnational organized crime. The initiative includes a US$ 1 million injection by the Trump administration for the acquisition of logistics and technological equipment. The parties agreed to share data quarterly and meet at least once a year to review goal compliance.

In parallel, both countries signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) aimed at consolidating resilient supply chains for critical minerals and rare earths, in the exploration, extraction, and processing stages. The document commits government and private support through guarantees, loans, investments, and regulatory facilitation.

## Key Decree Withdrawn for Pension Reform

The government withdrew from the Comptroller's Office a key decree for implementing the pension reform, amid observations made by the oversight body. This is Supreme Decree No. 7, which defines the rates and payment modalities for services that the Social Security Institute (IPS) must provide to pension funds (AFPs). The decision was adopted by the Subsecretariat of Social Security at the end of March, with the objective of reviewing the document's content and correcting objections before resubmitting it. The Executive assures that the decree will be resubmitted once objections are addressed, although they acknowledge it is a key piece for the operability of the new system. The decree's withdrawal introduces a new front in the reform's implementation, since without this regulation the IPS could not operate as a back and front office service provider.

## Entel Closes an Era

Businessman Juan Hurtado Vicuña stepped down this week as president of Entel. The company's vice president, Luis Felipe Gazitúa, highlighted that under Juan Hurtado's leadership, revenues multiplied 25 times and the customer base grew to over 20 million between Chile and Peru. Entel announced investments of US$ 575 million for 2026, focused on the mobile market, 5G deployment, and home business expansion.

## The Paulmann Era Begins at Cencosud

A new era begins at Cencosud. Manfred Paulmann Koepfer, eldest son of Horst Paulmann, assumed the presidency of the company this Friday. He left the presidency of Cenco Malls—which was assumed by Jaime Soler—although he will remain as a director of that company. Manfred Paulmann has been part of Cencosud's board since 2021 and, after assuming his new position, stated: "I assume this responsibility with a long-term vision, focused on ensuring continuity of strategy and continuing to promote a disciplined agenda of innovation and adaptation (...)." The move marks the return of the Paulmann family to direct leadership of the group's board.
