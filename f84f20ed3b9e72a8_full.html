# Kast's Flagship Project Submission and High Tensions over Codelco Marked the Agenda

After intensive days of negotiation in the previous days to pave the way for its parliamentary processing, the government finally submitted the national reactivation and reconstruction project, the flagship initiative of the start of its administration. Along with the expected submission of the plan, the milestone achieved early Wednesday at La Moneda was accompanied by the landing of the main effects of the megaproject in terms of spending, lower revenues and tax collection for the Treasury. In calculations that do not include the spending cuts planned by the Executive for this year—approximately US$ 3 billion—nor incorporate the impact of economic incentive measures, the financial report established a negative balance of US$ 944 million in the first year. The actions with the greatest impact are the tax credit for employment (US$ 752 million) and the VAT exemption for housing (US$ 476 million). Likewise, when considering the effect of growth stimulus actions, it predicts that the burden will moderate until reaching a net positive—greater tax collection than income reduction—starting in 2035. In that context, the director of Dipres, José Pablo Gómez, reported that the spending cut planned by the Executive for this year amounts to US$ 1.762 billion, out of a total of US$ 3 billion planned for 2025.

## Harsh Clashes over Codelco

The Codelco 2026 shareholders meeting was marked by the tense clash between board president Máximo Pacheco and Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz and Mining Minister Daniel Mas, in an atmosphere of high pressure. While Pacheco defended his management, assuring that the state-owned company "is not in crisis," the government questioned the tone and lack of self-criticism. The most tense moment occurred when Quiroz directly confronted Pacheco: "Is there no criticism? Does the administration not make any self-criticism of anything?" The person in question responded by acknowledging mistakes: "There are many things we should have done differently (...) We have learned to be humble." Beyond the exchange, the day highlighted the structural challenges facing the state-owned company: falling production, rising costs, aging deposits, and a sharp increase in debt to finance its investment portfolio.

## Fire in Hormuz

The truce continues to stretch and the second round of peace negotiations between Iran and the United States has yet to materialize. U.S. Vice President JD Vance canceled his trip, Iran did not confirm its attendance, and talks remain suspended, while uncertainty grows about a diplomatic solution. The Strait of Hormuz continues under maximum tension and is practically restricted. Markets continue operating on a real roller coaster. Every sign of de-escalation pushes Wall Street. The S&P 500 touched highs above 7,100 points when Iran announced temporary openness, but each new escalation hits investors again. In that scenario, Brent oil has remained this week above US$ 100 per barrel, well above levels prior to the conflict.

## The Defense of Warsh

Kevin Warsh, nominated by Donald Trump to lead the Federal Reserve (Fed), appeared before the Senate in a hearing marked by doubts about his independence, his wealth, and the direction of monetary policy. In the instance, he argued that the entity requires a profound transformation, warning that it carries inflation problems from the pandemic that it has failed to correct. Under pressure from lawmakers, the economist defended his autonomy and assured that he will not be "a puppet" of the president. In that line, he proposed a "regime change" at the Fed, with modifications in the conduct of monetary policy and its communication.

## Agreements on Critical Minerals and Security

Chile and the United States signed an amendment to the Letter of Agreement on narcotic control and law enforcement, with the purpose of strengthening the capacities of the Chilean State against transnational organized crime. The initiative includes an injection of US$ 1 million by the Trump administration for the acquisition of logistics and technological equipment. The parties agreed to share data quarterly and meet at least once a year to review compliance with goals. In parallel, both countries signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) aimed at consolidating resilient supply chains for critical minerals and rare earths in the exploration, extraction, and processing stages. The document commits government and private sector support through guarantees, loans, investments, and regulatory facilitation.

## Key Decree Withdrawn for Pension Reform

The government withdrew from the Comptroller's Office a key decree for the implementation of pension reform, amid observations made by the supervisory body. This is Supreme Decree No. 7, which defines the fees and payment modalities for services that the Social Security Institute (IPS) must provide to AFPs. The decision was adopted by the Undersecretary of Social Security at the end of March, with the objective of reviewing the content of the document and correcting objections before resubmitting it. The Executive assures that the decree will be resubmitted once the observations are addressed, although they acknowledge that it is a key piece for the operability of the new system. The withdrawal of the decree introduces a new front in the implementation of the reform, since without this regulation the IPS could not operate as a back and front office service provider.

## Entel Closes an Era

Businessman Juan Hurtado Vicuña left the presidency of Entel this week. The company's vice president Luis Felipe Gazitúa highlighted that under Juan Hurtado's leadership, revenues multiplied 25 times and the customer base grew to more than 20 million between Chile and Peru. Entel announced investments of US$ 575 million for 2026, with focus on the mobile market, 5G deployment, and expansion of the home business.

## The Paulmann Era Begins at Cencosud

And a new era begins at Cencosud. Manfred Paulmann Koepfer, eldest son of Horst Paulmann, assumed the presidency of the company this Friday. To do so, he left the presidency of Cenco Malls—which was assumed by Jaime Soler—although he will remain as a director of that company. Manfred Paulmann has been part of Cencosud's board of directors since 2021 and, after assuming his new position, stated: "I assume this responsibility with a long-term perspective, focused on giving continuity to the strategy and continuing to drive a disciplined agenda of innovation and adaptation (...)." The move marks the return of the Paulmann family to direct leadership of the group's board.
