OPINION: Bill on Fraudulent Management Does Not Prevent Crime and Generates Insecurity

12 April 2026 · 03:10 AM

# OPINION: Bill on Fraudulent Management Does Not Prevent Crime and Generates Insecurity

The French writer André Gide warned that "everything has already been said, but since nobody listens, one must always start again." Looking at Bill 1.335/2026, by Senator Renan Calheiros, which aims to create crimes of fraudulent and reckless management of public companies by inserting article 177-A into the Penal Code, I find myself obliged to start again. This is because the justification used—that such crimes would have a deterrent effect on the administrators of public companies—directly contradicts accumulated experience in criminal policy. Deterrence does not come from a new law, but from a new culture. A poorly crafted law, instead of improving, worsens the current situation.

The bill repeats the formula of white-collar crime legislation, but without asking why it works in the case of financial institutions and why it may not work in the new context. The explanation is simple. White-collar crime law operates within a dense and well-defined regulatory universe. Institutions authorized by the Central Bank are subject to a rigorous set of specific administration rules: Basel indices, credit concentration limits, liquidity requirements, risk management standards issued by the National Monetary Council. When a bank administrator engages in reckless management, for example, the criminal judge has a place to look for the parameter: there are concrete rules that were violated, and it is this violation that anchors the criminal unlawfulness.

Other public companies, however, live in another world. Brazil had 703 public companies with active registration at the end of 2025, a number that tends to grow with the FÁCIL regime, launched by the CVM to facilitate companies' access to the capital market. This universe ranges from large conglomerates to shoe manufacturers, logistics companies, machinery, agriculture, and regional retailers. None of these sectors has a set of prudential rules comparable to what governs banks. The Corporate Law establishes general duties of diligence and loyalty, but does not define risk limits, does not prescribe specific management policies, and does not provide objective thresholds that, once exceeded, signal irresponsible management. There is a regulatory gap between the generic duties of corporate law and the level of detail that a serious criminal statute demands.

This gap has a direct consequence: without sources of regulation from which specific duties of administration can be extracted, the criminal judge will be left without a parameter to decide when management was or was not reckless. The proposed criminal statute offers no guidance. It does not require a serious violation of an identifiable duty. It does not require demonstrating a causal link between the administrator's conduct and the damage suffered by the company. The task of distinguishing legitimate business error from crime is left to the interpreter's discretion.

In analyzing the criminalization of excessive risk-taking in English, French, and German systems, the compliance and law enforcement professor at the Université du Luxembourg, Stanislaw Tosza, is emphatic: the criminal judge cannot be the arbiter of the quality of business decisions. To fulfill this role with legitimacy, he needs an external source of standards, established by corporate law or sectoral regulation, that tells him what was expected of the administrator in that position, in that market, at that moment. Without this, any significant business loss becomes a suspicion of crime.

The probable result is not greater legal security for investors. It is the opposite: insecurity for those who administer, with the risk that bold but legitimate decisions are criminalized retrospectively, when the context has changed and the result has disappointed expectations. Criminal law has an important role in combating corporate fraud, but it must be exercised with precision. Criminalizing fraudulent and reckless management of public companies without clearly defining its typical elements is not rigor. It is legislative improvisation dressed up as severity.

*Rodrigo Falk Fragoso is a criminal attorney, partner at Fragoso Advogados, and author of "Reckless Management of Financial Institution" (2024), resulting from his doctoral thesis at USP.*
