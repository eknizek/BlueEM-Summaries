Opinion: Bill on Fraudulent Management Does Not Prevent Crime and Creates Uncertainty

12 April 2026 · 03:10 AM

# Opinion: Bill on Fraudulent Management Does Not Prevent Crime and Creates Uncertainty

The French writer André Gide warned that "all things have already been said, but since no one listens, we must always start over." Looking at Bill 1.335/2026, by Senator Renan Calheiros, which aims to create crimes of fraudulent and reckless management of publicly held companies by inserting article 177-A into the Penal Code, I find myself compelled to start again. This is because the justification used—that such crimes would have a deterrent effect on administrators of publicly held companies—directly collides with accumulated experience in criminal policy. Deterrence does not come from new law, but from new culture. A poorly drafted law, instead of improving, worsens the current situation.

The project repeats the formula of the white-collar crime law but fails to ask why it works in the case of financial institutions and why it may not work in this new context. The explanation is simple. The white-collar crime law operates within a dense and well-defined regulatory universe. Institutions authorized by the Central Bank are subject to a rigorous set of specific administration rules: Basel indices, credit concentration limits, liquidity requirements, risk management norms issued by the National Monetary Council. When a bank administrator practices reckless management, for example, the criminal judge has a place to look for parameters: there are concrete rules that were violated, and this violation is what anchors the criminal wrongfulness.

Other publicly held companies live in another world. Brazil had 703 publicly held companies with active registration at the end of 2025, a number that tends to grow with the FÁCIL regime launched by the CVM to facilitate companies' access to capital markets. This universe ranges from large conglomerates to shoe manufacturers, logistics companies, machinery producers, agricultural enterprises, and regional retailers. None of these sectors has a set of prudential rules comparable to what governs banks. The Corporation Law establishes general duties of diligence and loyalty but does not define risk limits, does not prescribe specific management policies, and does not establish objective thresholds that, once exceeded, would signal irresponsible management. There is a regulatory gap between the generic duties of corporate law and the level of detail that a serious criminal statute requires.

This gap has a direct consequence: without sources of regulation from which specific administrative duties can be extracted, the criminal judge will have no parameters to decide when management was or was not reckless. The proposed criminal statute offers no guidance. It does not require a serious violation of an identifiable duty. It does not require demonstrating a causal nexus between the administrator's conduct and the damage suffered by the company. It leaves to the interpreter's discretion the task of distinguishing legitimate business error from crime.

When analyzing the criminalization of excessive risk-taking in English, French, and German systems, Stanislaw Tosza, a compliance and law enforcement professor at the University of Luxembourg, is emphatic: the criminal judge cannot be an arbiter of business decision quality. To fulfill this role with legitimacy, he needs an external source of standards, established by corporate law or sectoral regulation, that tells him what was expected of the administrator in that position, in that market, at that moment. Without this, any significant business loss becomes a crime suspect.

The probable result is not more legal certainty for investors. It is the opposite: insecurity for those who administer, with the risk that bold but legitimate decisions are criminalized retrospectively when the context has changed and results disappointed expectations. Criminal law has an important role in combating corporate fraud, but it must be exercised with precision. Criminalizing fraudulent and reckless management of publicly held companies without clearly defining their typical elements is not rigor. It is legislative improvisation dressed up as severity.

*Rodrigo Falk Fragoso is a criminal lawyer, partner at Fragoso Advogados, and author of "Reckless Management of Financial Institution" (2024), based on his doctoral thesis at USP.*
