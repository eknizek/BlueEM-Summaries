# Opinion: Fraudulent Management Bill Does Not Prevent Crime and Creates Uncertainty

The French writer André Gide warned that "all things have already been said, but since no one listens, one must always start over." Looking at Bill 1,335/2026, by Senator Renan Calheiros, which aims to create crimes of fraudulent and reckless management of publicly traded companies by inserting article 177-A into the Penal Code, I find myself compelled to start anew. This is because the justification employed—that such crimes would have a deterrent effect on administrators of publicly traded companies—directly collides with accumulated experience in criminal policy. Deterrence does not come from new laws, but from new culture. A poorly crafted law, instead of improving, worsens the current situation.

The project repeats the formula of the white-collar crime law, but without asking why it works for financial institutions and why it may not work in this new context. The explanation is simple. The white-collar crime law operates within a dense and well-defined regulatory universe. Institutions authorized by the Central Bank are subject to a rigorous set of specific administration rules: Basel ratios, credit concentration limits, liquidity requirements, risk management standards issued by the National Monetary Council. When a bank administrator practices reckless management, for example, the criminal judge has a parameter to consult: there are concrete rules that were violated, and it is this violation that anchors criminal unlawfulness.

Other publicly traded companies live in a different world. Brazil had 703 publicly traded companies with active registration at the end of 2025, a number that tends to grow with the FÁCIL regime, launched by the CVM to facilitate companies' access to the capital market. This universe ranges from large conglomerates to shoe manufacturers, logistics companies, machinery makers, agricultural enterprises, and regional retailers. None of these sectors has a set of prudential rules comparable to those governing banks. The Corporations Law establishes general duties of diligence and loyalty, but does not define risk limits, does not prescribe specific management policies, and does not establish objective thresholds that, once exceeded, signal irresponsible management. There is a regulatory void between the generic duties of corporate law and the level of detail that a serious penal statute requires.

This void has a direct consequence: without sources of regulation from which specific administration duties can be extracted, the criminal judge will have no parameter to decide whether management was or was not reckless. The proposed penal statute offers no guidance. It does not require the serious violation of an identifiable duty. It does not require proof of a causal nexus between the administrator's conduct and the damage suffered by the company. The task of distinguishing legitimate business error from crime is left to the interpreter's discretion.

In analyzing the criminalization of excessive risk-taking in English, French, and German systems, Stanislaw Tosza, professor of compliance and law enforcement at the Université du Luxembourg, is emphatic: the criminal judge cannot be the arbiter of business decision quality. To fulfill this role with legitimacy, he needs an external source of standards, established by corporate law or sectoral regulation, that tells him what was expected of the administrator in that position, in that market, at that moment. Without this, any significant business loss becomes a crime suspect. The probable result is not greater legal certainty for investors. It is the opposite: insecurity for those who manage, with the risk that bold but legitimate decisions are criminalized retroactively, when the context has changed and the result disappointed expectations.

Criminal law has an important role in combating corporate fraud, but it must be exercised with precision. Typifying fraudulent and reckless management of publicly traded companies without clearly defining their typical elements is not rigor. It is legislative improvisation dressed in severity.

Rodrigo Falk Fragoso is a criminal lawyer, partner at Fragoso Advogados, and author of "Reckless Management of Financial Institution" (2024), based on his doctoral thesis at USP.
