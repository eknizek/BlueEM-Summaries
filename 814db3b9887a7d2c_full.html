# Chile's Finance Minister Quiroz Defends Spending Cuts and Pledges to Protect Social Benefits

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz conducted a self-critique regarding the way the government communicated spending cuts it seeks to implement for this year and the 2027 Budget, which generated strong controversy by questioning the continuity of programs such as school feeding or the Universal Guaranteed Pension (PGU).

In an interview with TVN, the state secretary stated that when preparing the controversial memo in which he instructed the cuts, it was not anticipated that this document would become public, so there was a failure to better explain that "discontinuing" did not mean eliminating public initiatives, but rather changing the manner in which certain programs were being executed.

"If one reads that memo—which is short, just two or three pages—it says on two occasions that each ministry's base budget must respect existing laws and contractual commitments. So truthfully, I considered it rather minor, it wasn't the first memo we sent," he said, adding that "there was a lack of care when the memo was written."

"There was an error, the memo had a communication error, there was a lack of care. What care was lacking? Imagining that the memo was going to be public," the economist explained.

"I think this is a week where clearly we've had to go out and explain things better. Perhaps there was a shortcoming in not explaining better, but our direction doesn't change. Spending is out of control and that confirms the absolute need to rationalize public spending," the official said.

He reinforced that social policies will not be touched: "We have said repeatedly that social issues are untouched, which does not mean we don't have to seek efficiency and there are many spaces for that. And also tackle abuses that we have been attacking with considerable success. So this issue is complex, but we don't lose sight of our direction and we're going to resolve it step by step. The objective is to recover control of public spending and zealously protect public finances," the Finance Minister emphasized.

Regarding the PGU, the state secretary stressed that "one thing is the law (that created the PGU) and another thing entirely is how the law is being applied." He exemplified that the law establishes that foreigners who have been in the country for more than 20 years can opt for the PGU, but he questioned how this is verified. "I'm not saying it's wrong, what I'm saying is that these memos call for examining how things are being done," he emphasized.

"Looking at it now, perhaps in the table one should not have put 15% (budget cut to the PGU) and should have put 'review.' Now, when one puts 'review,' there is always dialogue with the ministers, and no minister likes to cut their budget," he concluded.

**Differences with Ministers**

Quiroz was also asked about criticisms raised regarding the work of the head of the Second Floor of La Moneda, Alejandro Irarrázaval, who is blamed for the error of not warning that the language used in the cut memos could generate a political crisis in the government.

On this, Quiroz said it is a "myth" when it is suggested that the Second Floor has so much influence over the ministries. He also dismissed the idea that there are "two souls" in the government, noting that he has good relations with his counterparts from Interior, Claudio Alvarado, and from the General Secretariat of the Presidency (Segpres), José García.

"I have excellent interaction with Ministers Alvarado and Minister García Ruminot. Excellent, excellent, excellent. We make a very good team," he stated.
