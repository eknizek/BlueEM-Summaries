# Quiroz Conducts Self-Criticism on Spending Cut Directive and Emphasizes Social Benefits Will Not Be Touched

Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz conducted self-criticism regarding the way the government communicated spending cuts it seeks to implement for this year and the 2027 Budget, which generated strong controversy by putting into question the continuity of programs such as school meals or the Universal Guaranteed Pension (PGU).

In an interview with TVN, the Secretary of State stated that when drafting the controversial memo where he instructed the cuts, it was not contemplated that this document would become public, which is why there was a failure to better explain that "discontinuing" did not mean eliminating public initiatives, but rather changing the manner in which certain programs were being executed.

"If one reads that memo—which is short, it's two or three pages—it states on two occasions that the base budget of each ministry must respect existing laws and contractual commitments. So, the truth is that I considered it a rather minor matter; it was not the first memo we sent," he assured, adding that "there was a bit of a lack of care when the memo was written."

"There was an error, the memo had a care error, there was a lack of care. What care was lacking? To imagine that the memo was going to be public," explained the economist.

"I think this is a week where we clearly had to come out and explain things better. Perhaps there is a shortcoming there in not having explained things better, but the direction does not change. Spending is out of control and that confirms the absolute need to rationalize public spending," said the official.

On this matter, he reinforced that social policies will not be touched: "We have stated on repeated occasions that social matters are not touched, which does not mean there is no need to seek efficiency and there are many spaces for it. And also to address abuses that we have been attacking with considerable success. So this issue is complex, but we don't lose sight of the direction and we will resolve it step by step. The direction is to regain control of public spending and zealously care for public finances," emphasized the Finance Minister.

Regarding the PGU, the Secretary of State stressed that "one thing is the law (that created the PGU) and another thing entirely is how the law is being applied." Thus, he exemplified that the law stipulates that foreigners who have been in the country for more than 20 years can opt for the PGU, but he questioned how this is accredited. "I am not saying that it is wrong, what I am saying is that these memos call for examining how things are being done," he emphasized.

"Looking at it now, perhaps the 15% (budget cut to the PGU) should not have been put in the table and instead 'review' should have been put. Now, when one puts 'review,' there is always dialogue with the ministers, and no minister likes to cut their budget," he concluded.

## Differences with Ministers

Quiroz was also asked about criticisms that have been raised regarding the work of the head of the Second Floor of La Moneda, Alejandro Irarrázaval, who is blamed for the error of not warning that the language used in the spending cut memos could generate a political crisis in the government. On this, Quiroz stated that it is a "myth" when it is argued that the Second Floor has so much influence over the ministries. He also rejected the idea that there are "two souls" in the government, noting that he has a good relationship with his counterparts from Interior, Claudio Alvarado, and from the General Secretariat of the Presidency (Segpres), José García.

"I have excellent interaction with Ministers Alvarado and Minister García Ruminot. Excellent, excellent, excellent. We make a very good team," he stated.
