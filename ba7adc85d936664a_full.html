# "It is not reasonable for the economy to pay for political differences": ANDI president on crisis with Ecuador

Bruce Mac Master described the commercial crisis with the neighboring country as "tremendously clumsy."

The escalation of commercial tensions between Colombia and Ecuador generated a reaction from the business sector. The president of the National Association of Businessmen of Colombia (ANDI), Bruce Mac Master, questioned the situation and called for acting with responsibility regarding decisions that are impacting the economy.

The business leader referred to the imposition of tariffs on Colombian products and described the scenario as "tremendously clumsy."

According to him, the conflict is in the hands of the presidents of Colombia and Ecuador, who have the capacity to resolve their differences. "I believe this is a tremendously clumsy situation that has been generated and is, without a doubt, something that the presidents of both countries can solve. It is in their hands to resolve their differences," he stated.

Mac Master also questioned that tensions end up transferring to the economic sphere. In that sense, he assured that it is not reasonable for differences to affect the economy.

"It is not fair nor is it reasonable that because of political differences between two people, between two leaders like President Petro and President Noboa, the entire Colombian economy ends up paying higher tariffs," he said in dialogue with Caracol Radio.

The leader explained the scope of the tariff measures being discussed, which reach levels of between 75% and 100%. According to him, these percentages prevent commercialization.

He explained that paying higher tariffs means that "we will not be capable of selling." According to Mac Master, a product that doubles in price loses viability in the market. "It is simply a product that does not sell," he stated.

In that regard, he warned about the impact on productive sectors that depend on trade with Ecuador. He pointed out that Colombia has industries designed to supply that market, particularly in some regions of the country.

"Colombia has a large industry, for example, in the Valle del Cauca area, in the Cauca area, Santander de Quilichao, that has been designed and works to be able to sell to Ecuador. If we have those tariffs, we won't be able to pay them," he indicated.

The ANDI president insisted that the origin is unclear to many citizens, but its effects are evident in the economy. In that context, he reiterated his call to the leaders to prioritize the general interest.

"We don't know what the differences are about, what the differences between the two presidents are, but they are differences that are above all political," he stated.

Finally, he sent a direct message to the heads of state of both countries, asking them to act with greater responsibility and maturity regarding the situation.

"I do tell them to act with much more responsibility, to act with much more maturity, to think of the Colombian people, to think of Colombian workers and Colombian businessmen," he expressed.

The leader also called for ending personal discussions that, according to him, are affecting the economic dynamics between the two countries.

"That they leave those personalistic and personal discussions and that, please, they allow them to sell their products in the economies," he concluded.
