# Buffett Says Market Lives in 'Casino Mood': 'We've Never Had People So Prone to Betting'

Mega-investor Warren Buffett of Berkshire Hathaway compared current market behavior to a "casino," drawing attention to investors' enthusiasm for short-term options amid the growth of prediction markets.

"People can move between the church and the casino, and I would say there are more people in the church than in the casino, but the casino has become very attractive," he said in an interview with CNBC during the lunch break of the company's annual shareholders meeting. This time, he attended the event from the audience with Greg Abel as the new CEO.

"If you are buying or selling options for one day, that is not investing, it is not speculation – it is gambling," Buffett added. "We've never had people so prone to betting as now."

The Oracle of Omaha, as Buffett is also known, also commented on a recent case of a U.S. soldier who allegedly used confidential information related to a military operation in Venezuela to earn $400,000 in a prediction market.

"Nobody can explain why someone would buy an option for a single day, unless they had a chance to make over 400 thousand dollars knowing when we were going to invade Venezuela. The amount of these options is simply unbelievable," he said.

The case is being investigated by the Department of Justice.

## The Risk of Inflation

Warren Buffett also commented on inflation and monetary policy. During the CNBC interview, the mega-investor said he "felt safe" with Jerome Powell in command of the Federal Reserve.

He also considered that episodes of uncontrolled inflation devastated dozens of countries during World War II and that rising prices remain one of the greatest threats to an economy.

"What destroys a country is when people lose confidence in the currency. If you lose confidence in money, the country becomes something else," Buffett said.

The mega-investor also stated that the United States is not immune to inflationary risk and highlighted that authorities have "limited control" over interest rate movements.

## The 'Golden Rule'

During the CNBC interview, Buffett also stated that the main rule that shareholders and partners should follow is "treat others as you would like to be treated" — also known as the Golden Rule.

Although the former CEO of Berkshire Hathaway said he is "not a religious guy," he emphasized that there has never been a better message conveyed "in some thousands of years than that."

"If the whole world lived by the Golden Rule, it would be a much more wonderful society," said Buffett.

"It doesn't cost you anything. In fact, it reflects better behavior toward you, so, I mean, it's a rather selfish thing in a sense, but I've never seen anyone unhappy who behaved that way," he said.
