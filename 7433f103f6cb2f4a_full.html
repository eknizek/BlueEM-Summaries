# Fertilizer Prices Surge: Rising Costs Pressure Agriculture and Force Companies to Adjust Field Strategies

Last year was extraordinary for the agro-export sector. For this new period, growth is also anticipated, although the alert status due to coastal El Niño raises caution.

By Maritza Saenz

In 2022, as a result of the war between Russia and Ukraine, the price of urea—the main fertilizer used in Peru—tripled due to global shortage. Four years later, uncertainty is setting in again in the agroindustry amid fears that the conflict in the Middle East could mark the beginning of a similar scenario in the fertilizer market. In fact, so far in 2026, urea has appreciated more than 77%, reaching prices of US$687.50 per ton. This represents an interannual variation exceeding 100%, according to information compiled by the Association of Agricultural Producer Groups of Peru (AGAP).

Other fertilizers are also registering significant increases. Potassium nitrate rose from US$850 per ton in January to US$1,300 at the end of March—a variation of 40%; potassium sulfate became more expensive by around 30%, with prices between US$750 and US$900 per ton; and ammonium nitrate is around US$650 per ton, an increase of up to 50%, according to the Association for Sustainable Agricultural Development (ADAS), which brings together 31 agro-export companies from La Libertad.

## Reasons for the Rise and Our Imports

The increases respond to a combination of global pressures intensified in recent months, in a context marked by the Middle East conflict. Luis Quiroz, director of the Master's in Agribusiness Administration at Esan, identifies five factors: the geopolitical context, which has altered production and trade; tensions in the Strait of Hormuz, through which a third of world urea exports transit; the rise in natural gas, a key input in nitrogen fertilizer production; the increase in maritime freight costs; and market behavior, with export restrictions—as in China's case—and speculative effects.

The situation is complex for Peru due to its high dependence on synthetic fertilizer imports. According to Mario Salazar Vergaray, president of the Agroindustries, Food and Beverages Committee of ADEX, 90% of nitrogen products are imported, mainly from China and Russia, with just over 40% corresponding to urea. "Any interruption in the global supply or logistics chain, as well as the increase in gas prices, directly impacts fertilizer costs, of which we import around 90%," he mentions to Día 1.

Port logistics also reflect the magnitude of the flow of these inputs. Since the beginning of operations, the Port of Chancay has mobilized around 146,000 tons of fertilizers and related products—such as ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, and phosphates—and projects surpassing an additional 140,000 tons this year.

Although the price increase affects all producers, two groups are most affected, according to Milton von Hesse, executive director of von Hesse Consultores and former Minister of Agriculture. The first is intermediate family agriculture, integrated into the commercial circuit and supplying the domestic market. It develops on the coast and high jungle; among its producers, those who demand the most urea are those who grow white potatoes, rice, and hard yellow corn.

A greater impact is faced by agro-exporters, who not only use urea but also more sophisticated fertilizers to guarantee productivity and quality. They concentrate in regions such as Ica, Piura, La Libertad, and Lima, and occupy less than 5% of the country's agricultural land. "Using cutting-edge fertilizers affects productivity, the amount of tons you produce per hectare, as well as product quality, and that allows you to meet frequent demands from markets."
