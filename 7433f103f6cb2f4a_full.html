# Fertilizer costs surge, pressuring agriculture sector and forcing strategic adjustments

The previous year was extraordinary for the agroexport sector. For this new period, growth is also anticipated, although the alert status due to coastal El Niño awakens caution.

By Maritza Saenz

06/04/2026, 03:01 p.m.

In 2022, as a result of the war between Russia and Ukraine, the price of urea—the main fertilizer used in Peru—tripled due to global scarcity. Four years later, uncertainty has returned to the agroindustry amid fears that the Middle East conflict marks the beginning of a similar scenario in the fertilizer market. In fact, so far in 2026, urea has appreciated more than 77%, reaching prices of US$687.50 per ton. This represents an interannual variation exceeding 100%, according to information compiled by the Association of Agricultural Producer Guilds of Peru (AGAP).

Other fertilizers are also registering significant increases. Potassium nitrate went from US$850 per ton in January to US$1,300 at the end of March—a 40% variation—; potassium sulfate increased by nearly 30%, with prices between US$750 and US$900 per ton; and ammonium nitrate hovers around US$650 per ton, an increase of up to 50%, according to the Association for Sustainable Agricultural Development (ADAS), which brings together 31 agroexport companies from La Libertad.

**Reasons for the increase and our imports**

The increases respond to a combination of global pressures intensified in recent months, in a context marked by the Middle East conflict. Luis Quiroz, director of the Master's Degree in Agribusiness Administration at Esan, identifies five factors: the geopolitical context, which has altered production and trade; tensions in the Strait of Hormuz, through which a third of the world's exported urea transits; the rise in natural gas, a key input in the production of nitrogen fertilizers; the increase in maritime freight costs; and market behavior, with export restrictions—as in the case of China—and speculative effects.

The situation is complex for Peru due to its high dependence on the import of synthetic fertilizers. According to Mario Salazar Vergaray, president of the Committee on Agroindustries, Food and Beverages at ADEX, 90% of nitrogen products are imported, mainly from China and Russia, and just over 40% corresponds to urea. "Any interruption in the global logistical or supply chain, as well as the increase in gas prices, directly impacts fertilizer costs, of which we import around 90%," he mentions to Día 1.

In that regard, port logistics also reflects the magnitude of the flow of these inputs. Since beginning operations, the Port of Chancay has moved approximately 146,000 tons of fertilizers and related products—such as ammonium nitrate, ammonium sulfate, and phosphates—and projects to exceed an additional 140,000 tons this year.

While the price increase affects all producers, two groups are the most affected, according to Milton von Hesse, executive director of von Hesse Consultores and former Minister of Agriculture. The first is intermediate family agriculture, integrated into the commercial circuit and supplying the domestic market. It develops on the coast and high jungle; and among its producers, those who demand urea the most are those who grow white potatoes, rice, and hard yellow corn.

Agroexporters face a greater impact, as they not only use urea but also more sophisticated fertilizers to guarantee productivity and quality. They concentrate in regions like Ica, Piura, La Libertad, and Lima, and occupy less than 5% of the country's agricultural land. "The use of latest-generation fertilizers affects productivity, the number of tons you produce per hectare, as well as product quality, and this allows you to meet the frequent demands of international markets," Von Hesse mentions.
