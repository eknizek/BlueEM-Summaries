# The 'rabbit punch' of pensions and government overspending: the serious consequences analyzed by Néstor Humberto Martínez

The stability of public finances in Colombia has entered a turbulent zone that awakens alarms among the country's most experienced experts. In his most recent analysis for EL TIEMPO, former prosecutor and analyst Néstor Humberto Martínez issues a severe warning about what he calls "delirious" fiscal management and an unprecedented "rabbit punch" to Colombian workers' savings.

Based on recent alerts from the Office of the Comptroller General of the Republic, Martínez paints a picture where excessive spending and falling tax collection are pushing the country toward a financial precipice.

The core of Martínez's criticism lies in the dangerous transformation of state income. According to the figures analyzed, tax revenues have suffered significant erosion, dropping from representing 66% of total income in 2023 to projecting just 56% for 2025. Given modest economic growth and what the analyst defines as "business apathy"—motivated by insecurity and current policies—the possibility of balancing the scales through new tax reforms is, in his judgment, unviable.

For the analyst, the logical solution would be a cut and prioritization of public spending. However, he points out that the Executive has chosen the opposite path, earning the label of "squanderer." This lack of fiscal discipline has not only led to billions in unfulfilled obligations in critical sectors like health, education, and court-ordered settlements, but has also begun affecting future years in infrastructure, sending a fatal signal of distrust for foreign investment.

## The 'rabbit punch' to Colombians' pensions

The most controversial point of Martínez's analysis focuses on the financing strategy that the Finance Ministry appears to have adopted to avoid default. The analyst denounces a financial "trick" of systemic proportions: the use of pension savings to cover the fiscal gap.

According to Martínez, the Government not only is preparing to use the 25 trillion pesos linked to the controversial "rabbit punch" in Colpensiones, but has gone a step further by requiring pension fund administrators (AFP) to repatriate 125 trillion pesos that were invested abroad.

The consequence of this measure is clear to the analyst: given that the local capital market lacks the depth necessary to absorb such a flow, most of those resources will end up invested in Treasury Bonds (TES). In practice, this means the Government will finance itself with more than 60 trillion pesos coming directly from worker savings, transferring to the working class a credit risk that international markets no longer appear willing to assume voluntarily.

Néstor Humberto Martínez concludes that this model of "populist and non-transparent spending," driven by electoral interests of the Historic Pact, is sacrificing the long-term security of savers to sustain a spending pace that "house Colombia" can no longer support. The Finance Ministry's silence in the face of these warnings only increases uncertainty about the future of pensions and the state's solvency in the years to come.
