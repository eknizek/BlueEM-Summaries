# Spirit Airlines Announces Gradual Closure of Operations and Cancels All Flights

The American low-cost airline Spirit Airlines, which served numerous destinations in Latin America, announced Saturday that it has canceled all its flights and begun an "orderly closure of its operations" following the failure of a financial rescue by the White House.

U.S. President Donald Trump had expressed interest in offering economic aid to save thousands of jobs at the company, which had declared bankruptcy twice in 2025.

The airline connected the United States, especially the state of Florida, with more than 20 destinations in Latin America and the Caribbean, including Mexico, Colombia, Costa Rica, Honduras, Peru, Puerto Rico, and the Dominican Republic.

In a statement, the parent company Spirit Aviation Holdings announced "with regret that the company has begun the orderly closure of its operations, effective immediately."

"All Spirit flights have been canceled, and Spirit passengers should not go to the airport," the statement said.

The company's website displays a message stating that "customer service is no longer available."

The airline stated that it will process refunds for already-purchased flights.

U.S. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy rushed to defend the Trump administration's position. A rescue plan that was reportedly being prepared never materialized.

"The president was like a dog with a bone trying to find a way to keep Spirit afloat," Duffy said at a morning press conference at Newark Liberty International Airport in New Jersey.

"In the end, this was a matter for creditors. Again, they have the final say on whether or not they want to make a deal with the government," he noted.

"But also, from the government's perspective, we generally don't have half a trillion dollars sitting in some extra account that we can dedicate to rescuing an airline," he concluded.

## Goodbye to the Yellow Planes

The company, known for its striking yellow aircraft and which began offering flights in 1992, established itself as a tough competitor to major airlines through its low-cost model.

Its president and chief executive, Dave Davis, explained that in March they had reached an agreement with creditors for a restructuring plan that "would have allowed us to emerge as a future business."

However, the sharp increase in fuel prices since the outbreak of war in the Middle East left the company with "no alternative but to carry out a gradual closure of the company," he stated in the announcement.

"Maintaining the business required hundreds of millions of dollars in additional liquidity that Spirit simply does not have and could not obtain. This is tremendously disappointing and is not the outcome any of us desired," he added.

## "Final Proposal"

On Friday, Trump explained that they had sent a "final proposal" to rescue the airline, which employed about 11,000 people in 2024.

"I imagine we are reviewing it. If we can do it, we will, but only if it's a good deal," he told the press at the White House at that time.

Senior officials in his administration criticized the previous Joe Biden administration for blocking a $3.8 billion acquisition offer by JetBlue, arguing it would harm consumers.

In its statement, Spirit stated that there were "broad and exhaustive efforts to restructure the business," but the lack of additional financing left it "with no choice but to undertake this closure."

Jan Brueckner, professor emeritus of Economics at the University of California, Irvine, told AFP last week that the rise in fuel prices was "the straw that broke the camel's back."

Following the closure announcement, American Airlines and United Airlines, two of the largest U.S. air transport giants, offered to help Spirit customers.

American stated it was offering "rescue fares" on Spirit routes to "mitigate the impact on communities," and United proposed "one-way tickets at maximum price from most of the cities" the low-cost airline served.

The Flight Attendants Association, a union representing about 5,000 Spirit employees, said it was in contact with other airlines to support Spirit staff.

"Each active flight attendant will be provided with a hotel and/or a flight to return home," the union added.

This closure is "the hardest news of our lives," it concluded.
