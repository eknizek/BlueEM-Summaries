# Bond Market Eyes Potential Shift in US Debt Strategy Under New Administration

Wednesday will be a sort of "groundhog day" for US bond traders, who—as has been happening for over a year—will be watching for any change in Treasury Department guidance in its latest debt issuance plan. Investors will search for any adjustment in Treasury guidance within its quarterly refinancing statement regarding expectations that no increases in note and bond issuance are anticipated "for at least the next several quarters."

Although longer-term debt is currently more expensive than short-term debt, relying on bills to finance an annual deficit near $2 trillion also carries its own risks.

"They can't just keep saying 'at least several quarters,'" said Jack McIntyre, portfolio manager at Brandywine Global Investment Management in Philadelphia. "They will try to resist it as much as they can," but at some point officials will need to think about increasing sales of interest-bearing debt, he noted.

Increasing the issuance of bills, which mature in up to one year, leaves the Government's debt costs more exposed to sharp movements in rates and changes in market sentiment, since auctions are more frequent. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) warned about these risks last month.

However, the Treasury has reason to believe that solid demand can absorb the increase in bill supply, at least for now. Money market funds have grown to approximately $7.6 trillion—of which about 42% is invested in Treasuries—and continue to expand. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has also argued that the GENIUS Act could channel trillions of dollars toward Treasuries, since it would require stablecoin issuers to maintain reserves in assets such as Treasury bills.

Before taking office last year, Bessent had criticized Treasury guidance under Janet Yellen. More recently, statements have hinted that officials are evaluating the potential for "future increases" in auctions of interest-bearing and floating-rate securities, without offering a timeline. This has left traders on alert for any change in language on Wednesday.

"If you remove 'at least' and leave only 'several,' then the market will say: okay, we have about three more quarters of this level of coupon issuance," said McIntyre.

What is unavoidable for the Treasury is the constant increase in total debt, which continues to raise the volume of issuance. That burden is only growing—with costs from the war with Iran, uncertainty about the future of trade tariffs, and slower growth generating warnings of broader budget deficits.

The department must update its estimate of financing needs for the current quarter on Monday, before the refinancing announcement. In February, it projected net borrowing of $109 billion for the three months through June.

As for next week's refinancing auctions, traders generally expect them to be composed of:

- $58 billion in 3-year notes on May 11

- $42 billion in 10-year notes on May 12

- $25 billion in 30-year bonds on May 13

As a sign of how closely the semantics of the Treasury's statement will be examined, Wall Street strategists offered various possible modifications. JPMorgan Chase & Co. sees "significant risk" that the Treasury will remove "at least" from its guidance. Barclays Plc expects "at least" to remain, but "several" to be replaced by "the next few" quarters. Wells Fargo suggests: no change "at least until the end of calendar year 2026," or perhaps a complete elimination of the phrase.

Any adjustment "could move prices" in the market, warned strategists at TD Securities. In fact, one reason Citigroup Inc. had expected the Treasury to have already prepared investors for an expansion in the size of coupon auctions starting this November was that the market impact "could be minimized by starting earlier."

"However, at this moment, we consider it very unlikely that Bessent will edit the next Treasury policy statement," wrote Citigroup strategists, including Jason Williams. The Citigroup team postponed its forecast for increases in coupon issuance to May 2027 "at the earliest" last month. Goldman Sachs Group Inc. also delayed its projection, now placing it in February 2027.

Among the factors the Treasury must consider are changes in public debt demand patterns. In recent months, the Federal Reserve has been a buyer of bills while seeking to rebuild bank reserves in the financial system, but those purchases have declined. Looking ahead, the Fed will soon have a new chairman in Kevin Warsh. Last month he told US senators that the central bank "should not hold long-term Treasury assets" and favors reducing its balance sheet over time. Both ideas would have implications for Treasury issuance.

The Trump administration's measures to reduce banking regulations also have the potential to affect the level of Treasury demand from banks. Before the next refinancing announcement, the Treasury consulted with primary dealers in its usual quarterly survey about how changes in banking regulation affect investor demand and liquidity in this $31 trillion market.

Regarding future guidance, a key panel of external advisors recommended more than a year ago that the Treasury abandon current language. In February, the group—the Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee, which includes certain traders and investors—maintained "robust" discussions about the best way to increase the size of auctions, according to its letter to Bessent. The debate centered on "the relative tradeoffs between increasing the size of auctions more gradually, possibly before it's necessary, versus a more accelerated path of increases when the financing gap is larger."
