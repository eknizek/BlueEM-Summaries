# Colombia's central banker says surprise rate decision helps avoid volatility

Colombia's surprise decision to halt interest rate increases this week was aimed at avoiding disruptions, but it may come at a cost, according to co-director of the central bank, Mauricio Villamizar.

The bank unexpectedly kept its benchmark interest rate unchanged at 11.25% on Thursday, in a unanimous decision following intense pressure from the Government to not raise borrowing costs further.

"From a strictly technical point of view, one could argue that a different calibration of policy would have been preferable," Villamizar said in a written statement. "However, monetary policy is applied in a broader institutional and market context, and preserving stability, credibility, and orderly conditions also carries weight."

The decision surprised analysts, who had almost universally forecast a third consecutive rate increase to curb inflationary pressure. Finance Minister Germán Ávila had threatened to boycott the meeting, which would have made any decision impossible, effectively sabotaging the bank's ability to conduct monetary policy.

Given the circumstances, keeping rates steady "maintained continuity and avoided an outcome that could have introduced additional volatility or unnecessary uncertainty in a delicate situation," Villamizar said. "Although this implies accepting a certain degree of suboptimality relative to a benchmark based purely on models, it also helps safeguard institutional functioning and clarity of the policy framework."

President Gustavo Petro intensified his attacks on the bank after it raised borrowing costs in March, even accusing policymakers of wanting to starve Colombians. In Colombia, the minister not only has a vote on the monetary policy board but also chairs the meetings. In March, Ávila walked out of the meeting as a sign of protest, fueling fears of a full-blown institutional crisis at the April meeting.

"These types of decisions are not without cost," Villamizar added. "To the extent that the stance may fall short of what a stricter technical assessment would require, it could imply that future adjustments must be more forceful to ensure inflation converges to target."

Still, the bank's commitment to act to achieve its 3% inflation target has not changed, Villamizar said. Annual inflation accelerated to 5.6% last month, the highest since 2024. The central bank meets again to set interest rates on June 30. By then, the country will have a newly elected president, who takes office in August.
