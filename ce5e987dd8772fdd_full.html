Productive decline with no solution in sight

25 April 2026 · 03:18 PM

# Productive decline with no solution in sight

There is no way out in sight for the industrial production slump. This concerns an activity that has already shown eight consecutive months of decline since June 2025, with drops of 24.6% in automobile manufacturing and 22.6% in textiles and footwear; 37.7% for agricultural machinery and 50% in computer equipment, television and communications. In other words, declines across the board.

Similar results emerge from a very recent INDEC survey measuring expectations and, in fact, the confidence of business owners regarding key indicators for decision-making, such as domestic demand, exports, imports, economic climate and employment. They hardly speak of a good moment to venture into investment, nor of the overflowing confidence suggested by official discourse.

For now, on the question of the "most important factor limiting their capacity to increase production," 52.5% of responses give a resounding answer: "insufficient domestic demand." If you prefer, insufficient wages or falling real incomes, while competition from imported products accounts for a modest 11.5% and uncertainty for 7.2%.

One peculiarity of the survey is that 58.8% of those surveyed considered access to credit "normal," although 35% answered "difficult" and only 6.2% said "easy." According to specialist reports, credit is expensive, fragmented, and therefore loan defaults by businesses and individuals are growing relentlessly—nothing related to the need to lift productive activity.

No peculiarity but rather approaches to reality: only 15.1% of business owners stated they are thinking of increasing production, while "will not change or will decrease" accounts for a broad 84.9%. It follows then that 79% give the response "will not change" when asked about increasing headcount in the next three months. Or to restate it: factories are overflowing with finished products, in other words, weak demand and unsold inventory.

At the same time, closures are beginning to multiply, even of emblematic brands. In this regard, economist Ricardo Arriazu, considered a master of local liberalism, argues that the so-called deregulatory and transformative model as interpreted by Javier Milei's libertarians generates "more job destruction than creation" and that the bulk of the social cost of the experiment falls on the populous Buenos Aires suburbs.

Data based on surveys by the National Labor Secretariat directly touching on the current economic management show that January 2026 versus August 2023 yields a loss of 72,731 registered industrial jobs, formal employment with pension contributions. Still showing red for construction, the count is 82,393 jobs and 4,759 in commerce. This is job destruction in quantity within productive activities that are precisely those that usually create jobs in quantity and that together concentrate over 30% of GDP. And as Arriazu affirms, official data reveal that Buenos Aires province bore 77,362 lost jobs—more than any other province—accounting for 38% of the total.

Back to the INDEC survey, we also have the "business confidence indicator," covering January 2025 to March 2026, going from red to red: 14 consecutive months with the same sign. In several of them, the negative sign exceeds 20%. Completing and reinforcing the picture is an official report that usually serves as a preview of annual GDP and describes the situation of 16 of the most representative and diverse sectors: from financial activity, agriculture and livestock to industry, commerce and construction. It is INDEC's Economic Estimator, and the key lies in one index that synthesizes everything: it shows eight months of decline between January 2025 and March 2026—that is, half the period.

It's hard to find where those at the Ministry of Economy, run by Luis Caputo, are grounding themselves to argue that Argentina will be "the country that grows most in the next 30 years" and insist on the "18 best months of recent decades." For now, patience: we are as we are, there is what there is, and it is distributed as is plainly evident. Patience again, August approaches with inflation starting with 0, leaving behind the 2% monthly figures mandated since late 2024 and the 3.4% of March 2026, and soon we will be moving through the time of annihilated inflation that Caputo himself has announced.
