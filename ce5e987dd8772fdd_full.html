Productivity slump offers no relief in sight

25 April 2026 · 03:18 PM

# The Productivity Slump, With No Relief in Sight

There is no end in sight for the industrial production collapse. This concerns an activity that has already shown eight consecutive monthly declines since June 2025, with drops of 24.6% in automotive vehicle manufacturing and 22.6% in textiles and footwear; 37.7% for agricultural machinery and 50% in computer equipment, televisions and communications. In other words, declines across the board.

Similar results are reflected in a recent INDEC survey measuring business expectations and, in fact, entrepreneurs' confidence in key indicators when making decisions, such as domestic demand, exports, imports, economic climate, and employment. These results do not suggest a good moment for taking investment risks, nor do they reflect the overflowing confidence of official discourse.

For starters, regarding the factor "most important that is limiting their capacity to increase production," 52.5% of responses give a resounding answer: "insufficient domestic demand." In other words, insufficient wages or declining real incomes, while competition from imported products accounts for just 11.5% and uncertainty 7.2%.

One oddity of the survey is that 58.8% of respondents considered credit access "normal," although 35% answered "difficult" and only 6.2% said "easy." According to specialist reports, credit is expensive and fragmented, and consequently corporate and personal loan defaults continue to grow—nothing related to the need to boost productive activity.

No oddity at all, but rather approximations to reality: only 15.1% of entrepreneurs stated they plan to increase production, while "will not change or will decrease" accounts for a broad 84.9%.

It then follows logically that 79% give the response "will not change" when asked about increasing payroll in the next three months. Or to restate it: factories are overflowing with finished products—in other words, weak demand and unsold inventory.

Simultaneously, closures are becoming more common, even of emblematic brands. Notably, economist Ricardo Arriazu, considered a master of local liberalism, argues that the so-called deregulatory and transformative model as interpreted by Javier Milei's libertarians generates "more job destruction than creation" and that the bulk of the social cost of the experiment falls on the populous Buenos Aires suburbs.

Data based on surveys by the National Labor Secretariat that speak directly to the current economic management show that January 2026 compared to August 2023 represents a loss of 72,731 registered industrial jobs in the formal sector with social security contributions. Construction shows 82,393 job losses and commerce 4,759. This is job destruction within productive activities that typically create jobs in significant numbers and that together account for over 30% of GDP.

And as Arriazu states, official data reveal that Buenos Aires province bore 77,362 job losses—more than any other province, representing 38% of the total.

Returning to the INDEC survey, we also have the "business confidence indicator," which covers the January 2025-March 2026 period, going from red to red: 14 consecutive months with the same sign. In several of them, the negative sign exceeds 20%.

Completing and reinforcing the picture is an official report that usually serves as a preview of annual GDP and describes the situation of 16 of the most representative and diverse sectors: from financial activity, agriculture and livestock to industry, commerce, and construction. It is the INDEC Economic Estimator, and the key lies in an index that synthesizes everything: it shows eight months of decline between January 2025 and March 2026—that is, half the period.

It is hard to see how the Economy Ministry, managed by Luis Caputo, supports its claim that Argentina will be "the country that grows most in the next 30 years" and insists on the "18 best months of recent decades."

For now, patience: we are as we are, there is what there is, and it is distributed as is plainly visible. Patience again—August inflation approaches, starting with 0, leaving behind the 2% monthly figures from late 2024 and the 3.4% of March 2026, and soon we will be living through the time of obliterated inflation that Caputo himself has announced.
