# Middle East conflict: Expensive oil, tight margins: Mexico's challenge

The war in the Middle East reminded us that when energy becomes expensive and scarce, the problem doesn't end with oil. The shock extends to transportation, food, fertilizers, inflation, and finally, to growth. The IMF summarizes it as a large, global, and asymmetric supply shock: at its peak, approximately 13% of the global daily oil flow and about 20% of liquefied natural gas were interrupted; Brent jumped from $72 to $120 per barrel before moderating, while disruptions accumulated in refineries, refined fuels, and supply chains.

Mexico enters this episode from a singular position within the developing world. It is not in the group of the most fragile economies that depend almost entirely on imported energy and lack credible external financing. But neither is it an economy protected by its oil status. Its vulnerability is not that of the typical net importer, but of an economy with very deep energy integration with the United States, especially in natural gas and gasoline. This feature is decisive.

Mexico is the primary destination for U.S. gasoline exports: in 2025 it absorbed 486 thousand barrels daily, equivalent to 54% of the total exported by the U.S. In natural gas, dependence is even more structural: pipeline shipments from the U.S. averaged 6.4 billion cubic feet daily in 2024, the highest annual average recorded. In other words, Mexico exports crude oil, but to sustain much of its fuel consumption and electricity generation, it depends on energy supplies.

This changes the nature of the risk. For Mexico, the problem is not just whether the international crude oil price rises, but how refined product prices and gas availability behave in North America, and how much international price increases are transmitted to domestic costs.

Here there is an important nuance: members of the Board of Governors of Banco de México have noted that the inflationary effects of the conflict could be less severe in Mexico because the price of natural gas in North America has remained relatively stable. This is a first important difference: Mexico is not immune, but it is partially cushioned by its regional integration.

The second difference is macrofinancial. Unlike many vulnerable emerging economies, Mexico has a credible central bank, international reserves of $255.489 billion as of April 1, 2026, and a Flexible Credit Line from the IMF of $24 billion (precautionary). This reduces the risk of a classic balance of payments crisis or an abrupt financial dislocation in the face of external deterioration. But having buffers is not a shield.

In the international environment, emerging sovereign spreads widened, the dollar appreciated, and financial conditions tightened after the conflict escalation. If expensive energy coincides with more expensive financing, adjustment will come through various channels: lower investment, lower consumption, and less fiscal maneuvering room. For Mexico, this constraint is not trivial. The IMF projected that gross public sector debt would reach 58.9% of GDP at the end of 2025, in a context where fiscal consolidation remains a pending task and the financial cost of global debt has risen.

Neither should we lose sight of the fact that if the conflict continues to drive up prices for diesel, jet fuel, and fertilizers, the impact will not be purely energetic. Logistical deterioration and higher agricultural input costs pressure food and transportation prices. At the global level, it was already warned that another 45 million people could fall into food insecurity and that the worldwide total would exceed 360 million. Mexico does not face this extreme risk at the same magnitude as poorer economies, but it would suffer from the combination of higher import costs, pressure on processed food prices, and additional tensions on household real income.

That's why the relevant discussion for Mexico is not whether it belongs to the most vulnerable group in the developing world. It doesn't. The discussion is different: whether its macroeconomic strengths are sufficient to compensate for an energy structure that remains incomplete. And there the answer is more nuanced.

Mexico has more defenses than many emerging economies, but maintains high operational dependence on imported fuels and gas, along with fiscal space that is not ample. Its risk does not look like an immediate crisis, but rather as gradual erosion: more inflationary pressure, lower budget margin, and greater exposure to external disruptions at a time of global uncertainty. Mexico is not on the front lines of collapse, but neither is it outside the impact zone.
