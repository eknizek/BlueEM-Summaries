# Middle East Conflict: High Oil Prices and Tight Margins Challenge Mexico

The war in the Middle East reminded us that when energy becomes expensive and scarce, the problem doesn't end with oil. The shock spreads to transportation, food, fertilizers, inflation, and ultimately to growth. The IMF summarizes it as a large, global, and asymmetric supply shock: at its peak, approximately 13% of the daily global oil flow and about 20% of liquefied natural gas were interrupted; Brent crude jumped from $72 to $120 per barrel before moderating, while disruptions accumulated in refineries, refined fuels, and logistics chains.

Mexico enters this episode from a singular position within the developing world. It is not among the most fragile economies that depend almost entirely on imported energy and lack credible external financing. But neither is it an economy protected by its oil status. Its vulnerability is not that of a typical net importer, but rather that of an economy with very deep energy integration with the United States, especially in natural gas and gasoline.

This characteristic is decisive. Mexico is the principal destination for U.S. gasoline exports: in 2025 it absorbed 486,000 barrels daily, equivalent to 54% of total U.S. exports. In natural gas, dependence is even more structural: pipeline shipments from the U.S. averaged 6.4 billion cubic feet daily in 2024, the highest annual average on record. In other words, Mexico exports crude oil, but to sustain much of its fuel consumption and electricity generation, it depends on energy supplies.

This changes the nature of the risk. For Mexico, the problem is not only whether international crude prices rise, but how refined product and gas prices and availability behave in North America, and how much international price increases are transmitted to domestic costs.

An important nuance here: members of Mexico's Central Bank Board have noted that the inflationary effects of the conflict could be less severe in Mexico because natural gas prices in North America have remained relatively stable. This is a first important difference: Mexico is not immune, but is partially cushioned by its regional integration.

The second difference is macrofinancial. Unlike many vulnerable emerging markets, Mexico has a credible central bank, international reserves of $255.489 billion as of April 1, 2026, and a precautionary Flexible Credit Line from the IMF of $24 billion. This reduces the risk of a classic balance-of-payments crisis or an abrupt financial dislocation from external deterioration. But having buffers is not a shield.

In the international environment, emerging sovereign spreads have widened, the dollar has appreciated, and financial conditions have tightened following the conflict escalation. If expensive energy coincides with more expensive financing, adjustment will come through several channels: lower investment, lower consumption, and less fiscal maneuvering room. For Mexico, that constraint is not trivial. The IMF projected that gross public sector debt will reach 58.9% of GDP at the end of 2025, in a context where fiscal consolidation remains a pending task and the financial cost of global debt has risen.

Nor should it be overlooked that if the conflict continues to make diesel, jet fuel, and fertilizers more expensive, the impact will not be merely energetic. Logistics deterioration and higher agricultural input costs pressure food and transport prices. Globally, it was already warned that another 45 million people could fall into food insecurity and the worldwide total would exceed 360 million. Mexico does not face that extreme risk to the same extent as poorer economies, but it would suffer from the combination of higher import costs, pressure on processed foods, and additional tensions on household real income.

For this reason, the relevant discussion for Mexico is not whether it belongs to the most vulnerable group in the developing world. It does not. The discussion is another: whether its macroeconomic strengths are sufficient to compensate for an still incomplete energy structure. And there the answer is more nuanced. Mexico has more defenses than many emerging markets, but maintains high operational dependence on imported fuels and gas, plus fiscal space that is not ample. Its risk does not look like an immediate crisis, but rather as gradual erosion: more inflationary pressure, less budgetary margin, and greater exposure to external interruptions at a moment of global uncertainty.

Mexico is not on the front line of collapse, but neither is it outside the zone of impact.
