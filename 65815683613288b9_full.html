Middle East Conflict: High Oil Prices and Tight Margins Challenge Mexico

19 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

The war in the Middle East reminded us that when energy becomes expensive and scarce, the problem doesn't end with oil. The shock spreads to transportation, food, fertilizers, inflation, and finally, to growth. The IMF summarizes it as a large, global, and asymmetric supply shock: at its peak, approximately 13% of the world's daily oil flow and about 20% of liquefied natural gas were disrupted; Brent jumped from 72 to 120 dollars per barrel before moderating, while disruptions accumulated in refineries, refined fuels, and logistics chains.

Mexico enters this episode from a singular position within the developing world. It is not among the most fragile economies that depend almost entirely on imported energy and lack credible external financing. But neither is it an economy protected by its oil status. Its vulnerability is not that of a typical net importer, but rather that of an economy with very deep energy integration with the United States, especially in natural gas and gasoline. This trait is decisive.

Mexico is the main destination for US gasoline exports: in 2025 it absorbed 486 thousand barrels per day, equivalent to 54% of the total exported by the US. In natural gas, dependence is even more structural: pipeline shipments from the US averaged 6.4 billion cubic feet per day in 2024, the highest annual average on record. In other words, Mexico exports crude oil, but to sustain much of its fuel consumption and electricity generation, it depends on energy supplies. This changes the nature of the risk.

For Mexico, the problem is not only whether the international price of crude rises, but how refined products and gas prices and availability behave in North America, and how much international price increases are transmitted to domestic costs.

Here there is an important nuance: members of Mexico's Central Bank Board have pointed out that the inflationary effects of the conflict could be less severe in Mexico because natural gas prices in North America have remained relatively stable. This is an important first difference: Mexico is not immune, but it is partially cushioned by its regional integration.

The second difference is macrofinancial. Unlike many vulnerable emerging markets, Mexico has a credible central bank, international reserves of 255,489 million dollars as of April 1, 2026, and an IMF Flexible Credit Line of 24 billion dollars (precautionary). This reduces the risk of a classic balance of payments crisis or abrupt financial dislocation in the face of external deterioration. But having buffers is not a shield.

In the international environment, emerging market sovereign spreads widened, the dollar appreciated, and financial conditions tightened after the conflict escalation. If expensive energy coincides with more expensive financing, adjustment will come through several channels: lower investment, lower consumption, and less fiscal room for maneuver. For Mexico, that restriction is not trivial. The IMF projected that the gross debt of the public sector will reach 58.9% of GDP at the end of 2025, in a context where fiscal consolidation remains an outstanding task and the financial cost of global debt has increased.

One must also remember that if the conflict continues driving up the price of diesel, jet fuel, and fertilizers, the impact will not be only energetic. Logistics deterioration and higher agricultural input costs pressure food and transportation prices. On a global scale, it has already been warned that another 45 million people could fall into food insecurity and that the world total would exceed 360 million. Mexico does not face that extreme risk in the same magnitude as poorer economies, but it would suffer from the combination of higher import costs, pressure on processed foods, and additional tensions on real household income.

Therefore, the relevant discussion for Mexico is not whether it belongs to the most vulnerable group in the developing world. It does not. The discussion is another: whether its macroeconomic strengths are sufficient to compensate for an energy structure that is still incomplete. And there the answer is more nuanced. Mexico has more defenses than many emerging markets, but maintains high operational dependence on imported fuels and gas, in addition to fiscal space that is not ample. Its risk does not look like an immediate crisis, but rather a gradual erosion: more inflationary pressure, smaller budget margins, and greater exposure to external interruptions at a moment of global uncertainty. Mexico is not on the front line of collapse, but neither outside the impact zone.
