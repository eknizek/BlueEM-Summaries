# Middle East Conflict: High Oil, Tight Margins Challenge Mexico

The Middle East war reminded us that when energy becomes expensive and scarce, the problem doesn't end with oil. The shock extends to transportation, food, fertilizers, inflation, and ultimately growth. The IMF summarizes it as a large, global, and asymmetric supply shock: at its peak, approximately 13% of the daily global oil flow and about 20% of liquefied natural gas were disrupted; Brent jumped from 72 to 120 dollars per barrel before moderating, while disruptions accumulated in refineries, refined fuels, and logistics chains.

Mexico enters this episode from a singular position within the developing world. It is not among the most fragile economies that depend almost entirely on imported energy and lack credible external financing. But neither is it an economy protected by its oil status. Its vulnerability is not that of a typical net importer, but of an economy with very deep energy integration with the United States, especially in natural gas and gasoline. This characteristic is decisive.

Mexico is the principal destination for U.S. gasoline exports: in 2025 it absorbed 486,000 barrels daily, equivalent to 54% of the total exported by the U.S. In natural gas, the dependence is even more structural: pipeline shipments from the U.S. averaged 6.4 billion cubic feet daily in 2024, the highest annual average on record. In other words, Mexico exports crude oil, but to sustain much of its fuel consumption and electricity generation, it depends on energy supplies. This changes the nature of the risk.

For Mexico, the problem is not only whether the international crude price rises, but how refined fuel and gas prices and availability behave in North America, and how much the international price increase is transmitted to domestic costs.

Here there is an important nuance: members of Mexico's Central Bank Board have noted that the inflationary effects of the conflict could be less severe in Mexico because natural gas prices in North America have remained relatively stable. This is a first important difference: Mexico is not immune, but it is partially cushioned by its regional positioning.

The second difference is macrofinancial. Unlike many vulnerable emerging markets, Mexico has a credible central bank, international reserves of 255,489 billion dollars as of April 1, 2026, and an IMF Flexible Credit Line of 24 billion dollars (precautionary). This reduces the risk of a classic balance of payments crisis or abrupt financial dislocation in the face of external deterioration. But having buffers is not a shield.

In the international environment, emerging sovereign spreads widened, the dollar appreciated, and financial conditions tightened after the conflict escalation. If expensive energy coincides with more expensive financing, adjustment will arrive through multiple channels: lower investment, lower consumption, and less fiscal maneuvering room. For Mexico, this restriction is not trivial. The IMF projected that gross public sector debt will reach 58.9% of GDP by the end of 2025, in a context where fiscal consolidation remains a pending task and the financial cost of global debt has risen.

Nor should it be overlooked that if the conflict continues driving up the cost of diesel, jet fuel, and fertilizers, the impact will not be only energetic. Logistics deterioration and higher agricultural input costs pressure food and transportation prices. On a global scale, it has already been warned that another 45 million people could fall into food insecurity and that the global total would exceed 360 million. Mexico does not face that extreme risk to the same extent as poorer economies, but would suffer from the combination of higher import costs, pressure on processed foods, and additional tensions on household real income.

Therefore, the relevant discussion for Mexico is not whether it belongs to the most vulnerable group in the developing world. It does not. The discussion is another: whether its macroeconomic strengths are sufficient to compensate for an energy structure that is still incomplete. And there the answer is more nuanced. Mexico has more defenses than many emerging markets, but maintains high operational dependence on imported fuels and gas, in addition to fiscal space that is not ample. Its risk does not look like an immediate crisis, but rather as gradual erosion: more inflationary pressure, smaller budgetary margin, and greater exposure to external disruptions at a moment of global uncertainty. Mexico is not on the front line of collapse, but neither is it outside the impact zone.
