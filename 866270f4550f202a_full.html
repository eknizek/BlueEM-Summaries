# Bets drain capital from consumption, says CEO of Mondial

Giovanni Martins Cardoso has divided attention. He plans the next strategic steps for MK Group, owner of the Mondial and Aiwa brands. He is also preparing for a trip to Bled, Slovenia, where the World Masters rowing championship will take place in September—a sport in which he is part of the Corinthians team, despite being such a Palmeiras fan that he has a box at the club's stadium. On the door is a plaque with the phrase: "Palmeiras has Mondial."

Having approximately 40% market share in the small appliances market means he doesn't even consider an IPO.

**Does Mondial have plans to expand abroad?**

The brand is in the USA, Argentina, Bolivia, Paraguay and Guatemala. This represents 2% of our business. We expect to jump to a number between 4% and 5%. The Argentine market changed under the Milei government?

There was a resumption of payment flows. Before, it was centralized. Money left the buyer, went to the Central Bank and was then released to the seller. Today, you negotiate directly, without intermediaries. We started to explore this market more. Entry became easier and we managed to export more. We set up a branch within Argentina to make deliveries very quickly and be our differentiator. The Argentine market is not used to fast deliveries of imported products. We want to be among the three main brands in the country by 2027.

**How is competitiveness in Brazil against Asian brands?**

The TV segment is the most attacked by Korean and Chinese companies. In small appliances, it's much less. It's a low value-added market, freight is expensive and logistics are difficult. The TV and white goods lines began to attract [Asians] because the margins are larger.

**Does the war scenario affect Mondial's business?**

The entire market is affected. I buy plastic from Braskem at the dollar rate. I was at a trade fair in China. There, the interest rate is 1% per year. In Brazil, I'm paying 18%. Brazilian businesspeople are heroes. It causes amazement abroad.

**Are you pessimistic about the macroeconomic scenario?**

These betting apps consume R$400 billion per year. It's absurd. It takes money away from consumption. The entire small appliances market totals R$180 billion. It's less than half of what bets consume. There is a very large drainage of capital, which leads to indebtedness. Families are in debt and gambling is one of the vectors. This should be prohibited. [The government] collects taxes, but the headaches families have with this and the budget nightmares—if the government analyzes it—don't compensate. Then it has to do a debt relief program...

**What brand perception does Mondial seek with consumers?**

The base of the pyramid needs good, cheap and competitive products. Some people say: "oh, I'm going to sell to class A." You won't sell anything! Our business is serving the masses. It's volume. Mondial serves all segments and our equipment is designed for all audiences. A top-tier brand in the market bills R$1 billion per year. I bill R$7 billion. We are class G. G for people. Wherever there are people, we're selling.

**Are there plans to vertically integrate production by buying other companies?**

We grow organically. I don't like buying companies because they come with closets full of skeletons. Not all of them, but it happens.

**And an IPO?**

No way! It's not part of our strategy to have more people here. Our market share increase is annual.

**What's new, thinking about products, from Mondial?**

It's a small appliance with a brushless motor and direct current, a new line of personal care products. The efficiency is enormous. It has rare earths in the rotor and permanent magnets. This motor started being developed three years ago, but now it's gained scale and speed. It's starting to reduce cost and become viable. It was five times more expensive than a normal motor. Today, it's twice as expensive.

**Profile: Giovanni Cardoso**

Born in Palmas, Paraná. He has a degree in electrical engineering from Universidade Presbiteriana Mackenzie, with specializations in marketing and neurolinguistic programming. He worked for national and multinational electronics and small appliances companies before founding Mondial in 2000. He also owns the Aiwa brand in Brazil.
