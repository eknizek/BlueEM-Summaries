# Betting Apps Draw Capital Away From Consumption, Says Mondial CEO

Giovanni Martins Cardoso has divided attention. He plans the next strategic steps for MK Group, which owns the Mondial and Aiwa brands. He is also preparing for a trip to Bled, Slovenia, where the World Masters rowing championship will take place in September—a sport in which he is part of Corinthians' team, despite being such a Palmeiras fan that he has a box at the club's stadium. On the door hangs a sign that reads: "Palmeiras has Mondial."

Having approximately 40% market share in the small appliances market means he doesn't even consider an IPO. Does Mondial have plans to expand abroad? The brand is in the USA, Argentina, Bolivia, Paraguay, and Guatemala. This represents 2% of our business. We expect to jump to a number between 4% and 5%. Did the Argentine market change under the Milei government? There was a resumption of payment flow. Before, it was centralized. Money would leave the buyer, go to the Central Bank, and then be released to the seller. Today, you negotiate directly, with no intermediary. We started exploring this market more. Entry became easier and we were able to export more. We set up a subsidiary within Argentina to make deliveries very quickly and make that our differentiator. The Argentine market is not used to fast deliveries of imported products. We want to be among the three main brands in the country by 2027.

How is competitiveness in Brazil against Asian brands? The TV segment is most attacked by Korean and Chinese companies. In small appliances, it's much less. It's a low value-added market; freight is expensive and logistics are difficult. The TV line and white goods began to attract [Asians] because margins are larger.

Does the global situation affect Mondial's business? The entire market is affected. I buy plastic from Braskem at the dollar rate. I attended a trade fair in China. There, the interest rate is 1% per year. In Brazil, I'm paying 18%. Brazilian entrepreneurs are heroes. It causes astonishment abroad.

Are you pessimistic about the macroeconomic scenario? These betting apps consume 400 billion reais per year. It's absurd. It drains money from consumption. The entire small appliances market totals 180 billion reais. It's less than half of betting apps. There is a very large drainage of capital, which leads to indebtedness. Families are in debt and gambling is one of the vectors. This should be prohibited. [The government] takes the tax, but the headaches families have with this and the budget nightmares—if the government analyzes it, they don't compensate. Then you have to create debt relief programs...

What brand perception does Mondial seek with consumers? The base of the pyramid needs good, cheap, and competitive products. Some people say: "Oh, I'm going to sell to class A." You won't sell anything! Our business is serving the masses. It's about volume. Mondial serves all segments and our equipment is designed for all audiences. A top-tier brand in the market bills 1 billion reais per year. I bill 7 billion. We are class G. G for people. Wherever there are people, we're selling.

Are there plans to verticalize production by buying other companies? We grow organically. I don't like buying companies because they come with skeletons in the closet. Not all of them, but it happens. And IPO? No way! It's not part of our strategy to get more people here. Our market share increase is annual. What's new in terms of Mondial products? It's a small appliance with a brushless motor and direct current—a new line of personal care products. The effectiveness is enormous. It has rare earths in the rotor and permanent magnets. This motor started being developed three years ago, but now it has gained scale and speed. It's starting to reduce costs and become viable. It was five times more expensive than a normal motor. Today, it's twice as expensive.

**Profile: Giovanni Cardoso**

Born in Palmas, Paraná. Graduated in electrical engineering from Universidade Presbiteriana Mackenzie, with specializations in marketing and neuro-linguistic programming. He worked for national and multinational electronics and small appliances companies before founding Mondial in 2000. He also owns the Aiwa brand in Brazil.
