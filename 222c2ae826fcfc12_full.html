# At Itaú's Recovery unit, debt renegotiation exceeds R$ 1 billion

In February 2025, Brazil recorded 81.7 million indebted citizens, an increase of 8.9% compared to the previous year. On the other side, the NPL market, which involves the purchase of delinquent debts by specialized companies, moved R$ 34 billion in 2025, with Recovery, from Itaú Unibanco, being one of the main players. The company manages R$ 150 billion in delinquent credits and completed 6 million renegotiation agreements last year. Recovery offers discounts of 50% to 70% on debts, with the possibility of up to 99%. The market has diversified, with more institutions selling portfolios, including fintechs and retailers. For 2026, Recovery plans to use artificial intelligence to improve negotiations and expand its operations in loans backed by vehicles, seeking to increase its market share in this segment.

A monthly survey by Serasa Experian, the Delinquency and Debt Negotiation Map for February this year pointed to a total of 81.7 million indebted Brazilians, an increase of 8.9% over the same period in 2025 and the highest number in the entire historical series, which began ten years ago. As these indicators reach record levels and draw attention, another area, rarely discussed, gains credit: the NPL market, based on the purchase of delinquent debt portfolios from financial institutions by companies specialized in debt renegotiation. In 2025, the sector moved R$ 34 billion in Brazil, an annual jump of 21.4%, according to Recovery data. The company is Itaú Unibanco's arm in this space. And despite not receiving much attention, it plays a relevant role in the operations of the country's largest private bank.

"Itaú is an ocean liner, we are the lifeboat," says Bruno Russo Franco, CEO of Recovery, in an interview with NeoFeed. "But even in the final cycle, which is collection, we are on the main track of any bank, which is credit. And we are not in a small market."

Recovery's numbers give a good sense of what is at stake. The company ended 2025 with revenue of just over R$ 1 billion – basically, the volume of recovered debts – and a package of 6 million renegotiation agreements. Currently, the company has approximately R$ 150 billion in delinquent credits under management and 33 million debtors in its database, which is predominantly made up of individuals, a segment that represents approximately 95% of purchased portfolios.

The portfolios are purchased from Itaú, as well as other financial institutions and economic sectors. These agreements are based on the face value of debts, plus an additional 60 days of interest. At Recovery, the average volume recovered from portfolios is 15%, which is already embedded in the price of these transactions. On the renegotiation side, the company's model includes offering discounts, on average, of 50% to 70% of the debt, but which can reach 99%. There is no limit on the number of installments, but rather on the value of the bills, necessarily above R$ 50. The greater the number of installments, the lower the discount.

One of the main players in this market, Recovery was founded in 2000 in Argentina. It subsequently expanded operations to Mexico and Brazil, the only operation that, years later, took off. In 2011, BTG Pactual purchased control of it. At the end of 2015, Itaú took control of the operation. The R$ 1.2 billion deal marked the bank's entry into the segment and, conversely, BTG Pactual's exit from this space. Eleven years later, Recovery is one of the main players in the market. But it is not alone in this race. The main competitors are Return Capital, from Santander; RCB Investimentos, from Bradesco; and Arc4, which acts as BTG Pactual's arm for individuals and marked the bank's return to the sector. Another player is Ativos, from Banco do Brasil, but more concentrated on the bank's own portfolios.

Portfolio diversification is being accompanied by two market changes. "The first was an anticipation in the aging of debts," Franco says. "Large banks had a high concentration of portfolios with longer delays, from 4, 5 to 7 years. And the cost of keeping these operations in-house." According to the executive, as they realized they could anticipate this revenue and, at the same time, gain a tax benefit from selling an asset that was already provisioned, these institutions began to sell portfolios, on average, within a timeframe of one and a half to two years.

The second came in the diversification of who was selling these portfolios. First, with the advance of credit fintechs, digital banks and credit cooperatives. Then with retailers. "Five years ago, you had 15 sellers in the market. Today, I speak with 150 per year and, in practice, 75 already sell," Franco says. Recovery data, whose market share in these transactions is around 35%, shows, for example, that, considering transactions conducted in the market as a whole, fintechs and digital banks registered annual growth of 26% in the volume of delinquent credits transferred in 2025.

According to Franco, there are more sectors willing to boost this account. He doesn't reveal names, but says that more recently, Recovery began buying portfolios from cosmetics and education groups. And that these companies are leading the way for other players in these segments. For 2026, the CEO adds other ingredients to enhance growth prospects in this base of sellers. And, consequently, the volume of delinquent portfolios available to the sector – today the estimated volume of inventory managed by the market as a whole is around R$ 800 billion.

"The expectation is that Bradesco, which did not sell any portfolios in 2025, will return to the market," he says. "And there is also the case of Caixa, which has an absurd inventory and made a first transfer now in February. The last one had been in 2015. Now, it seems they are coming with energy for 2026 onwards."

On the other side of the segment, collection, he highlights the record number of indebted Brazilians. And he adds: "We have never had so many debts per CPF in the country. Three years ago, the average was 2.2 debts. Today, that index is at 3.5."

One of Recovery's focus areas in 2026 will be to improve negotiations with these customers – with more flexibility and in real time. And for this, the company's main bet is advances in generative artificial intelligence, with models in training and production for about three months.

"Today, AI can actually converse. It's as if this customer were entering a store and could negotiate," Franco says. "Before, we couldn't. Negotiation was more packaged and we didn't have the technology for it."

In addition to real-time negotiations, which have already brought a reduction in granted discounts in a short time, the technology has allowed Recovery to advance in offers that combine different debts from the same CPF, which simplifies the process and opens opportunities for better agreement terms. In line with what Itaú has done, what made the company capable of making these moves was the migration, about three years ago, of all its back office infrastructure to the cloud, in this case, Azure, from Microsoft.
