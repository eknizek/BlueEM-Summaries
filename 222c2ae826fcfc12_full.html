# At Itaú's Recovery unit, debt renegotiation 'gains credit' and surpasses R$ 1 billion

In February 2025, Brazil recorded 81.7 million indebted Brazilians, an increase of 8.9% compared to the previous year. On the other hand, the NPL market, which involves the purchase of defaulted debts by specialized companies, moved R$ 34 billion in 2025, with Recovery, from Itaú Unibanco, being one of the main players. The company manages R$ 150 billion in non-performing loans and completed 6 million renegotiation agreements last year. Recovery offers discounts of 50% to 70% on debts, with the possibility of up to 99%. The market has diversified, with more institutions selling portfolios, including fintechs and retailers. For 2026, Recovery plans to use artificial intelligence to improve negotiations and expand its operations in loans secured by vehicles, seeking to increase its market share in this segment.

As monthly surveys from Serasa Experian's Map of Default and Debt Negotiation for February this year pointed to a total of 81.7 million indebted Brazilians, an increase of 8.9% over the same period in 2025 and the highest number in the entire historical series begun a decade ago, another area, rarely discussed, is gaining credit: the NPL market, based on the purchase of non-performing debt portfolios from financial institutions by companies specialized in debt renegotiation.

In 2025, the sector moved R$ 34 billion in Brazil, an annual jump of 21.4%, according to Recovery data. The company is Itaú Unibanco's arm in this space. And even without much media attention, it plays a relevant role in the operations of the country's largest private bank. "Itaú is a transatlantic ship, we are the lifeboat," says Bruno Russo Franco, CEO of Recovery, in an interview with NeoFeed. "But even in the final cycle, which is collection, we are in the main pipeline of any bank, which is credit. And we are not in a small market."

Recovery's numbers give a good sense of what is at stake. The company ended 2025 with revenue of just over R$ 1 billion – basically, the volume of recovered debts – and a package of 6 million renegotiation agreements. Currently, the company manages approximately R$ 150 billion in non-performing loans and has 33 million debtors in its base, which is formed predominantly by individuals, a segment that represents approximately 95% of purchased portfolios. Portfolios are purchased from Itaú, as well as other financial institutions and sectors of the economy. These agreements are based on the face value of debts, plus an additional 60 days of interest.

At Recovery, the average volume recovered from portfolios is 15%, which is already embedded in the price of these transactions. On the renegotiation end, the company's model includes offering discounts of, on average, 50% to 70% of the debt, but which can reach 99%. There is no limit on the number of installments, but rather on the value of the bills, necessarily above R$ 50. The greater the number of installments, the lower the discount.

One of the main players in this market, Recovery was founded in 2000 in Argentina and subsequently expanded operations to Mexico and Brazil, the only operation that, years later, took off. In 2011, its control was purchased by BTG Pactual. At the end of 2015, in turn, Itaú took control of the operation. The R$ 1.2 billion agreement marked the bank's entry into the segment and, conversely, BTG Pactual's exit from this space. Eleven years later, Recovery is one of the main market players. But it is not alone in this race. The main rivals are Return Capital, from Santander; RCB Investimentos, from Bradesco; and Arc4, which acts as BTG Pactual's arm for individuals and marked the bank's return to the sector. Another player is Ativos, from Banco do Brasil, more concentrated, however, on the bank's own portfolios.

Portfolio diversification has been accompanied by two changes in the market. "The first was an early aging of debts," Franco says. "The large banks had a large concentration of portfolios with longer delays, of 4, 5 to even 7 years. And the cost of keeping these operations in-house." According to the executive, upon realizing that they could anticipate this revenue and, at the same time, obtain a tax benefit from selling an already provisioned asset, these institutions began selling portfolios on average within a timeframe of one and a half to two years.

The second came in the diversification of those selling these portfolios. First, with the advance of credit fintechs, digital banks, and credit cooperatives. Then, with retailers. "Five years ago, you had 15 sellers in the market. Today, I speak with 150 a year and, in practice, 75 already sell," Franco says. Recovery data, whose market share in these transactions is approximately 35%, show, for example, that, considering transactions carried out in the market as a whole, fintechs and digital banks recorded annual growth of 26% in the volume of non-performing loans ceded in 2025.

According to Franco, there are more sectors willing to reinforce this count. He does not reveal names, but says that more recently, Recovery began buying portfolios from cosmetics and education groups. And that these companies are leading the queue of other players in these segments.

For 2026, the CEO adds other ingredients to enhance growth prospects in this base of cedents. And, consequently, the volume of non-performing portfolios available to the sector – today the estimated volume of inventory under management by the market as a whole is approximately R$ 800 billion. "The expectation is that Bradesco, which did not sell any portfolio in 2025, returns to the market," he says. "And there is also the case of Caixa, which has an absurd inventory and made a first cession in February. The last one had been in 2015. Now, it seems they are coming with energy for 2026 onwards."

On the other end of the segment, collection, he emphasizes the record of indebted Brazilians. And adds: "We have never had so many debts per CPF in the country. Three years ago, the average was 2.2 debts. Today, this index is at 3.5".

Real-time artificial intelligence

In this scenario, one of Recovery's focuses in 2026 will be to improve negotiations with these clients – with more flexibility and in real time. And for this, the company's major bet is advances in generative artificial intelligence, with models in training and production for approximately three months. "Today, AI can actually have a conversation. It's as if this customer was entering a store and could negotiate," Franco says. "Before, we couldn't. The negotiation was more packaged and we didn't have the technology for it." In addition to real-time negotiations, which in a short time have already brought a reduction in granted discounts, the technology has allowed Recovery to advance in offers that reconcile different debts from the same CPF, which simplifies the process and opens opportunities for better agreement conditions.

In line with what Itaú has done, what made the company capable of making these moves was the migration, approximately three years ago, of all its back office infrastructure to the cloud, in this case, Azure, from Microsoft.
