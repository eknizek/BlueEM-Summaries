# Colombia's U.S. Exports Surge as Investment Decline Signals Confidence Concerns

Washington emerges as alternative market for the country amid Ecuador's tariff increases on exports.

The start of 2026 delivered a dual signal for Colombia's economy: while trade with the United States gains momentum, foreign investment is beginning to show signs of caution—a contrast that raises alerts about the country's competitiveness, according to an analysis by the Colombian-American Chamber of Commerce (AmCham Colombia).

Between January and February 2026, Colombian exports to that market reached US$2,669.8 million, a growth of 22.5% compared to the same period in 2025, cementing its role as the primary commercial destination.

This result reflects not only greater volume but also a transformation in export structure. Non-mining-energy goods accounted for 71% of sales, totaling US$1,891.5 million and growing 24%. The United States is consolidating itself as the primary platform for diversifying Colombia's export basket.

Within this segment, agriculture exported US$1,053 million with a 13% increase, while manufacturing grew 40.4%. The depth of this relationship is also evident in the business base. More than 3,000 Colombian companies export annually to that market, equivalent to roughly one-third of total exporting firms. In January 2026 alone, 1,229 companies had already made sales to the United States, confirming that the commercial relationship is not concentrated among major players but represents broad opportunities for different business scales.

Regarding imports, the relationship maintains a strategic character. Approximately 70% of goods from that country are products not manufactured in Colombia and are fundamental to national production chains. In January 2026, these imports totaled US$1,240.8 million. Although a 7.1% decline was recorded compared to the previous year, the link continues to provide key supply for local industry.

However, the picture changes when analyzing foreign investment. The United States remained in 2025 as Colombia's primary investor, with flows of US$3,375 million, equivalent to 29.4% of the total received.

The alert is not in the total amount but in the quality of investment arriving in the country. Fresh capital fell from US$7,427 million to US$5,408 million, a reduction close to 27%. In contrast, profit reinvestment increased from US$4,818 million to US$5,112 million, indicating that already-installed companies maintain their commitment, but the same dynamism is not observed in new capital.

The message is clear: those already in Colombia continue betting on the country, but the arrival of new investors is cooling. This signal reflects a perception of caution regarding the investment environment.

"This situation must be a wake-up call for the country and especially for those aspiring to govern it. Colombia needs to recover competitiveness and confidence as an investment destination," warned María Claudia Lacouture, president of AmCham Colombia. The business leader insisted that maintaining attractive country discourse is not enough. "Investment is not sustained by discourse but by clear rules," referring to the need for legal certainty and regulatory stability.

This behavior is more concerning when contrasted with the regional environment. Latin America and the Caribbean received US$188,962 million in foreign direct investment, with growth of 7.1%, according to figures from Cepal. Brazil and Mexico concentrated 38% and 24% of those flows, respectively, while the United States consolidated itself as the primary source of investment in the region, with 38% of the total.

The region is attracting capital, but Colombia is losing relative traction in attracting new resources. This suggests that the problem is not availability of investment but internal conditions.

## Ecuador Pressures and Opens Window to United States

In parallel, the commercial front also presents a strategic realignment. The 50% increase in Ecuador's tariffs on Colombian products is forcing companies to reconsider their export destinations. An AmCham Colombia analysis identified that 1,208 products currently sold to Ecuador can already enter the U.S. market, making them immediate candidates for diversification.

These are goods with export history, regulatory compliance, and established commercial channels, allowing viable redirection in the short term, between zero and nine months, according to the study. Additionally, there are 905 products with potential in the United States that, although facing barriers such as certifications and sanitary requirements, represent an expansion opportunity in the medium term.

The United States thus emerges as the primary alternative market against regional commercial shocks. However, capitalizing on these opportunities will depend on business capabilities, logistics, and access to commercial channels.

Beyond trade and investment, the United States remains a structural partner for Colombia's economy. In 2025, it was the primary source of remittances, with US$6,455.1 million, equivalent to 49% of the total received. It also led the flow of international tourists. Between January and February 2026, 177,531 travelers from that country entered, representing 22% of non-resident visitors arriving in Colombia.
