# Colombian Exports to U.S. Surge, but Investment Decline Signals Confidence Warning

Washington emerges as an alternative market for the country amid Ecuador's tariff increase on exports.

The start of 2026 left a dual signal for the Colombian economy: while trade with the United States gains momentum, foreign investment is beginning to show signs of caution, a contrast that raises alarms about the country's competitiveness, according to an analysis by the Colombian American Chamber of Commerce (AmCham Colombia).

Between January and February 2026, Colombian exports to that market reached US$2,669.8 million, a growth of 22.5% compared to the same period in 2025, cementing its role as the primary trade destination.

This result reflects not only greater volume but also a transformation in the export structure. 71% of sales corresponded to non-mining-energy goods, which totaled US$1,891.5 million and grew 24%. The United States is consolidating as the main platform for diversifying Colombia's export basket.

Within this segment, agriculture exported US$1,053 million with an increase of 13%, while manufacturing grew 40.4%. The depth of this relationship is also evident in the business base. More than 3,000 Colombian companies export to that market annually, equivalent to about one-third of the total number of exporting firms. In January 2026 alone, 1,229 companies had already made sales to the United States, confirming that the commercial relationship is not concentrated among large players but represents broad opportunities for different business scales.

Regarding imports, the relationship maintains a strategic character. Nearly 70% of goods from that country correspond to products not manufactured in Colombia and are fundamental for national production chains. In January 2026, these imports totaled US$1,240.8 million. Although a decline of 7.1% was recorded compared to the previous year, the link continues to provide key supplies for local industry.

However, the picture changes when analyzing foreign investment. The United States remained in 2025 as the principal investor in Colombia, with flows of US$3,375 million, equivalent to 29.4% of the total received.

The alert is not in the total amount but in the quality of investment arriving in the country. Fresh capital fell from US$7,427 million to US$5,408 million, a reduction close to 27%. In contrast, profit reinvestment increased from US$4,818 million to US$5,112 million, indicating that already-established companies maintain their commitment, but the same dynamism is not observed in new capital.

The message is clear: those already in Colombia continue to bet on the country, but the arrival of new investors is cooling. This signal reflects a perception of caution regarding the investment environment.

"This panorama should be a wake-up call for the country and especially for those aspiring to govern it. Colombia needs to recover competitiveness and confidence as an investment destination," warned María Claudia Lacouture, president of AmCham Colombia. The business leader insisted that maintaining discourse about being an attractive country is not enough. "Investment is not sustained with rhetoric, but with clear rules," referring to the need for legal certainty and regulatory stability.

This behavior becomes more concerning when contrasted with the regional environment. Latin America and the Caribbean received US$188,962 million in foreign direct investment, with growth of 7.1%, according to ECLAC figures. Brazil and Mexico concentrated 38% and 24% of those flows, respectively, while the United States consolidated itself as the primary source of investment in the region, with 38% of the total.

The region is capturing capital, but Colombia is losing relative traction in attracting new resources. This suggests that the problem is not availability of investment but internal conditions.

## Ecuador Pressures and Opens Window to the United States

In parallel, the commercial front also presents a strategic realignment. Ecuador's 50% increase in tariffs on Colombian products is forcing companies to reconsider their export destinations. An AmCham Colombia analysis identified that 1,208 products currently sold to Ecuador can already enter the U.S. market, making them immediate candidates for diversification.

These are goods with export history, regulatory compliance, and established commercial channels, allowing viable redirection in the short term, between zero and nine months, according to the study. Additionally, there are 905 products with potential in the United States that, although facing barriers such as certifications and sanitary requirements, represent an expansion opportunity in the medium term.

The United States thus emerges as the principal alternative market in the face of regional trade shocks. However, capitalizing on these opportunities will depend on business capabilities, logistics, and access to commercial channels.

Beyond trade and investment, the United States remains a structural partner for the Colombian economy. In 2025 it was the principal source of remittances, with US$6,455.1 million, equivalent to 49% of the total received. It also led the flow of international tourists. Between January and February 2026, 177,531 travelers from that country entered Colombia, representing 22% of non-resident visitors who arrived in the country.
