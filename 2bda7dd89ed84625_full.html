# Realpolitik in Business

Just as in geopolitics, power asymmetry redefines the scope of contracts in the corporate world, writes Guilherme Forbes

In reading recent geopolitical news, I have come across a quote attributed to Thucydides in his account of the Peloponnesian War: "The strong do what they can; the weak suffer what they must." A good example, which garnered significant repercussion, was the speech by Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney at the World Economic Forum in Davos, in which he formulated a convincing argument for pragmatism in international relations. His central thesis: in an environment of great power asymmetry, clinging to agreements designed to equalize countries, imposing limits on the behavior of the stronger for the benefit of the weaker, is merely an exercise in nostalgia for a rules-based order that, to some extent, was a fiction.

An interesting parallel can be drawn with the private sector. Every company is inserted in a complex network of relationships with regulators, competitors, financiers, clients, and suppliers, which exercise different degrees of power over its business. And even within the governance structures of the companies themselves, it is common for there to be actors with divergent interests and different levels of influence over the most strategic decisions.

These relationships are governed by contracts that, like treaties between countries, create a system of rules, procedures, incentives, and penalties that should, at least in theory, regulate the behavior of the parties. In negotiating these contracts, one seeks to anticipate all possible scenarios and economic and legal consequences that may materialize over the course of implementing the transaction. This demands days and nights of discussions, multiple exchanges of contract drafts, and sometimes heated debates about the choice of one or two words that may, in the view of the parties and their advisors, alter the course of a future dispute over the contract. This entire exercise is based on the fundamental premise that the contract will indeed be fulfilled—either voluntarily or through an effective enforcement mechanism.

However, just as in the geopolitical arena, in the real world of business, the power asymmetry between parties and the concrete consequences of litigation can, in many cases, diminish the practical effectiveness of the contract. The reaction of different countries to the tariffs imposed by the United States showed the limits of agreements as instruments for inducing expected behaviors. Faced with the actions of an actor with enormous economic power, its more vulnerable counterparts frequently chose not to rigidly adhere to previously existing agreements. What was observed was a pragmatic reaction: few formal retaliations or disputes in multilateral bodies, and quick engagement in new negotiations. This same dynamic permeates private economic relations. For a supplier, aggressively demanding compliance with a contract can jeopardize the relationship with a strategic client. In markets with restricted supply of critical goods or services, the calculation usually reverses: clients hesitate before initiating litigation that could compromise access to essential inputs for their operations.

In Brazil, other factors make these tactical decisions even more complex. An institutional environment marked by legal uncertainty and procedural delays introduces another relevant source of asymmetry between the parties. When one party has greater tolerance for reputational risks, there is fertile ground for abuse. It is not uncommon for one party to rationally decide to breach a contract, betting on the exploitation of inherent weaknesses in the usual mechanisms for dispute resolution.

This in no way diminishes the importance of a well-drafted contract. On the contrary. The well-designed contract, which reconciles smart incentives for interest alignment and realistic and efficient enforcement mechanisms, is the main pillar in an architecture for governing economic relationships between the parties. Assessing the track record of counterparties, understanding the structure of incentives involved in the business, and anticipating possible points of tension are essential exercises. And as divergences arise, the application of the contract should be integrated into a more comprehensive strategy that combines the ability to impose concrete costs in case of breach with the flexibility necessary to renegotiate when circumstances require it.

Behind the doctrine of realpolitik is the recognition that it is more realistic to expect that those with greater power will exercise it for their own interests, without demonstrating constraint before rules or principles aimed precisely at limiting this exercise. The contract that ignores this reality may, when tested, reveal itself to be a mere paper tiger.
