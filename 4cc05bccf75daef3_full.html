Inflation carry-over effect: May fuel price increases set high floor for inflation index

20 April 2026 · 09:53 PM

# Inflation carry-over effect: May fuel price increases set high floor for inflation index

Amid the volatility of international oil prices due to the extension of the Middle East conflict and the back-and-forth over the opening and closing of the Strait of Hormuz, experts rule out an immediate drop in fuel prices when the 15-day freeze agreement promoted by YPF ends on May 15. For that to happen, there must be a change in trend, they anticipate. That is, a truce that persists and is respected, as well as a stabilization of crude values, which continues with constant fluctuations. Despite last week's decline, the barrel rose again on Monday. At the close of this note, Brent was trading at US$95.78, almost 6% above how it ended on Friday.

They point out that prices could fall if the value of oil eventually drops significantly, a situation they consider unlikely to occur in the short term, as long as fluctuations in negotiations continue and there are contradictions in public statements from leaders, and it takes time to recover global production with infrastructure damaged by attacks.

Added to the Government's decision to postpone the planned fuel tax increase to early April, the price freeze at pumps functioned as a temporary anchor—a buffer—in the words of Horacio Marín, CEO of YPF, to prevent a larger jump in prices that would affect sales and have an inflationary effect. Had it not been applied, fuel would have continued rising by at least 30% since the start of the war, while with the tax update, a liter of super would have climbed above $2,000 on average in the City, to $2,250, according to estimates by analysts such as Nadin Argañaraz, from the Argentine Fiscal Analysis Institute (Iaraf), and Daniel Dreizzen, former planning secretary and now at Aleph Energy.

When this 45-day freeze ends in mid-May, YPF will evaluate the situation to determine next steps. But, in principle, prices will not fall, they anticipate at the company, because once the agreement with the sector ends, they will have to compensate for that month and a half of losses. Moreover, there is still a lag in what the public pays relative to costs. However, looking ahead, oil executives are confident that when the war ends or the international price finds equilibrium, the trend will indeed be downward. The question they ask themselves is what the new floor for the barrel will be. They consider it positive that it has fallen from the US$120 it reached, but they believe it must continue falling, though they think it will take time to return to pre-war levels, below US$70.

## Impact of fuel price increases on inflation

In parallel, the effect will be felt again in inflation. In March, fuel registered increases greater than 20% that were not fully evident in the index released last week. This smaller impact is explained by the measurement dynamic, which captures increases according to when in the month they are applied. Thus, the increases were reflected partially in the last 3.4% figure published by Indec corresponding to last month. Therefore, the carry-over will again impact April's CPI and leaves a high floor.

"The impact of the fuel price increase on March's CPI was 7%, according to Indec, and for April we estimate it will be 6% due to moderation in increases from YPF's stabilization decision, the natural price maker followed by competitors," said Ricardo Delgado of Analytica. Gonzalo Carrera of Equilibra agreed that "there's still some fuel shock that will hit at least half in April."

According to the latest weekly survey by EcoGo, "beyond the fuel freeze, the statistical carry-over of 8% inherited from March still persists." Meanwhile, for Fundación Capital, "fuel puts a high floor on inflation for the fourth month." "March's increase was reflected partially in the CPI, leaving a significant carry-over. Thus, April's figure has a floor of 0.6 points," it noted.

However, consulting firms project a slowdown. "Fuel could rise about 13% compared to last month due to the effect of increases throughout March that, therefore, affect April. That 13% would contribute about half a point of inflation this month, but it has been holding since the second week," considered Camilo Tiscornia of C&T Economic Advisors. "The carry-over effect is diluting in April and we are returning to the deceleration trend. If there are no new fuel increases, that trend will continue," added Aldo Abram of Libertad y Progreso.
