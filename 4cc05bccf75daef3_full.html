# Spillover effect: fuel price increases will keep inflation floor high in May

Fuel price increases in March were only partially reflected in the latest inflation data. The spillover from March's increases will impact this month's CPI again and leave a high floor. At the start of the week, the barrel rose again.

Amid volatility in international oil prices due to the extension of the Middle East conflict and the back-and-forth over the reopening and closing of the Strait of Hormuz, analysts rule out an immediate drop in fuel prices when the 45-day freeze agreement promoted by YPF ends on May 15. For that to happen, there must be a change in trend, they anticipate. That is, a truce that persists and is respected, as well as a stabilization of crude oil value, which continues with permanent fluctuations. Despite last week's decline, the barrel rose again on Monday. At the close of this note, Brent was trading at US$ 95.78, nearly 6% above where it closed on Friday.

Analysts note that fuel prices could drop if oil value eventually descends markedly, a situation considered unlikely in the short term, as long as fluctuations in negotiations continue and there are contradictions in public statements from leaders, and global production recovery is delayed with infrastructure damaged by attacks.

Added to the government's decision to postpone the planned increase in fuel taxes to early April, the freeze at the pumps functioned as a temporary anchor—a buffer, in the words of YPF CEO Horacio Marín—to prevent a larger price jump that would affect sales and have an inflationary effect. Had it not been applied, fuel would have continued rising by at least 30% since the war began. With the tax update, the liter of premium fuel would have climbed above $2,000 on average in the City to $2,250, according to estimates from analysts like Nadin Argañaraz from the Argentine Institute of Fiscal Analysis (Iaraf) and Daniel Dreizzen, former planning secretary and now at Aleph Energy.

When this 45-day brake ends in mid-May, YPF will evaluate the situation to determine next steps. But, in principle, prices will not drop, the company anticipates, because once the agreement with the sector ends, that month and a half of losses will need to be compensated. Furthermore, there is still a lag to the public regarding costs. Nevertheless, the oil company is confident that when the war ends or the international price finds equilibrium, the trend will be downward at that point. The question they are asking is what the new floor for the barrel will be. They consider it positive that it has fallen from the US$ 120 it reached, but believe it must continue falling, though they think it will take time to return to pre-war levels, below US$ 70.

## Impact of fuel increases on inflation

In parallel, the effect will be felt again in inflation. In March, fuel registered increases greater than 20% that were not fully evident in the index released last week. This smaller impact is explained by the measurement dynamics, which capture increases according to when they are applied during the month. Thus, the increases were only partially reflected in the last 3.4% figure published by Indec for last month. Therefore, the spillover will impact April's CPI again and leaves a high floor.

"The impact of fuel increases on March's CPI was 7%, according to Indec, and for April we estimate it will be 6% due to moderation of increases from YPF's stabilization decision, as the natural price setter that competition follows," said Ricardo Delgado of Analytica. Gonzalo Carrera of Equilibra agreed that "there is still some fuel shock that will hit at least half in April."

According to the latest weekly survey by EcoGo, "beyond the fuel freeze, there is still a statistical carryover of 8% inherited from March." Meanwhile, for Fundación Capital, "fuel puts a high floor on fourth-month inflation." "The March increase was partially reflected in CPI, leaving significant carryover. Thus, April's record has a floor of 0.6 points," they noted.

However, consulting firms project a slowdown. "Fuel could rise around 13% compared to last month due to the effect of increases throughout March that, therefore, affect April. That 13% would contribute roughly half a point of inflation this month, but since the second week it has been holding steady," said Camilo Tiscornia of C&T Economic Advisors. "The spillover effect has been diluting in April and is returning to the deceleration trend. If there are no new fuel increases, that trend will continue," added Aldo Abram of Libertad y Progreso.
