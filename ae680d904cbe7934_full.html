# Wall Street Closes April at Peaks: S&P 500 and Nasdaq Seal Best Month Since 2020

U.S. stocks closed Thursday with gains, bringing the S&P 500 to a new all-time high, confirming April's strong momentum, which recorded the best monthly performance since 2020.

The optimism was supported by signals of economic strength, a rebound in corporate investment linked to artificial intelligence, and corporate results that collectively reinforced expectations for earnings growth. The S&P 500 accumulated a gain of more than 10% for the month and closed above 7,200 points, while the Dow Jones gained 7.14%, and the Nasdaq rose 15.29%, also its largest gain since 2020. The performance occurred in a context where risk appetite again dominated. An index of small companies advanced around 2%, reflecting confidence in domestic activity.

The recovery of markets from March lows found support in economic data showing accelerating growth in the United States, even against inflationary pressures associated with the Middle East conflict.

U.S. gross domestic product grew at an annualized rate of 2% in the first quarter, exceeding the weakness recorded in late 2025 due to the government shutdown. At the same time, consumer spending, which represents nearly two-thirds of economic activity, advanced 1.6%, driven mainly by services, while corporate investment in equipment and structures expanded 10.4%, its fastest pace in nearly three years, largely linked to the artificial intelligence boom.

The labor market continues to show strength. Initial unemployment benefit claims fell by 26,000 to 189,000 in the latest week, well below the 212,000 expected by economists and at their lowest level since 1969.

The artificial intelligence engine again occupied the center of analysis. The earnings season provided evidence that major tech companies are capitalizing on their investments in this segment, although with clear differences between companies. Alphabet showed visible returns from its infrastructure and development spending, while Meta faced a negative market reaction after announcing a debt issuance of $25 billion and increased spending. This contrast showed that investors reward effective execution in an environment where technology spending already exceeds $700 billion among major players.

Consumption, a historical pillar of U.S. growth, maintained its strength and helped sustain expectations for corporate revenues. Chris Zaccarelli, chief investment officer of Northlight Asset Management, noted that "as long as the economy continues to grow and companies manage to expand their profits, higher stock prices are possible even amid expensive energy and inflation." However, he warned that prolonging the armed conflict could introduce correction episodes as risk perceptions change.

The behavior of commodities offered partial relief. Oil retreated from four-year highs and closed near $114 per barrel on the Brent benchmark, after exceeding $126 earlier. The decline occurred in an environment of low liquidity and high volatility, influenced by technical adjustments and lower participation from traditional investors. Nevertheless, the backdrop remains dominated by tensions between the United States and Iran, which keeps flow through the Strait of Hormuz restricted and raises concerns about inflation and global growth.

In parallel, gold rebounded to levels near $4,600 per ounce, driven by dollar weakness amid speculation of currency intervention in Japan. The precious metal, which had retreated in previous sessions, found additional support in demand from central banks, which increased their reserves at the fastest rate in over a year during the first quarter. Christopher Wong, strategist at Oversea-Chinese Banking Corp., indicated that the current environment demands caution, though he noted that structural demand remains firm. Nevertheless, the geopolitical front continues as a determining factor. The lack of progress in negotiations between Washington and Tehran, along with signals of possible military escalation, maintain elevated uncertainty.

Adam Turnquist of LPL Financial stressed that the recent momentum in stocks also responds to expectations of eventual de-escalation, though he warned that risks persist and could sustain volatility episodes.

Looking ahead to May, seasonal patterns enter discussion again. Although historically the month has shown weaker performance, data from the last decade suggest average positive returns. With indices at peaks and an environment marked by solid growth but latent geopolitical risks, the market begins the new month with a combination of optimism and caution.

**How is the Dollar Performing Today in Latin America?**

In Latin America, the dollar's performance and oil price increases have tested the resilience of regional currencies. BBVA highlights that "emerging market and LatAm currencies continue to be influenced primarily by the global risk tone and the Iran conflict."

During today's session, before the Friday holidays for Labor Day, regional currencies rose. The Chilean peso, Argentine peso, Brazilian real, Mexican peso, and Peruvian sol advanced. The Colombian peso stood out and fell.

While the Spanish bank acknowledges that Latin American currencies have shown some preference within the emerging universe, it warns that they remain highly exposed to the evolution of the Iran conflict.
