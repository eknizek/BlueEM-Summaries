# Wall Street closes April at record highs: S&P 500 and Nasdaq seal best month since 2020

U.S. stocks closed Thursday with gains, bringing the S&P 500 to a new all-time high, a session that confirmed the strong momentum of April, which recorded the best monthly performance since 2020.

Optimism was supported by signals of economic strength, a rebound in business investment linked to artificial intelligence, and corporate results that together reinforced expectations for earnings growth. The S&P 500 accumulated gains of more than 10% in the month and closed above 7,200 points, while the Dow Jones gained 7.14%, and the Nasdaq rose 15.29%, also its largest gain since 2020. The performance occurred in a context where appetite for risk returned to dominance. An index of small companies advanced around 2%, reflecting confidence in domestic activity.

The recovery of markets from March lows found support in economic data showing acceleration of growth in the United States, even amid inflationary pressures associated with the Middle East conflict.

U.S. gross domestic product grew at an annualized rate of 2% in the first quarter, exceeding the weakness recorded at the end of 2025 due to government shutdown. At the same time, consumer spending, which represents about two-thirds of economic activity, advanced 1.6%, driven mainly by services, while business investment in equipment and structures expanded 10.4%, its fastest pace in nearly three years, largely linked to the artificial intelligence boom.

This scenario was joined by a labor market that continues to show strength. Initial unemployment benefit claims fell 26,000 to 189,000 in the latest week, well below the 212,000 expected by economists and at their lowest level since 1969.

The artificial intelligence engine again occupied the center of analysis. The earnings season provided evidence that large technology companies are capitalizing on their investments in this segment, although with clear differences between companies. Alphabet showed visible returns on its infrastructure and development spending, while Meta faced a negative market reaction after announcing a $25 billion debt issuance and increased spending. This contrast showed that investors reward effective execution in an environment where technology spending already exceeds $700 billion among major players.

Consumption, a historical pillar of U.S. growth, maintained its strength and helped sustain expectations for corporate revenues. Chris Zaccarelli, investment director at Northlight Asset Management, noted that "as long as the economy continues to grow and companies manage to expand their earnings, it is possible to see higher stock prices even amid expensive energy and inflation."

However, he warned that prolonged conflict could introduce correction episodes as risk perceptions change.

The behavior of commodities offered partial relief. Oil pulled back from four-year highs and closed near $114 per barrel on its Brent benchmark, after having exceeded $126 during the session. The decline occurred in an environment of low liquidity and high volatility, influenced by technical adjustments and lower participation from traditional investors. Nevertheless, the backdrop remains dominated by tension between the United States and Iran, which keeps flows restricted in the Strait of Hormuz and raises concerns about inflation and global growth.

In parallel, gold rebounded to levels near $4,600 per ounce, driven by dollar weakness amid speculation of currency intervention by Japan. The precious metal, which had fallen in previous sessions, found additional support in central bank demand, which increased its reserves at the fastest pace in more than a year during the first quarter.

Christopher Wong, strategist at Oversea-Chinese Banking Corp., indicated that the current environment demands caution, though highlighted that structural demand remains firm. Nevertheless, the geopolitical front continues as a determining factor. The lack of progress in negotiations between Washington and Tehran, along with signals of possible military escalation, maintains elevated uncertainty.

Adam Turnquist of LPL Financial stressed that the recent momentum in stocks also reflects expectations of eventual de-escalation, though warned that risks persist and can sustain volatility episodes.

Looking ahead to May, seasonal patterns come back into discussion. Although historically the month has shown weaker performance, data from the last decade suggests positive average returns. With indices at records and an environment marked by solid growth but latent geopolitical risks, the market begins the new month with a combination of optimism and caution.

## How is the dollar performing today in Latin America?

In Latin America, dollar performance and rising oil prices have tested the resilience of regional currencies. BBVA highlights that "currencies in emerging markets and LatAm continue to be influenced primarily by the tone of global risk and the Iran conflict."

During today's session, before holidays on Friday for Labor Day, regional currencies rose. The Chilean peso, Argentine peso, Brazilian real, Mexican peso, and Peruvian sol advanced. The Colombian peso broke away and fell.

Although the Spanish bank recognizes that Latin American currencies have shown some preference within the emerging universe, it warns that they remain highly exposed to the evolution of the Iran conflict.
