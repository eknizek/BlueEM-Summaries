Lojas Quero-Quero Doubles Loss in 1st Quarter and Shares Close in 21% Collapse

# Lojas Quero-Quero Doubles Loss in Q1 and Stock Plummets 21%

Lojas Quero-Quero (LJQQ3) reported results below market estimates in the first quarter of 2026. The company posted a net loss of R$ 61.7 million for the period, representing a 98% increase in losses compared to Q1 2025. Markets reacted negatively, and on Friday (May 8), LJQQ3 closed down 21.03%, trading at R$ 1.54.

According to Itaú BBA, the results were impacted by margin compression in the financial services division. In view of this performance, the bank reduced its estimates for the company. The recommendation remains a buy, with a year-end 2026 price target of R$ 3.60, down from the previous R$ 1.97.

The adjusted net loss for Lojas Quero-Quero reached R$ 35.5 million for the period. Although gross revenue came close to expectations, the consolidated gross margin fell 0.8 percentage points below forecast. Revenue totaled R$ 790.2 million, representing a 3.3% year-over-year increase, with margins hitting 26.8%. Adjusted earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation and amortization (Ebitda) remained nearly flat at R$ 0.6 million.

## Financial Services as Main Negative Highlight

The primary source of disappointment in the results, according to bank analysts, was the financial services margin. The Selic rate maintained pressure on profitability, compressing results. The margin declined 9.2 percentage points year-over-year, falling to 34.1%. BBA attributes this compression to higher funding costs due to elevated Selic rates and increased provisions. Additionally, a deliberate shift toward lower-risk credit lines impacted results.

The company's financial expenses also surged, rising 30.2% year-over-year. Analysts also attribute these new figures to higher interest rates. According to BBA, the company remains one of the most interest-rate-sensitive companies within its coverage. The bank notes that an interest-rate cutting cycle could serve as an important catalyst for the remainder of the year. This sensitivity stems from its exposure to the construction materials sector, high dependence on proprietary credit, and a financing structure tied to floating rates.
