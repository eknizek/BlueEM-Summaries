# After shifting to imports, Lumilagro to hire 60 staff for new retail expansion

The Argentine thermos company Lumilagro, which Finance Minister Luis Caputo defends as an example of business restructuring, has taken a new step in line with this change in business model. The company, which recently deactivated production at its Tortuguitas plant and laid off more than 170 people through dismissals and voluntary retirements to take advantage of cheap imports from China, will now open its own retail locations. For this project, it announced a hiring plan for 60 new employees.

Martín Nadler, executive director of the company founded more than 85 years ago, said they plan to open 15 locations across the country as a result of sustained company growth following the launch of the Termo Pampa, similar to the famous Stanley brand but cheaper.

"Thanks to the record sales we had in recent months with the new Termo Pampa, which has established itself as the best on the market and the only one specially designed for drinking mate, we are announcing a hiring plan for 60 new employees," said Nadler, the fourth generation of the founding family.

"With the new model we have a better thermos at a better price, which generated record sales and allowed us to create more jobs," the director affirmed.

The new positions will be in customer service, distribution logistics, and operations at the new stores. By the end of this year, the company plans to open locations in Buenos Aires, Rosario, Córdoba, and Salta. The recruitment for the first positions—for the store opening soon at the OH! Shopping center in Recoleta—has already been posted on the brand's social media.

Nadler clarified that "this is the first stage of a broader expansion plan, which includes selling new products, greater presence in all cities across the country, and consolidating our foreign sales."

Although the factory remains in the company's hands, it is no longer active. The firm stated that importing from Asia costs 35% less than manufacturing locally. This is how they stopped manufacturing their classic glass thermoses in Argentina after 83 years. Today, they import glass ampules from India and Vietnam, and bring stainless steel thermoses from China.
