Alicorp pursues Rama acquisition: M&A strategy includes Flora Food purchase in Colombia

24 April 2026 · 01:29 PM

# Now it's Rama's turn: Alicorp's strategy to strengthen itself in mass consumption includes purchases in Colombia

The Peruvian multilatin company Alicorp took a new step in its regional expansion strategy with a move that focuses on the final consumer. The company filed a pre-evaluation request with the Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) to acquire the food business of Flora Food Group in several Latin American countries, including Colombia, which opens the door to acquiring highly recognized brands such as Rama.

The document filed with the competition authority is clear about the scope of the operation: Alicorp seeks to buy 100% of Flora's operating companies in South America (except Brazil), Central America (except Mexico), and the Caribbean. This is a regional acquisition that, in practice, would allow it to consolidate a portfolio of mass consumption products in categories such as margarines, spreads, creams, and culinary solutions.

Beyond the size of the transaction, the file reveals the strategic foundation. The integration is essentially vertical. Alicorp participates in the initial links of the chain, with the production and marketing of refined vegetable oils and industrial fats, while Flora operates in the final segment, with ready-to-consume products. The company identifies three key markets affected in Colombia: vegetable oils for industrial use, industrial vegetable fats and margarines, and table spreads.

This chain linkage is the core of the bet. Alicorp already sells inputs to the food industry—bulk oils and fats used in baking, snacks, or sauces—and, with the purchase of Flora, would advance toward controlling the final product that reaches households. The objective: capture more value and reduce intermediaries while strengthening its presence in stores and supermarkets.

The analysis presented to the SIC insists that there are no relevant horizontal overlaps in Colombia; that is, the companies do not directly compete in the same markets. Rather, the relationship is complementary. Alicorp does not produce mass consumption margarines in the country, while Flora does not participate in industrial oil production. In this context, the operation would not eliminate competitors nor significantly increase concentration. In fact, the data provided indicate that market shares are below 20%, while in margarines and spreads they are just above that threshold. Additionally, the document emphasizes that there are multiple national and international suppliers, which limits any possibility of restricting access to inputs or blocking competitors.

Another key point is market definition. The operation distinguishes between refined oils for industrial use—marketed in bulk and intended for production processes—and packaged oils for final consumption, which are not part of the same segment. Similarly, it separates white vegetable fats (used as industrial input) from yellow fats, where margarines are located. This segmentation reinforces the thesis that these are different links in the same chain, rather than direct competitors.

Parallel to this move, Alicorp has been building a broader portfolio in mass consumption, and one of the most relevant pieces is its entry into the home care business. In early 2026, the company completed an agreement to acquire detergent and cleaning product brands from Unilever in Colombia and Ecuador. With this operation, it incorporated references widely positioned in the market, which allowed it to fully enter a different but complementary category. The logic behind this acquisition is similar to that of Flora: taking already positioned brands with consolidated distribution networks and adding them to its regional platform. In the case of cleaning products, the objective is to leverage commercial reach and logistics synergies to strengthen its presence in traditional and modern channels.

This dual front—food and home care—shows that Alicorp is not building an isolated portfolio, but an integral mass consumption structure. In practice, it seeks to replicate the model of large multinationals that operate simultaneously in several categories of household daily spending.

Colombia appears as a strategic market in this expansion. The country not only has a broad consumer base but also a diverse distribution structure that includes supermarkets, discount stores, and a strong traditional channel. For a company betting on volume and reach, these conditions are key. Additionally, the analysis of the operation highlights that the Colombian market presents low barriers to imports in industrial segments, which guarantees competition and input availability. This is relevant for the regulator, who evaluates whether integration could affect access to essential raw materials for other players.

The process before the SIC is just beginning. Following the publication of the start of the proceedings, interested third parties can provide information for the analysis of the operation. The authority must determine whether the integration generates risks to free competition or if, as the companies argue, it is a transaction with no restrictive effects. Beyond the regulatory decision, the move fits into a strategic expansion plan.
