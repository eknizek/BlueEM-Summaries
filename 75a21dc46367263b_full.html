# Alicorp Targets Rama: Food Acquisition Strategy in Colombia Includes Flora Food Divestiture

The Peruvian multilatina Alicorp took a new step in its regional expansion strategy with a move that focuses on the final consumer. The company submitted a pre-evaluation request to the Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) to acquire the food business of Flora Food Group in several Latin American countries, including Colombia, which opens the door to acquiring highly recognized brands such as Rama.

The document filed with the competition authority is clear about the scope of the operation: Alicorp seeks to buy 100% of Flora's operating companies in South America (except Brazil), Central America (except Mexico), and the Caribbean. This is a regional acquisition that would practically allow it to consolidate a portfolio of mass consumption products in categories such as margarines, spreads, creams, and culinary solutions.

Beyond the size of the transaction, the file reveals the strategic foundation. The integration is essentially vertical. Alicorp participates in the initial links of the chain, with the production and commercialization of refined vegetable oils and industrial fats, while Flora operates at the final stage, with ready-to-consume products. The company recognizes three key affected markets in Colombia: vegetable oils for industrial use, industrial vegetable fats and margarines, and table spreads.

This linkage is the core of the bet. Alicorp already sells inputs to the food industry—bulk oils and fats used in baking, snacks, or sauces—and with the Flora purchase, would advance toward controlling the final product that reaches households. The objective: capture more value, reduce intermediaries, and strengthen its presence in stores and supermarkets.

The analysis presented to the SIC insists that there are no relevant horizontal overlaps in Colombia; that is, the companies do not compete directly in the same markets. Rather, the relationship is complementary. Alicorp does not produce mass consumption margarines in the country, while Flora does not participate in the production of industrial oils. In this context, the operation would not eliminate competitors nor significantly increase concentration. In fact, the data provided indicate that market shares are below 20%, while in margarines and spreads they are just above that threshold.

Additionally, the document emphasizes that there are multiple national and international suppliers, which limits any possibility of restricting access to inputs or blocking competitors.

Another key point is the market definition. The operation distinguishes between refined oils for industrial use—marketed in bulk and destined for production processes—and packaged oils for final consumption, which are not part of the same segment. Similarly, it separates white vegetable fats (used as industrial input) from yellow fats, where margarines are located. This segmentation reinforces the thesis that these are different links of the same chain, rather than direct competitors.

Parallel to this move, Alicorp has been building a broader portfolio in mass consumption, and one of the most relevant pieces is its incursion into the home care business. At the beginning of 2026, the company completed an agreement to acquire detergent and cleaning product brands from Unilever in Colombia and Ecuador. With this operation, it incorporated widely positioned references in the market, which allowed it to fully enter a different but complementary category. The logic behind this acquisition is similar to Flora's: take already positioned brands with consolidated distribution networks and add them to its regional platform.

In the case of cleaning products, the objective is to leverage commercial reach and logistic synergies to strengthen its presence in traditional and modern channels. This double front—food and home care—shows that Alicorp is not building an isolated portfolio but an integrated mass consumption structure. In practice, it seeks to replicate the model of major multinationals that operate simultaneously in several categories of household spending.

Colombia appears as a strategic market in this expansion. The country not only has a broad base of consumers but also a diverse distribution structure that includes supermarkets, discount stores, and a strong traditional channel. For a company betting on volume and reach, these conditions are key. Additionally, the analysis of the operation highlights that the Colombian market presents low import barriers in industrial segments, which guarantees competition and availability of inputs. This is relevant for the regulator, which evaluates whether integration could affect other players' access to essential raw materials.

The process before the SIC is just beginning. After the publication of the start of the proceedings, interested third parties can provide information for the analysis of the operation. The authority must determine whether the integration generates risks for free competition or if, as the companies argue, it is a transaction without restrictive effects. Beyond the regulatory decision, the move fits into a broader strategy. Alicorp is betting on growing through acquisitions.
