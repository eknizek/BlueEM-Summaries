# After leaving Batista family company, Fluxus founder seeks to raise $300 million for new project

Businessman Ricardo Savini left the helm of Fluxus, an oil and gas company owned by J&F, the holding company of brothers Joesley and Wesley Batista. Savini founded the company in 2023 alongside partners Jorge Lorenzón and Vitor Abreu, and made a deal with the Batista brothers the following year. He was retained as CEO by the controllers until early April of this year.

In an interview with Folha, Savini states that his departure from the company was peaceful and that the Batista brothers imposed no restrictions on new investments in the oil and gas sector. In fact, he has launched a new company called Bossa Energy to explore petroleum across South America, operating on similar lines to Fluxus.

"We are actively talking with potential investors and banks in Brazil and abroad," says Savini. "It's a process that should take a few more weeks."

The businessman, who is also a geologist by training, says he has assets mapped out in Argentina and Bolivia. "What Bossa is doing right now is trying to raise around $300 million to acquire these assets we are negotiating," he says. "We know exactly what we want to buy. We already have conversations with most of the sellers, but we still need to put the money on the table."

In addition to Fluxus, Savini is one of the founders of 3R Petroleum, which merged with Enauta and is now called Brava Energia, one of the country's largest publicly listed oil companies on B3. In the past, he was one of the leaders of operations for Pérez Companc and Petrobras in Venezuela.

During his time at Fluxus, Savini led acquisitions in Argentina, Bolivia, and Peru, and established a commercial office in Venezuela to explore opportunities in the country. "We delivered everything we had planned. An active asset in Neuquén Province, Argentina; we bought Pluspetrol in Bolivia, with small fields that also produce gas; and we tried to develop business in Peru and Venezuela," he says. "It was two and a half very intense years."

He does not provide details, however, about the Batista brothers' investments in Venezuela. He only says there will be an opening in the country that could bring new investments. "Today the risk for investing in Venezuela is lower than it was a year ago, which was already lower than it was before 2019," Savini summarizes.

In 2024, the Batista brothers met with Pedro Tellechea, then minister of petroleum and president of PDVSA (Petróleos de Venezuela). Joesley and Wesley have also built a direct relationship with Nicolás Maduro and have already met with Delcy Rodríguez.
