# Parliamentary Group Pushes to Replace Payroll Tax with Revenue-Based Contribution; Explained

BRASÍLIA - The Parliamentary Front for Entrepreneurship is coordinating a change in how companies pay social security contributions, which it claims would foster job creation and help make Social Security accounts more sustainable.

The topic will be debated at a seminar held by the Front this Tuesday, May 5, in Brasília. The change is contained in a constitutional amendment proposal (PEC) presented in February by Senator Laércio Oliveira (PP-SE). The text suggests an alteration to the constitutional article dealing with Social Security financing, replacing the 20% employer contribution based on payroll with a 1.4% charge on companies' gross revenue.

The senator said he consulted the Federal Revenue Service about the change. According to him, the tax authority indicated there would be tax neutrality with the change—meaning the tax collection impact would be the same.

In the PEC's justification, the parliamentarian states that the current contribution format based on payroll depends mainly on the number of formal workers in the labor market. Therefore, labor changes recorded in recent years, the deceleration of the growth rate of the working-age population, and the aging of the population could make this payroll-based contribution "increasingly insufficient in the face of pension spending," he argues.

In practice, those with higher revenue would pay more, while today those with a larger payroll pay more. Because it is a constitutional change, the measure, if approved, would apply to all economic segments—unlike the payroll relief program, for example, which is directed at the 17 sectors that employ the most.

Oliveira tells Estadão that he spoke about the PEC with the president of the Senate, Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP), and says that "he has the perception that this is a very relevant agenda for the country." The senator advocates for approving the proposal still this year to take effect in 2027, and rules out any negative influence from the electoral calendar.

"This is a topic whose debate cannot be postponed. We did the tax reform for consumption and income. Now we need to address the discussion of the heavy employer pension contribution on payroll," he states.

For Oliveira, the current format of employer pension contribution creates dysfunctions in the labor market, such as informality and "distortions in the use of independent contractor arrangements." "It is necessary to preserve legitimate and necessary independent contractor work. But we are seeing cases of using independent contractors and micro-entrepreneurs in typical employment situations, which causes legal uncertainty and distortions in competition between companies," he argues.

In his assessment, the consequence of the current model is a scenario of higher interest rates and a damaged business environment, which hampers the country's growth.

## No Connection to 6x1

Oliveira denies that the proposal is a counterpart to the possible approval of the PEC that ends the 6x1 work schedule and reduces working hours, which the senator has criticized. "The PEC ending 6x1 seeks changes in the work schedule model. In my view, it further restricts labor legislation," he says.

"Like the rest of the world does, I think we should increasingly encourage collective bargaining to adapt schedules and shifts to the different realities of productive activities, which is good for companies, workers, and consumers," he emphasizes.

He also acknowledges that his PEC may face some reaction from sectors that make intensive use of labor, but argues that "absolutely everyone gains from a more sustainable pension system and a healthier business environment."

FPE President, Deputy Joaquim Passarinho (PL-PA), states that the PEC could fit into the 6x1 debate, even if that is not the intention. "Laércio's PEC doesn't help 6x1 at all. It doesn't link to anything. Can we take advantage of this? We can. Instead of charging companies the percentage he proposes on billing, we could decrease that percentage," he says.

"If we decrease that percentage, then that's fine. Then it could also be an element of compensation."

Unlike the senator, Passarinho sees little room for the PEC to proceed this year, which is shorter in Congress due to elections. "I think the Senate could vote this year and the Chamber could leave it for after the election," he says. "If approved in the Senate, we've already made it halfway."
