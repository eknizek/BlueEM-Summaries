# Rare Earths Race in Brazil Attracts Private Equity Fund

The potential of rare earths has caught the attention of MadCap. With an eye on the possibility of investing in this new strategic segment of mining, the private equity manager has launched an international fundraising effort. With the goal of attracting $200 million to develop 10 projects already mapped in the country, MadCap began the roadshow in February for the LTWSK Mining Fund, which will be based in Delaware, United States, with an eye on the global nature of the critical minerals market. In less than 60 days, the fund already has an advanced agreement with three major investors interested in exploring the set of 17 chemical elements from the periodic table, essential for manufacturing wind turbines, electric vehicles, and defense applications, among other key products of the digital age.

MadCap, created less than a year ago, has R$ 330 million under management and aims to reach R$ 1 billion by December in the private equity strategy, in the verticals of agribusiness and consumer goods. Its investment portfolio includes companies such as Oakberry, Liquid Death, Casadonna, Ease Labs, and Nave Bebidas Premium, among others. The third vertical is precisely the mining fund, which is led by Mozart Litwinski, former CEO of Ferrous and former Vale executive, and includes Elmer Salomão, former director-general of the National Department of Mineral Production and one of the country's leading experts in the field; plus Guilherme Jácome, Felipe Litwinski, and Yuri Litwinski, all with experience in developing projects in the mining sector.

The business model created by MadCap is based on a formula that combines low risk, high technical capacity of the team, and asset diversification, without favoring any specific critical mineral. In other words, instead of researching unexplored opportunities in the country's subsoil, the LTWSK Mining Fund decided to bet where there is already relative certainty of returns, considering some selection criteria based on the knowledge of its executives.

"We prioritize setting up projects in locations with prior discovery rights, low socio-environmental risk, that is, away from indigenous and quilombo communities, and with good local acceptance, in addition to viable infrastructure and cost competitiveness," says Jácome to NeoFeed.

In total, ten projects were established with diversified critical materials, without betting on a specific mineral, but with different stages of maturity and evolution. They include projects for the exploration of rare earths, nickel, copper, graphite, lithium, and platinoids. "It's difficult to single out a mineral that will have higher profitability in the future because the market today is very dynamic," Jácome continues. "Three years ago it was lithium, today it's rare earths, soon it could be something new, we need to prioritize projects with minimized risk," he adds.

The portfolio includes everything from greenfield projects to those already approved and in development, but which still need to advance with some technical-economic feasibility studies and licensing. According to him, the total fundraising target of $200 million will be sufficient to support the projects that take off. The capital will be allocated in tranches conditional on results. For example: $1 million initially, with new contributions as targets are achieved and committee approval, directing 70% to 80% of capital to projects with greater technical and economic merit. Governance also has established rules. The manager says it is not just looking for financial contributions, but for experience to develop, implement, and license projects responsibly. "Our preference is for actionable control early on to conduct management, including minority participation with a board seat in advanced Capex situations, ensuring rapid decisions and strategic alignment," Jácome states.

Regarding the destination of production, the fund intends to make decisions case by case, between exporting concentrates or processing and refining, with a favorable view toward adding value locally. "Diversification helps reduce volatility, so our focus is to supply critical minerals to the USA, EU, Japan, and Korea, with broad demand aimed at the global competitiveness of projects, starting with rare earths and copper," says the expert.

## Terrabras in Sight?

The proposal behind the launch of the LTWSK Mining Fund gives an idea of the country's growing weight in the contested global market for critical minerals, whose potential can be measured not only by the fact that Brazil has the world's second-largest reserve of rare earths (19% of the total), the crown jewel of critical minerals, but because it has only mapped 23% of its subsoil.

Since China accounts for more than 50% of the production of 18 critical minerals, in addition to controlling much of the refining and processing of rare earths, graphite, vanadium, and intermediate stages of lithium and cobalt, this market has become strategic in the trade war with the United States, generating an appeal from American President Donald Trump to Brazilian reserves. Brazil already has rare earth mining projects in Minas Gerais, Goiás, Bahia, and Amazonas. These projects are financed by both Brazilian and international companies (USA, Australia, and China).

An estimate by the Brazilian Mining Institute (Ibram) foresees a pipeline of investments in critical minerals projects in the country of approximately R$ 100 billion by 2029, mostly with funding from abroad. The pressure from the American president to attract Brazil as a supplier of critical minerals—and preferably isolating China—ended up turning the topic initially into a major geopolitical debate that is already being directed toward the election campaign.

At the end of March, during an ultraconservative event in the USA, pre-candidate Flávio Bolsonaro made a speech putting Brazil's rare earths at Donald Trump's disposal. "Brazil is the solution for the USA to break its dependence on China for critical minerals," he said.

In response, two bills emerged in the Chamber of Deputies providing for the creation of Terrabras, a new state company for Brazil's rare earths and critical minerals chain. One is signed by the PT leader in the House, Pedro Uczai (SC), plus 73 deputies from PT, PV, and PC do B, the parties that make up the Brazil of Hope Federation. The other is from deputy Rodrigo Rollemberg (PSB-DF). Ibram and two other entities, the SGB (Brazilian Geological Service) and Cetem (Mineral Technology Center), issued a statement on Monday, April 13.
