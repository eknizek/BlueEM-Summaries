# Interest Rate Uncertainty: Iran Conflict and Public Account Tensions Drive Modest Rate Cut

Things have worsened over the last 40 days, but as expected, the Copom reduced basic interest rates (Selic) by only 0.25 percentage points to 14.5% per year, rather than making deeper cuts.

Following the escalation of fuel prices determined by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, inflation has again escaped its target. One could argue that high interest rates serve to contain inflation caused by increased consumption, as they reduce the amount of money in the economy, but not to restrain increases produced by supply shocks, as is happening now. In fact, the Central Bank (BC) opted for a reduced rate cut and to keep the money supply rationed to contain the spread of inflation.

The IPCA-15, which measures the evolution of the cost of living from the 15th of one month to the 15th of the following month and provides a preview of current inflation, showed an increase of 0.89% for April, which is not concentrated solely on fuel (despite current subsidies), but is already spreading.

The statement issued after the Copom meeting points to increased uncertainties surrounding the future. These are not limited to the nature and uncertain outcome of the Iran War, but also to its internal focus: the deterioration of public accounts. The Lula government, concerned with guaranteeing its reelection, shows no scruples in pouring money into the economy, contrary to what the BC does with its interest rate policy.

One cannot ignore the BC's action on the field of expectations. Also because interest rate policy is being sabotaged by the government's fiscal policy, price-setters believe that monetary policy is losing effectiveness. For this reason, they impose their increases without fear of unsold merchandise or loss of demand for their services.

The best way the BC has to act on the unanchoring of expectations is to show firmness in setting interest rates and ensure unity in decisions. As with so many things in markets, future conditions matter. This is the case with long-term contracts, interest payments due in a few months, and investment returns. Those in the market want to know the next steps of the Copom. But this is a time of low visibility, of a war with no deadline to end, and of an electoral game that can produce casualties.

For this reason, the BC opted for "serenity and caution." It leaves open the trajectory of future interest rates. It is the game of facts and expectations that will determine the Copom's behavior for the rest of the year.
