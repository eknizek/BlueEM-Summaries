# Is American private credit the new subprime? JGP explains why not (and what Brazil does differently)

The private credit market in the United States, estimated at $3 trillion according to Morgan Stanley, faces challenges due to its lack of regulation and transparency. Managers like Blackstone and KKR are dealing with redemption requests from investors worried about defaults, especially in the technology sector. The Business Development Companies (BDC) is a vehicle that finances small and medium-sized enterprises, but presents risks due to its hybrid structure, allowing limited redemptions. Despite concerns, Alexandre Muller from JGP does not see the risk of a new crisis like subprime, as there is no leverage and banks are not directly exposed. In Brazil, private credit is more regulated, and JGP seeks to adapt to market trends, while political uncertainty and global events impact investor sentiment. Muller believes that a new political agenda could bring opportunities to the market.

The private credit segment in the United States is estimated at $3 trillion, according to Morgan Stanley. This industry, which is larger than the GDP of 97% of countries, has no official data. And this absence of measurement stems from a market characteristic. "Abroad, when we talk about private credit, it's private indeed. This market, which has grown a lot, doesn't go through the regulator. It's over-the-counter, with no registration, no supervision," says Alexandre Muller, partner and manager responsible for JGP's private credit funds, to NeoFeed.

In recent months, American private credit has been questioned precisely because of its opacity. Managers like Blue Owl, KKR, Apollo and Blackstone have witnessed a rush of redemption requests from retail investors frightened by billion-dollar investments in technology companies threatened by the rise of artificial intelligence. Since these managers buy over-the-counter and place these private securities in funds listed on stock exchanges, there is a lock limiting redemptions to only 5% of the vehicle's assets per quarter. When the volume of requests exceeds this limit, the investor waits for the next window.

The instrument they use is the Business Development Companies (BDC), which at first glance seems controversial, but has a relevant role in financing companies in a growth phase. "The BDC itself is not a weird instrument. It's interesting and Brazil should look at it to solve the problem of [tax-exempt] funds. The BDC is like a real estate fund, a vehicle listed on the exchange, that can only finance small and medium-sized companies in the US. It's the right way to solve this constant confusion we have where the government always wants to tinker with LCI, LCA, etc.," says Muller.

The major concern, however, is that this problem spreads from Wall Street into a systemic crisis in the financial sector, especially because American banks have lent about $300 billion to private credit funds, according to Moody's. This caused the KBW Nasdaq Bank index, which tracks the performance of major banks and financial companies with shares on American exchanges, to accumulate a decline of just over 16% this year, at its worst point in mid-March. But by Thursday, April 9, the indicator had recovered and practically returned to stability (a decline of -0.34%).

The JGP specialist does not see the risk of a new subprime. "Definitely not. First because there is no leverage component that existed in subprime. Second, it's not something that affects banks' balance sheets. There's not the slightest chance of any kind of bailout with taxpayers' money. It's a situation in a specific asset class."

In this interview, Muller analyzes private credit in the American market, explains the differences to this industry in Brazil, and what he expects from the elections for local assets. Check out the main excerpts below:

**What is the differential of these private credit funds that are being heavily questioned?**

They have two merits. They are better at customizing capital solutions at the end for the company. It's more or less the same here in Brazil: if you have a farm and you go to a big bank saying you want to implement a reforestation project and finance part with carbon credits, you probably won't get it. There it's machinery financing, invoice discounting or working capital. This carbon credit business is not on the shelf. Abroad, these funds customize more of these capital solutions at the end.

**Give an example.**

Blackstone and Blue Owl recently launched a fund solely to finance Meta's data center expansion. Long-term money matched with the asset.

**Why do they differentiate themselves from banks?**

There is a regulatory benefit vis-à-vis banks, the ability to access long-term liabilities and fundraising scale in a low-interest environment. It was the asset class that grew the most in the United States within the asset management industry over the last 10, 15 years.

**Who are the investors?**

As this group grew, many launched shares on the stock exchange at very high multiples, which implied the premise that they would continue to grow significantly. To continue registering this rate of growth, they moved away from more traditional channels [insurance companies and institutional investors] and began to grow toward retail. And to grow toward retail, they used different instruments, especially BDCs.

**What does a Business Development Company have that's different?**

The BDC itself is not a weird instrument. It's interesting and Brazil should look at it to solve the problem of [tax-exempt] funds. The BDC is like a real estate fund, a vehicle listed on the exchange, but it can only finance small and medium-sized companies in the US. It's the right way to solve this constant confusion we have where the government always wants to tinker with LCI, LCA. In other words, there's a specific instrument for the tax-exempt. The way would be to do what was done in the US. Everyone preserved – agro, infrastructure, real estate – but if the economic group that is raising this debt has a net worth greater than R$ 1 billion, it cannot enjoy the benefit. The benefit goes to the little guy who needs it, like the United States did with BDCs. They have a tax incentive for the retail investor, but restricted to this small and medium-sized company segment.

**And where is the controversy?**

They used a structure called "semi-liquid vehicles." The BDC has shares that trade on the exchange, like a real estate fund share. If the investor needs to sell, he goes there and sells on the exchange, all properly. But, at the same time, he can request a redemption from the vehicle, limited, almost always, to 5% of the vehicle's assets per quarter. It's a kind of hybrid thing: you can request redemption and also trade on the exchange.

**And what is the fear regarding this industry?**

There is great fear that these companies will be disrupted by artificial intelligence. The fear is about defaults in this segment. Then comes the dynamic: the fund account is marked near 100, the secondary market is marked near 70, and the client has the right to request a redemption on the equity account limited to 5% per quarter.
