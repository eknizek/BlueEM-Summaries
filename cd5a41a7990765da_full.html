# Opinion: Why Brazilians Are Addicted to Credit

The issue of family debt has returned to the headlines, bringing with it various explanations for the phenomenon. The most commonly cited reason would be families' lack of financial literacy. However, the Central Bank's recent Financial Citizenship report indicates that we are at the global average regarding financial literacy, very close to the average of OECD member countries. Another reason cited would be low competition in the credit market, which would prevent a decline in interest rates charged. But in recent years, competition in the unsecured credit segment for individuals has increased, as shown by data from the BC's Financial Stability Report from April 2024.

There are more exotic explanations, such as one that justifies high indebtedness by the level of interest rates charged, defying simple economic logic. Higher interest rates should reduce family indebtedness; reducing them would increase it. There are also those who believe that the financial system has low risk appetite for granting this credit. However, the individual credit limits currently approved by banks would allow their indebtedness to quintuple. In other words, there is no shortage of credit supply.

Others blame betting. Recent studies, such as one by LCA Consultoria, show that the total amount spent on betting in Brazil represents 0.5% of total consumption and 3.3% of average monthly income. Total family indebtedness, meanwhile, represents 50% of annual income. Therefore, justifying indebtedness this way does not seem appropriate.

So, at a moment when a new debt relief program is being discussed, it is worth reflecting on structural issues in this market. From a macro perspective, Brazil stands out among emerging markets regarding consumption. The share of total income spent on consumption in Brazil is among the highest among countries with per capita income similar to ours. This is reflected in the very low domestic savings rate (15% of income) when compared to other emerging countries (25% or more). Furthermore, our per capita consumption, adjusted for purchasing power, is well above the average for emerging countries. In other words, everything indicates that we have a greater propensity to consume. Numerous countries with per capita income lower than ours save more. So this is not due to income level.

What differentiates the Brazilian case is the structure of the State. We decided to have a Government that offers a broader social safety net. Spending at all its levels, as a percentage of GDP, is the highest among countries with similar income, even when we exclude spending on interest. In terms of provision of public services, government consumption as a proportion of GDP, we are 15% above the global average. With respect to social transfers via pensions, unemployment benefits, and social protection programs, we are by far the largest. All of this was chosen when our Constitution was written in 1988.

Economic literature recognizes that the larger the social protection network—that is, the provision of public services to the population plus income transfers—the lower the propensity to save. This makes intuitive sense. In this context, the need for precautionary savings is greatly reduced. IBGE's POF data shows that people earning up to three minimum wages consume 100% of their income or more. At the same time, such families spend about 13% of their income on interest.

If the above diagnosis is correct, the adoption of prudential rules by the Central Bank that prevent people with high income commitment from going further into debt would be inevitable. This would be unpopular, but necessary both from a social perspective and for the health of the financial system.

*Ricardo Gallo is a partner at Ethica Serviços Financeiros.*
