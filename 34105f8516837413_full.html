# Dollar Under Pressure: What to Expect for End-of-2026 Closure? Here's What Some Analysts Predict

The dollar closed the previous week at 3,551.17 pesos—which remains the official rate this Monday—and in recent days has traded at minimum prices close to 3,530 pesos, the lowest levels observed in the last five years. Thus, the US currency is at its lowest levels in five years.

## What Will Happen with the Peso and Dollar?

Experts are making their calculations about where the currency could move in the future. Despite recent days, the dollar would end the year near 3,740 pesos, according to projections from Banco de Bogotá.

Although recent dollar movements in Colombia are below 3,600 pesos, analysts agree this is atypical behavior within currency market dynamics. A Banco de Bogotá report indicates this level responds to a favorable international environment for emerging market currencies and global dollar weakness.

Amid the Central Bank's publication of its monthly survey of economic expectations, a downward adjustment was evident in analysts' projections for the Representative Market Rate (TRM) to 3,743 pesos for year-end, compared to 3,800 pesos estimated in March. According to Banco de Bogotá, "this adjustment would respond to changes in the country's political outlook, according to recent polls, as well as modifications in interest rate expectations for both the Central Bank and the Federal Reserve (Fed)."

The report also warns that the exchange rate has consolidated below that threshold in line with global dollar weakness, in an environment where investors have reduced their exposure to safe-haven assets.

Another analysis from Banco Itaú agrees that the Colombian peso has gained ground amid a more stable international environment and greater appetite for risk assets. The last time the dollar traded at similar figures was March 20, 2021, when the TRM stood at 3,553.34 pesos. This means the exchange rate has returned to levels seen five years ago.

Compared to the beginning of 2026, the current price is about 206 pesos lower, while compared to the same period last year the decline exceeds 688 pesos. On an annual basis, the Colombian peso has appreciated approximately 16.24 percent according to Itaú, establishing itself as one of the best-performing currencies globally.

Juan David Ballén, director of Economics and Market at Aval Asset Management, noted that "the dollar would continue relatively weak globally as long as a recession in the United States does not materialize." He added that although the probability of a recession has increased, partly due to shocks such as oil price increases, it has not yet materialized, so the short-term bias remains toward dollar weakness rather than structural strengthening.

The analyst also explained that in Colombia's case, exchange rate behavior would not only replicate the global trend but would depend on internal factors. "The peso could appreciate in a weak dollar environment, as long as investor confidence and economic policy credibility are preserved. In contrast," he warned, "signals of fiscal deterioration, institutional interventions, or changes affecting legal stability could generate peso depreciation, even in a favorable international scenario."

## External Factors

Oil behavior adds to this, with prices showing recent rebounds, favoring foreign currency inflows to the country. Greater dollar supply in the market has also influenced this, from both foreign investors and local agents who have liquidated currencies for tax payments. In this context, Davibank analysis explains that the current dollar level responds to a combination of local and external factors.
