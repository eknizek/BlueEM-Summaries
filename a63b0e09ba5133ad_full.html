# SIC Opens Formal Investigation Against Claro for Alleged E-Commerce and Financing Irregularities

The Superintendency of Industry and Commerce (SIC) has placed the digital operations of Comcel S.A., commercially known as Claro, under scrutiny. On April 13, 2026, the Consumer Protection Investigation Directorate announced the filing of formal charges against the company. The decision is based on a series of preliminary findings suggesting possible systematic violation of the rights of users who purchase mobile devices and technological products through its e-commerce portal, as well as those who use its direct financing systems.

This administrative action is not an improvised process. The entity reached this stage following a preliminary investigation that included detailed information requests, technical visits to the company's website, and a thorough review of advertising materials and financing contracts signed by customers. The results of this initial analysis suggest that the company may have engaged in practices that affect transparency and free choice of citizens in the digital environment.

## Failures in Information

One of the central axes of the investigation focuses on how Claro presents its offers to the public. The SIC detected possible irregularities in the dissemination of promotions, noting that apparently the company failed to include essential conditions in its advertising. Among the failures mentioned are the lack of clear identification of benefits, lack of knowledge of requirements to access discounts, and the absence of information about the exact amount or percentage of the discount offered. Adding to this is the suspicion that the company provided misleading information about the availability of its products. In e-commerce, the accuracy of stock is vital, but the investigation suggests that Claro did not clearly and promptly inform about the quantity of units available for offers. Additionally, the authority detected that the total sale price to the public may not have been visibly displayed. This implies that users apparently did not know from the outset all the additional costs that could derive from purchasing technology or appliances on the platform.

## Charges Related to Financing

The second set of charges is of particular gravity, as it directly affects the wallets of consumers who resort to credit. The SIC is investigating alleged charging of interest above the legal limits allowed in operations carried out through Claro's own financing systems. This finding would put into question the legality of the rates applied by the provider in its installment sales, a very common modality for purchasing high-end mobile phones.

On the other hand, the entity questions the conditioning of the exercise of the right of withdrawal. According to the preliminary investigation, the company allegedly required the presentation of the purchase invoice as a mandatory requirement for users to return products and request their money back. This administrative requirement apparently becomes an unnecessary obstacle that limits a legal guarantee that all buyers have in non-face-to-face channels.

Finally, the SIC points out a possible general non-compliance with e-commerce regulations due to the lack of adequate mechanisms to manage Petitions, Complaints, and Claims (PQR). For the authority, it is imperative that companies have channels that guarantee guidance and real assistance to consumers in the after-sales service of technological goods. The absence of these appropriate tools leaves users in a position of vulnerability vis-à-vis the provider after the transaction is completed.
