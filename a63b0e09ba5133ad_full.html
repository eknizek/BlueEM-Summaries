# Colombia's SIC Opens Formal Investigation Against Claro for Alleged E-commerce Irregularities and Financing Violations

The Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) has scrutinized the digital operations of Comcel S.A., commercially known as Claro. On April 13, 2026, the Consumer Protection Investigation Department announced the filing of formal charges against the company. The decision is based on a series of preliminary findings that suggest a possible systematic violation of the rights of users who purchase mobile devices and technological products through its e-commerce portal, as well as those who use its direct financing systems.

This administrative action is not an improvised process. The entity reached this stage after a preliminary investigation that included detailed requests for information, technical visits to the company's website, and a thorough review of advertising pieces and financing contracts signed by customers. The results of this initial analysis suggest that the company may have engaged in practices that affect transparency and free choice of citizens in the digital environment.

## The Gaps in Information

One of the central axes of the investigation focuses on how Claro presents its offers to the public. The SIC detected possible irregularities in the dissemination of promotions, noting that apparently the company failed to include essential conditions in its advertising. Among the defects mentioned are the lack of clear identification of benefits, ignorance of requirements to access discounts, and the absence of information about the exact amount or percentage of the discount offered. Added to this is the suspicion that the company provided misleading information about the availability of its products. In e-commerce, the veracity of inventory is vital, but the investigation suggests that Claro did not clearly and promptly inform about the quantity of units available for offers. Likewise, the authority detected that the total selling price to the public was not displayed visibly. This implies that, allegedly, users did not know from the beginning all the additional costs that could result from purchasing technology or appliances on the platform.

The second set of charges is particularly serious, as it directly affects the wallets of consumers who resort to credit. The SIC investigates a presumed charging of interest above the legal limits permitted in operations conducted through Claro's own financing systems. This finding would call into question the legality of the rates applied by the provider in its installment sales, a very common modality for acquiring high-end mobile phones.

On the other hand, the entity questions the conditioning of the right of withdrawal. According to the preliminary investigation, the company allegedly required the presentation of the purchase invoice as a mandatory requirement for users to return products and request their money back. This administrative requirement apparently becomes an unnecessary obstacle that limits a legal guarantee that all buyers have in non-face-to-face channels.

Finally, the SIC points out a possible general breach of e-commerce regulations due to the lack of adequate mechanisms to manage Petitions, Complaints, and Claims (PQR). For the authority, it is imperative that companies have channels that guarantee genuine guidance and assistance to consumers in the after-sales of technological goods. The absence of these appropriate tools leaves the user in a vulnerable position with respect to the provider after completing the transaction.
