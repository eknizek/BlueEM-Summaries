# Drug prices depend on more than just the market: oil and plastics drive costs higher

The rising cost of medicines in Colombia does not respond solely to regulatory decisions or market price dynamics, but rather to a transformation of global supply chains and increased costs of key inputs such as oil and plastics. This was confirmed by Eduardo Franciosi of Alifar at the Third Latin American Pharmaceutical Forum held in Bogotá.

According to Carlos Francisco Fernández, executive president of Asinfar, Colombia has historically approached health as an accounting matter, when in reality it is a structural problem: "Medicines are not an expense, nor a variable for adjustment," Fernández stated, explaining that the global context has changed drastically. Supply chains, once guided by efficiency, are now permeated by power factors and geopolitics, conditioning the flow of pharmaceutical inputs and products.

## Pharmaceutical prices depend on oil and industrial inputs

One of the central points presented at the forum is that the pharmaceutical industry is highly dependent on oil. Alifar explained that increases in hydrocarbon prices directly impact medicine production, not only through active ingredients but also through associated materials. This is compounded by rising costs of plastic and aluminum, essential inputs for packaging and distribution processes. The situation is worsened by a 30% increase in maritime and air transport costs, further raising the price of imported medicines. Although there is no widespread shortage, there are "tensions amid global uncertainty," introducing volatility in supply.

These factors, far from being temporary, become arguments for promoting a reindustrialization strategy for the sector. Within this context, insisting on importing medicines at low cost could be counterproductive. "Importing cheaply or paying monopolies is a renunciation of health policy," warned Asinfar, noting that this dependence limits the country's capacity to respond to international tensions. The discussion, therefore, transcends price and focuses on productive autonomy. Colombia depends on imported medicines for over 70%.

## Pharmaceutical policy: from low prices to sustained availability

The debate at the forum points to a shift in public policy focus. "Access is not guaranteed with low prices, but with sustained availability," Asinfar emphasized, highlighting that supply stability is the true indicator of a robust health system. This implies strengthening national production and reducing exposure to external shocks.

From the Ministry of Commerce, Industry and Tourism, Mario Alejandro Valencia supported this vision, stating that the Government's objective is to strengthen national production amid a complex geopolitical environment. "Our mission is to seek national production," he said, indicating that Colombia has studied models from countries like Mexico and Brazil, with similar goals for pharmaceutical industrialization. Strengthening presence in global supply chains is also part of this strategy. It is not merely about replacing imports, but about integrating more solidly into international production to respond to crises and guarantee internal supply. Pharmaceutical policy, in this sense, is proposed as a state policy and not a circumstantial one.

## Global risks and pending lessons after the pandemic

Francisco Augusto Rossi Buenaventura, general director of the National Institute for Drug and Food Surveillance (Invima), warned that while the world could face a new pandemic, Colombia has not corrected the failures evident during the COVID-19 crisis. "A new pandemic will come and we are not prepared," he stated, noting that problems persist such as vaccine concentration among few actors and fragmentation of supply chains. This scenario reinforces the need for an independent pharmaceutical policy. Experience has shown that in crisis contexts, countries with greater economic capacity gain access to medicines and vaccines first, leaving developing economies at a disadvantage. Colombia, despite having relevant institutional figures in global health, remains exposed to these dynamics.

In this line, Rossi Buenaventura insists that discussions on health no longer concentrate exclusively on multilateral organizations like the World Health Organization (WHO), but are increasingly influenced by strategic and economic interests. This forces a rethinking of the country's role on the international stage.

In this order of ideas, the problem of access to medicines in Colombia is not solved with isolated price controls. The pressure from oil, the rising cost of plastics, and increased logistics costs are symptoms of a global system in transformation. Faced with this, the country faces a fundamental decision: strengthen its pharmaceutical policy or accept a dependence that conditions its health system.
