# 99Food's Delayed Expansion Impacts Global Valuation of Chinese DiDi

DiDi, the controlling company of 99, has felt the bitter results of implementing its food delivery operation in Brazil through 99Food. A report from Australian bank Macquarie downgraded the company's recommendation due to the high costs of the food delivery operation in Brazil.

The document, signed by analyst Ellie Jiang, shows that the company recorded a loss of 2.1 billion yuan (US$ 307 million), driven precisely by the international segment, where Brazil is located—posting a negative result of 3.4 billion yuan (US$ 497.8 million), four times larger than the previous year.

"The losses were largely linked to the company's entry into the food delivery sector in Brazil, where expansion and incentives for users and drivers increased costs," states the Macquarie report.

The financial institution's forecast is that DiDi's international segment will record a loss of 10 billion yuan (US$ 1.47 billion) in 2026. The reasons are the same: growth in transaction volume and continuation of spending on the Brazilian operation.

With this, DiDi's stock fell from outperform to neutral. The price target for the next 12 months, according to the Australian institution's assessment, plummeted from US$ 9.30 to US$ 3.90, a reduction of 42%.

"Given its significant spending and limited visibility toward an IPO in Hong Kong, we do not see a near-term catalyst and are downgrading to neutral," states the Macquarie analyst.

On Monday, April 13, the company's shares were trading at US$ 3.78 at 4 p.m. (U.S. time).

In practice, the food delivery service in Brazil is currently seen simultaneously as a possible growth vector but one that significantly pressures the company's near-term results. In the balance sheet, DiDi acknowledges a 46% increase in sales and marketing expenses in 2025, caused by the growth of discounts for consumers and spending to expand food delivery operations in the Brazilian market.

The Chinese company resumed meal delivery operations in Brazil in August last year, covering cities such as São Paulo, Guarulhos, Osasco, and the ABC Paulista region. The operation also began functioning in Goiânia. Rio de Janeiro and Salvador were the most recent markets the company began to operate in.

The service had been interrupted in 2023 and resumed after the announcement of a R$ 1 billion investment plan by the Chinese company in all of the company's operations in Brazil, including delivery, financial solutions, freight services, and the urban mobility segment.

In September, DiDi CEO and founder Will Wei Cheng was in Brasília, meeting with President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, to discuss the company's investments in the country. During the conversation, the executive stated he would double the investments to R$ 2 billion by June 2026, with the ambition to reach 20 cities in the first months of the year.

It was precisely the need to develop other areas that generated more profitability for the company, such as 99Moto, and the intense competition in Brazil's food delivery market with iFood that caused DiDi to stop operating the food delivery model. Today, in addition to the market leader, 99Food faces competition from fellow Chinese company Keeta in the country.

DiDi's revenue in the fourth quarter of 2025, however, recorded 10% growth over the same period last year, with 58 billion yuan (US$ 8.5 billion). In the balance sheet, the company acknowledges increased costs with marketing actions and subsidies, especially in the international market.

In 2026, DiDi's shares in the OTC market, a kind of U.S. stock exchange, have accumulated a 28.2% loss. In six months, the devaluation is 39.5%. The Chinese company is valued at US$ 17.6 billion.
