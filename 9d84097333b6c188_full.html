# AI and Brazilian Student Money Reshape Higher Education in Portugal

Lisbon — The debate over the use of artificial intelligence in European universities gained, in 2026, an additional layer that goes beyond pedagogy: economic impact. In Portugal, where foreign students — especially Brazilians — have become a structural part of the higher education system, the way AI is regulated can affect not only classrooms, but revenues, urban markets, and the financial balance of institutions.

In April, Brazil's Ministry of Education published the document "Artificial Intelligence in Basic Education: Paths for the Curriculum and Teaching Practice," which guides schools and universities to integrate the technology as a pedagogical tool. The directive bets on critical training and guided use of AI, treating it as an instrument for expanding research capacity.

In Europe, the advancement of the EU AI Act follows different logic. The regulation does not prohibit the use of AI in education, but imposes a risk classification system and strict transparency obligations. In practice, universities have adopted more restrictive internal policies, especially to avoid plagiarism accusations and guarantee academic integrity.

This difference in approach occurs at a time when Portuguese higher education depends increasingly on international students.

## A System Sustained by Foreigners

Data from DGEEC (Directorate-General for Education and Science Statistics) indicate that Portugal exceeded 447,000 students in higher education in 2025/2026. Within this universe, Brazilians represent the largest foreign group, with approximately 19,000 enrollments, according to DGES (Directorate-General for Higher Education) and AIMA (Agency for Integration, Migration and Asylum).

The weight of this contingent goes beyond academic diversity. It has direct impact on institutional finances. International students pay significantly higher tuition — on average between 4,000 and 7,000 euros per year. Based on these values, the Brazilian presence generates estimated annual revenue of around 95 million euros in tuition alone.

But this is only the first level of impact.

## The Invisible Economy of the Student

Outside universities, the effect is even broader. An international student in cities like Lisbon or Porto spends, on average, between 900 and 1,500 euros per month on housing, food, transportation and services. Applying a conservative value of 12,000 euros annually per student, the approximately 19,000 Brazilians inject approximately 228 million euros per year into the local economy.

Combining tuition and consumption, the total economic impact exceeds 300 million euros annually, or R$ 1.8 billion/year. This flow directly benefits sectors such as:

- Real estate market (student rentals)

- Food service

- Transportation

- Telecommunications

- Financial services

In practice, higher education has become a form of service export — a silent, but relevant industry for the Portuguese economy.

## The Regulatory Paradox

It is in this context that the question of artificial intelligence gains economic weight. While Brazil encourages the use of AI as a tool for academic productivity, students in Portugal face a more restrictive environment. Reports from Portuguese media outlets point to a climate of distrust and fear regarding the use of these tools, with reports of academic sanctions associated with their misuse.

For Brazilian students, this creates a formative mismatch. Tools that are encouraged in their country of origin become seen as disciplinary risk in Portuguese universities.

The insecurity increases with the use of AI detection systems. Studies conducted by institutions such as Stanford University and MIT indicate that these tools have significant failures, including false positives — which raises doubts about their use as a basis for academic punishments.

## Dependence and Risk

The current model brings clear benefits, but also fragilities. Portuguese universities face:

- Reduction in the number of national students

- Demographic aging

- Need for own revenues

In this scenario, international students — especially Brazilians — have ceased to be complementary and have become structural. This dependence creates risks:

- Changes in migration rules

- Increased cost of living

- Perception of academic hostility

- Competition from other European countries

Any change in these factors can directly affect the financial sustainability of courses and institutions.

## More Than Technology, a Choice of Model

The divergence between Brazil and Europe in the use of artificial intelligence reflects, fundamentally, two visions of education. On one side, a model that bets on rapid adaptation to new tools; on the other, a system that prioritizes control, integrity and risk mitigation.

For Portugal, the question is particularly sensitive. The country needs to balance academic rigor with the maintenance of an ecosystem that depends, in significant part, on foreign students.

For Brazilians, who invest their own resources in pursuit of international education, the challenge is different: navigating between two systems that treat the same technology in radically distinct ways.
