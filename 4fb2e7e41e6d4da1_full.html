# Failed with a dotcom in 1996 and sold Paperless in 2017: The story of Pablo Vicuña

In 1996, Pablo Vicuña received a call from a cousin—technically an uncle, though their generational relationship made them peers—who had grown up in Portland, Oregon and come to Chile at age 20. They called him "Gringo" and he was usually six years ahead of the rest of the world, Vicuña says. "Buddy, we have to start a dotcom," he told him one afternoon, and Vicuña, who had no idea what a dotcom was, found himself dragged into the idea. Gringo started his own, Golazo.com, and Vicuña inherited "the bug." Together they tried to build something like Skype by buying minute packages from telecom companies and plugging them into an application that practically nobody had. It never worked, but along the way he met Decidir.com, an Argentine dotcom that had raised millions of dollars and was trying to survive by buying other companies, one of which had the representation of Baltimore Technologies, a global pioneer in digital signatures. Between trips to Boston and conversations with the Americans, Vicuña became convinced of something he would carry for years: that digital signatures would eventually matter.

Decidir.com closed, Baltimore entered Chapter 11, and he returned to Chile with that intuition.

## Zero salary, Don Juan Vicuña and the SII

In 2001, he joined forces with Pedro Cabezón—his lifelong partner and friend—and together they raised a small fund from the family office of the unrelated Vicuña Fernández family. This is how they began developing Paperless, a company that created solutions for digital signatures and electronic invoices. "Don Juan would tell me: 'I understand little of what you do, but what's clear to me is that in 10 years, continuing to sign papers won't make much sense. This thing has to catch on somewhere,'" he recalls.

They decided to develop everything from scratch just as the SII—during "the golden age of Javier Etcheberry," he says—was running a massive tender to provide electronic invoices to all companies in the country. The SII quickly pivoted toward an open model, with each provider competing under their own standard, and Paperless, which already had the solution ready, started with clients like Entel and VTR. The first three years were rough, with no salary: they lived off their last earnings while evangelizing a market that was just beginning to understand what PKI (public key infrastructure) was. Over time, they accumulated more and more clients.

## $3 million and a plane to São Paulo

In 2007, Ricardo Neves from the Treasury Department of Rio Grande do Sul called them—a public official who, according to Vicuña, "was obsessed with replicating in Brazil what Chile had done with electronic invoices and receipts." Vicuña began working pro bono with the Brazilian government on the regulatory draft, saw the size of the market, and together with Felipe Camposano—at that moment with Aurus, now Taram Capital—closed a $3 million round after several failed attempts. The company then had about 70 to 80 people, they hired a professional general manager, and Vicuña boarded a plane to São Paulo with his wife and three children. The youngest had just turned one month old.

At first, Brazil was also rough. "We didn't sell anything, and the things we did sell we delivered poorly; it was a disaster," he admits. They had to lower their target customers and only when they reached mid-sized companies, waited for retail receipt regulations, and spent two years evangelizing the major players in the sector one by one did Paperless start to gain traction: when the regulation came out in 2011, Pão de Açúcar, Walmart, and Cencosud were already ready to connect, and volumes became, according to Vicuña, "brutal."

Looking back, he lists three things he believes he did right: going in person, hiring only Brazilians—he put his son in a Brazilian school, distanced himself from the expat circuit—and building a complete local structure for product, operations, and support. "It's not viable to centralize; you need someone who has lived their whole life with the Brazilian tax system inside their head," he says.

## The sale

Vicuña returned to Chile in 2015 with Paperless operating in Chile, Brazil, Colombia, Peru, and Uruguay, landing in Mexico and with country managers in each country. That's when he and his partner started getting bored with execution: they were torn between day-to-day operations and the long-term projects they wanted to push as founders. "We wanted to do things that would pay off in 10 years, and that wasn't happening. The day-to-day always pulled harder," he acknowledges.

At that point, some funds approached them to evaluate a purchase, but nothing materialized. Finally, they hired Altis as their investment bank. The process took a year and a half and was exhausting, he says. Plus, there was a surprise that Vicuña still repeats: the two companies that seemed to him to be the obvious buyers didn't have Paperless on their radar. Three interested parties arrived, two remained, and the final negotiation stretched for six months until closing in July 2017, with the founders exiting 100%. What he would change today, he says, is internal communication: "After the sale, I heard comments from people saying: 'You sold us, you betrayed us.' We made a mistake there, out of respect for the people who work their tails off."

## Beagle Ventures, Rankmi and the studio

Between July and December 2017, they allowed themselves to rest, and in 2018 they launched Beagle Ventures—an $8 million fund with Link Capital and Altis partners—under a narrow thesis: B2B corporate, technology-based services, Latam. The first investment was Rankmi. In total, they closed four investments. For example, an online art company that fell with the pandemic and another for electronic titles for real estate in the U.S. that ended up being sold to Real Brokerage in less than a year. In 2021 and 2022, they hit a hard stop with the venture market's breakneck growth. They didn't close a single new investment, and when the cycle deflated, they wound down the fund to get their hands back into their own operations.

Today they operate a studio: they bought 100% of Factura Chile from the Chamber of Commerce and merged it with another to create Factura X—headed by Juan Cristóbal Palacio, operating in Chile and Peru—and they launched Asteroide, which takes retail electronic receipts and converts them into an interactive receipt with AI. "When I put AI in the product, it changed how you relate to the customer; both are learning at the same time," Vicuña concludes.
