From failed dot-com to $8M venture fund: The entrepreneurial journey of Pablo Vicuña

02 May 2026 · 07:21 PM

# From failed dot-com to $8M venture fund: The entrepreneurial journey of Pablo Vicuña

In 1996, Pablo Vicuña received a call from a cousin—technically an uncle, though they treated each other as peers due to generational differences—who had grown up in Portland, Oregon and moved to Chile after turning 20. They called him "Gringo," and he was always about six years ahead of the rest of the world, according to Vicuña. "Buddy, we have to start a dot-com," he said one afternoon, and Vicuña, who had no idea what a dot-com was, ended up getting dragged into the idea. Gringo started his own, Golazo.com, and Vicuña caught "the bug." Together they tried to build something like Skype by purchasing minute packages from telecom companies and plugging them into an application that practically no one had. It never worked, but along the way he met Decidir.com, an Argentine dot-com that had raised millions of dollars and was trying to sustain itself by buying other companies, including one that brought the representation of Baltimore Technologies, a world pioneer in digital signatures. Between trips to Boston and conversations with Americans, Vicuña became convinced of something he would carry with him for years: that digital signatures would eventually matter.

Decidir.com shut down, Baltimore filed Chapter 11, and he returned to Chile with that intuition.

## Zero salary, Don Juan Vicuña, and the SII

In 2001, he joined forces with Pedro Cabezón—his lifelong partner and friend—and together they raised a small fund with the Vicuña Fernández family office, unrelated to them. This is how Paperless began, a company that created solutions for digital signatures and electronic invoices. "Don Juan would tell me: 'I understand little of what you do, but what's clear to me is that in 10 years, continuing to sign papers won't make much sense. This thing has to catch on somewhere,'" he recalls.

They decided to develop everything from scratch just as the SII—during "the golden era of Javier Etcheberry," as Vicuña says—was mounting a massive bid to deliver electronic invoicing to all companies in the country. The SII quickly pivoted toward an open model, with each provider competing under its own standard, and Paperless, which already had the solution ready, started with clients like Entel and VTR. The first three years were rough, working without salary: they lived off every last bit of income while evangelizing a market that barely understood what a PKI (public key infrastructure) was. Over time, they added more and more clients.

## $3 million and a plane to São Paulo

In 2007, Ricardo Neves, from the Finance Department of Rio Grande do Sul, called them. A public servant who, according to Vicuña, "was obsessed with replicating in Brazil what Chile had done with electronic invoices and receipts." Vicuña started working pro bono with the Brazilian government on the regulatory draft, saw the size of the market, and together with Felipe Camposano—who was at Aurus at the time, now Taram Capital—closed a $3 million round after several failed attempts. The company had around 70 to 80 people at that point. They hired a professional general manager, and Vicuña boarded a plane to São Paulo with his wife and three children. Their youngest had just turned one month old.

Initially, Brazil was also tough. "We didn't sell anything, and the things we did sell we delivered poorly; it was a disaster," he admits. They had to lower their target customers and only when they reached mid-sized companies, waited for retail receipt regulation, and spent two years evangelizing one by one with the sector's major players did Paperless start to take off: when the regulation came out in 2011, Pão de Açúcar, Walmart, and Cencosud were already ready to connect, and volumes became, according to Vicuña, "brutal."

Looking back, he lists three things he believes he did right: going in person, hiring only Brazilians—he put his son in Brazilian school, stayed away from the expat circuit—and building a complete local structure for product, operations, and support. "It's not viable to centralize; you need someone who has lived their whole life with the Brazilian tax system inside them," he says.

## The sale

Vicuña returned to Chile in 2015 with Paperless operating in Chile, Brazil, Colombia, Peru, and Uruguay, landing in Mexico and with country managers in each country. That's when he and his partner started getting bored with execution: they lived torn between day-to-day operations and long-term projects they wanted to push as founders. "We wanted to do things that would pay off in 10 years, and that wasn't happening. Day-to-day always pulled harder," he acknowledges.

At those moments, some funds approached to evaluate a purchase, but nothing materialized. Finally, they hired Altis as their investment bank. The process lasted a year and a half and was exhausting, he says. Plus, there was a surprise that Vicuña still repeats: the two companies that were obvious buyers to them didn't have Paperless in their pipeline. Three interested parties came, two remained, and the final negotiation stretched six months until closing in July 2017, with the founders exiting 100%.

What he would change today, he says, is internal communication: "After the sale, I heard comments from people saying: 'You sold us out, you betrayed us.' That's where we made a mistake, out of respect for the people who work their tails off."

## Beagle Ventures, Rankmi, and the studio

Between July and December 2017, they took time to rest, and in 2018 they launched Beagle Ventures—an $8 million fund with Link Capital and Altis partners—under a narrow thesis: B2B corporate, technology-based services, Latin America. The first investment was Rankmi. In total, they closed four investments. For example, an online art company that fell with the pandemic and another electronic titles company for real estate in the U.S. that ended up being sold to Real Brokerage in less than a year.

In 2021 and 2022, they hit the brakes with the market's vertiginous venture growth. They didn't close a single new investment, and when the cycle deflated, they wound down the fund to get their hands back into their own operations.

Today they operate a studio: they bought 100% of Factura Chile from the Chamber of Commerce and merged it with another to create Factura X—led by Juan Cristóbal Palacio, operating in Chile and Peru—and they launched Asteroide, which takes retail electronic receipts and converts them into an interactive receipt with AI. "When I put AI in the product, it changed how you relate to the customer; both are learning at the same time," Vicuña concludes.
