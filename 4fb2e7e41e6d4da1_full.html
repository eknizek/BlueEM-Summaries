Failed With a Dotcom in 1996 and Sold Paperless in 2017: The Story of Pablo Vicuña

# Failed With a Dotcom in 1996 and Sold Paperless in 2017: The Story of Pablo Vicuña

In 1996, Pablo Vicuña received a call from a cousin—technically an uncle, though they treated each other as peers due to their generational crossing—who had grown up in Portland, Oregon and came to Chile after turning 20. They called him "Gringo," and he was usually six years ahead of the rest of the world, according to Vicuña. "Buddy, we have to start a dotcom," he said one afternoon, and Vicuña, who had no idea what a dotcom was, found himself dragged into the idea. Gringo started his own, Golazo.com, and Vicuña was left with "the bug." Together they tried to build something like Skype by buying minute packages from telecom companies and plugging them into an application that practically nobody had. It never worked, but along the way he met Decidir.com, an Argentine dotcom that had raised millions of dollars and tried to sustain itself by buying other companies, one of which brought the representation of Baltimore Technologies, a global pioneer in digital signatures.

Between trips to Boston and conversations with Americans, Vicuña became convinced of something he would carry for years: that digital signatures would eventually matter. Decidir.com closed, Baltimore entered Chapter 11, and he returned to Chile with that intuition.

## Zero Salary, Don Juan Vicuña and the SII

In 2001, he joined forces with Pedro Cabezón—his lifelong partner and friend—and together they raised a small fund from the family office of the unrelated Vicuña Fernández family. This is how Paperless began, a company that created solutions for digital signatures and electronic invoicing. "Don Juan told me: 'I understand little of what you do, but what's clear to me is that in 10 years more, continuing to sign papers won't make much sense. This thing has to catch on somewhere,'" he recalls.

They decided to develop everything from scratch just as the SII—in what Vicuña calls "the golden age of Javier Etcheberry"—was mounting a massive bidding process to deliver electronic invoicing to all companies in the country. The SII quickly pivoted toward an open model, with each provider competing under its own standard, and Paperless, which already had the solution built, started with clients like Entel and VTR. The first three years were rough, without salary: they ate through every penny while evangelizing a market that was just beginning to understand what a PKI (public key infrastructure) was. Over time, they accumulated more and more clients.

## $3 Million and a Plane to São Paulo

In 2007, Ricardo Neves from the Treasury Secretary's office of Rio Grande do Sul called them—a public employee who, according to Vicuña, "was obsessed with replicating in Brazil what Chile had done with electronic invoicing and receipts." Vicuña started working pro bono with the Brazilian government on the regulatory draft, saw the size of the market, and together with Felipe Camposano—then at Aurus, now Taram Capital—closed a $3 million round after several failed attempts. The company had around 70 to 80 people at the time, they hired a professional general manager, and Vicuña boarded a plane to São Paulo with his wife and three children. The youngest had just turned one month old.

In the beginning, Brazil was also rough. "We didn't sell anything, and the things we did sell we delivered poorly; it was a disaster," he admits. They had to lower their customer targets and only when they reached mid-sized companies, wait for retail receipt regulation, and spend two years evangelizing one by one the big players in the sector, did Paperless start to gain traction: when the regulation came out in 2011, Pão de Açúcar, Walmart, and Cencosud were already ready to connect, and volumes became, according to Vicuña, "brutal."

Looking back, he lists three things he believes he did well: going there in person, hiring only Brazilians—he put his son in a Brazilian school, distanced himself from the expat circuit—and building a complete local structure for product, operations, and support. "It's not viable to centralize; you need someone who has lived their entire life with the Brazilian tax system inside them," he says.

## The Sale

Vicuña returned to Chile in 2015 with Paperless operating in Chile, Brazil, Colombia, Peru, and Uruguay, landing in Mexico and with country managers in each country. That's when he and his partner started getting bored with execution: they were pulled between day-to-day operations and long-term projects they wanted to push as founders. "We wanted to do things that would pay off in 10 years, and that wasn't happening. Day-to-day always pulled harder," he acknowledges.

At that point, some funds approached them to evaluate a purchase, but nothing materialized. Finally, they hired Altis as their investment bank. The process lasted a year and a half and was exhausting, he says. Plus, there was a surprise Vicuña still repeats: the two companies that seemed like the obvious buyers didn't even have Paperless in their pipeline. Three interested parties arrived, two remained, and the final negotiations dragged on for six months until closing in July 2017, with the founders exiting 100%.

What he would change today, he says, is internal communication: "After the sale, I heard comments from people saying: 'You sold us, you betrayed us.' We made a mistake there, out of respect for people who work their tails off."

## Beagle Ventures, Rankmi, and the Studio

Between July and December 2017, they allowed themselves to rest, and in 2018 they set up Beagle Ventures—an $8 million fund with Link Capital and Altis partners—under a focused thesis: B2B corporate, technology-based services, Latam. The first investment was Rankmi. In total, they closed four investments. For example, an online art company that fell with the pandemic and another for electronic titles for real estate in the U.S. that ended up sold to Real Brokerage in less than a year. In 2021 and 2022, they hit the brakes hard with the vertiginous growth of the venture market. They didn't close a single new investment, and when the cycle deflated they wound down the fund to get back to hands-on operations.

Today they operate a studio: they bought 100% of Factura Chile from the Chamber of Commerce and merged it with another to set up Factura X—run by Juan Cristóbal Palacio, operating in Chile and Peru—and launched Asteroide, which takes the retail electronic receipt and converts it into an interactive receipt with AI. "When I put AI in the product, it changed the way you relate to the customer; both are learning at the same time," Vicuña concludes.
