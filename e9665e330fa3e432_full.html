# Trump orders naval blockade of Strait of Hormuz after failed Iran negotiations

U.S. President Donald Trump ordered a naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday after accusing Iran of maintaining an "inflexible" stance on its nuclear ambitions during negotiations in Pakistan aimed at ending six weeks of war in the Middle East.

The failure of negotiations in Islamabad has raised concerns about the fate of the fragile ceasefire and the possibility that a resumption of fighting could drive up energy prices with consequences for the global economy.

The U.S. president acknowledged that the talks had gone "well" and that agreement had been reached on most points, except on the nuclear issue, in a post on Truth Social. Trump announced that the U.S. Navy would begin "effective immediately" a blockade of all ships attempting to enter or leave the Strait of Hormuz and said they would begin to "destroy" sea mines placed by Iran.

"The blockade will begin soon. Other countries will be involved," he added, without providing further details.

Since the start of the war—which has caused thousands of deaths, particularly in Iran and Lebanon—Tehran has maintained strict control of passage through this strait, through which one-fifth of global oil exports transited. Iran has allowed passage of ships from countries considered allies, such as China, and there are unconfirmed reports that Tehran wants to charge tolls on this route.

In Iran, the Islamic Revolutionary Guards responded to Trump, stating that their forces maintain "complete control" of the strait and warning that their enemies would be caught in a "deadly whirlwind" if they make any misstep. Iran's Fars News Agency reported Sunday that two Pakistani-flagged tankers heading toward the strait had turned around.

Iran reported Sunday that the Institute of Forensic Medicine counted 3,375 deaths during the war. Meanwhile, the U.S.-based NGO Human Rights Activists News Agency (HRANA) had registered at least 3,597 deaths as of April 6, of which 1,665 were civilians, including at least 248 children.

The United States and Israel attacked Iran on February 28, and the Islamic Republic responded with bombardments of Gulf countries, U.S. targets, and missiles launched against locations in Israel, dragging the entire Middle East into a conflict that has shaken the global economy.

## "End Iran"

Trump resumed his strategy of threats on Sunday, stating that he "could end Iran in a single day" in an interview with Fox News. The president warned that he can "end their entire energy system" and cautioned China that if it provides military aid to Iran, he will impose 50% tariffs.

Trump's most recent ultimatum apparently came as a result of failed negotiations to secure an agreement enabling an end to the war. U.S. Vice President JD Vance, who led the American delegation, lamented the lack of a "firm commitment" from Iran to renounce nuclear weapons.

Iran insists on its right to maintain a nuclear program for civilian purposes, but Western countries accuse it of wanting to acquire nuclear weapons.

"I have always said, from the beginning, and many years ago, that IRAN WILL NEVER HAVE A NUCLEAR WEAPON," Trump said alongside the announcement of the naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz.

Experts have said that preventing transit through the crucial maritime passage in the middle of a two-week ceasefire, following negotiations in Islamabad, would damage U.S. credibility with the world.
