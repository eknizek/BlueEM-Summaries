99Food offered R$300,000 to prevent restaurant from operating with Rappi and Keeta

25 April 2026 · 06:00 PM

# 99Food Offered R$300,000 to Prevent Restaurant from Operating with Rappi and Keeta

99Food, the delivery platform from 99 that returned to Brazil last year, offered R$300,000 to ensure that a restaurant would not join the platforms of two smaller competitors—the Chinese Keeta and the Colombian Rappi. Terms of a contract obtained by Folha show that the company proposed an initial financial investment ("upfront," in corporate jargon) in exchange for guaranteeing that the restaurant owner would not do business with the two competitors. In case of non-compliance, the restaurant could pay a fine equal to one and a half times the upfront value—in the case of the document analyzed by the reporting, R$450,000—in addition to commissions previously exempted.

The practice is not new—it has already motivated a lawsuit and the opening of an inquiry at Cade (Administrative Council for Economic Defense)—but some details of the contracts offered by the Chinese giant remained unpublished. "The establishment commits itself not to celebrate, directly or indirectly, during the contract's validity, any type of commercial, contractual or institutional relationship with companies belonging to the economic group of Meituan/Keeta and/or Rappi," says an unsigned contract obtained by the reporting. "In return for the commercial restriction undertaken by the establishment in the clause above, 99Food commits to pay the amount of R$300,000 as an initial financial incentive as an investment in the establishment," the text proposes. The clause was optional.

When contacted through its press office, 99 stated that it has worked since the beginning of operations in Brazil offering different contract models so that establishments can choose the conditions that best suit their needs and business. "Our commercial agreements, by mutual agreement with restaurants, comply with applicable practices and rules in the delivery market," states a note sent by the company.

For its part, Keeta, also through its press office, argued that exclusivity clauses, especially those that prohibit establishments from working with specific new entrants, put free competition at risk. "The delivery market has long been distorted by exclusivity clauses imposed by competitors, which prevent restaurants from freely choosing their platforms and limit consumer options," states a note sent by the company. Also in a statement, Rappi said it reinforces its commitment to an open, competitive, and healthy delivery market. "The company does not adopt clauses that limit restaurants' operations on multiple platforms and repudiates practices of this nature, as it understands they harm free competition and sector development."

In August of last year, Keeta, which is China's largest delivery app and seeks to gain ground in the Brazilian market, asked the courts to ban the "semi-exclusivity" clauses offered by 99Food to restaurant partners. A decision by the Court of Justice of São Paulo in October granted the platform's request and overturned the clauses, but it was reversed on appeal. Keeta appealed and also sought out Cade, the antitrust authority linked to the Ministry of Justice.

Since 99Food's so-called banning clauses came to light and became the target of litigation, the Chinese platform claims to have removed mentions of specific companies from its contracts. According to market players interviewed by Folha, however, the company continues to propose that its restaurant partner sign contracts with only one other platform. In the assessment of these people, the result is the same, because no restaurant would be able to abandon the largest platform in the market, iFood, to close with Keeta or Rappi (in addition to 99Food).

In an interview with O Globo newspaper in October of last year, 99Food's communications director, Bruno Rossini, defended the practice. "We are protecting the space we conquered. If we don't do that, there will be a wave of companies entering the market to dispute the 20% that are not iFood's," he said. Rossini's statement motivated a new representation by Keeta to Cade, which at the end of March opened an inquiry to investigate whether 99Food's clauses represent anti-competitive practices.

The current dispute between Keeta and 99Food fits into a larger context of market competition in Brazil's delivery sector, which is still dominated by iFood. One of the nerve centers of this fight is precisely iFood's exclusivity contracts.
