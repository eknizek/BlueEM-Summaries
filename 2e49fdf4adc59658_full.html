# Second-round candidates' proposals under scrutiny: What they propose for Peruvian agriculture

The agricultural sector continues to concentrate some of the country's largest structural gaps, including the highest levels of poverty and informal workers. The proposals from Fuerza Popular (FP), Juntos por el Perú (JPP), and Renovación Popular (RP) identify real problems, but contain significant risks: goals without technical support, interventions that compromise free trade, and deliberate exclusions of modern agriculture.

## Diagnosis and 2025 Balance

The agricultural sector grew 5.5% in 2025, above total GDP (3.4%), driven almost entirely by agro-exports. These reached a historic record of US$12.6 billion, 13 times more than two decades ago, led by blueberries, grapes, avocados, and mangoes. Thus, production destined for external markets grew 15.5%, tripling its contribution to GDP growth compared to 2024 and providing employment to approximately 375,000 workers, on average, during the year. According to Adex, Peru has great potential in the food and beverage market, mainly due to the performance of agro-exports.

Meanwhile, production destined for the domestic market fell 0.9% in 2025, interrupting the sector's recovery in 2024. This decline is explained by climate vulnerabilities that reduced yields of crops such as potatoes, corn, and quinoa in the highlands, as well as floods in the north. This is not an isolated episode: in 2023, the El Niño Phenomenon (FEN) contracted domestic market production by 4.9% and agricultural GDP fell 3.3%. For 2026, the National FEN Study warns of conditions that could activate in the second half of the year, and the BCRP projects a moderation in agricultural growth to 2.5%.

A key aspect in sector development is irrigation infrastructure, but it lags behind. Between 1994 and 2024, according to INEI, Peru's agricultural frontier doubled, from 5.5 million to over 11 million hectares. However, in that same period, irrigated surface area increased only from 32% to 34%. Moreover, significant gaps persist between regions: while on the coast 84% of agricultural production hectares have irrigation, in the highlands and jungle it only reaches 16%. In this scenario, promoting large irrigation projects would allow adding up to 985,000 hectares under irrigation, four times the surface currently destined for agro-exports.

According to Midagri, 9 out of every 10 agricultural units are family farms. This explains why 95% of agricultural workers are informal as of 2024, compared to 72% of the national average. Furthermore, 39% of households whose head works in the sector lives in poverty, the highest rate among all sectors. This is especially concerning considering that agriculture employs 36% of Peruvian workers.

## Irrigation and Water

Facing the irrigation access problem, FP proposes promoting and unblocking major projects such as Chavimochic III and Majes II, with the goal of raising irrigated surface area to 60%. However, without a clear strategy that reverses the stagnation of the last three decades, this objective lacks technical support.

JPP proposes enabling 500,000 hectares under irrigation and allocating them to cooperatives and community enterprises, excluding modern agriculture from its plan. Furthermore, it proposes a water canon where those who earn more pay more for water use. This distorts the concept of canon and seeks to establish a water tariff that would not reflect its acquisition cost.

RP, meanwhile, does not propose any clear strategy for developing irrigation infrastructure.
