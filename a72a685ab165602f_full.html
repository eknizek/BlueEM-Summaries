# IPSA Closes with Sharp Decline, Approaching 11,000 Threshold as Strait of Hormuz Remains Blocked Despite Ceasefire Extension

The Santiago and New York stock exchanges took opposite directions this Wednesday. The United States will extend its ceasefire with Iran until closing a peace agreement, news that nonetheless failed to bring down oil prices, given that crossing the Strait of Hormuz remains a journey only for the brave.

The S&P IPSA plummeted 1.1% to 11,001.88 points in this fourth consecutive day of losses, dragged down largely by shares of Latam (-3.3%), Banco de Chile (-2.6%) and Cencosud (-1.7%).

Swap rates rose for the third consecutive day, as operators are once again taking seriously the idea that the Central Bank would raise the Monetary Policy Rate, perhaps as soon as June. Foreign agents have rushed to sign contracts where they buy variable rates, positions that would benefit from such a scenario.

Looking at the rest of Latin America, Brazil's stock exchange index posted an even deeper decline than the IPSA. Meanwhile in Europe, selective indices posted moderate declines. Different was the case on Wall Street, where the Nasdaq grew 1.6%, the S&P 500 increased 1.1% and the Dow Jones gained 0.7%.

The earnings season for U.S. companies has provided particular support to the world's main stock exchange. With this advance, both the Nasdaq and S&P 500 achieved new all-time highs.

## Strait Situation

Western markets in general had closed in red on Tuesday, amid nervousness over news that the U.S. delegation suspended its trip to Pakistan—seat of peace negotiations—due to lack of Iranian response to its proposals. This occurred just before what had been set as the deadline for Wednesday afternoon.

However, after the close of the U.S. market, Trump announced this indefinite extension of the ceasefire. According to the president, it was at the request of Shehbaz Sharif, the Pakistani prime minister, until Iranian leaders and representatives "can present a unified proposal," in a context of fragmentation of the Persian regime.

But energy prices did not yield. In fact, Brent oil was gaining 3% to surpass the $100 per barrel mark, seeing that there are still no signs of an imminent normalization of energy traffic from the Persian Gulf.

"The impact of the announcement was limited, and oil prices barely moved. The situation on the ground has not changed significantly since the start of the conflict, with the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed and approximately one-fifth of world oil off the global market," noted ActivTrades director Ricardo Evangelista.

According to Dilin Wu, studies strategist at Pepperstone, "markets now largely assume that both sides will continue to use negotiations as leverage, without an immediate resolution of long-standing structural issues, such as the nuclear program and disputes over strategic sea routes. These are deeply rooted disagreements that are unlikely to be resolved in the short term."

Already aware of Trump's announcement, news soon emerged that Tehran detained two ships in the Strait of Hormuz, escorting them to its territorial waters for "endangering maritime security." This followed reports from the UK maritime authority indicating that two vessels were attacked in the passage.

Given that much of the initial optimism over the bombing ceasefire has already been discounted in prices, according to Wu's explanation, "attention is shifting toward a more important variable: whether the high and prolonged price increase will ultimately force a supply and demand adjustment that begins to dominate price-setting."

Evangelista warned that "the longer this situation persists, the greater the impact of the supply crisis caused by the blockade, and the higher the price traders will be willing to pay to secure future crude deliveries. In this context, prices are likely to remain near current levels in the short term, with upside risks that will increase the longer the disruption continues."
