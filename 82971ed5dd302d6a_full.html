# Somos Internet Startup Challenges Telecom Giants with Disruptive Technology

With Millicom's consolidation in Colombia, the telecommunications market enters a new phase marked by concentration. Two major players will compete not only in the mobile segment but also in fixed internet for homes. Faced with this scenario, new players are seeking to compete and differentiate themselves with more competitive proposals in price, technology, and user experience.

With 81,432 active users in the fixed segment, Somos Internet is beginning to position itself as one of these emerging players. The startup, founded in 2018 in Medellín and recently backed with a $40 million investment, is advancing with a different proposal: proprietary technology, no permanence clauses, and a focus on improving service. This is explained by Forrest Heath, CEO of the company, who attributes accelerated growth to a simple premise: "offering something really better."

In dialogue with Portafolio, Heath asserted that the sector is entering a stage where being big is not enough; rather, responding better to user needs and raising service quality standards matters.

**How is the company doing in Colombia?**

We're at a very interesting moment. We started almost six years ago in popular neighborhoods in Medellín, without telecommunications experience and, in my case, without speaking Spanish. Today we are one of the fastest-growing internet companies. It's a somewhat crazy story, but it reflects how this project has been built.

**How quickly are you growing?**

We're practically tripling the size of the business year after year. We'll close this year being three times larger than the previous one, and the goal is to maintain that pace. If you look two years back and two years forward, the ambition is clear: we want to be one of the largest players in the country.

**How has adoption been in such a competitive market?**

There's something key—many people aren't happy with their current operator. They tolerate it, but they're not satisfied. The sector's problem is that historically it hasn't focused on offering better service but on keeping the customer just enough so they don't leave. We arrived with a different proposal: better service, better price, and a different experience.

**How do you see the sector's future with Tigo and Movistar's integration in Colombia?**

The integration could result in a market with less effective competition. And when that happens, the risk is that users end up with fewer options and lower quality. We see an opportunity there: to be the player that disturbs, that forces quality to rise, and that demonstrates you can compete with a different proposal, even in a near-duopoly scenario.

**What's failing in the traditional model?**

Today's market is dominated by technologies like HFC (Hybrid Fiber-Coaxial) and GPON (Gigabit-Capable Passive Optical Network), which are essentially evolutions of old systems. In many cases, several operators offer practically the same thing: same infrastructure, similar prices, and little differentiation. Additionally, with market consolidation, real competition is decreasing.

**Does that impact service quality?**

Yes. There's a financial incentive to add more users to the same network to improve profitability, but that deteriorates quality. In Colombia, for example, there are networks where up to 128 or even 256 users share the same capacity. In other developed markets, that number is much lower. More users on the same infrastructure means worse experience.

**Where's Somos' difference?**

We don't use the traditional model. We design and manufacture our own technology. That's unusual in telecommunications. The analogy we use is SpaceX versus Boeing: while some integrate third-party components, we control the entire value chain. That allows us to have better performance and lower costs.

**What does that mean in terms of service?**

Our network is based on an architecture similar to data centers, not traditional telecommunications networks. We deliver practically a dedicated connection to each client. That allows us to offer higher speeds and scale to much greater capacities in the future.

**What have been the main challenges?**

Building everything from scratch. Many decisions we made weren't the easiest in the short term. It would have been faster to buy existing technology, but we bet on building a solid long-term foundation. That meant more investment, more time, and many technical challenges.

**What's next for the company?**

We're at an inflection point. After years of investment, we now see results in growth. We want to expand to more cities in Colombia and we're already preparing entry into Mexico. But the focus remains this country; we're seeking to be in the top 3 best.

**You've also talked about eliminating permanence clauses. Why?**

Because we believe customers should stay because they want to, not because they're obligated. In Colombia, many companies have replaced clauses with high installation costs, which in practice end up being the same thing. We don't believe in that. If the customer isn't satisfied, they should be able to leave.

**How are you working with other ecosystem players, like ISPs?**

We're developing infrastructure that benefits not just us. With Génesis, our DWDM backbone network, we connect eight major cities in the country with high capacity and low latency. Over that network, through InterSomos, we offer dedicated optical spectrum.
