# Food inflation and caution regarding interest rates put consumer optimism in check

Consumer and construction confidence advance with Selic rate cuts, in contrast to the decline in indicators for commerce, services, and companies more affected by global instability.

The package of measures announced by the government to contain fuel and cooking gas prices due to international oil market costs – up 59% for the year, although slightly decelerating due to the provisional ceasefire agreed between the US and Iran – does not shield consumer sentiment. Positive, but exposed to the uneasiness created by the unstable external scenario affecting businesses.

Surveys by the Brazilian Institute of Economics at the Getulio Vargas Foundation (FGV Ibre) reveal that the Consumer Confidence Index rose 2 points in March and ended the quarter at 88.1 points. The Construction Confidence Index followed a similar trajectory – a gain of 2.1 points to 93.6 points. Despite the uncertainty stemming from the armed conflict, undermining expectations over six consecutive weeks, this pair of newly released indicators was positively influenced primarily by domestic variables: the beginning of the Selic reduction process by the Central Bank (BC), expectations that further cuts will follow, and that the labor market will remain strong with favorable income.

In contrast, the Commerce Confidence Index and the Services Confidence Index recorded sharp declines. Also in March, the first fell 2.7 points to 84.6 points and the second fell 1.8 points to 88.4 points. The Business Confidence Index fell 0.4 points to 91.9 points in the second consecutive decline, while the Economic Uncertainty Indicator, more sensitive to the external scenario as it considers the conflict's media impact, jumped 9.2 points to 115 points. Higher, therefore, than 110 points which already characterizes high uncertainty.

These negative data reflected unfavorable expectations for the near future driven by the Iran war in a two-week truce but in full development in March, global developments, uncertainties about the conflict's duration, and also about the impacts of the oil price increase, risks to the fertilizer chain and perception regarding food inflation increases, particularly food, pointed out Anna Carolina Gouveia, an FGV economist responsible for monitoring the economic uncertainty indicator, when presenting the data at the end of March.

Additionally, companies, mindful of Central Bank president Gabriel Galípolo's statements regarding the need for caution amid global instability, do not rule out the possibility that the Selic – currently at 14.75% – may fall less than expected by year's end, with the BC adopting a more vigilant stance regarding inflation expectations and the expansion of consumer debt. This, despite the government's willingness to adopt new measures to ease debtors' financial burden – a Herculean task that may, however, bring only temporary results and debt postponement, while also compromising the Worker's Time-in-Service Guarantee Fund (FGTS) as a kind of "insurance" for workers if withdrawals are approved for debt settlement.

Regarding indebtedness, the National Confederation of Commerce of Goods, Services and Tourism (CNC) published the Consumer Indebtedness and Default Survey for March. The survey reported an expansion in the percentage of families with outstanding debts to 80.4%, renewing the record of 80.2% registered in February. However, the entity identified positive stability in default rates at 29.6%. Still, CNC projections show that indebtedness is expected to continue advancing in the first half of this year until the effects of monetary policy easing, initiated on March 18, reach consumers. Regarding default, its pace will depend on possible inflationary impacts from items such as energy and fuel in family budgets.

## Dollar in the government's favor

Consumer sentiment positive at the end of the first quarter could, however, be dampened by a higher food bill, according to economist José Francisco Lima Gonçalves, professor at FEA/USP, master's and doctorate in Economics from Unicamp. "Food inflation should give a shock," he warns.

To NeoFeed, Lima Gonçalves explained that even with demand cooling due to restrictive monetary policy, food prices do not follow this logic. "The typical reaction of these prices is on the supply side. Discounting seasonal effects, there is climate, transportation costs, medium-term effects on production costs, commodity price shocks and exchange rate effects," he enumerates.

The professor believes that the only "remedy" the government could use to face the rise in food prices, which have substantial weight in the family budget, would be inventory management. And even then, for certain products. But he acknowledges that inventory policy "doesn't fall from the sky." On the contrary, it requires investments in logistics infrastructure, in addition to investment in building the inventory itself. "In short, this policy requires planning and financing of the food sector and the stocks formed. And managing all of this implies a non-negligible financial cost. And the initiative would still be subject to criticism, which still exist, regarding what is considered an interventionist State," he states.

Lima Gonçalves adds that strategic reserves are only accepted in the case of wealthy countries and cites oil in the Northern Hemisphere. "A combination that brings together some conditions. Rising commodity prices, including oil, freight and international insurance, with some compensation through exchange rates," observes the professor, for whom there is no way out for the government except to incorporate food into the planning of initiatives, "or every so often we'll have scarcity."

In the government's favor in the current scenario is the exchange rate, which acts as a mitigating factor for inflation risks and pressures. In recent days, the dollar fell below R$ 5.10 – the most favorable quote in two years. For the year through Thursday, April 9, the decline is 7.5%, which adds to the 11.2% drop seen in 2025.
