# Airlines raise prices and reduce routes amid fuel crisis: how is the situation in Latin American and Peruvian flights?

The commercial aviation sector faces a new reality: today, flying is more expensive. The surge in oil prices from the crisis unleashed worldwide due to the war in the Middle East is pressuring transportation costs, particularly for 'jet fuel', the fuel used by aircraft. With one-fifth of global petroleum trade strangled by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz—after nearly two months of intermittent closure and uncertain announcements of reopening that are not materializing—the International Air Transport Association (IATA) and the International Energy Agency (IEA) have raised their voices and warned of imminent risk in 'jet fuel' supply for airlines.

"Europe has about six weeks of aircraft fuel," warned Fatih Birol, executive director of the IEA, last week. Thus, he cautioned that flight cancellations could occur soon if petroleum supplies continue to be blocked by the war in the Middle East. IATA, meanwhile, said that the situation is concerning and that by the end of May, more cancellations in Europe could be seen due to lack of fuel, something that is already occurring in some parts of Asia.

But the consequences are already visible in Europe and the United States. German airline Lufthansa announced just days ago that it will cancel 20,000 short-haul flights over the coming months, as with high fuel prices these routes are not profitable. Several airlines, including KLM-France, Delta, and Air Canada, also temporarily canceled some flights, while others increased ticket prices, passing the cost to customers, which will be accentuated if the conflict extends longer.

In light of this panorama, what is happening in Latin America and Peru?

Latin American airlines are also feeling the impact on their costs and this is already reflected in ticket prices. Roberto Alvo, CEO of LATAM, told El Comercio that they are paying for 'jet fuel' double what they paid just over a month ago. "What has never been seen is such a rapid, such violent price increase," he stated the second week of April at IATA's Wing of Changes Americas event. He was also clear about the effect on fares, confirming that ticket prices have indeed risen.

Aircraft fuel requires different refining, which limits its availability. "The increase in 'jet fuel' cost has to be passed on to prices and, if the problem persists, we will also see impacts on supply in the coming months," adds Alvo. In other words, less profitable routes could be cut if the scenario continues, which is precisely what we are already seeing in other parts of the world.

"Before the conflict began, crude was trading at around US$70 per barrel. Today it exceeds US$110. In the case of aviation fuel, the jump was even greater: it went from US$90 to about US$220 per barrel," noted Peter Cerdá, vice president for the Americas at IATA, in early April.

In fact, according to IATA's 'jet fuel' Monitor, reviewed by Dia1, the average global price of this fuel was US$184.6 per barrel as of the close of the week of April 17. In Latin America, the average weekly price was US$179.6, an increase of 93.7% compared to last year. This undoubtedly represents a critical challenge for the industry.

"With net margins expected to be below 4% in 2025, airlines cannot absorb increases of this level," said Cerdá.
