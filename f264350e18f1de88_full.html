# Eternit Defies Slowdown, Announces USD 3.5 Million Investment and Doubles Capacity in Colombia

The multinational company Eternit Colombia opted for a countercyclical strategy: invest to grow. The company, which celebrates 84 years of operations in the country, announced an investment of approximately 3.5 million dollars aimed at modernizing its industrial plants in Barranquilla and Yumbo, thereby doubling its productive capacity and generating more than 20 direct jobs.

The announcement is particularly relevant at a moment when the sector faces one of its most marked contractions in recent years. However, the company is betting on a structural transformation of the market, driven by the demand for more sustainable construction solutions.

As a result of this investment, Eternit launched two new architectural roofing products manufactured with 100 percent recycled polypropylene. The first is Ecolit, designed for warm climates and produced in Barranquilla, while the second, Upp Super Crestya, is produced in Yumbo (Valle del Cauca) with three-layer technology. Both products were officially presented in mid-April in Barranquilla.

"Construction is changing, and our role as an industry is to get ahead of that transformation. These developments are not just new products; they are a different way of thinking about how we build in the country," affirmed Jorge Andrés Palacio, general manager of Eternit Colombia.

The initiative responds to a growing trend toward materials with lower carbon footprints, driven by both international regulations and projects seeking environmental certifications such as Leed or Edge, the executive noted.

According to the company, the new solutions incorporate significant improvements in technical performance and sustainability. The Upp Super Crestya roof reduces energy consumption in manufacturing by 80 to 90 percent compared to virgin resins, while also reducing 1 to 1.5 tons of CO₂ equivalent per ton produced. Meanwhile, Ecolit allows reducing interior building temperatures by up to 10 degrees compared to traditional metal roofing, while transmitting up to 15 times less noise.

One of the most relevant data points is the use of recycled materials. At full capacity, both plants will require approximately 290 tons monthly of recovered polypropylene, equivalent to removing about 180 million snack packages from the market per month. This indicator positions the initiative as a tangible case of circular economy applied to industry.

Beyond the new products, Eternit promotes the lightweight construction system as a key model for sector development in Colombia. This approach, based on modular panels with verified environmental certifications, allows construction up to four times faster, reduces construction waste by 50 percent, and generates savings of up to 40 percent in indirect costs.

The company supports this strategy with measurable sustainable advances including water recirculation systems that prevent the waste of 18.2 million liters annually, a solar park at its Muña plant in Bogotá that covers 10 percent of its energy consumption, and the acquisition of 100 percent renewable certified energy, which in 2024 represented savings of 539,206 kWh.

With this investment, the company indicates that Eternit not only expands its industrial capacity but redefines its market positioning, even during times of slowdown, with a commitment to innovation, sustainability, and long-term growth.
