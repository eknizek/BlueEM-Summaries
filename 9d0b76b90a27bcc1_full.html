# Foreign investors inject R$65 billion into stock market, hit record, while Brazilians stay on sidelines

The increase in foreign capital flows to the Brazilian stock market should extend throughout the rest of the year, amid a recovery in risk appetite in global markets, according to executives from major banks interviewed by Bloomberg News. Foreign investors injected nearly R$65 billion into the stock exchange through April 20, a figure that already exceeds the combined total of 2024 and 2025. The increase in external flows helped the Ibovespa index reach successive all-time highs, comfortably surpassing both the MSCI Emerging Markets index and the S&P 500.

"The trend points to a significant and continuous inflow of foreign capital into the Brazilian stock market," said Guilherme Silveira, head of equities at Banco Santander's unit in the country. "Emerging markets remain on investors' radar, and Brazil stands out as one of the primary beneficiaries of these flows."

The first quarter marked the strongest start to a year for foreign capital flows since 2022, with investors diversifying their investments, moving away from American assets and seeking emerging markets. Brazil's high liquidity, expectations of falling interest rates, and hopes for market-friendly policies following the October presidential election — as well as distance from Middle East conflict and its position as a petroleum exporter — made the country one of the primary options.

Meanwhile, local investors remained on the sidelines. Hurt by double-digit interest rates that fueled persistent outflows to fixed-income products, domestic institutional and retail investors reduced equity allocations to historic lows, according to data compiled by B3. The participation of foreign investors in the Brazilian stock market rose to a record 62%, the data shows.

The Central Bank's monetary easing cycle may begin to push local investors back into the stock market. The basic Selic rate was reduced by 0.25 percentage points last month.

"History shows us that when interest rates begin to fall, flows reverse," said Ben Laidler, head of equity strategy at Bradesco BBI. "This happens because local investors need to catch up at some point, or because rates fall enough that money stops flowing into fixed income."

Still, market volatility persists while there is no resolution to the Middle East war. The oil shock led many investors to reduce their bets on a broader monetary easing cycle, and they now forecast that the Central Bank will reduce the Selic rate by approximately 225 basis points in total, compared to the 300 basis points expected at the end of February.

Brazilian investors will also have to contend with the upcoming presidential elections. Flávio Bolsonaro has taken a slight lead against President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva in the race for the October elections, but with expectations of a closely contested race until the last minute, markets are not making bets on the elections at this time, said Marcelo Okura, co-head of global markets for Latin America at UBS Group.

For now, foreign investors will continue injecting money into Brazilian stocks, he said, adding that markets could rise even further if there are signs that Middle East tensions may come to an end. "Brazil can stand out as an investment alternative for being geographically distant from the war, being a commodity exporter with a liquid market and relatively stable institutions," said Okura. "The only caveat would be if we entered a very deep global crisis and had a widespread flight to extremely low-risk assets."
