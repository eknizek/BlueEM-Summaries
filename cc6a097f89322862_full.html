# Peru's currency strengthens to S/3.44 per dollar; equities of mining and financial firms fall over 5% amid second-round election uncertainty

The advancement of Roberto Sánchez, from Juntos por el Perú, as a possible contender against Keiko Fujimori in the second round of the presidential elections—according to the official count by ONPE—has generated nervousness in the market. This has been reflected in a rise in the exchange rate and a fall in the shares of local companies, according to experts consulted by El Comercio.

At the close of today, the exchange rate ended the trading day at S/3.443 per dollar in the Peruvian market, an increase of 1.5% compared to yesterday's price. The currency opened the day at S/3.44 and reached a maximum of S/3.4480 during the session, detailed Jimena Torres, manager of Currency Trading at Renta4 SAB. "Given the updates made by ONPE in recent hours, the exchange rate opened 650 basis points (bps) higher, if we compare it with yesterday's opening price. The trend during the day remained upward, rising a total of 230 bps during the session," she notes.

The influence of the electoral situation

According to Luis Eduardo Falen, professor of Economics at the University of the Pacific, the behavior of the exchange rate in recent days responds primarily to local political factors, since at the international level the context pointed to a weaker dollar. In fact, currency swap placements by the Central Bank were greater than those of the previous day and exceeded S/2 billion. In fact, today there were currency swap expirations for sales of S/102 million, which were renewed in their entirety. Additionally, the entity placed S/1.8 billion at three- and six-month terms.

"This happens because investors and all portfolio managers find coincidences between the process we are experiencing now and the one five years ago," Falen points out. It should be remembered that in the previous elections, Pedro Castillo's victory brought the exchange rate to S/4.15 per dollar.

In that vein, Juan Acosta, international business professor at UPC, considers that the current scenario bears similarities to that of 2021, especially regarding the ideological and economic positions of the candidates currently leading the official count. "This translates into a high scenario of uncertainty that motivates investors to take safe positions, which in this case is the dollar. That is why we see how this currency is taking a clear upward trend," Acosta explains.

The professor also highlights the composition of Parliament, which could exert strong opposition against any eventual government. "In some way it will give a certain stability if the winner wishes to implement radical measures both politically and economically," he mentions.

Uncertainty reaches corporate shares

Meanwhile, Jorge Ramos, director of Capital Markets and Investor Relations at Fibra Prime, observes nervousness in the market as a typical reaction to the lack of political clarity. However, he notes that panic has not yet been reached. "In the short term, this can translate into more currency volatility, caution in local assets, and some more pressure on prices linked to imports or payments in foreign currency. But, for now, it does not seem to be a signal of structural deterioration of the Peruvian economy, but rather a temporary premium for electoral uncertainty."

The political factor reached the shares of mining and financial companies: Volcán fell 4.48% and Nexa Atacocha 7.94%, while BBVA fell 5.61%. The Peruvian ETF EPU fell 4.85% at the close of trading.
