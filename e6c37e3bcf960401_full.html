# Rappi Issues Stern Warning to Delivery Workers Following Protests: "Safety on the Platform is Non-Negotiable"

The protests by delivery workers registered this Monday, April 20, in northern Bogotá are occurring amid the impact of the recent sanction by the Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) against the Rappi delivery application, which exceeds $4 billion. The decision, motivated by service failures, unauthorized charges, and problems with user service, has reignited tensions within the platform's ecosystem.

In this context, delivery workers denounced alleged "arbitrary account blockages," which, according to them, directly affects their income and working conditions. The demonstrations concentrated on strategic points in the city, making visible a discontent that goes beyond the sanction and points to the relationship between the company and those who operate as delivery workers.

## Rappi's Response

In response to the situation, Rappi reiterated its institutional position, indicating that it receives the authorities' decisions "with respect" and that it maintains willingness to work jointly on improving service. The company also defended the scale of its operations in the country, stating that it "operates millions of orders daily," the vast majority of which are completed satisfactorily. However, it acknowledged that there are aspects to improve: "every case counts and we must continuously improve our users' experience," the company stated in its statement.

In parallel, the company confirmed that it will file legal appeals against the sanction and will continue investing in technology and strengthening its service channels, with the objective of reducing response times and resolving service failures.

## Structural Tensions in the Model

The background of the conflict reveals deeper tensions regarding the business model of digital platforms. The SIC has maintained that Rappi does not act solely as an intermediary, but as a provider, as it has control over payments, deliveries, and customer service, which implies greater responsibilities toward users and partners.

In this scenario, delivery workers' reports of blockages and lack of guarantees add to regulatory questioning, broadening the debate over working conditions in the platform economy. While the protests evidence discontent at the operational base, the company's response aims to defend its functioning and correct failures without altering the core of its model. The outcome of this conflict—between regulation, operation, and working conditions—could set the course for digital platforms in Colombia.
