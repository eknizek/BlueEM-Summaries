# Reserve requirement flexibility seen as opportunity to lower rates, but credit recovery doubts persist

The flexibility in reserve requirements for banks, in effect since Friday, could help lower lending rates. However, financial analysts remain cautious about talking of a credit reactivation, affected by growing default rates among families and businesses and the loss of purchasing power of wages.

To unlock a $1 billion disbursement from the International Monetary Fund, the Government committed to the organization to work on a series of guidelines. The Central Bank's monetary policy was one of them: system liquidity was affected in the second half of last year, when the economic team aimed to "dry out pesos" from the market to prevent greater volatility in the exchange rate.

On one hand, the BCRA reduced for banks the requirement for minimum daily cash integration (from the monthly average) from 75% to 65%. Additionally, it allowed banks to integrate new public securities into their reserves, so entities can now add very short-term bonds, such as LECAPs, or longer-duration bonds, such as CER bonds. At the same time, though in an "unofficial" manner, it reactivated active repos, an instrument through which the organization provides liquidity to banks.

"The reduction is positive because it limits intra-month volatility in short-term rates. With a lower daily floor, banks gain room to manage their liquidity position without being forced to compete aggressively for funding in the busiest trading rounds," explained analysts at Delphos.

"A priori, both measures go in the same direction that the economic team has pointed to since late February: ease monetary conditions, reduce rates and above all mitigate their volatility," explained Pedro Siaba Serrate of PPI, and detailed: "The objective is to reduce the difference between active and passive rates, or reduce the intermediation margin. Ultimately it will depend on the impact on supply and demand in that market, but we are inclined to think that active rates are the ones that could achieve greater compression and improve the credit outlook."

Until now, while the Central Bank pushed for a rate cut that was felt in passive rates—those that banks pay their customers for, for example, placing a fixed-term deposit—there has yet to be an impact on active rates—those that entities charge for granting a loan.

"For several weeks now, the Government has managed to keep the one-day reference rate of the system around the 20% floor set by the BCRA in the A3 REPO round, but the truth is that, at least for now, that has not reduced personal loan rates (which remain very high in real terms for various reasons, including precisely the high default rate, but also how difficult it is to predict the Government's rate policy in the current monetary scheme)," explained analysts at consultancy 1816.

The expectation then is that this gap now narrows and makes it more convenient for families and businesses to seek bank financing. "It's a good measure because it helps generate conditions for banks' active rates to fall, particularly personal loans, which remain around 70%," noted Claudio Caprarulo of Analytca.

The uptick in credit appears to be a fundamental piece of economic reactivation. While the financial sector analyzes the impact of these measures and sees a rate cut in the short and medium term, analysts are more cautious. "The jump in default rates and perspectives that don't change in many sectors mark a limit to credit expansion beyond rate dynamics," Caprarulo pointed out.

In this sense, Gabriel Caamaño Gomez of Outlier added: "Much more progress needs to be made in this direction and the rise in default rates is not something that will be resolved so quickly with real incomes in decline, but it's in the right direction. It's a good signal that needs to be deepened and complemented with income recovery."

For his part, Martín Polo of Cohen predicted a rate cut and a slight credit rebound: "Credit has been falling, but it's true that the decline is increasingly smaller, so in that sense the measure helps push it a bit more," he said.
