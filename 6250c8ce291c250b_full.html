# Colombia's Electric Vehicle Sector Expects to Close 2026 with Over 40,000 New Units: BYD

The electric vehicle sector continues gaining ground in the country. In the context of rising fuel prices in recent years and mobility restrictions such as odd-even license plate schemes, people increasingly see electric transportation as a viable option.

According to Fenalco data, in March 2026, electric vehicle registrations grew 267%, reaching 5,083 units registered compared to the same month in 2025, representing 18.1% of total new vehicles registered in March.

Juan Pablo Molano, commercial manager of BYD in Colombia (a Chinese multinational that manufactures electric and hybrid vehicles), explained in an interview with Portafolio that the company's goal is to reduce Earth's temperature by at least one degree. However, he also insisted that the country needs to advance in infrastructure and regulation so that the transition to the new energy sector is more orderly and rapid.

## What is the role of electric vehicles in the energy transition process?

From BYD, we have a very ambitious mission: helping to reduce Earth's temperature by one degree Celsius. That may sound quite ambitious, and that is the big dream: achieving a one-degree reduction in temperature. If you think about whether one degree is much or little, it might seem small, but one degree is the difference between chaos and a sustainable future. Really, one degree makes the difference.

With electric mobility, we are helping this transition. The key here is how quickly we are able to transition. Ten years ago in Colombia, the number of 100% electric vehicles sold was practically zero. Last year we managed to reach 19,500 electric cars. It took us a few years to reach that figure, which already represents around 8% market share among all vehicles. And based on our projections, it is very likely that this year we will be close to 40,000 electric vehicles, which is almost double what was sold last year. The truth is that the transition is happening very quickly, and we hope that within a couple of years we can be talking about figures close to 40% or 50% transformation of the automotive industry.

## Is people in Colombia becoming more interested in this sector each day?

This year the figure could be close to 13% market share of electric vehicles compared to the total. Now, what happens in Bogotá does not necessarily reflect the reality of the entire country. In Bogotá, electrification has been very accelerated, particularly due to issues like vehicle restrictions and odd-even license plate schemes. In Bogotá, a person who owns a combustion car can use it around 60% of the time, because the other 40% has mobility restrictions. This means that a person who acquires a combustion car really has to leave it parked much of the time. That restriction in the electric and hybrid vehicle industry has helped us a lot to accelerate the transition. Unfortunately, that is not the same reality throughout the country. There are many cities in Colombia that do not have vehicle restrictions, and without them, there is no incentive to change a combustion vehicle for an electric one.

Now there is a new reality: the price of gasoline, which has increased significantly in recent years. That compensates a bit for that benefit of odd-even restrictions to consider switching to an electric vehicle. When people spend so much of their own money to fill up their combustion vehicle, they start thinking about what else they can do to lower costs and optimize resources. That is where electric mobility comes in very well: helping people save money. So it is not only about the environmental aspect, which is fundamental for us, but also the economic aspect. Each day there is more access to green credit, more access to charging stations in homes. That is, technology has been made easier to achieve that transition.

## How is Colombia doing in terms of regulation of the electric fleet sector?

I think important efforts have been made and Colombia has advanced enormously in the electrification of its fleet. However, there is much work to do and much to coordinate. One case where we could help greatly: new housing construction, new buildings being built, should already have charger facilities in the garage. Today that is not an obligation. So we are still at the mercy of builders wanting to put those kinds of facilities.

## Have you already proposed having those dialogues?

Of course. We are working on that and the national government and local governments have been very interested in accelerating that transition. Particularly in the case of Bogotá, the fleet is already above 1,800 electric buses. All this confidence that generates helps the market understand that electric technology is a reality that works and is already within reach for everyone to think more about having an electric vehicle.

## How are the company's numbers this year? How are they projected?

Last year was a very good year for the automotive industry. We closed near 250,000 vehicles. If we compare it to the last ten years, it was one of the three best years of the automotive industry in Colombia. But the point here is what happened with the electric industry. It was the highest year of sales of 100% electric vehicles. Around 19,500 electric vehicles were sold among all brands. Of those 19,500, BYD obtained 54% of the market, that is, we were very close to 11,000 units.
