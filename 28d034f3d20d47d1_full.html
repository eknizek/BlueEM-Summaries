Food industry alerts about 'lack of scientific rigor' in new labeling project

25 April 2026 · 08:08 PM

# Food Industry Alerts About "Lack of Scientific Rigor" in New Labeling Project

The Chamber of the Food Industry of the Andi launched a strong warning about the risks implied by the new front-of-package labeling resolution project promoted by the Ministry of Health, noting that the initiative could generate significant economic impacts, legal uncertainty, and regulatory decisions without sufficient technical support.

According to the industry group, the proposal seeks to repeal Resolution 810 of 2021 to implement a new labeling scheme, which would force a complete redesign of food packaging throughout the country. This measure would affect a sector composed of 51,200 companies, of which 98.5 percent are micro and medium-sized, with potential impact on employment and national supply.

One of the main criticisms points to the lack of scientific evidence supporting the proposed changes. "This new technical regulation is not properly justified; it is done without the scientific evidence that must support such a profound change," stated Camilo Montes, executive director of the Chamber of the Food Industry of the Andi.

The industry group also questions the absence of prior dialogue. According to the document, the Ministry would not have responded to requests to establish technical tables that would allow analysis of the measure's effects, which limits participation of key actors in regulatory construction.

## Technical Changes

The project introduces modifications with high impact, according to the Andi. Among these are the creation of an "ultra-processed" category that, according to the business group, does not exist in international regulatory frameworks, as well as the obligation to include a specific warning for products containing certain industrial additives.

The proposal also introduces changes such as the implementation of "micro-seals" for small packages; prohibition of using QR codes in labeling; new restrictions on health claims; modifications in the presentation of nutritional tables; and a transition period of only six months.

For the industry group, these measures are "disproportionate" compared to the magnitude of required adjustments, especially considering the times needed for redesign, printing, and regulatory approval of labels.

## Risks for Trade

Another critical point is the possible conflict with international standards. The Andi warns that the project could constitute a technical obstacle to trade, by not fully complying with the requirements of organizations such as the World Trade Organization (WTO) or the Andean Community.

Additionally, the industry group notes that the regulatory change is proposed without an ex-post evaluation of the current labeling model, implemented less than three years ago and recently adjusted. This, according to the entity, contradicts the good regulatory practices adopted by Colombia within the framework of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD).

"It is fundamental that the Government listen to those who produce food in Colombia; since the real impact of this measure on companies has not been measured," added Montes, warning that additional costs could be passed on to the production and distribution chain.

Faced with this situation, the Chamber of the Food Industry made a call to business people, academics, scientists, and other sector actors to review the draft resolution and submit comments before the close of the public consultation process. With this initiative, the debate on nutritional labeling returns to the center of public discussion.
