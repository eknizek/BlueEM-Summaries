# Perfect Storm Threatens Maritime Logistics and May Further Strain Santos Port

Companies operating maritime transport logistics are calculating what could be a perfect storm in the sector. The combination of winter's arrival, the prolongation of the war in Iran, fuel prices, and tax reform could create market pressure and further overload Santos port, the country's largest.

One aspect little debated, according to businesspeople heard by the column, is how tax reform will discourage unloading at smaller ports. The sector is still trying to measure the increase in cargo this will cause in Santos, a complex that, according to them, is increasingly saturated and with access difficulties.

The view is that the change in tides during winter, which begins in June, and logistical difficulties typically make the process of ship docking and departure slower and more time-consuming. Most national ports do not support equipment that facilitates movement in stronger tides and fog.

The effects of the war in Iran can be felt in increased costs even for operators who depend less on routes passing through the Strait of Hormuz. This is reflected in the price of ship fuel, a heavy diesel that is highly polluting, which is 20% more expensive because of the conflict. There is also travel time due to route changes.

However, a problem already being felt that could worsen is the quantity of available containers. Ships carry the cargo units to their destination, but these need to return to be used again in Brazil, in a cycle of comings and goings. Currently, because of logistical problems, TEUs (20-foot container units) are departing more than they are returning.
