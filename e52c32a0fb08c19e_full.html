Decrees, Auctions, and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Missing for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

# Decrees, Auctions, and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Missing for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

The Brazilian government is rushing to hold the first auction for low-carbon hydrogen in the final months of this year, given current market uncertainties. At stake are R$18.3 billion in federal subsidies that will be injected by the government through these auctions over four years. The idea is to jumpstart the development of this industry, which still doesn't exist in Brazil, and foster domestic production of the input, designed to replace hydrogen based on fossils — it can be used in fertilizers, as raw material for synthetic fuel, and also used in cargo, maritime, and aviation transport.

After Congress approved the legal framework for green hydrogen in 2024, the ball is now in the Executive's court, which has started a race to regulate the contracting of this demand and hold the first auction still in 2026. Before that, the Ministries of Finance and Mines and Energy expect to publish in the coming weeks the first decrees that will dictate the general rules on the design of the auctions. It is still being discussed, for example, whether the auctions will be separated by products (ammonia, green fertilizer, green hydrogen, or methanol) or by regions. Last week, Finance Ministry authorities were even in Vienna, Austria, at an event on energy and hydrogen to seek international partnerships in formatting the auctions. The World Bank, for example, should also provide support to the auctions.

"The Brazilian government is closing a partnership with Unido, the UN arm for industrial development. They are supporting us in formatting the auction," says Rafael Dubeux, the special secretary of the Finance Ministry, to NeoFeed.

The policy to encourage domestic green hydrogen production is part of a broader environmental agenda of the government, which had been stimulated by former Finance Minister Fernando Haddad and also includes the carbon market and the offshore wind energy framework. The law, which came into force just over a year ago in Brazil, also brings tax incentives for low-carbon hydrogen production, such as PIS and Cofins zero for five years (as of January of last year) for the purchase of raw materials, intermediate products, packaging, inventory, and construction materials made by enabled low-emission hydrogen producers. It also created a certification system for this hydrogen.

## Market Vision

For Fernanda Delgado, president of the Brazilian Association of the Green Hydrogen Industry (ABIHV), Brazil will still face an initial challenge to develop this industry, which is complex, similar to the structure of a refinery, requires high added value, and will demand many adjustments in the first years and even probable "mistakes." As for the auctions, she considers that the expectation is that one will come out still at the end of this year, small and more "niche," possibly focused on just one product, to gauge the market's appetite to serve promptly. And that it is most likely that the first auction will include green fertilizer, but only one company is currently operating in that area, she assesses.

"It's a tumultuous year for everyone, with wars and our own fiscal issues, but our expectation is that the decrees will come out in the first half," says Delgado. "But today there is no primacy for the domestic market, because there is no appetite for buying hydrogen in the internal market, especially because there are no greenhouse gas emission targets yet. And no project has left the ground yet," she adds.

She explains that there is no domestic production of low-carbon hydrogen in the country, as regulatory framework is still lacking for the industry. And the National Petroleum Agency (ANP), which will oversee oversight, is still working toward that end. For now, there are only experimental projects, funded by companies or universities. Such is the case of an initiative by Neoenergia, an electricity distributor, in Brasília, which has been testing green hydrogen in buses in the city's public transport. And a green hydrogen industrial plant by Tramontina in Rio Grande do Sul has already been announced. There are also advanced projects for ammonia and methanol production for maritime fuel. But those at commercial scale have not yet left the ground, waiting for the time being for government decrees and auctions, she emphasizes: "These projects await a regulatory signal from the government to finally enable investments."

A source from the electricity sector monitoring discussions with the government told NeoFeed that the Brazilian market understands that industry development will bring a paradigm shift in the future. But in the short term there are "uncertainties" and the private sector prefers to wait before it becomes reality. "There is no clarity on how it will evolve yet."
