Decrees, Auctions, and R$ 18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Missing for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

13 April 2026 · 10:19 PM

# Decrees, Auctions, and R$ 18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Missing for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

Brasília — In a shorter year due to the electoral calendar and with a global war scenario, the government is rushing to launch the country's first low-carbon hydrogen auction. According to sources heard by NeoFeed, the expectation is that the auction will take place in the final months of this year due to current market uncertainties. At stake are R$ 18.3 billion in federal subsidies that the government will inject through these auctions over four years. The idea is to give an initial boost to the development of this industry, still non-existent in Brazil, and to promote national production of the input, which is meant to replace hydrogen based on fossil fuels — it can be used in fertilizers, as a raw material for synthetic fuel, and also used in cargo transportation, maritime and aviation.

On the other hand, the private sector believes that initially the focus should be on exports to the external market, especially the European Union and Asia, where the greatest potential demands are located, while the national industry develops and takes its first steps.

After Congress approved the green hydrogen legal framework in 2024, the ball is now in the Executive's court, which has begun a race to regulate the contracting of this demand and hold the first auction still in 2026. Before that, the Ministries of Finance and Mines and Energy expect to publish in the coming weeks the first decrees that will dictate general rules about the auction design, according to NeoFeed's investigation. What is still being discussed, for example, is whether the auctions will be separated by products (ammonia, green fertilizer, green hydrogen, or methanol) or by regions.

Last week, authorities from the Finance Ministry were even in Vienna, Austria, at an energy and hydrogen event, to seek international partnerships in formatting the auctions. The World Bank, for example, should also provide support to the auctions.

"The Brazilian government is closing a partnership with Unido, the UN's industrial development arm. They are supporting us in auction formatting," says Rafael Dubeux, Special Secretary of the Finance Ministry, to NeoFeed.

The policy to encourage national production of green hydrogen is part of a broader environmental agenda from the government, which had been stimulated by former minister Fernando Haddad (Finance) and also includes the carbon market and the offshore wind energy framework. The law, which came into force just over a year ago in Brazil, also brings tax incentives for low-carbon hydrogen production, such as zero PIS and Cofins for five years (starting last January) for the purchase of raw materials, intermediate products, packaging, inventories, and construction materials made by enabled low-emission hydrogen producers. It also created a certification system for this hydrogen.

## Market Vision

For Fernanda Delgado, president of the Brazilian Association of Green Hydrogen Industry (ABIHV), Brazil will still face an initial challenge in developing this industry, which is complex, similar to a refinery structure, requires high added value, and will demand many adjustments in the first years and even probable "errors."

Regarding the auctions, she notes that the expectation is for a first, small, and more "niche" auction to come out still at the end of this year, possibly aimed at only one product, to measure market appetite to respond promptly. And that it is most likely that the first auction will include green fertilizer, but only one company is currently operating in that area, she evaluates.

"It's a tumultuous year for everyone, with wars and our own fiscal issues, but our expectation is that the decrees will come out in the first semester," says Delgado. "But today there is no priority for the national market, because there is no appetite for buying hydrogen in the domestic market, precisely because there are no greenhouse gas emission targets yet. And no project has left the drawing board yet," she adds.

She explains that there is no national production of low-carbon hydrogen in the country because regulatory rules for the industry are still lacking. And the National Petroleum Agency (ANP), which will handle oversight, is still working on this. For now, there are only experimental projects, funded by companies or universities. Such is the case with a Neoenergia initiative, an electricity distributor, in Brasília, which has been testing green hydrogen in the city's public transportation buses. And a Tramontina green hydrogen industrial plant has already been announced in Rio Grande do Sul. There are also advanced projects for producing ammonia and methanol for maritime fuel. But commercial-scale ones have not yet left the drawing board, waiting for now for government decrees and auctions, she emphasizes: "These projects await a regulatory signal from the government to finally make investments viable."

A source from the electricity sector that follows discussions with the government told NeoFeed that the Brazilian market understands that industry development will bring a paradigm shift in the future. But in the short term there are "uncertainties" and the private sector prefers to wait before it becomes reality. "There is no clarity yet on how it will evolve."
