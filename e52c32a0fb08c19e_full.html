Decrees, Auctions, and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Needed for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

# Decrees, Auctions, and R$ 18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Needed for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

Brasília - In a shorter year due to the electoral calendar and amid a global war scenario, the Brazilian government is rushing to launch the country's first low-carbon hydrogen auction. According to sources heard by NeoFeed, the event is expected to take place in the final months of this year due to current market uncertainties. At stake are R$ 18.3 billion in federal subsidies that the government will inject through these auctions over four years. The idea is to kick-start the development of this industry, which still doesn't exist in Brazil, and to foster domestic production of the input, which is designed to replace hydrogen based on fossil fuels — it can be used in fertilizers, as raw material for synthetic fuel, and also in cargo transport, maritime, and aviation.

On the other hand, the private sector believes that initially, the greater focus should be on exports to the external market, especially the European Union and Asia, where the greatest potential demands are located, while the domestic industry develops and takes its first steps.

After Congress approved the legal framework for green hydrogen in 2024, the ball is now in the Executive's court, which has begun a race to regulate this demand and hold the first auction still in 2026. Before that, the Ministries of Finance and Mines and Energy expect to publish in the coming weeks the first decrees that will set the general rules for the auction design, according to NeoFeed's findings. It is still being discussed, for example, whether the auctions will be separated by products (ammonia, green fertilizer, green hydrogen, or methanol) or by regions.

Last week, authorities from the Finance Ministry were even in Vienna, Austria, at an event on energy and hydrogen, to seek international partnerships in formatting the auctions. The World Bank, for example, should also provide support to the auctions. "The Brazilian government is closing a partnership with Unido, the UN's industrial development arm. They are supporting us in formatting the auction," says Rafael Dubeux, the special secretary of the Finance Ministry, to NeoFeed.

The policy to encourage domestic green hydrogen production is part of a broader environmental agenda by the government, which had been stimulated by former Finance Minister Fernando Haddad and also includes the carbon market and the offshore wind energy framework. The law, which entered into force just over a year ago in Brazil, also brings tax incentives for low-carbon hydrogen production, such as zero PIS and Cofins for five years (starting in January last year) for the purchase of raw materials, intermediate products, packaging, inventory, and construction materials made by certified low-emission hydrogen producers. It also created a certification system for this hydrogen.

## Market Vision

For Fernanda Delgado, president of the Brazilian Association of the Green Hydrogen Industry (ABIHV), Brazil will still face an initial challenge in developing this industry, which is complex, similar to a refinery structure, requires high added value, and will demand many adjustments in the first years and even probable "mistakes."

Regarding the auctions, she notes that the expectation is that a first small and more "niche" auction will be held by year-end, possibly aimed at only one product, to gauge market appetite to respond promptly. And that the most likely scenario is that the first auction will include green fertilizer, but there is only one company operating in this area today.

"It's a tumultuous year for everyone, with wars and our own fiscal issues, but our expectation is that the decrees come out in the first semester," Delgado says. "But today there is no priority for the domestic market, because there is no appetite for buying hydrogen in the internal market, especially because there are no greenhouse gas emission targets yet. And no project has gotten off the ground yet," she adds.

She explains that there is no domestic production of low-carbon hydrogen in the country because there is still a lack of regulatory framework for the industry. And the National Petroleum Agency (ANP), which will oversee supervision, is still working on this. And for now there are only experimental projects, backed by companies or universities. Such is the case of a Neoenergia initiative, an electricity distributor, in Brasília, which has been testing green hydrogen in public transport buses in the city. And a green hydrogen industrial plant by Tramontina in Rio Grande do Sul has already been announced. There are also advanced projects for ammonia and methanol production for maritime fuel. But those at commercial scale have yet to get off the ground, awaiting for now the government's decrees and auctions, she emphasizes: "These projects await a regulatory signal from the government to finally make investments viable."

A source from the electrical sector that follows discussions with the government told NeoFeed that the Brazilian market understands that industry development will bring a paradigm shift in the future. But in the short term there are "uncertainties" and the private sector prefers to wait before it becomes reality. "There is no clarity on how it will evolve yet."
