Decrees, Auctions, and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Missing for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

13 April 2026 · 10:19 PM

# Decrees, Auctions, and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Missing for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

The Brazilian government is rushing to hold the country's first low-carbon hydrogen auction in the final months of this year, amid a shorter calendar due to electoral considerations and an uncertain global scenario marked by conflict. At stake are R$18.3 billion in federal subsidies that the government will inject through these auctions over four years. The goal is to jumpstart the development of this industry, which still doesn't exist in Brazil, and encourage domestic production of the input, designed to replace fossil-based hydrogen. It can be used in fertilizers, as raw material for synthetic fuel, and in cargo transport, maritime shipping, and aviation.

However, the private sector believes that initially, the focus should be on exports to external markets, particularly the European Union and Asia, where the greatest potential demand exists, while the domestic industry develops and takes its first steps.

After Congress approved the legal framework for green hydrogen in 2024, the ball is now in the Executive's court, which has begun a race to regulate this contracting and hold the first auction still in 2026. Before that, the Ministries of Finance and Mines and Energy expect to publish the first decrees in the coming weeks that will establish the general rules for the auction design, according to NeoFeed's reporting. Still under discussion, for example, is whether the auctions will be separated by products (ammonia, green fertilizer, green hydrogen, or methanol) or by regions.

Last week, Finance Ministry authorities were even in Vienna, Austria, at an event on energy and hydrogen, to seek international partnerships in structuring the auctions. The World Bank, for example, should also provide support to the auctions. "The Brazilian government is closing a partnership with Unido, the UN's industrial development arm. They are supporting us in the auction format," says Rafael Dubeux, Special Secretary of the Finance Ministry, to NeoFeed.

The policy to encourage domestic green hydrogen production is part of a broader environmental agenda by the government, which was being stimulated by former Finance Minister Fernando Haddad and also includes the carbon market and the offshore wind energy framework. The law, which took effect just over a year ago in Brazil, also brings tax incentives for low-carbon hydrogen production, such as zero PIS and Cofins for five years (starting last January) for the purchase of raw materials, intermediate products, packaging, inventory, and construction materials made by enabled low-emission hydrogen producers. It also created a certification system for this hydrogen.

## Market Vision

For Fernanda Delgado, president of the Brazilian Green Hydrogen Industry Association (ABIHV), Brazil will still face initial challenges in developing this complex industry, similar in structure to a refinery, which requires high added value and will demand many adjustments in the coming years and likely "errors."

Regarding the auctions, she considers that the expectation is that a first, small, and more "niche" auction will be held by year's end, possibly focused on just one product, to measure market appetite and respond promptly. And that the most likely outcome is that the first auction will include green fertilizer, but only one company is currently operating in this area, she assesses.

"It's a tumultuous year for everyone, with wars and our own fiscal issues, but our expectation is that the decrees will come out in the first half of the year," says Delgado. "But today there's no priority for the domestic market, because there's no appetite for purchasing hydrogen in the domestic market, mainly because there are no greenhouse gas emission targets yet. And no project has gotten off the ground yet," she adds.

She explains that there is no domestic production of low-carbon hydrogen in the country because there is still a lack of regulatory framework for the industry. And the National Petroleum Agency (ANP), which will oversee oversight, is still working on this. And for now there are only experimental projects, funded by companies or universities. Such as an initiative by Neoenergia, an electricity distributor, in Brasília, which has been testing green hydrogen in the city's public transportation buses. And a green hydrogen industrial plant by Tramontina in Rio Grande do Sul has already been announced. There are also advanced projects for ammonia and methanol production for marine fuel. But commercial-scale ones still haven't gotten off the ground, awaiting for now the government's decrees and auctions, she emphasizes: "These projects await a regulatory signal from the government to finally make investments viable."

A source from the electricity sector monitoring discussions with the government told NeoFeed that the Brazilian market understands that industry development will bring a paradigm shift in the future. But in the short term there are "uncertainties" and the private sector prefers to wait before it becomes reality. "There's no clarity yet on how it will evolve."
