Decrees, Auctions and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Needed for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

13 April 2026 · 10:19 PM

# Decrees, Auctions and R$18.3 Billion in Incentives: What's Needed for Green Hydrogen to Take Off?

In a shorter year due to the electoral calendar and with a global war scenario, the Brazilian government is racing to launch the country's first low-carbon hydrogen auction. According to sources heard by NeoFeed, the event is expected to take place in the final months of this year due to current market uncertainties. At stake are R$18.3 billion in federal subsidies that the government will inject through these auctions over four years. The idea is to give an initial push to develop this industry, which is still non-existent in Brazil, and to promote domestic production of the input, designed to replace fossil-based hydrogen — it can be used in fertilizers, as a raw material for synthetic fuel, and also in cargo, maritime, and aviation transport.

On the other hand, the private sector believes that initially the focus should be on exports to the external market, especially the European Union and Asia, where the greatest potential demands are located, while the domestic industry develops and takes its first steps.

After Congress approved the legal framework for green hydrogen in 2024, the ball is now with the Executive Branch, which has begun a race to regulate this demand contracting and raise the first auction still in 2026. Before that, the Finance and Mines and Energy ministries expect to publish the first decrees in the coming weeks that will dictate the general rules for the auction design, according to NeoFeed's findings.

It is still being discussed, for example, whether the auctions will be separated by products (ammonia, green fertilizer, green hydrogen, or methanol) or by regions. Last week, Finance Ministry authorities were even in Vienna, Austria, at an event on energy and hydrogen to seek international partnerships in formatting the auctions. The World Bank, for example, should also provide support for the auctions.

"The Brazilian government is closing a partnership with UNIDO, the UN arm for industrial development. They are supporting us in the auction formatting," says Rafael Dubeux, special secretary of the Finance Ministry, to NeoFeed.

The policy to encourage domestic green hydrogen production is part of a broader environmental agenda of the government, which had been stimulated by former Finance Minister Fernando Haddad and also includes the carbon market and the offshore wind energy framework. The law, which came into force a little over a year ago in Brazil, also brings tax incentives for low-carbon hydrogen production, such as zero PIS and COFINS for five years (starting last January) for the purchase of raw materials, intermediate products, packaging, inventory, and construction materials made by enabled low-emission hydrogen producers. It also created a certification system for this hydrogen.

## Market Vision

For Fernanda Delgado, president of the Brazilian Green Hydrogen Industry Association (ABIHV), Brazil will still face an initial challenge in developing this industry, which is complex, similar to a refinery structure, requires high added value, and will demand many adjustments in the first years and even probable "mistakes."

Regarding the auctions, she considers that the expectation is that a first, small, and more "niche" auction comes out by the end of this year, possibly aimed at only one product, to measure market appetite to respond promptly. And that it is most likely that the first auction will include green fertilizer, but only one company is currently operating in that area, she assesses.

"It's a tumultuous year for everyone, with wars and our own fiscal issues, but our expectation is that the decrees will come out in the first semester," says Delgado. "But today there is no primacy for the domestic market, because there is no appetite for buying hydrogen in the domestic market, mainly because there are no greenhouse gas emission targets yet. And no project has gotten off the ground yet," she adds.

She explains that there is no domestic production of low-carbon hydrogen in the country because the regulatory framework for the industry is still lacking. And the National Petroleum Agency (ANP), which will oversee oversight, is still working on this. And for now there are only experimental projects, funded by companies or universities. Such as an initiative by Neoenergia, an electricity distributor, in Brasília, which has been testing green hydrogen in public transportation buses in the city. And a green hydrogen industrial plant by Tramontina in Rio Grande do Sul has already been announced. There are also advanced projects for ammonia and methanol production for marine fuel. But commercial-scale ones have not yet gotten off the ground, awaiting for now the government's decrees and auctions, she emphasizes: "These projects await a regulatory signal from the government to finally make investments viable."

A source from the electricity sector monitoring discussions with the government told NeoFeed that the Brazilian market understands that industry development will bring a paradigm shift in the future. But in the short term there are "uncertainties" and the private sector prefers to wait before it becomes reality. "There is no clarity on how it will evolve yet."
