# MBRF boosts Uruguay beef capacity in $70 million expansion

MBRF, the meat processing company formed from the merger of Marfrig and BRF, on Thursday inaugurated the expansion of its beef unit in Tacuarembó, northern Uruguay, following a $70 million investment.

With the upgrade, the company is bringing to Uruguay an integrated industrial model already implemented in Brazil. According to MBRF, the plant's production capacity has been significantly increased, particularly in processed products. Hamburger output is expected to more than quadruple, rising from 200 tonnes to 900 tonnes per month. Daily cattle slaughter capacity will increase to 1,400 from 900 head, a 40% jump that will make the facility the largest of its kind in Uruguay. The investment also expanded pre-chilling capacity from 1,800 to 2,800 animals.

"This industrial model allows us to operate at greater scale, efficiency, safety and standardization, enhancing our ability to serve multiple markets and the most demanding clients with quality and speed," Chairman Marcos Molina said in a statement.

The plant will supply both the domestic Uruguayan market and exports. "Uruguay is a strategic market for the company, recognized for its production quality, strong sanitary standards, and broad access to international markets," said CEO Miguel Gularte.

Uruguayan President Yamandú Orsi attended the inauguration alongside Molina, Gularte and MBRF's local CEO, Marcelo Secco.

While MBRF is now strengthening its strategy in Uruguay, Marfrig had attempted to divest some of its assets in the country about two years ago. In 2023, the company agreed to sell 16 plants across South America—including operations in Brazil, Argentina, Chile, and Uruguay—to Minerva Foods in a R$7.5 billion deal. The Uruguayan portion of the transaction faced resistance from local industry groups, prompting a prolonged review by the country's antitrust authority, the Comisión de Promoción y Defensa de la Competencia, which took more than 2 years to reach a final decision.

After initial rejections, Minerva proposed in February last year that its purchase of three plants be conditioned on the resale of one unit, in Colonia, to Allana Group, a producer and exporter of halal meat. However, as the approval process dragged on, Marfrig said in August that the deadline to complete the deal had expired—two years after it was first announced. In September 2025, Uruguay's antitrust authority again denied approval for the transaction.
