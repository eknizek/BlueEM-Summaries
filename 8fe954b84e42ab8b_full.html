Brazilian international travel spending surges 28% in March, central bank reports

24 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Brazilian international travel spending surges 28% in March, central bank reports

Brazilians spent almost 28% more on international travel in March 2026 compared to the same month the previous year. Expenses rose from US$ 1.6 billion to US$ 2.0 billion, while foreign tourism revenues in Brazil remained stable at US$ 0.9 billion. The result, released Friday (24th) by the Central Bank, raised net travel expenses to US$ 1.1 billion — a 68.3% increase compared to March 2025.

The data comes alongside a relevant change in how the Central Bank itself calculates these statistics. The monetary authority announced an extraordinary methodological revision in international travel revenues, incorporating new data sources and a new compilation methodology. The effect was an improvement in the accumulated current account deficit in the 12 months ending in February: the number was revised from 2.71% of GDP to 2.61% of GDP.

The revision covers the period from October 2023 to February 2026 and consisted mainly of reclassifying transactions that were recorded in the Financial Account — under other investments, currencies and deposits — to international travel revenues. With this, gross travel revenues were revised upward by US$ 0.1 billion in 2023, US$ 1.1 billion in 2024, US$ 2.6 billion in 2025, and US$ 0.9 billion in the first two months of 2026. The revision of gross expenses was residual, accumulating US$ 0.6 billion in the period.

The source of the distortion lies in a change in exchange rate legislation, according to the Central Bank. Law no. 14,286, from December 2021, altered the reporting of information on transactions carried out through accounts of non-resident account holders denominated in reais. With the new rule, part of the financial flows related to the use of credit cards by foreign travelers in Brazil came to be recorded as deposit constitution — and not as travel revenue. The Central Bank identified the problem by cross-referencing additional information from exchange contracts with data provided directly by market participants in the card industry.

According to XP, the increase in net expenses in the travel account was the main determinant of the services account result in March, "probably still reflecting exchange rate appreciation," according to analysts at the bank in a report. In the first quarter of 2026, this account expanded by US$ 0.7 billion, a 36% increase year-on-year.

## Current account below expectations

The current transactions deficit stood at US$ 6.0 billion in March 2026, below the median of US$ 6.3 billion projected by 17 economists consulted by Bloomberg. The result, however, represents a significant deterioration compared to the deficit of US$ 2.9 billion recorded in the same month of 2025.

In the accumulated 12 months ending in March, the deficit totaled US$ 64.3 billion, equivalent to 2.71% of GDP, compared to US$ 61.2 billion (2.61% of GDP) in the previous month.

The deterioration compared to March 2025 stemmed mainly from three factors, according to the Central Bank: a reduction of US$ 1.6 billion in the goods trade surplus, an increase of US$ 1.1 billion in the primary income deficit, and an increase of US$ 0.6 billion in the services deficit. The secondary income surplus grew by US$ 0.2 billion and partially offset the movement.

The goods trade surplus reached US$ 5.6 billion in March, down from US$ 7.2 billion in March 2025. Exports totaled US$ 31.7 billion, a 9.5% increase year-on-year, with expansion concentrated in commodities — especially crude oil. Imports advanced 19.9%, to US$ 26.1 billion, with increases spread across major economic categories.

The services account deficit totaled US$ 4.8 billion in March, compared to US$ 4.2 billion in the same period of 2025. Beyond travel, the result was driven by higher net expenses in telecommunications, computing and information (+27.4%, to US$ 0.9 billion), intellectual property services (+9.2%, to US$ 1.2 billion), and transportation (+7.5%, to US$ 1.2 billion). Over 12 months accumulated, the services deficit reached US$ 50.3 billion.

The primary income deficit totaled US$ 7.4 billion in March, an increase of 17.8% compared to US$ 6.3 billion in March 2025. Net expenses with profits and dividends totaled US$ 4.8 billion (+10.7%), and net interest payments reached US$ 2.6 billion, an increase of 33.5%, with preponderance of higher gross expenses in intercompany operations. Over 12 months accumulated, the primary income deficit reached US$ 84.0 billion.

Direct investments in the country (FDI) registered net inflows of US$ 6.0 billion in March, below the median of US$ 7.0 billion projected by the 17 economists consulted by Bloomberg. The result was also below the US$ 6.3 billion captured in March 2025. The inflows were composed of US$ 4.3 billion in capital participation — of which US$ 1.2 billion in equity capital and US$ 3.2 billion in profit reinvestment — and US$ 1.7 billion in intercompany operations. Over 12 months accumulated, FDI totaled US$ 75.7 billion, equivalent to 3.18% of GDP.

## What analysts say

For Itaú BBA, the current account deficit "worsened again at the margin," reflecting the deceleration of the trade balance — "in which the positive effects of the increase in crude oil export prices have not yet been fully observed, but are already present in April data." The bank also highlighted the deterioration in external financing, with deceleration in both FDI and portfolio flows, "possibly associated with the global risk aversion movement after the start of the Middle East conflict." For the year, Itaú projects a current account deficit of US$ 66 billion (2.6% of GDP).

XP assessed that the current account was "slightly below expectations," with the primary income result being the main factor of divergence from the institution's projection. Remittances of profits and dividends, which typically show seasonality in March, "were less intense than expected." XP projects a current account deficit of US$ 58.0 billion (2.2% of GDP) in 2026, assuming an average Brent price of US$ 90 per barrel, and FDI of US$ 75.0 billion.

Goldman Sachs classified the result as a "moderate current account deficit," with FDI "below expected, but positive portfolio flows." The bank noted that, over 12 months, the current account deficit remains at a "moderate" level of 2.7% of GDP, with FDI at 3.2% of GDP. The American institution added that "a deep fiscal adjustment to reduce the negative savings of the public sector" remains, in its assessment, an essential condition for enabling a permanent structural adjustment in the current account.

The 4intelligence consultancy highlighted the improvement in the balance of payments in the first quarter overall: the current account deficit fell from US$ 22.7 billion to US$ 20.3 billion between 1Q25 and 1Q26, a decline of 11%. The consultancy projects a current account deficit of US$ 54.2 billion for 2026, "with downside bias depending on crude oil exports," and FDI of US$ 70.0 billion.
