# Dried Fruit Exporter DeliTierra Eyes Mexican Market Amid Retail Channel Expansion

DeliTierra, a Peruvian healthy snacks company, is driving growth in the modern retail channel while planning entry into the wholesale channel and the Mexican market.

DeliTierra, a Peruvian healthy snacks company with five years in the market and a plant in Lurín, reported an increase in sales volume at the start of 2026, driven by adjustments to its offerings and the reactivation of agreements with retail chains. The company is advancing its expansion strategy with new points of sale, strengthening its digital channel, and entering new distribution segments and international markets, along with developing its contract manufacturing line for third parties.

Álex Alfaro, commercial manager of DeliTierra, reported that the Peruvian healthy snacks company closed the first quarter of 2026 with 106% growth in sales volume compared to the same period of the previous year. This result is explained by the return to an important chain with greater clustering. "We resumed our presence in Dollar City after adjusting our proposal, as they requested larger sizes at the same price, which we achieved by optimizing the production chain. Additionally, we are entering Oxxo in mid-April with four to six high-rotation products," he stated.

Currently, the modern retail channel accounts for 60% of the company's sales, followed by distributors (13%) and e-commerce (12%), the latter being its "main strength," as they identified a niche overlooked by other brands in the category. "We identified an opportunity and entered with our full portfolio, with more than 18 SKUs on Rappi and PedidosYa. Today we are the second largest brand in the dried fruit category on the e-commerce channel," he affirmed.

Last year, DeliTierra projected reaching 8% market share; however, according to the executive, it currently stands at approximately 6%. "In 2025 we closed with strong growth, opening more sales channels, achieving 103% growth compared to 2024, with an average of S/ 1.5 million in annual sales. This year we project reaching S/ 2 million in sales and growing 48%," he noted.

DeliTierra's expansion strategy for 2026 will focus on expanding into more distribution channels. For example, it has the wholesale channel in its sights. "We seek to consolidate in this channel and, in the coming months, enter the cruise ship segment, where several brands already operate. To achieve product rotation in that space, it is key to enter with a hard currency price (S/ 1)," the executive explained.

The company projects positioning three products from its portfolio—peanuts with raisins, broad beans, and candied peanuts—at a hard currency price. "I am negotiating raw material imports to achieve the required costs. Cruise ship operators buy through wholesalers, so we estimate reaching this segment in early second semester, coinciding with winter when dried fruit consumption increases. The objective is to expand toward a more traditional market throughout Lima," he added.

DeliTierra is betting on contract manufacturing and negotiating entry into Walmart Mexico. In parallel, the firm seeks to strengthen its contract manufacturing service, which it began in 2025, aimed at chains with private label dried fruit brands, which according to the executive represents a new trend in the category. "We are in talks to supply a provincial chain; I have resumed negotiations. It is a chain from the northern part of the country. Additionally, we seek to enter one of the three main retailers—Supermercados Peruanos, Tottus, or Cencosud—and we hope that in the coming months we can take on the manufacturing of some of their products," he commented.

Since last year, the company has been providing contract manufacturing to an important growing chain, specifically a convenience store chain. Regarding its international expansion, Alfaro noted that entry into Argentina was discarded (the company decided not to continue expansion) due to issues with the distributor and ultimately "they did not see it as profitable."

Instead, the company plans to enter the Mexican market this year. "By mid-third quarter we expect to close an agreement with a client in Mexico, which is the Walmart chain," he advanced.

The executive explained that in the snacks and candy market in the Mexican market there are brands from around the world, but the advantage is that there are differentiated products like Peruvian ones that are only produced in the country. "The goal for this year is to integrate the operation backward; that is, to import raw materials and directly handle production," he assured.

More data about DeliTierra:

**Origin:** Its history began in Ica, when Peruvian businessmen decided to plant date palm trees from Israel on their land. That is where El Dátil was born, DeliTierra's "older sister company," a healthy snacks producer.

**Plant:** DeliTierra has a 500 m² plant in Megacentro, Lurín, where several food companies are located. It currently operates at 80% of its capacity.

**Healthy snacks market:** In the large 150-gram format, DeliTierra competes mainly with Villa Natura and Valle Alto, leaders in the modern retail channel. In the case of small sizes (25-35 grams), they compete with Granuts and Karinto.

**Distribution channels:** The company has presence in supermarkets such as Plaza Vea, Vivanda, and Tottus, in specialized stores (Flora y Fauna), chains like Dollar City, gas station chains (Repsol and LiSTO!), at Jorge Chávez Airport, and e-commerce.
