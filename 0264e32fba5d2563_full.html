# Belo raises $14M Series A funding with Tether participation

About six months after announcing its entry into the Brazilian market, Argentine fintech Belo has just received a significant boost to accelerate its strategy. The international accounts and payments fintech has raised $14 million in a Series A round led by Tether, one of the leading international names in stablecoins. The investment also brings new investors such as Titan Fund, as well as existing shareholders like Venture City, Mindset Ventures, and G2.

With these resources, the plan is to accelerate entry into new markets such as Mexico, Chile, Colombia, Peru, Bolivia, and Paraguay, strengthening the fintech's payment infrastructure vision. According to co-founder and CEO Manuel Beaudroit, Brazil remains firm in this strategy, expanding operations with freelancers, remote workers, and users who deal with cross-border money transfers. Additionally, the company plans to develop a B2B payments solution serving businesses that deal with suppliers or customers in other Latin American countries.

"Brazil is a very large market that has its challenges and has brought us much learning—building a team, knowing our customers, and adjusting the product to improve our offering," Manuel states, noting that the company already registers 50% month-over-month growth in new user acquisition while operating with positive cash flow.

On the numbers front, Manuel reveals that the expectation by year-end is for Brazil to represent 10% to 15% of Belo's total user base—the majority is still in Argentina. "This would mean reaching half a million users in Brazil and a total GMV of R$ 750 million. It's an ambitious but achievable goal," he points out.

Currently, Belo has five people in its Brazilian operation and, with the new funding, plans to bring in new talent, especially in marketing and customer service areas. "The Brazilian user is very demanding about the service offered. We want to be up to the challenge."

## Bringing in Tether

Belo was founded in 2021 as a fintech for crypto and blockchain transactions, a market where fellow Argentine Ripio made its name. However, the company has adjusted its focus and is evolving into a global payments platform, connecting Latin American users to financial ecosystems in other countries.

According to Manuel, having Tether as an investor demonstrates how Belo has established itself as a renowned player in payment infrastructure. The company's model uses crypto infrastructure behind the scenes to simplify this process. Tether's support indicates a broader movement to expand stablecoin-based payment systems in markets where demand for dollar-pegged assets remains strong. Additionally, Tether has invested in systems that enable real-time value transfer between different markets and invests in models that expand this access in regions where it remains unequal.

"Having a partner like Tether for company growth is extremely important and gives us validation and global exposure that few companies have," he evaluates. "We are a company that has grown sustainably over several years. That was something they always appreciated, in addition to ideological and conceptual questions about how we see the future of cryptocurrencies and stablecoins," he adds.

In a conversation with Startups last year, Manuel had mentioned that new fundraising was not in Belo's plans, as the fintech's cash had been positive for some time. When asked about the change of mind for this new round, the CEO, like a good Argentine, turned to a football metaphor.

"You can even say you don't want to play football, but if Messi comes up to you and asks if you want to play, won't you play? Having support from a company like Tether automatically increases the company's price and stature. For us, it was strategic to receive this investment," he concludes.
