# Market volatility returns, sovereign risk spreads near 530 basis points

A new chapter of tension in the Middle East hits global markets this Monday. Following Friday's aborted peace announcement, oil resumes its upward trend at the start of the week, which means bad news for global risk assets. The three main Wall Street indices are moving downward, with the technology-focused Nasdaq leading the way, losing 0.6%.

Despite the poor external climate, Argentine shares trading in New York show mixed results: on the gains side, Tenaris stands out, advancing 2.1%, followed by Cresud which rises 1.3% and Ternium which gains 1.1%. Although YPF's paper rebounds due to rising crude prices, it does so with less force: advancing 1.3%.

"Renewed concerns about the Middle East conflict, which trigger a rebound in oil, lean Wall Street toward a more cautious tone after challenging highs, but for now in a moderate manner as traders continue betting on a resolution," said economist Gustavo Ber, who highlighted: "This northern climate transfers locally mainly toward banking stocks and dollar bonds, given that the oil rebound allows energy companies to dodge it while traders remain attentive to the dynamics of the political scenario and 'micro' signals."

Despite signals from the economic team to strengthen financing channels and face debt maturities in the coming months, dollar bonds reacted with declines on Monday and broke their last winning streak. "In the last month, sovereign bonds registered a monthly increase of 3.8% with better performance of bonarex (4.0% monthly) compared to globals (3.6% monthly)," analysts at Rava explained at the start of trading.

"This good performance of Argentine debt in dollars, which has an increase of 2.7% in 2026, was favored by an improvement in financial conditions globally in the context of reduced tensions in the Middle East," they added.

In the foreign exchange market, the dollar rises 0.6% in the wholesale segment and this trend is replicated in retail: the official rate reaches $1,395 in banks. The Central Bank has purchased US$1,637 this month and its interventions are what prop up the dollar's price decline amid increased supply.

"With agriculture accelerating and financial flows still present, the discussion centers on what the BCRA's strategy will be. The monetary authority will have to decide on the dose of purchases (assumed to maintain the current pace as a floor) and exchange rate declines. It should not be overlooked that the real exchange rate is at historically low levels: 31.5% below the long-term average (excluding Alberto Fernández's tenure) and just 1.5% above the level inherited by Massa (November 2023 average)," analysts at PPI noted.

"With an eye on inflation (via price reductions or ceilings on tradable goods increases), the BCRA could allow an additional exchange rate decline, although this would put more pressure on the margins of tradable goods producers," they explained.
