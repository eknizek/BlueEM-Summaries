# Petroperú: Reform at Risk? Next Senate Would Not Guarantee Its Continuity

Petroperú faces a structural crisis that has generated high costs for the State and deteriorated its sustainability. In response, Emergency Decree (DU) No. 010-2025 represents only a first step to reverse its situation: the company requires comprehensive transformation that will depend on the political commitment of the next authorities and technical decisions.

## Financial and Institutional Deterioration

Petroperú's financial situation has reached critical levels. As of 2025, the company accumulated four years with negative operating margins, meaning it does not generate sufficient income to cover the cost of its operations, even before considering debt interest payments. This results from a combination of factors stemming from serious governance problems; a multi-million investment in the New Talara Refinery, which today operates with failures that limit its production capacity; a pricing policy requiring revision; and a rigid payroll structure.

Consequently, between 2010 and 2025, the company's debt-to-equity ratio tripled, while its capacity to have cash available to cover short-term payments was reduced to one-third. Similarly, profitability deteriorated steadily in the last five years, after being positive in the previous decade. Additionally, Fitch Ratings withdrew its credit rating in January 2026 because there was insufficient financial information to issue a rating.

On the institutional front, the situation is even more serious. Over the last 10 years, the company has had 19 general managers and 21 board presidents, with an average duration of only seven and six months, respectively. This instability reflects high political interference. Each change in top management makes any planning process difficult, undermines continuity, and erodes the organization's technical capacity.

## Costs to the State

Given its poor results, Petroperú has been able to refinance its obligations in the past thanks to the implicit guarantee of the Peruvian State. In this regard, the Peruvian Institute of Economics (IPE) estimates that since 2013, the company received the equivalent of S/25,300 million in state support in the form of capitalizations, loans, and state guarantees. This is equivalent to, for example, more than 300 High-Performance Schools (COAR), almost 100 high-complexity hospitals, or 23 times the annual budget of the Juntos Program.

This fiscal cost could rise to more than S/32 billion in 2026 following announcements of a new bailout of approximately US$2,000 million by the Executive. The urgency of new support confirms the company's high dependence on public resources to sustain its operations.

## A Roadmap for Petroperú

Faced with deterioration on multiple fronts, it is essential to protect the reform contained in the DU from political setbacks. Even more so when projections about the composition of the next Senate do not guarantee a majority in favor of these measures. With 77% of votes counted, projections about Senate composition indicate that 30 of the 60 seats would be occupied by representatives of parties with nuanced positions favoring reform, while another 23 would be opposition and 7 still ambiguous.

This is key because the DU must be consolidated into legislation with the force of law to reduce the risk of reversal. Similarly, sustainably transforming the company will require correcting its governance failures and incorporating private capital. A sustainable roadmap must address at least four fronts: (i) ensuring solid governance to guarantee independence from internal or external pressures; (ii) reorganizing assets to incorporate private participation and guarantee sustainability; (iii) achieving operational and commercial stability to have income and expense predictability; and (iv) dynamizing the hydrocarbon sector by building a favorable environment for new investments.

## Urgency for a Sustainable Route

By Miguel Alzamora, Senior Economist at IPE

Continuing to allocate public resources to sustain an unviable financial situation is unacceptable. Instead, it is necessary to deepen the path opened by the latest Emergency Decree and ensure the political commitment needed to undertake a deeper reform that includes private operator participation and guarantees a viable company. This is a complex reform that will require time and consistency in its execution, as well as capacity to resist setbacks. With the election of new authorities, seizing this opportunity or postponing it again will have direct implications for fiscal sustainability and the development of the hydrocarbon sector in the country.
