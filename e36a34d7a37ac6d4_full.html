# AmCham Proposes 60% Growth in Exports to the US with Plan in Agriculture, Manufacturing, and Services by 2030

At a critical moment for the economy, AmCham Colombia presented a roadmap to transform the relationship with the United States toward 2030. The plan seeks to move from a reactive approach to one of execution with direct impact on commerce, investment, and national employment.

During the forum "Colombia–United States: Building What Comes Next 2025–2030," María Claudia Lacouture, president of the business chamber, warned about the current state of the bilateral relationship. The leader alerted that the relationship is not in crisis, but neither is it at its best strategic or operational moment. "On the traffic light of the binational relationship, we are not in red, but neither in green," she stated.

The chamber's central message combines warning and proposal. Any deterioration in the relationship with the United States would have direct effects on critical variables such as employment, investment, and household welfare in Colombia. "If the relationship becomes more expensive or encounters friction, the impact does not stay in the headlines: it is felt in family income, employment, investment, and the well-being of Colombians," she noted.

The diagnosis is supported by the structural weight of the North American partner. The United States represents 30% of exports, 30% of foreign investment, and one of every four dollars of total Colombian trade. Additionally, the Chamber warned that more than five million jobs depend directly on this commercial relationship.

In this context, the roadmap sets an ambitious goal. The objective is to increase bilateral exports and investment by 60% by 2030, with emphasis on non-mining-energy sectors that allow economic diversification. The strategy identifies concrete opportunities in 28 U.S. states to expand Colombian presence.

The plan incorporates a short-term shock strategy. A 180-day timeline is proposed aimed at restoring investor confidence and improving real market access under competitive conditions. This startup contemplates actions from the first hours of the new government until verifiable results in six months.

The Chamber proposes that within the first 72 hours of the new government, a strategic signal must be sent reaffirming commitment to the bilateral relationship. Subsequently, the first month will focus on organizing and protecting sensitive issues such as security. The operational component concentrates on the first 100 days. During that period, the activation of a package of real market access is planned to facilitate trade and reduce structural barriers. The goal is that by day 180 there are already measurable results in investment, security, and border management.

In sectoral terms, the bet is clear. Agroindustry is projected as one of the main engines, with potential to grow from US$5.9 billion to US$9.4 billion by 2030. The portfolio includes specialty coffees, avocado, fruits, tilapia, processed foods, and value-added products.

The manufacturing sector also appears as an axis of diversification. Exports could increase from US$4 billion to US$6.3 billion, driven by high-value niches and greater technological sophistication. Among them, medical devices, auto parts, chemicals, packaging, technical textiles, and electrical components stand out.

However, the greatest dynamism is anticipated in services. Colombia exported approximately US$7.1 billion in services in 2024 and could reach US$11.3 billion in 2030 with specialized solutions. Opportunities concentrate in cybersecurity, software, video games, healthtech, and engineering.

AmCham insists that to attract U.S. capital, it is essential to resolve structural problems such as legal security and regulatory stability. These factors are determinant for long-term decisions in strategic sectors. The analysis identifies priority sectors for investment. Energy, logistics, digital infrastructure, agroindustry, and business services concentrate the greatest interest from U.S. investors in the current context. Additionally, a territorial approach is proposed in key states such as California, Texas, Florida, New York, and New Jersey.

An additional element of the plan introduces a regional dimension. The roadmap incorporates Venezuela as an opportunity for controlled expansion, with Colombia as a logistical platform to serve that market. The proposal suggests the development of distribution centers and specialized services.

Finally, the Chamber proposes an institutional component. To ensure sustainability, the plan requires legislative anchoring in both the U.S. Congress and the Colombian Congress. The objective is to protect the relationship against political changes and prevent it from depending on circumstances.

AmCham's conclusion synthesizes the urgency of the moment. The relationship with the United States is a strategic asset that not only must be preserved but strengthened through concrete decisions that drive growth, employment, and investment. "This relationship cannot be managed from reaction or circumstance," Lacouture reiterated.
