# Gustavo Petro Reverses 100% Tariff Increase on Ecuadorian Imports

Colombian President Gustavo Petro said Monday that Colombia will not impose 100% tariffs on products imported from Ecuador, reversing an announcement made last Friday by his Minister of Commerce, Industry and Tourism, Diana Marcela Morales, in response to a similar measure by that country.

"There are no 100% tariffs, Minister of Commerce, we are not that foolish; everything that is necessary for Colombia enters at 0%," Petro stated during a cabinet meeting held in Ipiales, in the department of Nariño, which borders Ecuador.

The minister announced on April 10 that Colombia would raise tariffs on Ecuadorian imports from 30% to 100% in response to the neighboring country's decision to increase from 50% to 100%, effective May 1, the "security tax" imposed by President Daniel Noboa's government, in a new escalation of the trade war.

The Ecuadorian president argues that Colombia is not doing enough regarding security on their shared 586-kilometer border, where organized crime groups operate, and therefore decided to impose that tariff called the "security tax."

Following Ecuador's decision, Minister Morales stated in a communiqué that facing the "hardening on the commercial front" ordered by Noboa's government, Colombia decided to align "with the new tariff proposed by Ecuador" on imports, a measure that Petro reversed today.

The president also instructed his Minister of Agriculture, Martha Carvajalino, to produce domestically everything that was previously imported from Ecuador and could be made in Colombia, even resorting to subsidies "to make it cheaper."

He also proposed a market substitution in which Venezuela could purchase Colombian products that cannot enter Ecuador due to the high tariffs.

"Everything produced in Colombia that cannot be exported to Ecuador because of the tariff must be exported to Venezuela, which needs it," Petro argued.

According to the president, the current crisis is the fault of "foreigners" who want to sow divisions between Colombia and Ecuador "and make us kill each other," so he warned against falling "into that trap, because if we divide we lose the power that Bolívar announced."
