# Codelco and Anglo American Pursue 'Dual-Track' Environmental Approach for Andina-Los Bronces Joint Project

Codelco and Anglo American plan to submit independent environmental studies for their joint mining plan Andina-Los Bronces, which contemplates the integration of both neighboring operations located in the mountain range of central Chile.

According to documents accessed by Reuters—described as an "unprecedented dual-track"—the strategy seeks to expedite the approval process for the initiative and considers the companies submitting two practically identical applications in December for the open-pit mine where they would jointly extract copper.

Codelco's board president, Máximo Pacheco, as well as a source from Anglo American, confirmed to the international agency that the companies plan to submit the two applications by year-end.

## Mirror Applications

In zones where the operations overlap, the companies proposed implementing identical environmental measures. They argued that a single application was not legally viable, since the Chilean Constitution requires that Codelco maintain ownership of its mining concessions, as stated in one of the presentations.

The mining firms also considered submitting three applications: one from each mining company to extend the operational life of their respective mines, and a third from a joint entity that would manage the shared operation. However, they ruled out that option because it would force them to renounce their current environmental operating permits to make way for the combined mine.

The dual structure would also allow the mines to potentially operate independently again in the future.

## Single Pit and Risks

The documents also detail plans to create a single pit. The current Los Bronces and Andina pits are adjacent, and the plan suggests that the rocky barrier separating them would also be exploited, creating a single operating pit while keeping the project largely within the current mine surface area.

Ore extracted from the joint pit would be sent indiscriminately to the processing plants of both operations, while waste rock would be deposited in each company's waste dumps. Changes would still be necessary to waste dumps, tailings facilities, pipelines, and support infrastructure for the two mines to function as an integrated system.

Shared infrastructure would avoid duplication of facilities, reduce freshwater consumption, and alleviate pressure on the surrounding area, according to the companies.

Among the risks warned of by Codelco and Anglo, they pointed to the need for close coordination with regulatory agencies, which could overload Chile's already slow environmental evaluation system. They highlighted the "great public impact" of the project and the risk that environmentalists and affected communities might argue that the two evaluations hide the magnitude of impacts.

According to one document, the companies plan to begin dialogue with local communities and other stakeholders in the second half of the year.
