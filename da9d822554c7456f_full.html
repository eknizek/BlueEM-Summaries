# From Brazil to Turkey, Emerging Markets Resume Global Bond Issuance in April

Emerging market bond issuances are gaining strength again after last month's stagnation, as issuers from Brazil to Turkey take advantage of market recovery to raise new capital. Sales of bonds denominated in dollars and euros from developing countries this month already exceed April last year's volumes by approximately 200%, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

A total of $46 billion was raised through Friday, as governments and companies rush to capitalize on improved market sentiment amid hopes for a peace agreement between the United States and Iran.

The recovery follows a decline in issuances last month, when war reduced appetite for higher-risk assets. Now, however, with markets in intense activity again and stock exchanges on the verge of recovering pre-war losses, emerging markets are once again the preferred investment for those seeking returns.

"The emerging market bond market appears to have broadly overcome war risk for now," said Laura Reardon, portfolio manager at MFS Investment Management. "Deals that were in the pipeline before the conflict and were suspended are now coming to market, and investors have cash to allocate following the recent market stabilization."

Reardon highlighted the recent track record of resilience among emerging economies—which recovered quickly from crises such as the pandemic and the war in Ukraine. While many countries were heavily hit by last month's oil price surge, a large group of energy-producing nations in Africa and Latin America benefit from elevated prices.

Additionally, the investment boom in artificial intelligence is seen as a favorable factor for the developing world. Investors absorbed bonds from energy exporters Brazil and Qatar, while issuances from Turkey and Poland were also well-received, as new bonds typically offer yields slightly higher than existing issuances.

Among companies, well-known names such as Banco do Brasil returned to the market, alongside smaller companies like Eldik Bank of Kyrgyzstan, which took advantage of the more favorable environment to make its debut in the dollar market.

All were encouraged by falling financing costs since Iran and the United States agreed to a two-week ceasefire. The average yield spread required by investors to hold emerging market sovereign dollar bonds relative to Treasuries fell to approximately 245 basis points—below levels observed shortly before the war began.

"With spreads where they are, it makes sense to issue," said Carmen Altenkirch, emerging market sovereigns analyst at Aviva Investors. "Sovereigns typically wait for periods of relative calm to issue, especially regular issuers like Turkey."

April's total issuances include nearly $19 billion from emerging market companies, up 88% compared to the same period last year, according to the data. Markets remained open on Monday, ignoring escalating war tensions, while Kazakhstan's railway operator hired banks for a two-tranche issuance. The operation followed those of Korea Ocean Business Corp and Chinese group Shuifa, which began marketing dollar bonds earlier in the day.

## Cash on Standby

The resumption of issuances means year-to-date volume already exceeds the same period last year by nearly 20%, reaching $281 billion. The recovery is similar to the increase in activity seen in developed markets. Sovereigns like Italy and the United Kingdom registered record demand for their new bonds, but subordinated debt issuances—generally considered higher-risk—also advanced strongly this month.

One reason is that many investors want to use cash accumulated during March's liquidation, as any deal to end the war is likely to trigger even greater recovery in global markets.

"Emerging markets are far from alone in having conditions that seem relatively immune to geopolitics," said Kieran Curtis, head of emerging market local debt at Aberdeen Group. "I have the impression that portfolio managers raised a lot of cash last month. Resource outflows were practically limited, so now investors need to allocate and issuers are taking the opportunity."

Brazil seized the moment to execute its largest bond issuance in history, raising 5 billion euros in its first euro-denominated issuance in over a decade. Fund managers also agreed to lend to Turkey, despite its vulnerability to higher energy prices, and absorbed its $2 billion issuance with orders three times the offered amount.

Nevertheless, buyers extracted from Turkey an issue premium of approximately 15 to 20 basis points, well above the five basis points obtained in previous issuances, estimates Nimrod Mevorach, strategist at UBS Group. Poland, another energy importer, paid a premium of 12 to 15 basis points when raising $6 billion in early April, he said, in contrast to practically no premium on its previous issuance.

An even more notable indicator of demand was the Democratic Republic of Congo's debut issuance of $1.25 billion. Investors placed orders four times the amount, possibly attracted by yields of 8.75% and 9.5% on the two tranches of the operation. Manuel Mondia, portfolio manager at Aquila Asset Management, said that the DRC, though fragile, has low debt levels and exports large volumes of copper, an essential input for AI-related investments.

"It was a good deal for bondholders, at a time when these spreads are becoming increasingly rare," said Mondia, who purchased the paper. "The hunt for yield outweighs fear of conflict and contagion."
