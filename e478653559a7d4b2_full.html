# The Great Technological Leap: The True Reason Behind $3.3 Trillion in Chinese Acquisitions

China is the greatest phenomenon in the global economy of the last 50 years. Since the economic opening of the Asian country in late 1978, under the leadership of Deng Xiaoping — which marked the transition from a planned and isolated economy to a market economy — China's GDP has increased no less than 86 times, while the country's per capita income has grown 58 times.

Researchers from various universities around the world have studied Chinese government policies during this period to leverage state and private enterprises on the international stage, with investments in research, subsidized development, and broad access to credit, among other initiatives. However, a recently released academic survey led by the chief researcher at the European Central Bank (ECB), Luc Laeven, brings a surprising revelation: the focus of Chinese companies in acquiring corporate assets abroad between 2012 and 2021 had technology transfer as the primary interest.

This motivation is behind approximately $3.3 trillion in global corporate assets acquired during the period by Chinese investors, both state and private, heavily concentrated in Europe (42% of foreign investment) and North America (38%). The investments, according to the study, concentrated in knowledge-intensive sectors, a focus that increased after the launch of the government initiative Made in China 2025.

The program, announced in May 2015, had as its central objective transforming China into a high-tech industrial power, reducing dependence on foreign technologies and increasing the global competitiveness of Chinese companies. In this sense, concludes the research, technology transfer contributed centrally to China's recent advancement.

The study surprises in the way it reached its conclusion. Laeven, director-general of the ECB's research department, and three co-authors — Georgetown University professor Jenny Bai; and Hong Ru and Yaojun Ke from Nanyang Technological University — assembled a set of micro-level data with 161,773 companies in 159 countries. They then constructed complex ownership chains to track capital through offshore tax havens to its final origin — which, according to them, represent more than 80% of the global assets of non-financial companies.

This is how they discovered the $3.3 trillion in global corporate assets acquired by Chinese investors in the analyzed period. According to the survey, almost two-fifths of this investment flowed through at least one tax haven. "This data is relevant because it reveals a Chinese global presence substantially broader than indicated by official FDI (Foreign Direct Investment) statistics," the study states.

The authors estimate that almost $800 billion in Chinese holdings are captured through the Cayman Islands, representing almost half of the country's non-financial corporate assets in the dataset. In practice, foreign assets controlled by Chinese expanded at a rate of 20% per year, reaching $2.1 trillion, or approximately 3% of global corporate assets in 2021.

## Strategy

Other data captured by the survey allow conclusions about the objectives behind these acquisitions. According to the researchers, Chinese investors — particularly state-owned enterprises — strategically target foreign companies with intensive R&D use and linked to supply chains. After acquisition, target companies increase capital stock and R&D spending, but these investments do not generate greater patent production, a result described by the researchers as "not only infinitesimally small, but also statistically insignificant."

Furthermore, profits of target companies suffered negative impact: average return on assets fell 1.1 percentage points compared to the control group of non-Chinese companies in the same period. Considering that the average return on assets of non-Chinese companies analyzed in the dataset is only 4%, this impact on profits appears significant.

Rather than speculating whether Chinese owners are simply incompetent at managing Western companies, the researchers sought a reason for this contradiction between high investment in foreign companies and low returns. And they discovered something strategic in the Chinese parent company that suggests an alternative explanation.

"We found evidence of 'spillover' effects of innovation: while target companies show no increase in patent registration, Chinese parent companies experience a sharp increase in the number of patents granted after their first acquisition in a developed economy," the study indicates.

At the end of the fiscal year in which Chinese companies acquired their first subsidiary in a developed foreign market, the average number of patents registered by Chinese parent companies more than tripled. For Chinese state-owned enterprises, the average number of patents registered quadrupled. That is, the patents were used by the Chinese parent company in the domestic market.

When analyzing their data to verify whether similar patterns appear in external investments from other countries, the researchers discovered that this pattern is unique to China. Thus, the study lists some possibilities. One is that investors who buy foreign companies with the intention of diverting profits need to deal with tax withholding — leaving a negative balance for foreign tax authorities. Diverting pre-patent intellectual property — if that really happened — could be a way to avoid scrutiny from foreign governments, boost domestic economic development, and also extract value without paying bothersome taxes. In other words, the old and worn-out tax evasion.

The idea that technology transfer is one of the main reasons for Chinese investment abroad is not new, merely confirmed by the study. "China thus represents a distinctly state-based model of global ownership that accepts weaker short-term performance to internalize technological capacity in its country," emphasize the researchers in their conclusion.

Strictly speaking, the study reinforces the well-known Chinese strategy of aiming for the long term, as Deng Xiaoping had already announced in 1978. The transformations caused by Chinese growth in the global economy can be summarized in two indicators.
