Opinion: Risks of Pre-Electoral Hyperactivity in Brazilian Government

14 April 2026 · 09:50 AM

# Opinion: Risks of Pre-Electoral Hyperactivity in Brazilian Government

The Federal Government has entered pre-electoral hyperactivity mode. Stung by unfavorable voting intention polls, it has begun trying to demonstrate action in areas where qualitative surveys point to its vulnerabilities. In just a few days, measures or intentions for new measures were announced regarding: renegotiating debts of families, companies, and agribusiness; attenuating increases in electricity bills; granting subsidized credit to exporters; and, above all, mitigating the impact of rising oil prices through tax reductions, subsidies, subsidized credit, and political pressure on Petrobras. A large number of hastily taken measures are already raising red flags regarding the risk of inadequate design, gaps, inefficiency, and harmful side effects. More than that, however, they reveal a government going in circles, trying to placate the negative effects of its own choices, as well as insistence on flawed formulas that have previously failed. The costs will not be negligible.

Let us first examine the problem of excessive indebtedness of families and companies, which has been pointed out as the cause of the electorate's discontent with the Government. A fundamental principle of economic policy has been to maintain public accounts in deficit as an instrument to stimulate demand, with the aim of increasing growth. With a positive GDP gap for several years and high and rising public debt, this policy leads to high short-term interest rates (to contain inflation) and long-term rates (due to increased default risk on public debt). High interest rates worsen the indebtedness of families and companies. Therefore, the root of high indebtedness stems from the Government's political choice. Furthermore, economic policy has emphasized stimulus for indebtedness. The Government launched programs such as "Acredita no Primeiro Passo" (credit for families in the Single Registry), "Procred 360" (for individual microentrepreneurs), "Pronampe" (for micro and small businesses), Private Payroll Loans, increased income brackets for Minha Casa Minha Vida, and credit for those affected by floods and droughts. Even though below market rates, many of these credit lines carry significant real interest. Not only is the Government centrally responsible for the indebtedness crisis, but it attempts to placate it with an instrument that has already proven ineffective: a refinancing program based on public guarantees and fiscal benefits to banks. Less than two years after the Desenrola Program, conceived precisely to "solve" the over-indebtedness problem, we are back to record levels of debt and default. Desenrola was ineffective as an indebtedness reduction policy. But not necessarily as a policy that generates political gains for incumbents, leaving the fiscal cost to be paid by everyone.

Regarding electricity bills, we have, once again, high increases as a consequence of inadequate public policies – here, it is important to note, with responsibility shared with Congress. The central cause is excessive unnecessary subsidies to renewable energies, to be compensated through tariffs. To avoid increases, instead of discussing subsidy reduction, a series of stalling and high-cost measures are put on the table: BNDES credit lines for distribution companies (again, government stimulus for indebtedness), postponement of increases by ANEEL, return of tax credits. The measures to deal with rising oil prices seem even more problematic. A commodity price shock of around 70%, with no forecast of stabilization in the coming months, is indeed a challenge for any government. There is room for a limited policy of impact mitigation, and to finance it with additional public revenues from the shock itself. But the way the Government is doing it contains several errors. First, it is not possible to isolate Brazil from the price shock, as the Government's discourse suggests. The President's statement that "the war is not the Brazilian people's and we don't have to be victims of this war" is obviously political-electoral rhetoric. But when it begins to guide economic policy decisions, the risk of high fiscal cost and economic distortions arises.

An appropriate approach would be to focus subsidies on vulnerable groups (cooking gas consumption by low-income populations, which already existed before the crisis) and on those that could cause major problems (a "truck drivers' allowance" to prevent strikes that would paralyze the country). Instead, the Government – echoing the President's electoral desire – entered with tax reductions and subsidies that attempt to reduce prices for everyone, from the poor to owners of diesel-powered pickup trucks. The apex of distortion is the subsidy to aviation kerosene and subsidized credit to airlines (again indebtedness on the menu of "solutions"), a mix of airline lobbying with subsidies for upper-class travel. It should be noted that, months ago, airlines had already managed to finance fuel purchases with public credit resources for exports. Not satisfied, the Government then placed pressure on Petrobras not to adjust prices, condemned and promised to annul a GLP sale auction conducted by the state company, and forced the dismissal of a company director who oversaw the auction. Political pressure on Petrobras is not greater only because the company appears to be pre-aligned with the intention to suppress price increases and, based on its obscure adjustment rules, allowed the lag in diesel and gasoline prices to skyrocket, even after it became clear that oil prices would not return to pre-war levels in the coming months. Feeling the blow from presidential criticism of its auction, the company returned part of the money paid by buyers.

Financing part of public subsidies through taxation of petroleum company revenues would be an acceptable measure to capture part of the "windfall profit" of oil companies, if properly designed. What should be done was taxation of additional company profits – for example, through increased special participations. But what the government did was an export tax, which taxes revenue regardless of the company's profit margin, not profit, which penalizes high-revenue, low-profit companies and benefits high-profit companies with lower revenue. Obviously a distortion and a yellow flag regarding sector regulatory stability.

It is also worth understanding how public finances are affected by these measures. All contemplated credit operations will have public guarantee or funding. These are resources that will come out of budgetary funds, such as the Social Fund or the National Civil Aviation Fund. When these resources leave the Treasury account and are directed to BNDES or another public bank, gross debt already increases. The alternative use of these resources would be debt reduction.
