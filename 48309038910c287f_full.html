# Copasa to Transfer R$350 Million to Minas Gerais Municipalities in Privatization Deal

Copasa, the state-owned sanitation company of Minas Gerais, will transfer approximately R$350 million to municipalities in Minas Gerais to advance the renewal of water and sewage concession contracts, a step considered fundamental for the company's privatization. The agreement, negotiated between the company and the AMM (Association of Minas Gerais Municipalities), was formalized at a meeting Thursday afternoon (23rd) in Belo Horizonte.

Earlier, Copasa announced in a statement to the market that its board of directors had approved the measure, but did not provide detailed figures. The transfers are an advance on revenues that the company would already deposit monthly into municipal sanitation funds between September 2026 and December 2028. City halls that replace their current contracts with Copasa with the new model—which extends the concession term until 2073—will have access to these values in advance.

Depending on the level of adhesion, the amount transferred could exceed R$350 million, according to the AMM. Payment will be made in two installments: the first in October 2026 and the second in March 2027. The advance of these values is a way to encourage municipal adoption of a step considered essential for privatization. This is because standardization and extension of concession contracts increase the value of Copasa's business and boost attractiveness for potential buyers of its shares.

According to the AMM, the agreement also provides that municipalities where Copasa will operate sanitation services can delay the start of charging this new fee until 2029. The measure aims to reduce the financial impact on consumers in municipalities where Copasa operates water supply and, with the renegotiation, will also assume sewage services.

Today, Copasa serves 636 municipalities (309 with water and sewage; 327 with water only). According to the AMM, at least 265 have already begun negotiations to renew their contracts. In a statement to the market, the state-owned company said that the regulatory treatment of the advance is being discussed with the regulatory agency, adding that the measure does not apply to Belo Horizonte, "whose contract was renewed separately."

**REVENUE TRANSFER IMPACTS TARIFFS**

Revenues that Copasa allocates to municipal sanitation funds have an impact on the tariff paid by consumers. This transfer—which is usually made monthly and will now be advanced in two installments—is based on a regulatory rule that authorizes the company to withdraw a portion of what it collects from water and sewage bills in municipalities and deliver it directly to city halls to invest in sanitation-related projects. The value is limited to 4% of the net operating revenue that Copasa obtains within the city.

The transfer to the municipal fund is considered a service cost and goes through "tariff recognition." In practice, this means the value is included in the calculation of the tariff paid by consumers so that Copasa does not incur losses from these transfers.
