# Why Brazil Became the "Boss" in Hugo Boss's Structure

While the brand realigns operations worldwide, Brazil gains prominence with accelerated growth and store expansion

Hugo Boss, after years of growth, is adopting a global realignment strategy focused on profitability, which may impact its sales by 10% in 2026. However, in Brazil, the brand continues to grow at an accelerated pace, with General Manager Romeo Bonadio affirming that the country presents growth superior to the global average. The brand expanded from 16 to 51 stores in eight years and plans to open three more in 2026. Bonadio highlights that Hugo Boss is seen as an accessible luxury brand, competing with names like Prada in quality. The brand is also investing in omnichannel channels and sports partnerships, such as with David Beckham and Formula 1. The new collection for the World Cup and the creation of a VIP space at Interlagos are examples of its marketing strategy. Hugo Boss seeks to reposition itself as a young and modern brand, attracting a broader audience.

After years of growth, with an eye on future profitability, the moment calls for brand realignment — which, according to CEO Daniel Grieder, may affect sales by 10% this year. In 2025, the company sold €4.2 billion. However, this trend should not reach Brazilian stores. If it depends on Romeo Bonadio, general manager of Hugo Boss in Brazil, the accelerator will continue to dominate the storefronts for good years to come.

"For us, the situation is a little different, as we have recorded growth higher than the global average, which gives us more room to continue advancing on the basis of double digits annually," says the executive in an interview with NeoFeed. "Even if those double digits are not so high, we do not expect to fall below them."

The more "aggressive" strategy in Brazil is due to a range of events, but mainly to the factor that Brazilian income is quite concentrated — which means that the luxury segment is not affected as much as in other countries. The United States, the brand's largest market, for example, ends up more harmed than Brazil. "This makes us more resilient," says Bonadio.

Bonadio is one of the main responsible for the brand's expansion in Brazil. In his eight years leading Hugo Boss and almost 20 years of experience at giants like Louis Vuitton and Prada, he inserted the country on the global map, raising its position in the ranking from 20th to 11th. During this same period, the number of stores grew from 16 to 51, not counting the three inaugurations projected for 2026 — one in Rio de Janeiro, another in Recife, and a third still mysterious. Last year, the last unit opened by Hugo Boss marked the brand's entry into the northern region, establishing itself in Belém, Pará.

"Here, we have the advantage of customers seeing us as a luxury brand, but with more accessible value," says Bonadio. "This gives us greater reach, as we are placed on the same level as names like Prada and, despite having the same quality, we offer much more attractive prices and can occupy spaces that these brands do not occupy. This positioning is extremely important for us."

For a brand that had no digital presence until 2022, this is effectively a positive point. Today, 85% of Hugo Boss's revenue still comes from physical points, while online and wholesale divide the remaining 15%.

Among marketing partnerships, the one with David Beckham is the longest-lasting and seeks to convey an air of versatility and timeless style. With a focus on sports, the brand also developed a collection aimed at the World Cup, which will have a pop-up dedicated to products. In Formula 1, the brand is a sponsor of the Aston Martin team, with drivers Fernando Alonso and Lance Stroll. For the Brazilian public, the brand created a VIP space within the paddock of the Interlagos stage of Formula 1, which receives about 150 guests per day and seeks to create a relationship between customers and celebrities.

Of the 51 stores, 30 are exclusive to the Boss brand, aimed at men and women seeking sophistication, while six carry the Hugo logo, which brings a younger and more relaxed style, but in the same minimalist energy of the entire brand. Overall, the most active customers range from 34 to 41 years old and spend, on average, R$2,200 on routine purchases. But don't be fooled. There, it is also possible to find suits in the range of R$40,000.

"Today our positioning is to dress the person 24/7, something that did not yet exist in the male world, mainly. At Hugo Boss, you can find everything from underwear to tuxedos, passing through sports outfits, performance and even beach fashion," says Bonadio. "I believe that having this entire range of products is a great attraction for consumers."

Thinking about strategy beyond products, the executive believes that now is the time to look inward, increase the productivity of existing stores, and develop omnichannel channels. To do this, he relies on data provided by Digital Campus, a space located in Porto, Portugal, focused on collecting and storing information from all activities carried out by the brand around the world. According to him, based on this data, it is possible to understand which strategic decisions to make, which direction to follow, and how to improve efficiency, without taking into account only the trends used in the fashion world.

The focus on the sports world

It is based on this data that the brand focused its strategy mainly on sports. Today, it holds partnerships with major market names, such as David Beckham, who develops collections with Boss designers, as well as sponsorships for tennis players, Formula 1 and basketball teams. In the automotive category, the brand is one of the major names of the Aston Martin team. Together, the companies developed a global strategy that includes Brazil. Here, Boss will set up, for the third consecutive year, its VIP space within the paddock of the Interlagos Autodrome in November. The space receives about 150 people per day, among customers, celebrities and personalities who work with the brand, with the aim of further connecting the Boss name to this type of experience.

For the World Cup, the brand developed a complete clothing collection for eight participating national teams, which will be launched soon. Along with it, a pop-up will be opened solely for the event, which still has no disclosed location.

"Before all this brand repositioning, people thought of Boss as something grandfather wore, or even father, right?! Now, that's no longer true. We really have a young brand, that does collaborations with artists, the most popular sports, and that makes a lot of sense," he says. "Today, Boss is a brand that dresses successful people, and that says a lot."
