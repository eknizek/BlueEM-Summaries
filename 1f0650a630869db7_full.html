Why Brazil became the 'boss' in Hugo Boss's structure

12 April 2026 · 07:50 PM

# Why Brazil became the "boss" in Hugo Boss's structure

After years of growth, focused on future profitability, the moment calls for realignment of brands — which could, according to CEO Daniel Grieder, affect sales by 10% in the year. In 2025, the company sold €4.2 billion. However, the trend should not reach Brazilian stores. If it depends on Romeo Bonadio, general manager of Hugo Boss in Brazil, the accelerator pedal will continue to dominate the storefronts for many years to come.

"For us, the situation is a bit different, as we have recorded growth higher than the global average, which gives us more room to continue advancing on the basis of double-digit growth annually," says the executive in an interview with NeoFeed. "Even if these double digits aren't that high, we don't expect to fall below them."

The more "aggressive" strategy in Brazil is due to a range of events, but mainly to the factor that Brazilian income is quite concentrated — which means that the luxury segment is not as affected as in other countries. The United States, the brand's largest market, for example, ends up more affected than Brazil. "This makes us more resilient," says Bonadio.

Bonadio is one of the main responsible for the brand's expansion in Brazil. In his eight years leading Hugo Boss and almost 20 years of experience at giants like Louis Vuitton and Prada, he put the country on the global map, raising its position in the ranking from 20th to 11th. In that same period, the number of stores went from 16 to 51, not counting the three openings planned for 2026 — one in Rio de Janeiro, another in Recife, and a third still mysterious.

Last year, the latest unit opened by Hugo Boss marked the brand's entry into the northern region, establishing itself in Belém, Pará. "Here, we have the advantage of customers seeing us as a luxury brand, but with more accessible value," says Bonadio. "This gives us greater reach, since we are placed on the same level as names like Prada and, despite having the same quality, we offer much more attractive prices and can occupy spaces that these brands don't occupy. This positioning is extremely important for us."

For a brand that had no digital presence until 2022, this is effectively a positive point. Today, 85% of Hugo Boss's revenue still comes from physical stores, while online and wholesale divide the remaining 15%.

Among marketing partnerships, the one with David Beckham is the longest-running and seeks to convey an air of versatility and timeless style. With a focus on sports, the brand also developed a collection aimed at the World Cup, which will have a pop-up dedicated to products.

Of the 51 stores, 30 carry the exclusive Boss brand, aimed at men and women seeking sophistication, while six carry the Hugo logo, which brings a younger and more casual style, but in the same minimalist energy of the entire brand. Overall, the most active customers range between 34 and 41 years old, and spend on average R$2,200 on routine purchases. But don't be fooled. There, it's also possible to find suits in the R$40,000 range.

"Today our positioning is to dress the person 24/7, something that didn't exist in the male world mainly. At Hugo Boss, you can find everything from underwear to tuxedos, passing through sports outfits, performance wear, and even beachwear," says Bonadio. "I believe that having this entire range of products is a great attraction for consumers."

Thinking about strategy beyond products, the executive believes that now is the time to look inward, increase the productivity of existing stores, and develop omnichannel channels. For this, he relies on data provided by Digital Campus, a space located in Porto, Portugal, focused on collecting and storing information from all activities carried out by the brand around the world. According to him, based on this data, it's possible to understand which strategic decisions to make, which direction to follow, and how to improve efficiency, without taking into account only trends used in the fashion world.

The focus on the sports world

It is based on this data that the brand focused its strategy mainly on sports. Today, it holds partnerships with major market names such as David Beckham, who develops collections with Boss designers, in addition to sponsorships of tennis players, Formula 1 teams, and basketball.

In the automotive category, the brand is one of the major names of the Aston Martin team. Together, the companies developed a global strategy that includes Brazil. Here, Boss will set up, for the third consecutive year, its VIP space within the paddock of the Interlagos Autodrome in November. The space receives about 150 people per day, including clients, celebrities, and personalities who work with the brand, with the aim of further linking the Boss name to this type of experience.

For the World Cup, the brand developed a complete clothing collection for eight participating teams, which will be launched soon. Along with it, a pop-up dedicated solely to the event will be inaugurated, which doesn't yet have a disclosed location.

"Before this entire brand repositioning, people thought of Boss as something that grandfather wore, or even dad, right?! Now, that's no longer true. We really have a young brand that does collabs with artists, the hottest sports, and this makes a lot of sense," he says. "Today, Boss is a brand that dresses successful people, and that says a lot."
