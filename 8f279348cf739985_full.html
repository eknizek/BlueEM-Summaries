# At the Top of the Beverage Sector, Two CEOs Share a Stage—But Agree Quite a Bit

CAMBRIDGE, Massachusetts – The two largest beverage companies in the world are under the leadership of Brazilians, and this is no coincidence. Henrique Braun took over as CEO of Coca-Cola in March, while Michel Doukeris has been leading Anheuser-Busch InBev since 2021. The two recently shared the stage here during Brasil Project, the event at Harvard and MIT that brought together some of the country's leading minds to discuss Brazilian dilemmas and challenges. The two CEOs not only shared leadership lessons but also learnings that helped them reach the top – and Brazil has much to do with it.

The volatility and uncertainty of the country can deter investments and frighten executives, but this "school" is what allowed Braun and Doukeris to learn how to navigate an equally volatile sector, where trends change very rapidly.

Speaking about how to deal with the uncertainties inherent in the sector and the global economy, Braun emphasized the importance of learning to navigate the unknown and not allowing oneself to be consumed by it. "The world, whether you're in a startup or a large company, faces an incredible amount of uncertainty. But I like to take a step back. At Coca-Cola, we celebrated 140 years this year. There is much knowledge that cannot be forgotten, because much of the uncertainties we face today we have experienced before, and we have the muscle memory of how we adapted and came out stronger."

According to Coca-Cola's CEO, one must "keep their eyes" on the long term while dealing with short-term uncertainties.

Doukeris, for his part, downplayed the impact of market crises and imbalances, and shared a stoic vision of leadership. According to him, "whoever has been in the position for five years, like me, has already gone through all the crises in recent years" and "every year you make your plan knowing you'll need to adjust it for something that's coming. You just don't know what it is yet."

"Resilience, by MIT's definition, is not that you don't break or don't change. It's that you can absorb the shocks and return to your original state," said Doukeris.

When questioned about the impacts of AI tools on business, the two agree that it is not an end in itself, but a lever to amplify existing competitive advantages.

"Simply put, Coca-Cola's application of AI is that we're using technology to do better what we already do best, amplifying our competitive advantage," said Braun. He said the company has been using AI for almost four years and that the tools are "at the forefront" of the brand's advertising campaigns.

"The last two Christmas campaigns, the biggest for the Coca-Cola brand, were 100% made with generative AI. This allows us to do creative work at a fraction of the cost and much faster than we did before. It's a great example of doing better what we do best, which is creating brands that will last into the future."

Doukeris, meanwhile, emphasized the use of AI in AB InBev's supply chain, "from the way we hedge our financial positions, to the way we buy inputs for the company, to the way we sell products to our consumers."

With AI, "if I know how much you're going to sell tomorrow, I can already plan better for production and supply from yesterday," he said.
