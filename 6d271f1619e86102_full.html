# ISA Energia's Underground Transmission Line Model Disrupts Power Market

ISA Energia Brasil, the leader in energy transmission in the country, is expanding its underground transmission line model with the completion of a R$ 1.1 billion project that connects São Paulo to São Caetano do Sul. This "underground ring road" spans 44.6 kilometers and aims to serve densely populated urban areas, increasing supply security for two million residents. Although more expensive than the aerial model, it is a viable solution in regions with limited space. The company plans to invest R$ 12.3 billion by 2030, focusing on renewable energy projects and improvements to existing infrastructure. Additionally, ISA is monitoring a battery auction, considering the technology as a solution for growing energy demand. In 2025, the company reported revenue of R$ 4.35 billion, with a significant increase in capex, while its shares appreciate on B3.

ISA Energia has advanced in a model of building electrical transmission lines different from the usual approach. Instead of aerial towers, the company is implementing an underground transmission line system in the Brazilian market. In this sense, the company, which holds 30% of Brazil's transmission system and 95% in São Paulo, has just completed an investment cycle of R$ 1.1 billion in the largest underground transmission line project in Brazil. The system, a kind of "underground ring road," is 44.6 kilometers long and connects the Cambuci neighborhood in the city of São Paulo to the municipality of São Caetano do Sul, in the Greater ABC region.

Unlike the traditional model, in which large towers are installed in extensive areas requiring significant land, underground systems are used in denser areas with limited available land, such as metropolitan regions. "The model works when it is necessary to overcome major obstacles or when we have urban occupation problems. In the capital of São Paulo, today it is very difficult to imagine a transmission line cutting through the city. That's why we use underground lines with modern and safe cables," says Rui Chammas, CEO of ISA Energia Brasil, in an interview with NeoFeed.

The Rio Grande project, delivered at the end of March this year, increases energy supply security for approximately two million residents of the São Paulo Metropolitan Region. In addition to the underground transmission line, the system also includes nine kilometers of aerial lines. Although it represents a slightly higher cost than aerial operation, the executive states that in some situations, it is the only viable solution for advancing electrical energy. The auction for this line took place in January 2020, with a discount of 57.9%.

The underground system still represents a small portion of ISA Energia's operation in Brazil. But the trend is toward growth, even though it may represent a slightly higher cost than the traditional tower. Today, the company's electrical system comprises more than 23 thousand kilometers of aerial circuits, 153 kilometers of underground transmission, and 26 kilometers in the underwater model.

"It is a long underground line that arose from the project of bringing greater robustness to the metropolitan region. Part of these lines are in radial circuits, and the project allows closing these rings, ensuring more safety and serving greater load. Ultimately, all these lines connect," explains Chammas.

Underground Operations

In practice, the system began operating in October, five months ahead of the deadline set by the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel), but was not yet at full capacity—full use depended on a distributor connecting to its system, which occurred in early this year. The project involves annual revenue of R$ 93.1 million for the company, considering the tariff cycle 2025/2026.

In addition to the underground line, ISA Energia also delivered a new substation with 800 megavolt-ampere (MVA) capacity in São Caetano do Sul, plus the expansion of two existing ones in the state capital. According to the CEO, the need to implement this model is also directly related to growing energy demand from economic growth in the region, the advancement of electromobility needs from electric vehicles, and the implementation of data centers.

Beyond the underground advancement, the company has other investment plans for the coming years. By 2030, the company is working with a pipeline of R$ 12.3 billion in contributions. Of this total, R$ 6 billion is for four greenfield projects from concessions won in auctions, plus R$ 6.3 billion for renovation of already installed facilities.

The four projects under construction—Piraquê (Minas Gerais and Espírito Santo), Jacarandá (São Paulo), Serra Dourada (Bahia and Minas Gerais), and Itatiaia (Minas Gerais and Rio de Janeiro)—involve expansion of aerial transmission lines and generate annual revenue of R$ 826 million as they come online.

According to Chammas, with the increased transition to a more sustainable energy model in Brazil in recent years, through renewable source auctions, particularly in connections between the Northeast and the South-Center, there has been a need for more investments to meet this increase in national demand. This explains the billion-dollar investment in improvements.

Last year alone, the company invested R$ 5.1 billion to improve the safety of the Interconnected National System (SIN), managed by the National Electric System Operator (ONS). Of this volume, R$ 1.7 billion went to improvement projects in São Paulo state.

Between 2013 and 2026, the company's portfolio more than doubled, increasing from 15 to 34 concessions in Brazil. This does not mean, however, according to the CEO, that ISA Energia's focus is exclusively on quantity. "Many people think that winning an auction is what makes the company grow. That's not it. What makes the company grow is studying the lot very carefully, winning it, and competitively managing to deliver the project," he explains.

The company, which did not participate in the last transmission auction held in March, featuring more than 38 thousand kilometers of transmission lines planned for Brazil by 2034, sees three rules to justify its participation in these competitions: that projects be profitable, that financial discipline be respected, and that they do not affect dividend payments to shareholders.

"We need to be very careful with the company's leverage in the coming years. With the R$ 12.3 billion volume by 2030, our leverage should approach 4 times, and we don't want to exceed that mark," he says.

At the end of 2025, ISA Energia's net debt to Ebitda ratio reached 3.63 times.

With this growth perspective, Bank of America (BofA) reported, in a report published on Monday, April 13, an upgrade in the company's stock rating, moving from sell to buy. The analysts projected an increase in the 12-month price target, rising from R$ 26 to R$ 35.

For him, what matters is that the model can be profitable for the company. In this sense, for the ISA executive, there is no concern about 2026 being an election year.
