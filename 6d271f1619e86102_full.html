# ISA Energia's "Underground Ring Road" Reshaping Brazil's Transmission Line Market

ISA Energia Brasil, the leader in energy transmission in the country, is expanding its underground transmission line model with the completion of a R$ 1.1 billion project connecting São Paulo to São Caetano do Sul. This "underground ring road" spanning 44.6 kilometers aims to serve densely populated urban areas, increasing power supply security for two million residents.

The company, which holds 30% of Brazil's transmission system and 95% in São Paulo, has just completed this investment cycle in Brazil's largest underground transmission line project. Unlike the traditional model, in which large towers are installed in extensive areas requiring significant land, the underground system is used in denser areas with limited available land, such as metropolitan regions.

"The model is used when it's necessary to overcome important obstacles or when we have urban occupation problems. In the state capital today, it's very difficult to imagine a transmission line cutting through the city. That's why we use underground lines with modern and safe cables," says Rui Chammas, CEO of ISA Energia Brasil.

The Rio Grande project, delivered at the end of March this year, enhances power supply security for approximately two million residents of the São Paulo Metropolitan Region. In addition to the underground transmission line, the system also includes nine kilometers of overhead lines. Although it represents a slightly higher cost than overhead operation, the executive states that in certain situations, it is the only viable solution for advancing electrical energy infrastructure.

The underground system still represents a small portion of ISA Energia's operations in Brazil. However, the trend is toward growth, even though it may represent a slightly higher cost than traditional towers. Today, the company's electrical system comprises more than 23,000 kilometers of aerial circuits, 153 kilometers of underground transmission, and 26 kilometers of underwater systems.

"It's a long underground line that emerges from the project of bringing greater robustness to the metropolitan region. Part of these lines are in radial circuits, and the project allows closing these rings, ensuring greater security and meeting higher loads. Ultimately, all these lines connect," explains Chammas.

In practice, the system began operating in October, five months ahead of the deadline set by the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel), but was not yet at full capacity—full utilization depended on a distributor's connection to the system, which occurred at the beginning of this year. The project involves annual revenue of R$ 93.1 million for the company, considering the 2025/2026 tariff cycle.

In addition to the underground line, ISA Energia also delivered a new substation with 800 megavolt-ampere (MVA) capacity in São Caetano do Sul, plus expansion of two existing substations in the state capital.

According to the CEO, the need to implement this model is also directly related to growing energy demand from economic growth in the region, the advancement of electromobility needs through electric vehicles, and the implementation of data centers.

Beyond underground expansion, the company has other investment plans for the coming years. Until 2030, the company is working with a pipeline of R$ 12.3 billion in investments. Of this total, R$ 6 billion are earmarked for four greenfield projects from concessions won in auctions, and another R$ 6.3 billion for renovation of already-installed facilities.

The four projects under construction—Piraquê (Minas Gerais and Espírito Santo), Jacarandá (São Paulo), Serra Dourada (Bahia and Minas Gerais), and Itatiaia (Minas Gerais and Rio de Janeiro)—involve expansion of overhead transmission lines and total annual revenue of R$ 826 million as they enter operation.

According to Chammas, with the increase in Brazil's energy transition toward a more sustainable model in recent years through renewable energy auctions, particularly in connections between the Northeast and Central-South regions, there has been a need for more investments to meet this increase in national demand. This explains the billion-dollar investment increases.

Last year alone, the company achieved R$ 5.1 billion in investments to improve the safety of the Integrated National System (SIN), managed by the National Electric System Operator (ONS). Of this volume, R$ 1.7 billion went to improvement projects in São Paulo state.

Between 2013 and 2026, the company's portfolio more than doubled, growing from 15 to 34 concessions in Brazil. This does not mean, however, according to the CEO, that ISA Energia's focus is exclusively on quantity.

"Many people think that winning an auction means the company grows. But that's not it. What makes the company grow is studying the lot very carefully, winning it, and competitively managing to deliver the project," he explains.

The company, which did not participate in the latest transmission auction held in March with more than 38,000 kilometers of transmission lines planned for Brazil through 2034, sees three rules to justify its presence in such contests: that projects be profitable, that financial discipline be respected, and that they do not affect shareholder dividend payments.

"We need to be very careful about the company's leverage in the coming years. With the R$ 12.3 billion volume through 2030, our leverage should approach 4 times, and we don't want to exceed that level," he says.

At the end of 2025, ISA Energia's net debt versus Ebitda ratio reached 3.63 times. With this growth perspective, Bank of America (BofA) reported in a note published on Monday, April 13, an upgrade in the company's stock rating from sell to buy. Analysts also projected an increase in the 12-month price target, moving from R$ 26 to R$ 35.
