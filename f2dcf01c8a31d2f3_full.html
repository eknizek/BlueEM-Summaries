# Peruvian Jockey Club Returns to Growth After Decade Decline; Targets 6%+ Expansion in 2026

The Jockey Club of Peru, which is celebrating its 80th anniversary this year, generated annual revenues exceeding S/ 100 million in 2025, with a structure in which horse racing activity accounts for approximately 60% of the business, while space rentals and surface rights contribute around 24% and social activity, 16%.

"All three business units have grown. There has been an increase close to 5% and we expect to close 2026 with growth exceeding 6%," says Danilo Chávez Abad, president of the Jockey Club of Peru.

## Recovery of Betting

The horse racing business shows signs of recovery after a prolonged contraction period, in line with a global trend associated with changes in consumption habits and increased competition from digital alternatives.

"The business was declining not only in Peru, but worldwide. But for a couple of years now we have been implementing communication strategies and we are seeing a slight recovery," explains Chávez.

In 2025, betting grew close to 4%, marking the first advance after ten years of decline. This trend is accelerating in 2026.

"In the first quarter, growth is close to 8% versus the same period in 2025," he adds.

The rebound responds to a combined strategy. "There is no single action that explains the recovery. It is the sum of opening agencies—we have more than 110 nationwide—improvements in the digital platform and reaching audiences we didn't reach before," he points out.

The digital channel, which today represents between 12% and 13% of the business, is gaining weight in that strategy.

"It has expanded the market because there are people who can now bet without going to the racetrack or agencies," he indicates.

The goal is to increase this participation to 20% over the next five years, supported by the launch of an application within the next 60 days.

## The Latin American Grand Prize as a Showcase and Corporate Events

In parallel, the Jockey Club is strengthening its positioning through events. The Grand Latin American Prize is one of the main horse racing competitions in the region that brings together delegations from different countries.

"It is equivalent to the Copa America," says Chávez.

This is consolidating as the main highlight of the calendar, with expectations of more than 25,000 attendees and strong impact on betting volume.

"Normally on a Sunday more than half a million soles are wagered and on that day we exceed S/ 800,000. For this year we expect to be close to a million," says Chávez.

The free event will take place on April 26.

However, the event is not seeking direct profitability. "The revenues cover the costs and it ends up being a breakeven point. It is not an event that generates profitability, but an investment in positioning," he states.

Beyond short-term income impact, the Latin American event allows the club to expand its reach and attract international visitors.

"A lot of people from abroad are coming, not just owners, but fans," he notes.

Additionally, space rentals are consolidating as one of the main business drivers. Following the restriction on holding concerts, in effect since February 2023 due to security, congestion and noise concerns, the club is reorienting its strategy toward the corporate segment.

The venue has more than 15 locations and hosts large-scale events, with attendance between 5,000 and 7,000 people.

"We have quite significant occupancy and it is growing every time," he adds.

This is complemented by hosting fairs such as Casacor and other cultural and social events, which reinforces the positioning of the racetrack as a multifunctional space.
