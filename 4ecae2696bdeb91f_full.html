# Bodytech bets on silver economy growth with founder Gigliola Aycardi

In the latest episode of Business Confessions, Portafolio's podcast dedicated to showcasing the stories of leaders building the country, Bodytech co-founder Gigliola Aycardi shared her vision for the future.

When projecting ahead a decade, the entrepreneur acknowledged that her life will take a turn toward the personal, after years dedicated to the company's growth. "In 10 years I'll be 66 years old, my goodness. I see myself living a life much more focused on family, on enjoying that time a bit more, time that for so many years has been so concentrated on the company," she noted.

However, she made clear that her connection to the business will not disappear, but will evolve toward a more strategic role: "Without disconnecting, but in a different role, more from the vision, from accompaniment, from sharing what I've learned."

In this episode, the entrepreneur expressed her optimism about the momentum entrepreneurship has gained in Colombia, while reflecting on her leadership style, personal growth, and the path she has traveled in building a recognized brand in the country, alongside her partner Nicolás Loaiza.

Regarding the company's future, Aycardi anticipated a transformation aligned with global wellness trends. According to her explanation, Bodytech will focus its growth on longevity, anti-aging, and the so-called "silver economy," a segment that is becoming increasingly relevant given the aging population. "People live more years and we need to live them well, with quality of life. So, we're going to serve a population that is aging, but that wants to stay active, independent, and healthy," she affirmed.

For the executive, this shift represents not only a business opportunity but also a structural impact on how exercise is understood. In her vision, physical activity should be consolidated as a key component of the health system. "My dream is that exercise is truly understood as part of the health system, as prevention. That the national government understands that the more you invest in preventive health, the less it costs the system in the long run," she explained.

Aycardi supported this position with evidence about the savings that physical activity generates in health systems. According to international studies, a person who exercises regularly can cost the health system around $2,000 less, reinforcing the need to promote active lifestyles through public policy and business strategies.

In this context, the co-founder of the gym chain reiterated that the challenge will be increasingly integrating the concept of wellness with that of health, expanding its impact on users' lives. "So I see myself in that process: accompanying Bodytech in that evolution, seeing how it adapts to these new needs and how we continue transforming lives through exercise, but increasingly hand in hand with health," she concluded.

Aycardi's vision reflects a strategic shift in the fitness industry, where prevention, quality of life, and active aging are emerging as central pillars of growth in the coming years. This strategic approach is also reflected in the evolution of Bodytech's business model, which has been migrating from being a traditional gym chain to a comprehensive wellness platform. In recent years, the company has strengthened complementary services such as sports medicine, physiotherapy, nutrition, and personalized programs, in line with a global trend seeking to integrate physical care with preventive health.

Adding to this is a phase of expansion that evidences confidence in the sector's growth. The group has announced investments close to 16 billion pesos for new locations in cities like Bogotá and Medellín, with spaces designed not only for training but to offer complete experiences around wellness.

The environment also plays in favor. Interest in physical activity in Colombia has been steadily increasing in recent years, consolidating exercise as a key component of household lifestyles.
