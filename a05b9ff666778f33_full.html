# Excess Information Redefines Purchasing Decisions of First-Time Parents in Childcare in Colombia

58% of parents recognize difficulties in trusting available information, which impacts their decisions.

Information overload in childcare is impacting the purchasing decisions of first-time parents in Colombia, to the point that seven out of every ten mothers report feeling overwhelmed when choosing how to care for their babies.

This is revealed in a report published by Bayer, which warns that this phenomenon is redefining consumption patterns and increasing the relevance of medical support and scientific evidence in an increasingly broad and competitive market. In this context, the document notes that modern motherhood develops in an environment saturated with recommendations, digital content, and commercial offers, which makes every decision a complex process. The arrival of a baby, traditionally associated with progressive learning, now involves navigating multiple sources of information that directly influence the choice of products and childcare routines.

The figures demonstrate the magnitude of the phenomenon. 68% of mothers report exhaustion during early parenting, while 58% of parents state that there is so much information available that it is difficult to identify reliable sources. This scenario not only impacts emotional well-being but also affects how households make decisions related to childcare.

The report warns that this information pressure translates into greater demands regarding products available on the market. Rather than decisions based solely on price or recommendation, parents are prioritizing attributes such as safety, transparency, and scientific backing. This change reflects an evolution in consumer behavior, which now seeks certainty amid uncertainty.

In this line, the role of specialists and medical organizations becomes increasingly relevant. Information validated by experts becomes a differentiating factor that allows reducing the margin of doubt in an environment where the oversupply of products and messages can be overwhelming. Trust, in this sense, is positioned as a central asset in the childcare market.

## Scientific Evidence Gains Prominence

The emphasis on scientific validation also responds to technical conditions specific to newborn care. According to pediatric dermatology research cited in the document, babies' skin can be up to 30% thinner than adults' skin, because their skin barrier is still in the process of maturation during the first months of life. This characteristic increases the need to use products formulated specifically for this stage.

According to Dr. Diana Jiménez, a pediatrics specialist, "during the first months of life, a baby's skin is especially sensitive and requires products that are formulated with their needs in mind."

The expert adds that scientific backing and medical recommendations offer greater peace of mind to parents when making choices. In Colombia, entities such as the Colombian Society of Pediatrics play a key role in this process. Its endorsement has become established as a reference for consumers, certifying products that meet quality and safety standards. This type of backing not only guides the purchasing decision but also contributes to raising the level of demand in the industry.

Amid this transformation, companies in the sector are adjusting their strategies to respond to new consumer demands. The document highlights that medical certifications and expert endorsements have become key tools for strengthening trust and differentiating themselves in a highly competitive market. An example of this trend is the case of Acid Mantle Baby, which received the endorsement of the Colombian Society of Pediatrics. According to the statement, it is the only three-in-one cream in its category in the country that has this backing, which validates its safety and quality standards in baby skin care.

Beyond a technical recognition, this type of certification responds to a growing need among parents to have clear references in an environment saturated with options. Consumer trust, according to the document, is increasingly associated with transparency, scientific evidence, and the backing of health experts.

Thus, the childcare market in Colombia is moving toward a model in which validated information and medical support become pillars of decision-making. In a scenario where motherhood faces multiple pressures, these elements allow reducing uncertainty and facilitating safer choices, in line with new consumer dynamics.
