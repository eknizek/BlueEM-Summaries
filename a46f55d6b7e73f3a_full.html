# Payment problems in family credit portfolios rise again: exceeds 11%

The Central Bank reported that in February the irregularity ratio of credit to the private sector stood at 6.7%, which represented an increase of 0.3 percentage points compared to January. This is a trend that has escalated strongly throughout 2025.

When broken down by type of debtor, according to the Banking Report published by the monetary authority, the indicator corresponding to households reached 11.2%, while that of companies stood at 2.9%. In this way, the percentage of credits with payment problems remains at the highest levels in two decades, a mark that had already been broken at the end of last year.

The level of sustained interest rates, well above inflation, seems to explain why default rates are not falling. According to an analysis by consulting firm 1816, the observed dynamics do not respond to a particular situation of any entity based on its credit policy, but rather constitute a "macro phenomenon." In that sense, they noted that in 28 of the 30 banks included in the sample, an increase was registered in the volume of loans with payment delays.

The most recent information collected corresponds to February. In its report, the consulting firm indicated that one of the keys to understanding the persistence of delinquency at elevated levels is linked to the financial costs facing households. According to their measurement, personal credit rates still do not show a significant decline. "They remain very high in real terms for various reasons, including precisely the high delinquency, but also how difficult it is to foresee the Government's rate policy in the current monetary scheme," they stated.

In numerical terms, bank personal credits maintained an annual nominal rate (TNA) close to 70%. When incorporating other charges, such as administrative fees and taxes, the total financial cost (CFT) would be around 100% annually.

For its part, EcoGo argued in another study that the percentage of default in households quadrupled in a year and a half: it was 2.5% in October 2024. For families, payment irregularity is the highest since 2004, they estimated.
