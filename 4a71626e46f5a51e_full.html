# Sogem May Attract Investors to Finance R$500 Million Legal Battle Against Totvs

Sogem may finance its legal dispute with Totvs, valued at R$500 million, with support from national and international funds. The model is being developed by the forensic accounting firm EvidJuri as an investment opportunity, with minimum contributions starting at US$5 million (R$25 million at current exchange rates). The litigation has already lasted 17 years.

"We are not looking for anyone. We were the ones being sought out, and investors are interested because of Brazil's Selic rate. The market is coming after this here in Brazil because outside the country this financing model is extremely common," says Sthefano Cruvinel, CEO of EvidJuri. He claims there are seven national funds and three other international funds interested.

The case, initiated in 2009, originates from a commercial relationship in which Totvs purchased RM Sistemas. The sale occurred in 2007 for R$206 million. Sogem was RM Sistemas' representative in Campinas and is seeking compensation for contract termination and financial absorption of revenues from Totvs customers in the region's municipalities. The allegation is that the multinational blocked sales transfers, unilaterally changed the business model, and centralized the client portfolio to financially strangle its Campinas representative.

The initial value of the lawsuit was R$69 million. It has reached R$500 million, according to Sogem's calculations, due to interest, monetary correction, and charges. In a statement sent to B3, Totvs says the value is based on "unilateral premises of the plaintiff," which also includes moral damages claims "disconnected from reality, even when compared to similar cases pending in the Judiciary." The multinational also states that EvidJuri is not part of the process, being a service provider contracted by Sogem.

For Totvs, the risk is remote for "almost the totality of the plaintiff's claims." With EvidJuri, Sogem structures three investment entry modalities. Through equity credit, the investor can acquire up to 40% of the asset; through preferred credit; and through a fixed return of 15% per year with proportional participation in any additional gains from the eventual final judicial decision.

"It is a defendant with an ultra-liquid profile," adds Cruvinel, referring to Totvs. Present in 41 countries, the multinational closed 2024 with net revenue of R$5.4 billion. There is still no first-instance judgment, and Cruvinel alleges the reason was Totvs's resistance to conducting an expert evaluation to establish the items of an indemnity nature. On February 9, the Superior Court of Justice (STJ) ordered the expert evaluation to proceed.
