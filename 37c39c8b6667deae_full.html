# Illegal Mining Mobilized Around US$12 Billion in 2025, Ten Times More Than Drug Trafficking

Between 2024 and 2026, S/7.195 billion in materials have been seized from illegal mining activities in the country, according to Rodolfo García, High Commissioner for the Fight Against Illegal Mining, a commission belonging to the Presidency of the Council of Ministers (PCM). He added that in two years, the number of interdictions carried out against this problem increased by more than 100%.

As he stated in his presentation at the 2026 International Congress of Prospectors and Explorers (proExplo 2026), organized by the Institute of Mining Engineers (IIMP), the fight against illegal mining requires not only interdiction efforts but also a comprehensive approach to the problem.

Thus, he recalled that a national strategy was approved to address this issue, starting with a diagnosis at the political, economic, and social levels, among others. In that sense, García pointed out that illegal mining mobilized almost US$7 billion in 2024 and in 2025 just over US$12 billion, a figure 10 times greater than illicit drug trafficking.

"This illegal mining problem is not only an environmental and social problem, but an economic threat to the country. Only in 2025, more than 11.500 billion were mobilized in our country. This is close to 2.7% of GDP," added Paolo Andrés Alzamora, acting executive president of the Geological, Mining and Metallurgical Institute (Ingemmet), in the roundtable "Challenges of Illegal Mining."

According to the High Commissioner for the Fight Against Illegal Mining, the seizure of related materials in the last two years reached S/7.195 billion. In turn, Carlos Gallardo, general manager of the Peruvian Institute of Economics (IPE), pointed out in his presentation that 23 mining projects were identified that were delayed due to causes beyond the companies' decisions, including illegal mining. This amounts to at least US$12 billion, according to the IPE. Had these projects been executed on schedule, GDP would have grown 12% more than what was recorded by 2022, he said.

Furthermore, due to these delays, 1.7 million people would not have escaped poverty by that same year.

For the fight against illegal mining, García considered that the State needs to shift from being reactive to being proactive, with greater investment in development, alternative economies, control, and enforcement toward 2030.

In that sense, he indicated that under the national strategy to reduce illegal mining, interdiction work, consolidation and presence, and maintenance of the State's presence must be carried out in areas where there is illegal activity. This requires multi-sector control posts, police presence or dedicated personnel in those jurisdictions.

For his part, Gallardo argued that formalization, traceability, interdiction and intelligence, alternative development, coordination between institutions, cross-border cooperation, and state presence must be pursued.

Similarly, Iván Arenas, director of Mining Accounts at Arenas Communications and Sustainability Consultants, considered in his presentation that economic corridors with illegal mining presence should be targeted. He added that this problem would continue in the future due to a possible increase in gold prices.

"To the extent that geopolitical conditions are marked by greater uncertainties, we will see gold rise to approximately US$8,000 around 2030-2031," he commented.
