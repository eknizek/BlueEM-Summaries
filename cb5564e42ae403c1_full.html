# Projects like La Granja and Michiquillay could sustain Peru's copper expansion over the medium to long term

Large-scale projects such as La Granja and Michiquillay emerge as the main options for achieving Peru's copper expansion over the next 10 to 15 years, in a context of growing global demand for this metal, which is key to the energy transition.

Adán Pino, president of proEXPLO 2026, stated that the country's current portfolio of 67 mining projects, valued at more than US$ 64 billion, is solid and includes initiatives capable of increasing production in the short term (Ccorocohuayco, Tia María, Zafranal) and, more robustly, in the medium to long term (La Granja, Michiquillay).

Among the most relevant projects are La Granja, which has more than 20 million tonnes of contained fine copper, and Michiquillay, still under evaluation, both considered world-class. These are joined by other smaller-scale but equally important projects, such as Haquira, Cañariaco, Los Chankas, Trapiche, and Cotabambas, among others.

"These are projects that can have an important impact on the growth of national production," Pino said in an interview with the Institute of Mining Engineers of Peru, emphasizing that Peru maintains high geological potential to continue being a relevant player in the global copper market.

However, the executive warned that the main challenge is not geological potential or resource availability, but rather the capacity to execute these projects and make them economically viable.

"All we need to do is ensure that these projects have appropriate and competitive conditions in terms of permits, infrastructure, and tax regime, so that mining companies can conduct their technical-economic evaluations swiftly. This way, Peruvian projects can become attractive options within their portfolios and facilitate investment decisions in the country," he noted.

He also emphasized that factors such as bureaucracy and permit processing times remain determining factors for attracting investment and maintaining competitiveness against other regional countries.

Pino also distinguished between the impact of current projects and the need to think long-term. He explained that while the existing portfolio can sustain growth in the coming years, the future of production will depend on new discoveries.

"A copper project can take between 15 and 20 years from discovery to becoming a mining operation. That's why it's crucial to invest resources in early-stage exploration (greenfield) to continue feeding the portfolio with new projects that increase the probability of discovering new mineral resources for the country," he stated.

In this regard, he warned that the decreased investment in early-stage exploration over the past 5 years (according to S&P Global data) could affect the availability of new resources in the long term, in a context where sustained growth in global copper demand is projected through 2050, led by the energy transition and artificial intelligence.
