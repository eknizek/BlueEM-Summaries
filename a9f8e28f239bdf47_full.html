# Central Bank Vice President Assures Investors of $8 Billion in Net Reserve Additions This Year

The Central Bank has accumulated foreign exchange market purchases exceeding $6.150 billion since January. This is the highest pace on record, surpassing all previous administrations in the same period, according to data from the BCRA itself presented this week to investors in Washington. In the first quarter alone, international net reserves increased by $1.500 billion. The agreement with the IMF projects another $3.500 billion for the second quarter and a total of $8.000 billion for all of 2026. The Central Bank is confident in achieving that net reserves target this year, in a context where the supply of dollars remains high and economic demand is depressed.

Central Bank Vice President Vladimir Werning presented "Argentina: Economic Program Phase 4. A More Encouraging Outlook for Both External and Internal Balance, Despite Global Uncertainty" during a series of meetings with investors in Washington D.C., from April 13-18. The official's thesis is that amid global shocks, the stability of Argentina's financial market stands out compared to other emerging peers. To support this argument, Werning assured that there are "cyclical" factors that help better resilience of the economy against possible external shocks. One of these is that dollar demand, which skyrocketed to historical levels last year ahead of elections, fell sharply after the vote. Since these purchases remain within the local financial system, resulting in an increase in dollar deposits, this does not affect reserves.

At the same time, Werning highlighted that after electoral volatility, corporate investors abandoned their "wait and see" stance and have begun unwinding their foreign exchange hedging positions to seek dollar financing that supports their activities. Among the "policy factors" explaining this strong financial performance, Santiago Bausili's deputy emphasized that the Central Bank has met the expectations it set for this year regarding foreign currency purchases and managed in these first four months to purchase more than 60% of the target it had set. In this way, the Central Bank's vice president assured they would add $8.000 billion in net reserves this year.

All of this occurred in a context of "less capital controls": Werning stressed that recently the BCRA lifted restrictions on dividend transfers by companies and that the CNV relaxed caps on capital market investments. Werning provided more details on the Government's financial strategy, which aims for foreign direct investment flows and repatriation of resident capital/internal savings to have priority over Argentina's financing in the debt market, to maintain fiscal balance. In this regard, he highlighted that both capital arriving through the major investment scheme, RIGI, and domestic dollar repatriation initiatives, such as the Tax Amnesty Law, will have a positive impact on the level of dollar deposits and credit. The stock of dollars in banks reached $38.000 billion in April and maintains the record since the end of convertibility.

"The Treasury has already implemented an unprecedented effort of 11% of GDP to restore solvency and credibility (5% fiscal adjustment + 2.5% tax cuts + 3.4% net debt payments)," Werning highlighted, while also noting the fall in sovereign debt spreads since the launch of the stabilization program.
