End of 6×1 work shift will be 'meteor' worse than Covid-19, shopping center association warns

08 April 2026 · 04:56 PM

# End of 6×1 work shift will be "meteor" worse than Covid-19, shopping center association warns

A study conducted by the Brazilian Shopping Centers Association (Abrasce) and revealed exclusively by NeoFeed shows that in the scenario of reducing from 44 to 40 weekly hours, the sector will lose approximately 130,000 jobs in the first year alone. This represents 12.2% fewer positions.

The sector, which currently has 658 shopping centers and 1.1 million direct jobs, could face a revenue drop of R$ 15 billion in 2025, representing a 7.3% reduction. Losses could extend for five years, with an increase in informality and store closures, especially among small retailers.

Entrepreneur Eduardo Peres, CEO of Multiplan, has expressed concern about the impact of reducing the 6×1 work schedule on shopping centers, but now this is more than just perception. The real prospect is that this change will be, in practice, a "meteor" larger than the Covid-19 pandemic.

Today, Brazil has 658 shopping centers in 253 cities. There are 125,000 retailers generating 1.1 million direct jobs, plus 3.3 million indirect jobs. The sector's revenue in 2025 reached R$ 200.9 billion.

"During the pandemic, a meteor fell on the shopping sector, such was the damage it caused. And that meteor could return, because our sector should be the most affected by this change," says Glauco Humai, president of Abrasce, in an interview with NeoFeed.

Using 2025 as a baseline, the shopping sector should lose approximately R$ 15 billion in revenue in year 1 of implementing the reduced work hours, representing a 7.3% drop. The issue is that, unlike the first year of the pandemic in 2020, when the sector had a 33% drop but recovered the following year, in this case losses should last at least five years, according to Abrasce.

In terms of workforce, the reduction in positions will remain high over the years. In year 3 after implementing the new schedule, the sector will lose 5% of jobs. In year 5, there will be 2.3% fewer positions.

According to Humai, the greatest impact will be on small retailers with up to four employees, who account for 60% of shops in Brazilian shopping centers. Beyond the large volume of layoffs, the change should increase the informality rate in the labor market. Brazil currently has 16,000 kiosks in shopping centers, which, according to the executive, should be the first to be affected.

"Many of them could disappear," he says. "The small ones are the largest occupants and cannot absorb this increase in costs. The margins of these retailers are already small because they compete directly with e-commerce. They will work at a loss or just to pay bills. It's a complete and bad equation for everyone," says Humai.

In practice, the change will also generate an increase in the pace of store closures. The current vacancy rate of about 5% should rise to up to 8%, also in the first year.

For the Abrasce president, there is a lack of understanding in Brasília that the moment is not appropriate for this type of discussion and, especially, immediate implementation. In Humai's assessment, at minimum the government should begin dialogue with a proposal for phased implementation, starting with larger chains and preserving smaller shops.

"This discussion makes no sense at this moment in the country. Some segments cannot absorb this cost, and the government needs to think about ways to compensate for this and reduce taxes," he states. "It's not that simple. We're not talking about a 1960s factory that you can automate and replace functions."

For the Abrasce president, the scenario is different from what happens at year-end, when retailers hire temporary employees to meet increased customer flow. "It would be an argument if sales in the other 11 months were the same as the Christmas period, if we could make revenue in other months also double, perhaps it would be possible to absorb. But that's not the reality," he says.

Now, the association president intends to intensify dialogue with the federal government to detail how much the sector will be harmed by these measures if implemented immediately, should the constitutional amendment proposal (PEC) be approved by May as planned.

"There's no understanding how this discussion happens just when we're near the beginning of the election period. It will be chaos for the economy and much worse for shopping centers."
