End of 6×1 Work Schedule Will Be 'Meteor' Worse Than Covid-19, Shopping Association Warns

08 April 2026 · 04:56 PM

# End of 6×1 Work Schedule Will Be 'Meteor' Worse Than Covid-19, Shopping Association Warns

The concern of businessman Eduardo Peres, CEO of Multiplan, about the impact of reducing the 6×1 work schedule in the shopping center sector is no longer just a perception. The real prospect is that this change will, in practice, be a "meteor" bigger than the Covid-19 pandemic.

A study conducted by the Brazilian Shopping Centers Association (Abrasce) and revealed exclusively by NeoFeed shows that, in the scenario of reducing working hours from 44 to 40 hours per week, the sector will lose, in the first year alone, approximately 130,000 jobs. That represents 12.2% fewer positions.

Today, Brazil has 658 shopping centers in 253 cities. There are 125,000 retailers generating 1.1 million direct jobs, plus 3.3 million indirectly. The sector's revenue in 2025 reached R$ 200.9 billion.

"During the pandemic, a meteor fell on the shopping center sector, such was the damage it caused. And that meteor could return, because our sector should be the most affected by this change," says Glauco Humai, president of Abrasce, in an interview with NeoFeed.

Based on the 2025 scenario, the shopping center sector should lose, in year 1 of implementing the reduced working hours, approximately R$ 15 billion in revenue, representing a 7.3% decline. The question is that, unlike the first year of the pandemic in 2020, when the sector had a 33% drop but recovered the following year, in this case losses are expected to last at least five years, according to Abrasce.

In terms of labor, the reduction in positions will remain high over the years. In year 3 after implementing the new schedule, according to the study, the sector will lose 5% of jobs. By year 5, there will be 2.3% fewer positions.

According to Humai, the greatest impact will be on small retailers with up to four employees, who make up 60% of the volume of stores in Brazilian shopping centers. Beyond the large volume of layoffs, the change should increase the informality index in the labor market. Brazil currently has 16,000 kiosks spread throughout shopping centers, which, according to the executive, should be the first to be affected.

"Many of them may disappear," he says. "The small businesses are the largest employers and cannot absorb this increase in costs. The margins of these retailers are already small because they compete directly with e-commerce. They'll work at a loss or just to pay bills. It's a complete and bad equation for everyone," says Humai.

In practice, the change will also generate an increase in the pace of store closures. The current vacancy rate of about 5% should rise to 8%, also in the first year.

For the Abrasce president, there is a lack of understanding in Brasília that the moment is not appropriate for this type of discussion, and especially not for immediate implementation. In Humai's assessment, at minimum the government should begin dialogue with a proposal for phased implementation, starting with larger chains and preserving smaller stores.

"This discussion makes no sense at this moment in the country. Some segments cannot absorb this cost, and the government needs to think about ways to compensate for this and reduce taxes," he states. "It's not so simple a discussion. We're not talking about a 1960s factory that you can automate and replace functions."

For the Abrasce president, the scenario is different from what happens at year-end, when retailers hire temporary employees to handle increased customer traffic. "It would be an argument if sales during the other 11 months were the same as the Christmas period, if we could make the revenue in other months also double, maybe it would be possible to absorb. But that's not the reality," he says.

Now, the association president intends to intensify dialogue with the federal government to detail how much the segment will be hurt by these measures if they come immediately, should the Constitutional Amendment Proposal (PEC) be approved by May, as planned.

"It's impossible to understand this discussion precisely when we're close to the start of the electoral period. It will be chaos for the economy and even more so for shopping centers."
