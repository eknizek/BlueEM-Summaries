# Global Economy at Critical Inflection Point Over Strait of Hormuz Oil Disruption

The global economy faces a critical moment, dependent on the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to avoid an energy crisis. Alex Fusté, chief economist at Andbank, warns that a prolonged blockade could lead to stagflation and a correction in financial markets, affecting even Brazil's monetary policy. He states that the current scenario can only be sustained for one or two more months, after which the world could enter recession. The energy shock is considered the most dangerous, potentially pushing oil prices to $200 and causing significant market declines. Fusté also highlights that the new Republican generation in the US creates uncertainty, and that Brazil, despite being an oil producer, will not be able to avoid the impacts of rising global prices. Monetary policy must adjust, with the possibility of interest rate increases to control inflation. China, despite buying oil from Russia, is also not immune to the energy shock.

The global economy is at a critical inflection point, dependent more on the complete reopening of the Strait of Hormuz for the resumption of oil flow than on the resolution of the conflict with Iran. A positive outcome to the strait impasse could eliminate the energy risk premium and boost markets. A prolonged blockade, however, could trigger a severe energy crisis, stagflation, and a deep correction in financial markets, with impacts even on Brazil's Central Bank monetary policy.

This is why it is important that the 15-day ceasefire between the United States and Iran, in effect from Wednesday, April 8, results in the complete opening of the Strait of Hormuz and the normalization of global oil transport flow.

The alert comes from Spaniard Alex Fusté, chief economist at Andbank, a financial group specializing in private banking, in this interview. Based in Andorra and present in 11 countries, Andbank—founded 95 years ago—manages the equivalent of R$360 billion worldwide.

"The current scenario can only be sustained for one or two more months using mobile and strategic oil reserves, which are already nearly depleted," says Fusté. "Beyond that limit, the world would enter recession with high inflation, characterizing global stagflation."

According to him, the duration of the shock is the central factor, as each additional day exponentially increases the risk of a systemic liquidity crisis. Fusté affirms that the oil shock is the most damaging to the global economy and no country—not the United States, China, nor Russia—is immune to its effects.

"There are various types of shocks, but the most dangerous of all is the energy shock, because oil is the trigger that affects everything—inflation, interest rates, liquidity, and growth," he says, predicting in the worst scenario an oil barrel price of $200 and financial market indices like the S&P falling by up to 30%.

Two other warnings from the economist stand out. First, that markets have not adequately priced in the political risk associated with the new style of governance in the US, which adds a structural layer of uncertainty. "The new Republican generation—like President Donald Trump, Vice President JD Vance, and Secretary of State Marco Rubio—differs from the traditional one and tends to generate more volatility and uncertainty, increasing the risk premium," says the economist. "The market has not understood that this new way of doing Republican politics, in a world where it seems rules no longer exist—and those who follow the rules lose," he adds.

He also does not rule out the possibility of Brazil's Central Bank reversing the current monetary policy, raising the Selic Rate again, should global instability persist in the coming months. "In the face of an energy shock, the correct monetary policy is always to raise interest rates, even if they are already close to 15%, as is the case in Brazil. Monetary policy needs to adapt to reality: I cannot maintain the same interest rates from a month ago when I now face an energy shock that did not exist before," says Fusté.

The ceasefire in the war with Iran brought relief to markets, but we still don't know what comes next. In your assessment, what will be the greatest impact of the conflict on the global economy in the medium term?

The central point for me is very simple: everything depends on the Strait of Hormuz. It is not the conflict itself that worries me, but how long the strait will remain closed. If the ceasefire really leads to the definitive reopening of the strait and alleviates the energy shock, the market breathes. If not, we enter dangerous territory. The situation is therefore binary: it can result in a very good or very bad scenario for the global economy.

What would the positive scenario look like?

If the ceasefire results in a more pragmatic Iran, without sanctions and without incentive to cause oil shocks, we—portfolio managers—could finally eliminate the energy risk premium we have carried for years, which would strongly boost risk assets.

What does this energy risk premium consist of?

It is the cost of protecting myself against market shocks. There are various types of shocks, but the most dangerous of all is the energy shock, because oil is the trigger that affects everything—inflation, interest rates, liquidity, and growth. And every two years, on average, we have some energy shock, almost always with the same protagonists: Russia or Iran. That's why, since 2010, I would need to maintain long positions in energy futures to ensure that if oil exploded, my portfolio would be protected. But this "insurance" is extremely expensive. If I had maintained this protection continuously since 2010, for example, I would have lost 70% of the portfolio's accumulated returns.

"The energy shock is the most dangerous because oil is the trigger that affects everything: inflation, interest rates, and liquidity."

And the negative scenario?

If the Iranian regime continues as it is, repressive and capable of keeping the Strait of Hormuz closed, the global financial market enters stress. With four weeks of blockade, we've already seen oil barrel prices reach $145 in the spot market. With eight more weeks, the price would go to $200. This could generate additional market declines, due to a 50% reduction in earnings per share growth and compression of valuation multiples.

So two months would be the deadline for the complete reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to avoid a global economic collapse?

The current scenario can only be sustained for one or two more months using mobile and strategic reserves, which are already nearly depleted. Beyond that limit, the world would enter recession with high inflation, characterizing global stagflation. In financial markets, front running accelerates the collapse: investors anticipate the worst and sell first, which can knock down indices like the S&P by up to 30% without needing to wait two months.

What other effects for the global economy?

All profit estimates made at the beginning of the year would no longer be valid, requiring revisions to zero or negative growth in 2026, both in Europe and possibly in the US. With smaller profits and contracting multiples, the impact on markets would be severe.
