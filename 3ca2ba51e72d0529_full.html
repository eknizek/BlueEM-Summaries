# Brazil's Response to the LNG Crisis

The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz for more than 30 days consolidates the largest energy shock since the 1973 oil crisis. This is an event with profound implications for the global market, but with particularly relevant effects for LNG. The prolonged interruption of flows from the Persian Gulf, especially from Qatar, removes approximately 12.8 million tons per year from the market for an estimated period of three to five years. In an environment already strained since 2022, this rupture redefines the global natural gas balance. LNG, which already operated with narrow safety margins, now functions under a regime of structural scarcity.

The difference compared to previous shocks lies in the low capacity for short-term response. Unlike oil, whose supply can be partially adjusted via strategic reserves or flow redirection, liquefied gas depends on a rigid supply chain, capital-intensive and with long investment cycles. The loss of relevant volumes from Qatar cannot be quickly compensated, and this amplifies pressure on prices and availability. This context helps explain the magnitude of market reaction. International LNG already registers increases between 40% and 100%, reflecting not only the physical reduction in supply but also the incorporation of a more durable risk premium. The logic of price formation ceases to be exclusively marginal and begins to reflect competition for supply security. In practical terms, whoever can pay more guarantees access to the commodity.

For Brazil, the impacts are relevant. Even though we import LNG mostly from the USA, we are exposed to global price formation. The origin of the cargo does not protect the domestic consumer from international volatility. The first effect appears in thermal dispatch, especially in plants contracted through the Capacity Reserve Auctions. The 2026 LRCAP contracted approximately 19 GW of power, with about 80% of that volume associated with natural gas plants. From a systemic perspective, the instrument is correct and necessary, as it responds to the growing need for backing in a system with greater participation of intermittent sources. But the current moment also exposes an important fragility: the growing dependence on imported LNG in a scenario of high global instability. This dependence translates directly into cost. With more expensive gas, the marginal operating cost of thermal plants rises significantly, pressuring tariffs and increasing the risk of prolonged dispatch. In parallel, Brent above $90 per barrel pressures diesel and gasoline, with direct effects on inflation, logistics, and economic activity.

Another sensitive point lies in fertilizers. Natural gas is an essential input for the production of ammonia and urea, and the increase of approximately 50% in these products already indicates a concrete risk for the next harvest. In a country that still depends on relevant imports in this segment, the escalation of prices tends to impact agricultural costs and associated production chains.

Facing this scenario, the discussion ceases to be merely cyclical and begins to demand structural responses. Diversification of supply sources gains centrality. Argentine gas, via Bolivia, emerges as a relevant alternative, offering an on-grid solution, less exposed to global market volatility and with greater cost predictability. On the domestic front, projects like Route 3 and the development of the Raia area reinforce the importance of expanding national production. The expansion of transportation and processing infrastructure is fundamental to reducing external dependence and increasing system resilience while reducing reinjection.

Nevertheless, these are solutions that require time, regulatory predictability, and investment coordination. In the power sector, the importance of complementary solutions also grows. The advancement of storage systems, especially batteries, combined with the expansion of renewable sources, can reduce the need for thermal dispatch during critical moments. The objective is not to replace gas, but to optimize its use, reserving it for situations where its flexibility is truly essential.

Even with these alternatives, the short term requires more immediate measures. The creation of a national strategic LNG reserve can be a relevant response. In this environment of recurring volatility, the ability to store volumes and smooth shocks can be decisive for system stability. Furthermore, the need to implement hedging mechanisms for gas contracts associated with LRCAP gains strength. Direct exposure to international spot prices amplifies cost volatility and complicates planning. Price protection structures can bring greater predictability for generators and consumers, reducing financial risks in adverse scenarios.

We are in the midst of a clear paradigm shift. LNG has ceased to be merely an operational solution for system flexibility and has come to occupy a central position in energy security. This transition demands a more integrated approach, combining source diversification, infrastructure expansion, and risk management instruments. The closure of Hormuz is not just an isolated event, but a clear signal of the fragilities of a highly interdependent global system. For Brazil, the main lesson lies in the need to align expansion with resilience. The use of natural gas will continue to be essential, but its incorporation must be accompanied by a robust risk mitigation strategy. Without this adjustment, the country risks transforming an efficient technical solution into a vector of economic vulnerability. The energy agenda must evolve, incorporating not only growth and expansion but also security, predictability, and capacity to respond to increasingly frequent external shocks.

*Adriano Pires is founder of the Brazilian Infrastructure Center (CBIE).*
