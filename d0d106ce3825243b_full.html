# Business Groups Seek Certainty Amid Political Volatility: Expectations and Growth Strategies for Peru's Next Government

The first day of the 2026 presidential elections is coming to an end, amid constant political volatility and an extremely uncertain global landscape. Despite this scenario, and before final results are revealed, most economic agents continue to anticipate that Peru will grow this year above 2%; a performance lower than that registered in 2025, but still driven by a greater flow of private investment and extraordinary terms of trade.

Even so, the expansion of Peru's economy continues to be far from its potential, and the reforms the country needs to grow more have been abandoned. In this context, various business leaders share with Día 1 their short and medium-term expectations for the next five years, as well as the challenges their sectors will face in the renewed local landscape.

## 1. The Central Reserve Bank (BCR) forecasts that private investment will grow 9.5% in 2026. What is required to achieve this goal?

**Jorge Zapata, President of Confiep:**

The most important condition is political and legal certainty. Without clear and stable rules of the game, investment, especially long-term investment, is postponed or leaves the country. It is therefore fundamental to avoid scenarios that generate uncertainty, such as unnecessary constitutional changes; and to ensure respect for property, contracts, and tax rules. A stable macroeconomic environment and legal predictability that sustains business confidence will generate the formal employment that Peru so badly needs. It is also essential to improve the execution of public investment. Our public procurement system must improve substantially. Additionally, we need to review the role of the General Comptroller's Office. Finally, we need to strengthen the State, appealing to meritocracy in public entities and in no way growing it unnecessarily.

**Jessica Luna, President of the Board of Directors of the National Fishing Society (SNP):**

To achieve this goal, political stability and predictability in regulations are required. Without clear and stable rules of the game, there will be no private investment. This is compounded by the urgent task of reducing red tape and bureaucracy that delays investments. In the case of the fishing sector, climate is another key factor. Environmental variability is a characteristic of the sector and can affect the development of fishing activity and investment plans. Here it is crucial that fishing management decisions are timely, quick, and always based on the best available scientific information. Finally, strengthening institutions. We need institutionality and technocracy so that officials with experience and capabilities can address the problems affecting Peruvian families, such as insecurity, and promote private investment to generate formal jobs.

**Lenin Alonso Rey, President of Comex-Perú:**

To achieve 9.5% growth in private investment, the first thing is to restore confidence and generate stability.
