# Brazil's Energy Sector Faces Structural Risk from Judicial 'Haircut' Ruling

After several recent requests for judicial and extrajudicial recovery by energy traders, the most recent case in the sector is generating even greater concern—with fears of a potential structural impact on the market. Tradener, one of the sector's pioneers, not only obtained judicial protection against its creditors, as is traditional in such actions, but also obtained authorization to change the energy delivery time in its contracts with customers. The adjustment in timing may seem like a detail, but it becomes key at a moment when prices in the short-term energy market have varied up to 2,700% within a single day. Megawatt-hour values can range from a floor of R$ 57, mainly during the day (with excess solar generation), to peaks of R$ 1,600 per MWh in the early evening (with high demand and without the sun's help).

Tradener stated in the proceedings that it faces a financial crisis resulting mainly from this "pronounced volatility" in electricity values. The bigger problem, market sources told Brazil Journal, is that the judge authorized Tradener to deliver energy according to solar generation modulation—in other words, only when sunlight is shining. In practice, this means the trading company will serve its customers during the hours of the day when the MWh is cheap, leaving them without supply during the peak demand (and prices) of the evening.

"It's an energy haircut with judicial approval," summarized an executive from another trading company. "They sold a Ferrari, which is having energy at 7 p.m., 8 p.m. And they're delivering a Fiat 600, which is energy at noon," said another source. "It's surreal if this decision stands. It will set a precedent for everyone."

The judicial decision is likely to further impact liquidity in the energy trading market, which has already diminished following recent problems at some trading companies. The controversial spot market energy prices are defined by mathematical models that take into account factors such as generation supply, demand, and hydroelectric reservoir levels. Volatility increased with the entry of renewable sources, such as wind and solar, whose production is intermittent, varying with the wind and sun. However, a change in the risk parameters used in price calculations last year led various market participants to question the model, which they consider "miscalibrated."

The Electrical Sector Monitoring Committee (CMSE), chaired by the Ministry of Mines and Energy, must decide by June whether the current price model will be maintained in 2027, or if adjustments will be made. The injunction obtained by Tradener, which guarantees judicial protection for 60 days, is likely to heat up this discussion in the sector even further.
