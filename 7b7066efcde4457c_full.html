# Gi Group Strengthens its Strategy in Training and Productivity in Colombia

Gi Group is advancing in Colombia with a strategic commitment centered on talent training and adaptation to the new work environment. According to Catalina Landinez, country manager for Colombia, the company launched the TACK TMI unit, focused on training consulting, with which it seeks to accompany companies in reskilling processes, strengthening soft skills, and developing technical skills, in a context marked by the emergence of artificial intelligence and the transformation of work models.

According to Landinez, this line responds to growing demand from organizations to strengthen their teams and improve indicators such as work climate, turnover, and performance. The executive highlighted that, despite the economic situation, the Colombian business sector has maintained its interest in investing in employee development, understanding that training is key to facing technological changes and maintaining competitiveness.

In parallel, the company has focused its operations on improving productivity and adapting to recent labor market changes, such as labor reform and the minimum wage increase.

**How does the company see itself from its new position? What are the 2025 indicators?**

GI is on a very good trend year after year. Since 2022 it has grown and is especially increasing its market share. We measure ourselves this way against our competitors. And last year's figures are spectacular: we closed with 450 billion pesos in revenue and a profit before taxes of 10 billion pesos.

**Did those revenues increase compared to 2024? By how much?**

We grew 31% compared to 2024.

**Which businesses would you highlight? Which areas are most dynamic?**

We focus on two models or two strategies. One is the volume strategy, where our temporary and outsourcing business is concentrated, where we measure ourselves by number of temporary workers. In that business we're at roughly 10,000 temporary workers, which is something to highlight, because what we do is help employability and formal work. These are 10,000 people or families that count on dignified work, with access to the social security system and the same for their family groups. So in that business we're doing very well.

On the other hand, we have niche or higher-margin businesses, like permanent placement, which is when clients hire direct positions, but we do the search and selection. We're developing it very well, because it also divides into headhunting, which allows us to move across the entire organizational pyramid, from operational positions to middle management. In these lines we've had growth during 2025 and this year we're aiming at a new unit of the group in Colombia. The group's strategy is to grow both organically and inorganically, the latter through acquisitions. In 2024 they acquired a very large company globally, which strengthened their presence in Europe. Also, we arrived with a new brand for Colombia, TACK TMI, which is the training consulting unit, where we help companies with reskilling, soft and technical skills. That's what we're betting on this year.

**Regarding the new business area, how much need is there in the Colombian market for this type of service?**

One might think that, given the current country situation, companies would want to halt soft skills development, but the Colombian business sector has continued investing. With the arrival of artificial intelligence, which doesn't come to displace strategic roles, we've seen that companies are interested in measuring their work climate, reducing turnover, and improving skills. They also seek tools to develop critical thinking regarding AI and strengthen the human footprint in processes. We're pleasantly surprised by the interest in training leaders and complete teams.

**Regarding the situation, how has the volume business evolved in the first quarter?**

There are two moments: before and after labor reform. We came out strengthened. GI has experience in Europe, where they've already faced work hour reductions, so we were prepared. During 2024 and 2025 we worked on adapting to new schedules and improving productive skills. We implemented productivity campaigns focused on doing more with less, without reducing staff. We didn't see significant employment impacts. Even with the minimum wage increase, the business sector managed to adapt without major layoffs. In some cases, we helped reorganize shifts or improve logistics.

**What are the goals for 2026?**

We focus on three strategies: productivity, competitiveness, and scalability. Productivity to do more with less; competitiveness to generate scale and strengthen our position against competitors; and scalability to expand the reach of our solutions to clients and candidates. In terms of numbers, we're being conservative. We come from growth of 30% and 32%, and this year we project growth of around 12%, that is, twice inflation, considering the environment.

**What are the challenges in human talent?**

The main challenge is leadership. There must be a message of hope from the heads of organizations. Despite the context, companies have done well and it's important to transmit optimism. In human resources, the challenge is understanding what people need to reduce turnover, which remains high in various industries. Improving leadership skills and listening to teams is essential.
