Budget Tightening for 2027: Quiroz Clarifies Controversy Amid Criticism Over Social Program Cuts

24 April 2026 · 11:04 PM

# Budget 2027: Quiroz Clarifies Controversy Amid Criticism Over Social Program Cuts

Calculators have begun operating rapidly in the public sector under a logic of fiscal constraint. As part of the beginning of preparations for what will be the 2027 Budget bill, the Ministry of Finance instructed all ministries last Tuesday to prepare their spending proposals for a five-year period, which must be backed up and justified in detail.

A circular memo, signed by the head of public finances, Jorge Quiroz, enumerated a series of guidelines that begin by determining a baseline that includes expenses that are mandatorily incurred linked to contractual commitments of the respective State department and its dependent or related services.

Second, a complementary spending proposal must be prepared, which, together with the baseline defined in the previous section, will constitute the total request within the medium-term framework of each ministry. It is not necessarily guaranteed to be approved, it is cautioned.

For the so-called "total request," a maximum spending limit has been defined at the line item level, which means requests must be prioritized, considering the needs of sectors, such as compliance with all current legal and contractual obligations of the portfolio. This stage, it was explained, considers joint work between the ministries and their dependent or related services and the Budget Directorate (Dipres), so it requests "defining and informing a joint work program that allows the objective to be met."

The proposal for the 2027 Budget bill and what is defined as the Financial Program 2028-2031 in that portfolio must include all its dependent services or those that relate to the Executive through it. In both cases, according to the circular, the permanent effect of fiscal adjustments made in the current fiscal year—set at 3%—must be incorporated for all years of the period, and include the effect of efficiency measures implemented in each of the ministries and their dependent or related services.

"The above becomes unavoidable considering that a medium-term horizon facilitates the management of all expenses, producing savings and improving resource allocation," the memo states.

To frame these requirements, an attached note to the memo explains that public finances face a "challenging and complex" situation, marked by deficits, increased debt, and the need to meet structural balance targets. For this reason, the idea is that financial planning be carried out under fiscal sustainability criteria and that permanent growth in spending similar to previous years should not be expected.

## Programs and Deadlines

Regarding the programmatic offerings of public services, it is requested to bear in mind the importance of directing resource allocation based on evaluation, with the objective of strengthening the effectiveness, efficiency, and relevance of public interventions. For these purposes, it is specified that the programs formulated must submit their designs to the ex-ante evaluation process to obtain a favorable recommendation rating from the closure of the budget formulation process, in order to ensure it constitutes relevant input for budget decision-making.

The deadline for submitting the final version of the program design, required to obtain the favorable recommendation and be considered in the budget, will be July 2, 2026.

Additionally, ministries must identify and report spending corresponding to climate change in order to improve investment projects with impact, as well as programmatic offerings. In terms of deadlines, institutions that make up each ministry must send the Budget Directorate the baseline for the five-year period on May 15, and complementary spending information on May 27, thus completing the Total Request.

## Controversy Over Social Programs

As details of the memos sent to ministries became known, strong controversy began to emerge. The reason? The instructions delivered by Finance and Dipres to the portfolios included a document detailing the performance of the state programs offered by the respective department, with recommendations such as "reduce budget," "no observations," and "discontinue."

In this last category, sensitive social programs were identified, such as Junaeb's school feeding program, the national writing plan, the human rights program, universal palliative care, programs related to gender identity, the national suicide prevention program, the organized crime program, the violence-free streets plan, the National Television Council (CNTV) fund, regional public transportation, Conadi resources, and funds for scientific research, to name just a few.

Recommendations regarding programs to be discontinued total around US$6,000 million.

Amid the controversy, which has generated reactions especially from opposition parliamentarians, Minister Quiroz was forced to clarify the issue. "The communication sent is a memo, not a decree. The memo is part of medium-term budget formulation. It contains programmatic guidance, but it is not a reduction decree; it corresponds to guidance for spending containment and evaluation," the official said in a statement distributed by the ministry.

The minister emphasized that these memos incorporate information from the public spending monitoring and evaluation system, developed by Dipres, with the objective of supporting technical analysis of existing programs. "These are precedents that allow identifying spaces for improvement in the execution of different programs, based on evaluations that have been carried out systematically over time," he noted.

In that vein, he clarified that the recommendations contained in the annexes refer exclusively to programs in their current format and do not imply a questioning of public policies. "Current public policies are not under discussion. What is evaluated is the way these are executed, to ensure that resources are used with greater efficiency and effectiveness," he indicated.

Quiroz added that these types of evaluations have made it possible, in different cases, to identify opportunities for improvement: "There are programs where, according to Dipres analyses, implementation problems have been detected, and what is appropriate is precisely to review how things are being done, to ensure that policies adequately fulfill their objective."
