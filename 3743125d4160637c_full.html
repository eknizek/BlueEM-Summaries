# It's not marketing, it's understanding what drives people: María Elisa Botero

María Elisa Botero doesn't talk about marketing like someone placing an advertisement or launching a product. She talks about audiences, behaviors, signals. About truly understanding people. And it's not a rehearsed speech: it's a way of working that she has built through hard work, passing through some of the places that today define global conversation: Twitter, Meta, TikTok. Today, from Nutresa, she is facing one of the most interesting challenges in the Colombian business world: connecting historic brands with a consumer who has changed, who demands coherence, values, and a much closer relationship, in a company that is writing one of the most interesting business chapters of recent decades.

**Tell me, what was María Elisa like at 20 years old?**

Oh... I was restless, very restless. I still am today, but at 20 I was unstoppable. Very curious, I asked everything, I wanted to understand everything. I was one of those people who didn't settle for one answer—I would go find another and another. I was at university and I dreamed of doing luxury marketing, with global brands, understanding how those aspirations are built.

**Were you already thinking about that from then on?**

Because I know you studied Social Communication.

Yes, I studied Social Communication, but when I discovered the world of media and, above all, audiences, I fell in love. That's when I understood that mine wasn't necessarily telling the story, but understanding why that story connected—or didn't—with someone. I became obsessed with that: with who's on the other side, what moves them, what makes them stay, what makes them leave.

**Was working in journalism never an option?**

Not really. I liked the numbers side more, studying patterns, understanding behaviors. I went very quickly into digital... when "digital" was still something else, when you would see them bringing computers to fix and they said that was digital, and I thought: "no, there's something more here." And that's how I got into it, learning, many times almost intuitively.

**What was your first job?**

Since high school I sold everything: brownies, candy... I was always looking for how to do something. Later I worked during vacations in marketing for businesses in Cali. I've been working since I was very little. I like being active, feeling that I'm contributing, that I'm generating value, even in small things. I think that forms you a lot.

**If it hadn't been this path, was there a plan B?**

The truth is no. I love what I do. It has changed, of course, it has had many variations, but that understanding of the consumer—of who's on the other side—is what drives me deeply. It's almost like an obsession: understanding why people do what they do.

**That jump from a traditional channel like Caracol where you worked to Twitter, and then to Meta and TikTok... it's almost a tour through the recent history of digital. How did you experience it?**

It was a complete paradigm shift. Entering Twitter in 2014, when it was barely starting in Colombia—I was employee number two, imagine!—was entering a completely different logic. Very Silicon Valley. At that moment the focus was working with media outlets to help them be relevant in real time, to understand how conversations moved and how they could participate in them. And that's when you started seeing what we now call content creators...

Exactly. It was one of the first versions of that. And understanding that Twitter was chronological—that what didn't happen in that moment was lost—forced you to think differently. It was breaking schemes all the time, unlearning what you brought from traditional media and understanding that conversation was no longer one-directional. Getting there isn't free. What sacrifices did you make?

More than sacrifice, it was prioritizing. I wanted to understand that world and I dedicated myself to it. I was young, I didn't have a family, I could invest a lot of time. And Twitter gave me something very powerful: understanding that everything you communicate has an immediate reaction, beyond comments, and that gives you signals to improve. It's almost a real-time laboratory.

You're a woman, young... and you reach leadership positions in very demanding environments...
