Brazil is H&M's growth engine globally, CEO says at opening first Rio store

# Brazil is H&M's growth engine globally, CEO says at opening first Rio store

Less than a year after H&M opened its first store in the country in São Paulo, Brazil has already established itself as an important driver for the Swedish fashion retailer globally. According to global CEO Daniel Ervér, part of the company's strategy to regain investor confidence and return to resilient growth passes through Latin America and the opening of new stores, such as the first in Rio de Janeiro, which will open this Saturday (25th).

"Brazil is an important growth engine," Ervér said in an interview with Bloomberg Línea via video during a visit to Rio to open the new store. "We have focused on strengthening our customer offering, as well as consolidating the company's financial resilience to build a solid foundation. We are working to build growth levers for the future, and one of them will be Brazil."

The CEO's assessment summarizes years of analysis before entering the country and the speed with which the company decided to accelerate expansion after confirming receptiveness from Brazilian consumers. According to him, the retailer spent years viewing Brazil as an opportunity because of the market with more than 200 million inhabitants and local interest in fashion.

"Furthermore, there is robust infrastructure for retail with large shopping centers, but it is also a complex market, with strong competition and demanding consumers," said Ervér.

H&M opened its first store in Brazil in August 2025 at Shopping Iguatemi in São Paulo and accumulated four units in the first months of operation. Five more stores across the country have signed contracts for 2026, including another in Rio, one in Sorocaba, and the first in Porto Alegre.

The CEO claims that the company has a strategy of progressive expansion in the country and is actively seeking other locations for stores in cities it considers important, such as Belo Horizonte and Brasília, as well as expanding to the north and south of Rio and São Paulo. The pace of expansion is determined by the ability to find these strategic locations in a mature and competitive retail market.

For Ervér, the opening in Rio serves a function beyond commercial expansion. "Rio is an important milestone to catalyze brand recognition in Brazil," said the CEO. "Physical store opening drives digital sales, and the two channels reinforce each other mutually."

This dynamic between physical store and e-commerce was described by the executive as a model that Brazil helped consolidate. Simultaneous entry into both channels, immediate adoption of Pix as a payment method, and the beginning of local production in some categories were classified internally as unprecedented strategies for the network.

"The way we arrived in Brazil with the multi-channel advance is very interesting. We are very curious to get to know the local consumer and find a way to build the launch campaign, also exploring local culture, being respectful and curious about it, and seeing how it resonates with the community. I think that is really a model for how to enter new markets," said Ervér.

## Global Recovery

On the global front, Ervér acknowledged that H&M is on a long-term journey to recover revenue growth. The company's operating margin rose from 7.4% to 8.1% in fiscal year 2025 and reached 8.4% in the last 12 months ending in February 2026 — but sales in local currencies grew only 2% in the year and fell 1% in the first quarter of 2026.

"The starting point of the recovery was to ensure that the customer offering is truly competitive and that the company has financial resilience to invest," said Ervér. "Now my focus is to start accelerating growth. It is a logical journey, step by step."

The CEO was also careful when talking about the target of at least 10% annual growth. "It is a long-term journey. I want to do each step with quality."

The geopolitical scenario, Ervér admits, brings unpredictability to global supply chains, which is worsened by wars and tariffs. Scenario planning, he said, has become a central part of management. But the CEO cautions against automatically reading that macroeconomic uncertainty equals falling consumption.

"We were too cautious about the American consumer in the second half of 2025. They proved more resilient than we expected."

## Expansion in Latin America

Latin America as a whole is integrated into the company's growth plan, according to the CEO. H&M has operated in Chile for ten years, is expected to open a store in Paraguay later in 2025, and recently announced entry into Argentina for 2027.

In the Brazilian case, the company says it adapts its business model and assortment for the Brazilian market considering local particularities and competition.

H&M noticed that the most appreciated part of its assortment by Brazilian customers is the women's fashion collection, which is a competitive differentiator. Additionally, they offer a wide range of products in other categories such as lingerie, beachwear, and sportswear, built on the basis of offering current fashion with good value for money.

According to the CEO, entry into Brazil also came with awareness of strong local and international competition in the country, with players like Zara and C&A. The company seeks to differentiate itself by offering current fashion and value, and the positive reception from customers in São Paulo indicates there is room for H&M to find its differentiator for the Brazilian consumer.

The CEO acknowledged that the global clothing market is "brutally competitive," but argued that with less than 2% global market share, there is room to grow if the company delivers on its promises.

"The market is very fragmented. If we do what we should do well, the opportunity is there."

Despite stated excitement about the Brazilian market, the CEO pointed out challenges faced by the company entering the country. According to him, an important issue early on was the reversal of seasons due to the hemispheric change. This requires assortment adaptation and options to be relevant to the market, considering, for example, longer summer periods and necessary seasonal adaptations.

The company established a joint venture with Grupo Dorben, which has more knowledge of the Brazilian market. According to the CEO, this partnership is crucial for navigating local complexities, from technological solutions and land negotiations to identifying the right contacts and networks to enter the market.

"Brazil is a mature retail market, with strong local and international competition, and finding the best spaces is ongoing work," he said. "But what we saw in São Paulo in these first months gives us confidence to move forward."
