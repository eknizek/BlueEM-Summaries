# "Losses are in the millions": estimate of one million hectares flooded in northern Santa Fe

Abundant rainfall recorded last week in northern Santa Fe has resulted in one million hectares flooded in the departments of 9 de Julio, General Obligado and Vera, and millions in losses in livestock activity, the main production in the area. Although the three districts were affected by precipitation that reached accumulated totals of up to 400 millimeters, the most affected was Vera, whose producers are requesting that an agricultural emergency be declared in the department and that the foot-and-mouth vaccination campaign deadline be postponed.

The Vera department is a low meridional zone, which facilitates water accumulation, not only from local rainfall but also because it receives accumulated water from neighboring regions. In conversation with Clarín Rural, the president of the Vera Rural Society, Carlos Chamorro, indicated that "until last week there were districts where more than 400 mm fell and in those areas the situation is alarming. The department average was between 200 and 300 mm, so the accumulated total is significant."

"The three departments are complicated, but especially ours, because it is a low meridional zone where all the accumulated rainfall from the west comes here," said Chamorro, who noted that the region's natural drainage is the Paraná River to the north and the Salado to the south, which today is overflowing from rains in Tucumán and Salta.

Livestock activity, the main activity in the area, is the most affected. It is for this reason that the local rural society president considers the situation "more than complicated." "People have been moving animals for several days to higher areas. Trucks cannot circulate; instead, livestock is moved by herding. And what little there was to harvest could not be done. That is why the losses are in the millions in every sense," Chamorro added.

According to a report submitted to the Confederation of Rural Associations of Santa Fe (Carsfe) to promote a request for agricultural emergency to the province and nation, Vera, a livestock department par excellence, currently has approximately 700,000 head of cattle. Due to flooding, the impact on the activity results in loss of body condition in herds, restricted access to quality water, difficulties in supplementation and food logistics, and health problems such as foot diseases and parasites.

While animal mortality has been recorded, Chamorro commented that these are still isolated cases. But the damage is not only productive: "Today there are isolated settlements, schools that do not hold classes because they cannot be reached, localities in the north without electricity... The situation is very complicated."

The rains and floods have also exposed infrastructure problems facing the agricultural sector, with rural roads as the main obstacle to production. "The complaints we are making today are the same ones we have been making for 30 years: we have very little road infrastructure, which today is impassable, and all this is aggravated by the season it is occurring in, entering winter, due to the scarce pasture we have, which with this water, simply does not exist," Chamorro concluded.
