# Losses estimated in millions as one million hectares flooded in northern Santa Fe

Abundant rainfall recorded last week in northern Santa Fe has resulted in one million hectares flooded in the departments of 9 de Julio, General Obligado, and Vera, with losses in the millions in livestock activity, the main production of the region. While all three districts were affected by precipitation that accumulated up to 400 millimeters, the department of Vera was the hardest hit, with producers requesting that an agricultural emergency be declared in the department and that the conclusion of the foot-and-mouth disease vaccination campaign be postponed.

The department of Vera is a low-lying area, which facilitates water accumulation, not only from local rainfall but also because it receives runoff from neighboring regions. In conversation with Clarín Rural, the president of the Vera Rural Society, Carlos Chamorro, indicated that "until last week there were districts where it rained more than 400 mm and in those areas the situation is alarming. The department average was between 200 and 300 mm, so the accumulated amount is significant."

"The three departments are in trouble, but especially ours, because it is a low-lying area where all the accumulated rainfall from the west comes here," said Chamorro, who noted that the region's natural drainage is the Paraná River to the north and the Salado River to the south, which is currently overflowing from rainfall in Tucumán and Salta.

Livestock activity, the main activity in the area, is the most affected. Given this situation, the head of the local rural society considers the situation "more than complicated." "People have been moving animals for several days to higher areas. Trucks cannot circulate, so cattle must be moved by herding. And what little had to be harvested could not be done. That's why the losses are in the millions in every way," Chamorro added.

According to a report submitted to the Confederation of Rural Associations of Santa Fe (Carsfe) to drive a request for agricultural emergency to the province and nation, Vera, a livestock department par excellence, currently has about 700,000 head of cattle. Due to the flooding, the impact on the activity results in loss of body condition in herds, restricted access to quality water, difficulties in supplementation and food logistics, and health problems such as hoof diseases and parasitic infections.

While there has been animal mortality, Chamorro commented that these are still isolated cases. But the damage is not only productive: "Today there are isolated settlements, schools that are not holding classes because they cannot be reached, towns to the north without electricity… The situation is very complicated."

The rainfall and flooding have also exposed the infrastructure problems facing agriculture, with rural roads as the main obstacle to production. "The complaints we are making today are the ones we have been making for 30 years: we have very little road infrastructure, which today is impassable and all this is aggravated by the timing, entering winter, by the scarce pasture there is, which with this water, simply does not exist," Chamorro concluded.
