# One Million Hectares Flooded in Northern Santa Fe; Losses Estimated in Millions

The heavy rains recorded last week in northern Santa Fe resulted in one million hectares flooded in the departments of 9 de Julio, General Obligado, and Vera, with losses in the millions in livestock activity, the main production in the region.

Although the three districts were affected by rainfall that accumulated up to 400 millimeters, the most affected was the department of Vera, whose producers are requesting that agricultural emergency be declared in the department and that the conclusion of the foot-and-mouth disease vaccination campaign be postponed.

The department of Vera is a zone of low submeridional terrain, which facilitates water accumulation, not only from local rainfall but also because it receives runoff from neighboring regions.

In conversation with Clarín Rural, the president of the Rural Society of Vera, Carlos Chamorro, indicated that "until last week there were districts with more than 400 mm of rainfall and in those areas the situation is alarming. The department average was between 200 and 300 mm, so the accumulated total is significant."

"The three departments are in difficult situations, but especially ours, because it is a zone of low submeridional terrain where all accumulated rainfall from the west flows here," said Chamorro, who explained that the natural drainage of the region is the Paraná River to the north and the Salado River to the south, which today is overflowing due to rains in Tucumán and Salta.

Livestock activity, the main activity in the area, is the most affected. Given this situation, the local rural society leader considers the situation "more than complicated."

"People have been moving animals for several days to higher ground. Trucks cannot circulate, so livestock is moved by herding. And the little that needed to be harvested could not be done. That's why the losses are in the millions in every sense," Chamorro added.

According to a report sent to the Confederation of Rural Associations of Santa Fe (Carsfe) to request agricultural emergency declaration from the provincial and national governments, Vera, a livestock department par excellence, currently has approximately 700,000 head of cattle.

Due to the flooding, the impact on livestock activity results in loss of herd body condition, restricted access to quality water, difficulties in supplementation and food logistics, and health problems such as hoof diseases and parasitic infections.

Although animal mortality has been recorded, Chamorro commented that these are still isolated cases.

But the damage is not only productive: "Today there are isolated settlements, schools that are not holding classes because they cannot be reached, northern towns without electricity... The situation is very complicated."

The rains and flooding have also exposed the infrastructure problems facing agriculture, with rural roads being the main obstacle to production.

"The demands we are making today are the same ones we have been making for 30 years: we have very little road infrastructure, which today is impassable, and all this is aggravated by the season it occurs in, entering winter, by the scarce pasture we have, which, with this water, simply does not exist," Chamorro concluded.
