# Master Case: US Court Allows Miami Office Owners to Accelerate Bank Eviction

Judge Scott M. Grossman of the US Bankruptcy Court for the Southern District of Florida made several decisions this week that allow the Master Bank insolvency and asset recovery process in the country to move forward.

On Wednesday, the judge ruled that the bank's liquidator and Watson Brickell LLC, owner of the building where the bank rented one and a half floors, can proceed with the agreement to accelerate Master's eviction. On Thursday, the judge also denied a request from Daniel Vorcaro's defense team, the bank's former controller, which sought to limit or prevent production of evidence subpoenas from EFB Regimes Especiais, the institution's liquidator. He also denied a request to suspend proceedings while Vorcaro appeals previous decisions.

Watson Brickell, owner of 830 Brickell Plaza in Miami, reached an agreement with EFB to vacate the space due to interest from two potential clients. One of Miami's most expensive corporate addresses, the 57-story building is located in the heart of the city's financial district and houses offices for major companies such as Microsoft and asset manager Citadel. The glass skyscraper also features an exclusive restaurant with panoramic views.

Master leased the 44th floor and half of the 45th floor in June 2024 but never occupied the space. The contract provided for monthly payments of $423,000 (approximately R$ 2 million), and in September of last year, Master failed to honor the commitment. In addition to rent, the bank made a security deposit of $3.6 million, which was used to cover payments in case of default.

After the US courts recognized the effects of the bank's liquidation in the country, Master's eviction became locked as part of the proceedings. Now that the judge has approved the agreement between Brickell and EFB, the building's owner can reclaim the space.

In the other decisions, the judge rejected the request to pause the case proceedings, allowing lawsuits to continue normally. Vorcaro sought to limit or prohibit subpoenas for document production and examinations issued by the bank's representative, which was denied. The decisions were made following a hearing on April 28.
