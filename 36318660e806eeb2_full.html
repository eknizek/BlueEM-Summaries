# Smugglers Positioned to Win Amid Colombia-Ecuador Tariff Crisis

The political and commercial crisis facing Colombia with Ecuador once again dominates the media agenda due to the impact both countries are experiencing from rising tariffs. The 100% tariff increase by the neighboring country and Colombia's staggered increase to Ecuador mark a new concern in the business and foreign trade sector, due to the inability to resume exports to the Ecuadorian nation. And while the effects are already becoming evident, according to the director of the Colombia-Ecuador Chamber, Oliva Díazgranados, the beneficiary of the diplomatic dispute is smuggling.

According to her explanation, the borders with Ecuador are practically nonexistent, considering that the economies of those territories function jointly. However, tariff restrictions are forcing products to be sold through illegal channels.

"What this entire war between the presidents is generating is a surge in smuggling. Really, what is being proposed about controlling illegality is what is giving all the way to all illegality and smuggling at the border," she indicated.

Regarding the impact on companies, the director reiterated that at this moment entrepreneurs face a very "complex" scenario and that at least 3,287 companies that export to Ecuador are at risk. "Of that total of companies, 80% are micro, small and medium enterprises. What worries them most is that each one of them has at least 10 workers, so more than 26,000 jobs are at stake," mentioned Díazgranados.

The executive also detailed that these would be direct jobs, but if indirect jobs were counted, more than 400,000 people would be affected, "which is basically the population of a city like Armenia," she said.

She also pointed to the possible closure of companies due to the restriction on sales because of the tariffs imposed by Ecuador of 100% and which begin to take effect as of May 1st of this year. "There is an idea that markets could be replaced, but that is not so easy. Behind it there is investment, company incorporation procedures, trademark registrations, certifications, health and phytosanitary registrations. There are at least ten procedures that companies have to complete to be able to export," questioned the business leader.

Thus, from a macroeconomic perspective, the measures introduce distortions in relative prices, alter intra-regional trade flows and generate a negative shock to supply and demand.

## Null Negotiation

Given that to stop the presidential dispute it is necessary for the two countries to enter into joint negotiations that imply less risk for companies, foreign trade actors see a resolution of problems between the countries as null.

Based on what was mentioned by Javier Díaz, president of the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex), the political tension between Presidents Daniel Noboa and Gustavo Petro continues to increase and everything seems to indicate that there will be no solution until August, according to what the Ecuadorian president has said. "Therefore, there will be a fall in bilateral commercial exchange. With a 100% tariff it is impossible to export to Ecuador," said Díaz.

Additionally, the president of Analdex noted that Colombia has already demonstrated willingness to negotiate, but the solution involves other political issues. "The complaint has already been presented to the Andean Community," he added. "What follows on the part of exporters is to diversify their sales to other markets, in order to be able to wait for a solution to be reached, without having to make massive staff layoffs or even close the companies," he opined.

For her part, the director of the Colombia-Ecuador Chamber highlighted that from the binational body approaches have been made to the Ecuadorian government, but these have borne no fruit. "President Noboa has told us that as long as Gustavo Petro is president in Colombia, there is no possibility of negotiating. He has been very emphatic that there is no chance of reaching an agreement while President Petro is in office. He has said it openly and publicly," she affirmed.

Likewise, the director maintains that if the situation continues this way, there would be eight months in which companies have no possibility of negotiating any binational exchange, which would prevent the entry and exit of products from both countries.

Regarding Colombia's tariffs on Ecuador, Díazgranados reiterated that imposing a 35% tariff on products such as tuna from the neighboring country, which are part of the family basic food basket, is affecting the cost of living for households in the country. "The tuna that Colombia produces is absolutely insufficient for the quantities consumed and where strata 1, 2 and 3 are those who buy it the most. How can we tell these families the price went up 35% because of tariffs, plus VAT. It is complicated," she indicated.

Finally, the business leader insisted that tuna is also part of the School Feeding Program (PAE), so the tariff would also affect the public policy for feeding children in Colombia.
