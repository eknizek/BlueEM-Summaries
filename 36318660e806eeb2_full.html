# Smugglers Poised to Win Amid Colombia-Ecuador Tariff Crisis

The political and commercial crisis facing Colombia with Ecuador has once again dominated media headlines due to the impact both countries face with rising tariffs. The 100% tariff increase from the neighboring country and Colombia's staggered increase to Ecuador mark a new concern in the business and foreign trade sector, due to the impossibility of resuming exports to the Ecuadorian nation. Although the effects are already becoming evident, according to the director of the Colombian-Ecuadorian Chamber, Oliva Díazgranados, the beneficiary of the diplomatic dispute is smuggling.

She explained that the borders with Ecuador are almost non-existent, considering that the economies of those territories function jointly. However, tariff restrictions would be forcing the sale of products through illegal channels.

"What is generating this entire war between the presidents is a waste through smuggling. Really, what is being proposed about controlling illegality is what is giving way to all illegality and smuggling at the border," she stated.

Regarding the impact on companies, the director reiterated that at this moment entrepreneurs face a very "complex" scenario and that at least 3,287 companies exporting to Ecuador are at risk. "Of that total number of companies, 80% are small and medium-sized enterprises. What worries them most is that each one of them has at least 10 workers, so more than 26,000 jobs are at stake," Díazgranados mentioned.

The director also detailed that these would be direct jobs, but if indirect jobs were counted, more than 400,000 people would be affected, "which is basically the population of a city like Armenia," she said.

She also pointed to the possible closure of companies due to the restriction on sales because of the tariffs imposed by Ecuador at 100%, which begin to take effect starting May 1st of this year.

"There is an idea that markets could be replaced, but that is not so easy. Behind it there is investment, company formation procedures, trademark registrations, certifications, health registrations, phytosanitary registrations. There are at least ten procedures that companies must complete to be able to export," the business leader questioned.

Thus, from a macroeconomic perspective, the measures introduce distortions in relative prices, alter flows of intraregional trade, and generate a negative shock of supply and demand.

## Null Negotiation

Considering that to stop the presidential dispute it is necessary for the two countries to enter into joint negotiations that involve fewer risks for companies, foreign trade actors see a resolution of problems between countries as unlikely.

Based on what Javier Díaz, president of the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex), stated, the political tension between presidents Daniel Noboa and Gustavo Petro continues to increase and everything suggests there will be no solution until August, according to what the Ecuadorian president has said.

"Therefore, a decline in bilateral trade exchange is coming. With a 100% tariff, it is impossible to export to Ecuador," Díaz said.

Additionally, the president of Analdex pointed out that Colombia has already demonstrated willingness to negotiate, but the solution involves other political issues.

"A complaint has already been filed with the Andean Community," he added.

"What follows for exporters is to diversify their sales toward other markets, to be able to wait for a solution to be reached, without having to make massive layoffs or even close companies," he opined.

For her part, the director of the Colombian-Ecuadorian Chamber highlighted that from the binational body there have been approaches to the Ecuadorian government, but these have not borne fruit.

"President Noboa has told us that while Gustavo Petro is president in Colombia, there is no possibility of negotiating. He has been very emphatic that there is no probability of reaching an agreement while President Petro is in office. He has said it openly and publicly," she affirmed.

She also maintains that if the situation continues this way, there would be eight months in which companies would have no possibility of negotiating any bilateral exchange, which would make it impossible for products from both countries to enter and exit.

Regarding Colombia's tariffs on Ecuador, Díazgranados reiterated that placing a 35% tariff on products like tuna coming from the neighboring country, which are part of the family basic food basket, is affecting the cost of living of households in the country.

"The tuna that Colombia produces is absolutely insufficient for the quantities consumed and where income levels 1, 2, and 3 are the ones who buy it the most. How do you tell these families the price went up 35% due to tariffs, plus VAT? It is complicated," she indicated.

Finally, the business leader insisted that tuna is also part of the School Feeding Program (PAE), so the tariff would also affect the public policy of feeding children in Colombia.
