# Mexico seeks to position itself in legacy chip market

The goal is to ensure that each state understands its participation in the different phases of the value chain. By 2026, Mexico will work to focus each entity in the country on its participation in the semiconductor market and the supply chain and legacy chip sector.

"What are these chips? They are those found in dryers, washing machines, refrigerators, those that complement very well with the industries we have in the country such as automotive, aerospace, and home appliances," explained Diego Flores, head of the Electronics and Digital Industry Sector at the Ministry of Economy (SE).

These chips are those which, in the current geopolitical and geostrategic logic, require supply chains in the hemisphere, detailed the official to Excélsior. Flores assured that Mexico has received clear signals and is carrying out joint work with the United States to develop the legacy chip industry.

Additionally, the official noted that the geopolitical landscape is playing in favor of the region as the West has transitioned from a model based strictly on price per unit to a landed price model. This includes considering factors such as national security, resilience against possible blockades, and mitigation of supply chain shocks in the final cost, Flores detailed.

## Local Experience

The official said that in 2026 the goal is to ensure that each state understands its participation in the different phases of the value chain. The agency seeks to have specific investment missions to attract these production lines, with the objective that by mid-2027, the first factories of this type operate in the country.

"This year the goal is to pursue investment missions for these production lines to begin having legacy chip factories established in Mexico by early or mid-2027," the official announced.

Flores highlighted that at the end of the year, the QSM company will begin operations, representing a notable project for having national capital. "It is at that moment when we already have consolidated what we specifically want within this large framework, and alongside design, if we already have factories in Mexico, at some point the design will be able to accompany very well all these two-way capabilities," the official explained.

This means that once manufacturing capabilities are established, the design area, where Mexico already has advances, can integrally support production, closing the value cycle. This integration will not only strengthen the local economy but will directly connect Mexico with a binational supply chain designed to reduce systemic risks.

The manufacture of a semiconductor can involve processes in more than 70 countries, so any small logistical friction affects the global matrix. 50% of helium production, a critical input for the industry, is concentrated in Qatar.

"If something happens and a disruption is generated, the entire industry, from the most advanced chips to the legacy ones, will suffer. That chain cannot be stressed further," Flores warned.
