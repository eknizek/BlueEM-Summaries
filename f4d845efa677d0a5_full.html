# Mexico targets legacy chip market amid geopolitical tailwinds

Mexico seeks to position itself in the legacy chip market. The goal is to ensure that each state understands its participation in the different phases of the value chain. By 2026, Mexico will work to ensure that each entity in the country focuses on its participation in the semiconductor market and the supply chain and legacy chip market.

"What are these chips? They are those found in dryers, washing machines, refrigerators, those that complement very well with the industries we have in the country such as automotive, aerospace, and home appliances," explained Diego Flores, head of the Electronic and Digital Industry Sector of the Ministry of Economy (SE).

These chips are those that, in the current geopolitical and geostrategic logic, require supply chains in the hemisphere, the official detailed to Excélsior. Flores assured that Mexico has received clear signals and is carrying out joint work with the United States to develop the legacy chip industry. Furthermore, the official commented that the geopolitical panorama is playing in favor of the region since the West has transitioned from a model based strictly on price per unit to one of landed price. This includes contemplating in the final cost factors such as national security, resistance to possible blockades, and mitigation of shocks in the supply chain, Flores detailed.

Local Experience

The official said that in 2026 the goal is to ensure that each of the states understands its participation in the different phases of the value chain. Therefore, the department seeks to have specific investment missions to attract these production lines, with the objective that by mid-2027, the first factories of this type operate in the country.

"This year the plan is to search for investment missions for these production lines to begin to have legacy chip factories already established in Mexico by early or mid-2027," the official announced in advance.

Flores highlighted that at the end of the year, the company QSM will begin operations, which represents a notable project for having national capital. "It is that moment where we already have consolidated what we specifically want within this large framework, and that alongside design, if we already have chip factories in Mexico, at some point the design will be able to accompany very well all these back-and-forth capabilities," the official explained.

This means that once manufacturing capabilities are established, the design area, where Mexico already has advances, can comprehensively accompany production, closing the value cycle. This integration will not only strengthen the local economy but will directly connect Mexico to a binational supply chain designed to reduce systemic risks.

The manufacturing of a semiconductor can involve processes in more than 70 countries, so any small logistical friction affects the global matrix. 50% of helium production, a critical input for the industry, is concentrated in Qatar.

"If something happens and a disruption is generated, the entire industry, from the most advanced chips to the legacy ones, will suffer, that chain cannot be stressed further," Flores warned.
