# Colombian Government Exits Coltel, Marking Shift in Telecom Sector History

This Friday, April 24, marks a turning point for the telecommunications sector in Colombia. The State, through the Ministry of Finance, will close its agreement with Millicom to sell its stake in Colombia Telecomunicaciones (Coltel), in an operation that not only redefines the sector's landscape but also ends more than seven decades of direct state presence in this market.

For over 70 years, the State participated in the sector, first through the defunct Telecom, the National Telecommunications Company of Colombia, and later through its participation in Coltel, in alliance with Telefónica. This phase would come to an end with a transaction exceeding US$200 million. It should be noted that while the Government withdraws from the end-user services market, it will remain present in the sector through infrastructure via Internexa, a specialist operator in connectivity and fiber optics.

## Times Are Changing

The Government's history in this sector is marked not only by advances, failed moments, or financial crises, but also by innovation milestones that transformed how Colombians communicate. Created by the State in 1947, Telecom pioneered the provision of telephone and telegraph services, and later the development of fiber optic networks, playing a key role in consolidating national connectivity. However, after years of overcoming economic challenges, one of the most critical points for the company occurred in 2003, when it was liquidated during President Álvaro Uribe Vélez's administration, marking the end of an era in Colombian telecommunications history.

Following its liquidation, Colombia Telecomunicaciones (Coltel) emerged, a company that in its early years was 100% public. However, its business plan required incorporating a strategic partner to strengthen its operations and financial viability. In 2006, an auction process began in which Telefónica participated, eventually consolidating itself as the State's ally. From that point on, the Spanish multinational entered Coltel with capital, strengthening a company that promised to continue connecting Colombians in the fixed-line segment. Two decades later, this alliance, which faced sector transformation, new infrastructure deployment, and growing mobile market competition, comes to an end with Millicom's entry, Tigo's parent company, which not only acquired Telefónica's operations in Colombia but also its stake in this joint venture.

## The Contrasts

The imminent exit of the Government from Coltel has generated divided opinions in the sector. While some warn that the operation could strengthen a duopoly in the market following Millicom's consolidation, others believe the State's withdrawal is a necessary decision after years of questionable results. In this vein, there are experts who characterize state participation in telecommunications as an "adventure" that resulted in significant destruction of public patrimony. Marc Eichmann, a telecommunications expert, argues that the State has been an inefficient administrator throughout its history in the sector and that its presence "has not added any value to the end user." The analyst goes even further, asserting that the Nation's exit from Coltel "is not cause for sadness but relief," considering that state management has ultimately "destroyed Colombian capital."

## New Beginnings

Given the changing landscape facing the sector and connectivity challenges, Giuseppe González, associate director for Latin America at Access Partnership, notes that the country is going through a decisive stage, marked not only by the reconfiguration of its main players but also by growing competitive pressure from the digital environment. "The telecommunications sector is going through a vital transformation period for the country's future. On one hand, the reconfiguration of the most relevant market agents presents the need to understand how competition will develop in fixed and mobile markets. On the other, the industry's natural dynamics suggest that not only telco matters; digital also adds competitive pressure," he stated.

Under this framework, the expert's analysis gains relevance from the users' perspective, in an environment where digitalization is advancing rapidly. According to recent data, González notes, 8 out of 10 financial transactions in Colombia involve digital means, representing significant progress but also presenting inclusion challenges. "Digitalization needs go hand in hand with connectivity needs. Those people without coverage or without access to digital services risk being left behind," he warned. Faced with this reconfiguration phase, which also includes the State's exit, the expert also emphasizes the need for regulatory stability, cautioning against introducing structural changes in spectrum allocation or sector policy amid uncertainty.
