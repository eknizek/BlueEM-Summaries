# Juan Valdez Accelerates Expansion in Spain with Local Production and European Consumption Strategy

The National Federation of Coffee Growers announced a new step in its international strategy with Juan Valdez's entry into domestic consumption in Spain, according to its manager, Germán Bahamón. The move marks a shift toward mass consumption in European households. The strategy seeks to take Colombian Coffee beyond commercial presence, focusing on direct penetration into the daily life of Spanish consumers.

According to the executive, "internationalization is not an exercise in presence. It is an exercise in depth," a vision that redefines the global expansion of Colombian coffee under criteria of integration and added value. This approach means moving away from fragmented models and advancing toward schemes where channels are articulated. "It's not about being there. It's about belonging," Bahamón said when explaining the logic behind the strategic move.

The central announcement is an alliance with Colombia's Lux Group, through its Merica company in Spain, which will allow structuring an operating model with comprehensive reach in the European market. The alliance seeks to deepen the presence of Colombian coffee in Spain. Rather than growing in the number of sales points, the objective is to consolidate a sustained relationship with the end consumer in multiple consumption spaces.

Production of 100% Colombian coffee will be carried out from Valencia, adapting the roast to the profile of Spanish consumers. This decision aims to improve product acceptance through adjustments to local preferences. The proposal also contemplates a brand and packaging strategy aligned with consumption habits in Spain, reinforcing the focus on customer proximity and cultural adaptation of the product.

The distribution model will be omnichannel, integrating neighborhood stores, supermarkets, digital platforms, and institutional consumption, allowing for broad and consistent coverage in different purchase moments. The strategy emphasizes entering the Spanish consumer's "pantry"—that is, becoming a regular product within the home, with constant presence in daily coffee consumption. "We will be in their cup every morning. In their pantry," Bahamón affirmed, describing the expected scope of the operation, which seeks to consolidate Colombian coffee as a conscious choice of origin and quality.

Global expansion is being redefined under a market depth approach. According to the executive, growth does not depend on geographic presence, but rather on the ability to generate roots with the consumer. With this step, Colombian Coffee enters a new phase of internationalization, in which closeness with the end consumer and channel integration become the main drivers of growth.
