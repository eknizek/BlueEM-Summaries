# After days of tension, Sancor partners approved bankruptcy

The crisis at SanCor reached a turning point on Thursday, April 30, when the partners' assembly held during the morning resolved by majority to accept the bankruptcy request promoted by the cooperative's board of directors. The decision came after several days of strong internal tension and legal filings that had cast doubt on the validity of the process. The meeting was held with the presence of observers from the National Institute of Associativism and Social Economy (INAES) and the provincial Cooperatives Bureau, on a day that concentrated the attention of workers, producers, and creditors.

From appeal to partners' endorsement

The outcome contrasted with the scenario presented just days before. A group of former workers, producers, and suppliers had filed an appeal against the bankruptcy decreed by the Justice of Rafaela, in an attempt to stop the measure and transfer the decision to the partners' assembly. The appeal, driven by lawyer Aldo Regali, questioned the judge's decision to proceed with the bankruptcy without the partners having previously pronounced themselves. According to the argument, the board of directors did not have the power to request a measure of such magnitude without the assembly's approval.

"The Council can only administer, but cannot dispose. It cannot request bankruptcy without the endorsement of the associates," the lawyer had stated, who also described the judicial ruling as "an absolute aberration" for considering it contradicted current regulations regarding cooperatives.

In that context, the April 30 assembly appeared as a key instance that could redefine the institutional direction of the company. There was even warning about the possibility of conflict if the partners rejected a bankruptcy already decreed by Justice. However, the result ended up leaning in the opposite direction to what the sectors promoting the appeal were proposing. The majority of partners decided to support the board's request, thus validating the path initiated in court and reconfiguring the conflict scenario.

The decision directly impacted the legal front, as the Chamber will now analyze the appeal in a context where the will of the partners was explicitly expressed. This new element could influence the resolution of the case.

In parallel, during the preceding days, alleged irregularities in the process had also been pointed out, such as the lack of INAES intervention and the limited role of the supervisory board, objections that formed part of the argument of those seeking to stop the bankruptcy.

Beyond the vote, different actors in the sector continued promoting alternatives to avoid the definitive liquidation of the cooperative. Among them, a rescue plan was being analyzed that included the creation of a corporation, debt-to-equity swaps for creditors, and the establishment of a trust fund designed to guarantee payments to producers. "The rescue remains in effect," Regali had assured beforehand, also anticipating further negotiations with provincial authorities.

However, the assembly's endorsement changed the starting point for any future negotiation. With this result, SanCor entered a new stage marked by uncertainty. The validation of the bankruptcy request by the partners themselves sealed one of the most transcendent decisions in the cooperative's recent history and left its future tied to judicial definitions and the eventual emergence of alternatives that allow the activity to continue.
