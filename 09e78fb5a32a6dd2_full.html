# After Days of Tension, Sancor Partners Approved Bankruptcy

The crisis of SanCor reached a turning point on Thursday, April 30, when the partners' assembly held during the morning resolved by majority to accept the bankruptcy request promoted by the cooperative's board of directors. The decision came after several days of strong internal tension and court filings that had cast doubt on the validity of the process. The meeting took place with the presence of monitors from the National Institute of Associativism and Social Economy (INAES) and the Provincial Cooperative Authority, on a day that concentrated the attention of workers, producers, and creditors.

From the appeal to partners' approval

The outcome contrasted with the scenario presented just days earlier. A group of former workers, producers, and suppliers had filed an appeal against the bankruptcy decreed by the Rafaela Court, in an attempt to stop the measure and transfer the decision to the partners' assembly. The appeal, promoted by lawyer Aldo Regali, questioned the judge's decision to proceed with bankruptcy without the partners having previously spoken. According to the argument, the board of directors did not have the authority to request such a measure without the assembly's approval. "The Council can only manage, but cannot dispose. It cannot request bankruptcy without the approval of the partners," the attorney had stated, also calling the judicial ruling "an absolute aberration" for contradicting current cooperative regulations.

In that context, the April 30 assembly appeared as a key instance that could redefine the institutional course of the company. There was even warning about the possibility of conflict if the partners rejected a bankruptcy already decreed by the Court. However, the result ended up leaning contrary to what was being proposed by the sectors promoting the appeal. The majority of partners decided to support the board's request, validating the path begun in court and reconfiguring the conflict scenario. The decision directly impacted the legal front, since the Chamber will now have to analyze the appeal in a context where the will of the partners became explicitly expressed. This new element could influence the resolution of the case.

In parallel, during the previous days, alleged irregularities in the process had also been noted, such as lack of INAES intervention and the limited role of the audit committee—objections that were part of the arguments of those seeking to stop the bankruptcy. Beyond the vote, different actors in the sector continued to promote alternatives to avoid the definitive liquidation of the cooperative. Among them, a rescue plan was being analyzed that included the creation of a corporation, debt-to-equity swaps for creditors, and the formation of a trust fund aimed at guaranteeing payments to producers. "The rescue remains in place," Regali had assured beforehand, also anticipating further negotiations with provincial authorities. However, the assembly's approval changed the starting point for any future negotiation.

With this result, SanCor entered a new stage marked by uncertainty. The validation of the bankruptcy request by the partners themselves sealed one of the most momentous decisions in the recent history of the cooperative and left its future tied to judicial decisions and the eventual emergence of alternatives that allow sustaining the business.
