# New Shopping Centre Project Launched in Cali Integrated with Mass Transit

Canales Desarrolladores and Concretesa presented what would be Cali's first shopping center model in the country that will be integrated with a station of the integrated transport system in Cali. This milestone has been in the making for over twelve years, when Canales Desarrolladores, a constructor, developer and executor of shopping centers, won the public call for the El Calvario Urban Renewal Partial Plan, contained within the Ciudad Paraíso Plan, to recover, renovate and gradually transform a depressed area in downtown Cali that also has a housing component.

María Alejandra Canales, vice president of Canales Desarrolladores S.A.S., explained to Portafolio the characteristics of the Idilio commercial complex, which will involve an investment of $400 billion and is expected to be ready in 2028, according to the schedule.

**Where exactly is the project located?**

This is a project framed within the urban renewal of downtown Cali. For over 12 years, the territorial planning plan defined this zone as strategic, with different partial plans within the Ciudad Paraíso macro project. Our project is one of those partial plans and acts as a spearhead for renewal. We have already completed the land management phase, which included the acquisition and demolition of existing buildings in the El Calvario neighborhood. It is located with a wide frontage on Carrera 10, between Streets 13 and 15, in the heart of downtown Cali.

**Is it a popular area?**

Yes. It was a highly deteriorated area. The El Calvario neighborhood was once a market plaza that degraded over time, with the presence of recycling warehouses, tenement housing, drug distribution centers and homeless people. The dynamics were very similar to what happened in El Cartucho in Bogotá. The intervention occurred following a public call from the Municipal Urban Renewal Company (EMRU) to seek a strategic private partner. That call was launched in 2012 and we won it together with our local partner, Concretesa. This was our first public-private initiative project in a zone that was a priority from an urban and social perspective.

The plan includes the construction of the Central station of the Mío mass transit system, which integrates the five trunk lines of the system into a node that did not previously exist. Additionally, the development includes the Idilio Shopping Center as the main project and housing phases. We already delivered the first phase with 331 apartments, currently inhabited.

**How much is still pending on the residential side?**

Our partial plan contemplates 800 homes. We have already built 331 and approximately 500 are missing in two additional phases. In the surrounding area, within Ciudad Paraíso, other partial plans are also advancing, such as San Pascual, with projects like Ámara (954 homes) and Ágora (635 homes), developed by Conaltura and Prodesa.

**Let's focus on the shopping center: area, concept, integration with the station, challenges and schedule.**

We plan to complete the shopping center by the end of 2028. It will have approximately 27,000 square meters of commercial area (GLA), with 53,500 square meters built. The exact GLA is 26,648 square meters, with 144 stores and 35 kiosks. The project was launched during EPICA - a shopping center event - in Cartagena last week, and has already generated interest from several merchants. The main anchor will be the Mío Central station, which will move approximately 95,000 people daily. This guarantees very high foot traffic. To put it in perspective, successful shopping centers like Mercurio in Soacha receive approximately 1.3 million visitors per month. In this case, the projected daily flow is even higher.

The main challenge has been precisely the station, which we built within the partial plan with public and private resources. Public financing was secured at the beginning of this year with the signing of an amendment for its construction, which begins in May, with the support of Alejandro Eder's administration.

**One thing is the shopping center and another is the station. What resources are committed to each?**

In the shopping center the investment is completely private, close to 400 billion pesos. The station is 107 billion pesos and has two phases. The first, already financed, amounts to 69.6 billion pesos. The second phase is around 13.2 billion pesos. Supervision costs 6.9 billion and land costs 17.5 billion pesos.

**Are those station resources public or is there a private component?**

There is a private component contributed by Canales Desarrolladores and Concretesa of 14.6 billion pesos, corresponding to the urban development charge of the project, in Phase 1.

**What does that urban development charge mean?**

Within the framework of the public-private partnership, the partial plan establishes commercial development and a redistribution of public and private spaces to ensure the functioning of the integrated system. The station is organized as a ring around the shopping center and we contribute that sum in construction as part of the developer's obligations, a figure similar to urban development charges in renewal projects in Bogotá.

**Will the station be ready at the same time as the shopping center in 2028?**

Yes. The first phase of the station has a construction period of 18 months, so it is expected to be operating by the end of the current administration.

**Are there international benchmarks for this model?**

At the international level, this model of integration between transportation and commerce is widely proven. In Asia there are emblematic cases.
