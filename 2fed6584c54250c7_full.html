New laws tighten controls on social housing misappropriation, though loopholes remain

27 April 2026 · 09:09 PM

# New laws tighten controls on social housing misappropriation, though loopholes remain

Social housing policy in São Paulo has gained a series of laws, decrees, and ordinances in recent years to prevent misappropriation of dwellings. However, loopholes remain that make it difficult to punish past deviations and monitor the current market, according to lawyers.

The topic gained visibility with a Parliamentary Commission of Inquiry (CPI) at the Municipal Chamber to investigate the irregular production and sale of Social Interest Housing (HIS). These dwellings have tax exemptions to serve low-income families. However, there have been cases where apartments ended up in the hands of investors and hospitality websites, being occupied by higher-income people.

HIS emerged in the 2014 Master Plan. Almost a decade later, in 2023, there was an update that reinforced the allocation of these dwellings for social purposes. At that time, the construction of buildings classified as HIS in noble neighborhoods, such as Itaim and Vila Olímpia, was already drawing attention from investors.

## New legal framework

Between 2024 and 2026, three decrees were published to detail the obligations associated with this type of housing. Decree 63.130/2024 established the need to include in the property deed that the property is classified as HIS and must be occupied by families with proper income certification. The measure aimed to close loopholes against transfers to other audiences.

The decree also defined the Housing Department (Sehab) as responsible for monitoring commercialization. Until then, there was no one responsible for this.

Decree 64.244, from 2025, expressly prohibited these apartments from being used for short-term rentals, as happens on Airbnb, Booking, among other platforms. Another important point was limiting the rental value to a maximum of 30% of family income.

New requirements also emerged to ensure transparency in sales. Construction companies were required to inform stands and promotional materials that the development is of the HIS type. There have been cases where consumers were induced to sign contracts without proper knowledge.

More recently, decree 64.895, from 2026, set maximum prices for HIS apartments, ranging from R$ 276,000 to R$ 537,000, according to family income brackets, ranging from three to ten minimum wages.

The new legal framework did not prohibit investors from buying these units. To clarify doubts, Sehab published Ordinance 61, from 2024, pointing out the criteria for families "eligible for HIS allocation," not the public for acquisition. That is: investors can buy as many apartments as they want, as long as they rent them to low-income families.

## What lawyers say

"The regulation came to correct the course of the policy and restore the guarantee of correct allocation of properties, which was the objective of the CPI," says lawyer Beatriz Bueno de Moraes, consultant at the Levy and Salomão law firm. "The updates to the law aim to prevent homes from being used for other purposes," adds Angela Di Franco, partner at the same firm. The pair do not work on any cases in this area.

In their assessment, the law was vague in the early years, giving loopholes for investors to buy apartments and later resell or rent them — which is heavily criticized by city councilors at the CPI. "There was a loophole, but no prohibition. And what is not written is not prohibited. Back then, no one thought about this," considers Beatriz.

Because of this, the CPI should be unable to punish these cases, they say. "There is no infraction in this context," points out Angela.

The possible indictment of companies should be limited to cases where there was proven deviation from public policy, such as allocating apartments to hospitality websites with use by higher-income people. "If the property ended up on Airbnb at some point, then there was indeed a mischaracterization of public policy," says Angela.

Another problem was the sale of properties without contracts clearly stating that this was social housing with restrictions on property use. This resulted in lawsuits that led to compensation for buyers. Some cases have already reached the second instance, which confirmed the right to compensation. "Buyers who feel harmed by lack of information can request reimbursement," adds Angela.

## Difficulties in practice

Even after gaining an extensive legal framework, HIS policy remains with challenges for implementation, says lawyer Daniel Cardoso Gomes, partner at Amatuzzi law firm and consultant to the Construction Union (Sinduscon-SP). One of them is monitoring whether such dwellings are actually being occupied by low-income families. "If the owner and resident are inspected and don't have documentation proving they meet the income requirement, they will have to answer for it. The problem is how the department will manage to conduct this on-site inspection, as there is a very large number of dwellings," he says. "This is a major difficulty in implementing the public policy."

Gomes also points to a "limbo" in bureaucracy regarding the obligation to register the HIS regime in the property deed. "The requirement for registration came from municipal law, but this was not translated into federal law, which deals with property registration," he notes. "There are cases where people cannot make this registration with some registrars. A huge legal limbo was created that has not been completely resolved yet."

## What the Housing Department says

Sehab reported that monitoring is conducted through data crossing, document analysis, and investigation of complaints. When there are signs of irregularity, administrative processes are initiated, with formal requests for information and documents from companies and responsible parties.

Based on technical analysis, which covers contracts, cadastral data, and other records, the department verifies the classification of units. If violations are found, it takes appropriate measures, including warnings and application of sanctions.

Currently, the municipality has 934 ongoing processes related to these types of developments, involving 159,026 housing units. As a result, 24 processes have already been concluded with fines totaling R$ 7.7 million, according to the department.
