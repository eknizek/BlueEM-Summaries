# Brazil's Risk Perception Improves Amid Global Instability

The recent positive assessments of the Brazilian economy, coming from the International Monetary Fund (IMF), Wall Street analysts, and Brazilian economists, may surprise those who until recently received a flood of adverse or, at best, neutral reports about the country's situation.

Much of the change in opinion can be explained by the context of war in the Middle East and market turbulence. It is a good moment to ask, in relative terms, whether Brazil improved or the world deteriorated. In my view, both perspectives converge. Brazil is more resilient to acute external factors, and this is not new. We are no longer an economy where a cold in some country would arrive here as severe pneumonia.

The world suffers from successive paradigm breaks. Some strongholds of global financial flows, such as the dollar and U.S. Treasury bonds, have become equivalent to common assets, subject to the volatility inherent in risk markets. In Brazil, this context has produced a benign cycle. The expectations have concrete reasons.

In April, after a ten-year absence from the European market, the National Treasury raised €5 billion. The IMF, in its Global Economic Perspective report, revised upward the country's growth projection for 2026, while reducing that for the global economy. At the IMF meetings in Washington, Brazil was one of the most sought-after countries by analysts and investors.

During this troubled period marked by wars and uncertainties, votes of confidence from investors and qualified observers acquire significance. The change in perspective attests to the evolution in the country's economic profile. To meet global demand, agricultural production breaks productivity records every year. For more than two decades, we have been among the world's three largest food suppliers. The consolidation of the pre-sal and Petrobras' professionalization have made the country self-sufficient in oil. In exchange rates, we have a free movement regime, and in monetary policy, a conservatism that allows the beginning of the inflation inflection curve due to signals of real economy penalization. Brazilian society is friendly toward technological advances and open to innovation. Brazil is a pole of security and reliability.

The challenge now is to ensure that the virtuous cycle becomes a long and consistent journey.
