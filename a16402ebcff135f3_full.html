# This Healthtech is Reinventing Workplace Healthcare

With over 35 million beneficiaries and 10 thousand companies in its customer base, Conexa uses data intelligence and continuous monitoring to anticipate diseases, reduce emergency room usage and cut costs in the corporate system.

The service operates 24 hours a day, with access via WhatsApp, app or website.

The traditional model of medical care in Brazil follows a well-known logic. Faced with any complaint, even the simplest ones, the patient almost always resorts to the hospital. The result appears mainly in the increasingly high costs of health plans and consequently for companies. In recent years, however, technology has begun to drive changes in the sector. The digitalization of access to health services, combined with intelligent use of data, has opened space for new ways of caring for corporate health. It was in this context that Conexa was born in 2017, today the country's largest digital ecosystem for physical and mental health. The company's solutions indeed deviate from the traditional standard. Rather than acting merely as an intermediary between patients and providers, Conexa acts with corporate health management, anticipates risks and acts before simple health problems advance to more complex—and therefore more expensive—conditions.

"For a long time, healthcare was fragmented," says Guilherme Weigert, CEO of Conexa. "An employee would go to the hospital, then to a cardiologist, look for a nutritionist elsewhere, and none of that communicated with each other. We came to change that cycle."

The proposal caught the attention of global investors such as Goldman Sachs, Vivo Ventures, General Atlantic and Patria Investimentos, and the funds raised were used for the company's growth and acquisitions like Zenklub, a startup specializing in corporate mental health. Today, Conexa serves over 35 million beneficiaries and has in its portfolio more than 10 thousand companies, 200 health operators and 40 distribution channels—among health cards and flexible benefits platforms—with clients such as Procter & Gamble, Havan, Afya, Klabin, Azul, Positivo, Grupo UOL and Volkswagen.

"Our model doesn't see the patient as a user of a service, but as someone who needs to be followed over time," says Gabriel Garcez, VP of Health at Conexa.

It was from this logic that Hospital Conexa was born, the company's most recent—and most ambitious—bet. "Hospital Conexa functions as the entry point for the entire health journey of the employee," affirms the executive. The service operates 24 hours a day, with access via WhatsApp, app or website. Among other solutions, it offers digital quick care, family doctor, specialist consultations and exam referrals.

When contracting the service, the company gains a dedicated clinical team for its employees, with more than 35 specialties in physical and mental health and a network of 6 thousand health professionals.

"We rescued that old concept of 'my' occupational doctor," says the VP. "But in the digital world."

When the family doctor cannot resolve the case remotely, the patient is referred to a specialist. For simple exams, such as dengue, Covid or blood pressure tests, they are directed to partner pharmacies or laboratories. With this model, only 2% of cases end up in the emergency room, compared to 6% recorded before adopting the system.

For the employee, the solution ensures faster access to care and quality assistance. For the company, it guarantees greater control of costs and claims, with less waste.

Garcez reinforces a central point: the system reduces excessive emergency room usage for low-complexity cases, which increase costs without improving patient health. "Imagine that 85% of people who go to a physical emergency room are there for low or medium complexity complaints and wouldn't need a hospital resource," says the executive. "That's exactly the space Hospital Conexa occupies."

The employee served by Conexa also has a health team with nursing professionals and other specialists, who guide the patient's next steps and help with scheduling, putting the employee on a healthcare journey that includes issues like sleep and stress, among others.

For companies with operations spread across the country, this tends to standardize care without the need to maintain medical structures in each unit.

"The trend is that corporate health will increasingly migrate to integrated models, in which technology and clinical coordination work together to generate real value for both companies and employees," emphasizes CEO Guilherme Weigert.

Conexa reports that its clients register, on average, a 16% reduction in per capita claims, a 20% decrease in exam performance and a 6% decrease in hospitalizations. Garcez also cites the use of artificial intelligence to identify signs of mental distress and direct employees to specialized care. The same applies to chronic diseases, such as diabetes and hypertension, which can be detected earlier.

Azul, Brazil's largest airline by number of cities served, with 137 destinations, demonstrates how Conexa's solutions are effective. With employees distributed throughout the country and varied work schedules, Azul always had difficulty ensuring medical care in traditional models. With Hospital Conexa, employees now have access to care regardless of where they are. In March of this year, 75% of elective consultations requested by the company were conducted digitally.

"It's a consistent evolution in the way we care for our people, with safety, more access, continuity and quality assistance," says Antonio Dibai, Director of People at the airline.
