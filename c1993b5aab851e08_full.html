# Brazil Lags Behind and Widens Per Capita GDP Gap Against Global Average

The Brazilian has fallen behind over the last 45 years. Trapped in the so-called middle-income trap, the country has seen global per capita Gross Domestic Product (GDP) growth consistently outpace its own pace. Worse: recent economic performance has been insufficient to reduce this gap.

Between 1980 and 2025, global per capita GDP rose from $3,380.47 to $26,188.94, an increase of 675%, according to International Monetary Fund (IMF) data. In the same period, Brazil went from $4,427.94 to $23,380.98, a rise of 428%. The numbers are calculated in Purchasing Power Parity (PPP), which makes comparison between countries and economic groups fairer. In practice, the indicator shows that the purchasing power of Brazilians has grown less than the global average over the past decades.

Per capita GDP shows the average wealth available per inhabitant of a country. In Brazil, the slower pace compared to other nations reflects low productivity, insufficient investments, and a still complex business environment. This is compounded by advances falling short of what is needed in education and innovation.

The world's per capita GDP has been higher than Brazil's since 2015, when the country began facing a harsh economic recession that dragged on until 2016. In that two-year period, the Brazilian economy contracted more than 3% each year.

"This scenario has to do with our stumbles, with this economic instability that translates into periods of faster growth and then periods of deceleration and even recession," says Alessandra Ribeiro, director of macroeconomics and sector analysis at Tendências Consultoria. "There is no sustained growth in a continuous manner."

From 1980 to 2025, Brazilian per capita GDP also grew less than advanced economies — which rose from $10,327.44 to $74,516.33, a growth of 621% — and less than emerging economies, which went from $1,499.81 to $18,413.23, an increase of 1,128%.

## Brazil's Breakdown

Another study conducted by Sergio Vale, chief economist at MB Associados, using another database from the Penn World Table (PWT), also helps highlight Brazil's lag relative to the world. He identified a "breakdown" in Brazil's growth pace starting in 1981.

"We couldn't recover that growth momentum we had until the 1970s," says Vale.

The economist's study sheds light on Brazilian growth compared to economies that had similar performance until the 1980s — and those with the closest characteristics were South Korea, Romania, and Botswana. In 2023, the PWT found that Brazil's per capita GDP was $18,492. This number is 42% lower than it would have been had the country kept pace with the growth rate of these nations — the average Brazilian income would be $13,400 higher and would have reached $31,900.

"It wouldn't be high income — which is $50,000, $60,000 and up — but it would be an income that would place the country at the threshold of escaping the middle-income trap and having a condition to be considered a higher-income country," says the chief economist at MB Associados.

The 1980s are known as the lost decade for Brazil. The country dealt with problems in the external sector — including debt default — and with hyperinflation, which was only tamed with the Real Plan in 1994.

What stands out is that the difference between current per capita GDP and what would be observed without the rupture has grown over the years. In 1981, when there was a breakdown in Brazil's growth pace, this difference relative to the three similar countries identified by the study was 7.3%. In a short time, by 1985, it rose to 19.2%.

"From the 1980s until the Real Plan, there were almost 15 years of deep crisis. Right from the start, we lost all those years," says Vale. "From the Real Plan onwards, it was playing catch-up. We made several reforms, but we didn't advance in various others."

## The Causes of Brazilian Lag

Indeed, there are several factors that explain low Brazilian productivity. They range from an economy poorly integrated with the rest of the world to an unfriendly and expensive business environment, as well as inadequate economic incentives and low workforce qualification.

Between 1950 and 1980, Brazil benefited from an import substitution model and the migration of resources from the countryside to industry, which helped raise the country's productivity at the time and consequently allowed the Brazilian economy to grow at more robust rates.

"The problem is that when the 1980s arrived, that model was exhausted. Brazil was no longer a low-income country, but a middle-income one. From the 1980s onward, the challenge became different: it was no longer about resource transfer between sectors, but about increasing productivity within each sector," says Fernando Veloso, research director at the Institute for Mobility and Social Development (Imds).

"From there, Brazil needed to increase productivity mainly in the services sector, because it's the most important. Today, 70% of GDP and employment are concentrated in the services sector. But sector data shows that the productivity of industry and the services sector hasn't grown since 1995," adds Veloso.

Economies that managed to surpass the middle-income bracket and achieve higher levels invested in human capital, built solid institutions, made efficient resource allocation, and integrated into global supply chains.

"Brazil missed the golden period of globalization. The country opened up commercially in the first half of the 1990s and remained more or less in line with other emerging economies. But then it did nothing more. I even think there were setbacks, because there were many protection policies for local content for the oil sector, for the naval industry," says Veloso.

"Only now came the agreement between Mercosur and the European Union, which is certainly positive, but it's still too little. The country missed the globalization boat and risks missing the artificial intelligence boat," he concludes.
