# Rising fertilizer costs hitting rice farmers: interview with Fedearroz leader

"It is not profitable for farmers to plant rice right now," says Rafael Hernández, president of the agricultural association.

Rice farmers continue to demand solutions to achieve profitable harvests. In addition to the low prices they have been complaining about over the past year, the sector faces problems such as smuggled grain entering from Ecuador and fertilizers needed for harvest that are becoming increasingly expensive due to the war in the Middle East.

In an interview with EL TIEMPO, Rafael Hernández, president of the National Federation of Rice Growers (Fedearroz), explains how this situation is affecting them. "A sustained increase in production costs can affect the profitability of the producer and, ultimately, the stability of national production," he assures.

**Rice growers have been asking for quick solutions for months to guarantee rice commercialization. You even requested the Government remove 250,000 tons. Has the uncertainty passed?**

What we are asking for is the return of storage incentives, which is the mechanism designed in the 1990s to remove temporary rice surplus from the market that is generated by harvest seasonality, especially in the Eastern Plains, and that was commercialized in the first semester of the following year when inventories decreased. It was a resource paid to the industry to remove about 250,000 tons for four or five months and thus not affect prices due to temporary oversupply. However, this Government eliminated it. We have been insisting that it be used again because its non-use has caused prices to fall. Added to this is that globally there has been rice oversupply due to high inventories, since India released much of what it had stored, which affected prices. Overall, national prices have fallen 10 percent in one year. I would say they are even below production costs and it is not profitable to plant right now.

**In February, the Government issued two resolutions to help rice growers solve commercialization problems. Did it solve the problems?**

It set minimum prices, which has prevented them from falling further but also hasn't allowed farmers to make a fair profit on their harvests. I would say they have remained stable, but at low levels.

**Do you think there is a risk of a rice farmer strike this year, as in previous years?**

Strikes occur precisely because of low prices. When the farmer starts losing money, he becomes desperate. Even though the government has decreed minimum prices, these are not enough for the farmer to cover production costs. So that is what generates uncertainty and desperation.

Rice is one of the products that has had the most difficulty in commercialization, partly due to concentration in just four large companies. How is the border closure with Ecuador and mutual tariffs affecting you?

We requested that rice be included in commercial retaliation measures indefinitely. Additionally, one of the things that most affects the local industry is the illegal entry of Ecuadorian rice. What we ask is that what enters legally arrives by sea to a single port to prevent illegal smuggling by land transport, but this is a measure that was taken late because more than 100,000 tons had already entered. This problem is a threat. The Ecuadorian government subsidizes rice production, but the aid given here is not sufficient to achieve competitiveness.

**What is the impact of the Middle East war on the harvest?**

It is affecting us in a very important way because rice is a crop that demands a lot of fertilizer and urea comes from that region. Approximately 25 percent of production costs come from fertilizers. We are already seeing increases of 40 to 50 percent in price. I would say that for the rice sector, a sustained increase in fertilization costs can affect producer profitability.
