# Rising fertilizer costs are hitting rice farmers: interview with Fedearroz leader

"It is not profitable for farmers to plant rice at this moment," says Rafael Hernández, president of the federation.

Rice farmers continue requesting solutions to achieve profitable harvests. In addition to the low prices they have been complaining about over the past year, the sector faces problems such as smuggled grain entering from Ecuador and fertilizers needed for the harvest that are becoming increasingly expensive due to the war in the Middle East.

In an interview with EL TIEMPO, Rafael Hernández, president of the National Federation of Rice Farmers (Fedearroz), explains how this situation is affecting them. "A sustained increase in production costs can affect producer profitability and, ultimately, the stability of national production," he assures.

**Rice farmers have been asking for quick solutions for months to guarantee rice commercialization. You even requested that the Government remove 250,000 tons. Has the uncertainty passed?**

What we are asking for is the return of the storage incentive, which is the mechanism designed in the 1990s to remove temporary rice surplus from the market that is generated by harvest seasonality, especially in the Eastern Plains, and which was commercialized in the first semester of the following year when inventories decreased. It was a resource paid to industry to remove about 250,000 tons for four or five months and thus not affect prices due to temporary oversupply; however, this Government eliminated it. We have been insisting that it be used again because its non-use has caused prices to fall. In addition, globally there has been an oversupply of rice due to high inventories, as India released much of what it had stored onto the market, which affected prices. In total, national prices have dropped 10 percent in one year. I would say they are even below production costs and that it is not profitable to plant at this moment.

**In February, the Government issued two resolutions to help rice farmers solve commercialization problems. Did it solve the problems?**

It set minimum prices, which has prevented them from falling further but also has not allowed farmers to obtain fair profits from their harvests. I would say they have remained stable, but at low levels.

**Do you think there is a risk of a rice farmer strike this year, as in previous years?**

Strikes are generated precisely by low prices because when the farmer starts losing money, he becomes desperate. Although the government has decreed minimum prices, these are not enough for the farmer to cover his production costs. So that is what generates uncertainty and desperation for them.

**Rice is one of the products that has had the most difficulty in commercialization, among other reasons, due to concentration in just four large companies. How is the closure of the border with Ecuador and mutual tariffs affecting you?**

We have requested that rice be included in commercial retaliation measures indefinitely. Additionally, one of the things that most affects the local industry is the illegal entry of Ecuadorian rice. What we are asking for is that what enters legally arrives by sea at a single port to prevent it from being smuggled by land transport, but this is a measure that was taken too late because more than 100,000 tons had already entered. This problem is a threat. The Ecuadorian government subsidizes rice production, but the aid given here is not sufficient to achieve competitiveness.

**What is the impact on the harvest from the war in the Middle East?**

It is affecting us in a very important way because rice is a crop that demands a lot of fertilizer and urea comes from that region. Approximately 25 percent of production costs come from fertilizers. We are already seeing increases of 40 or 50 percent in prices. I would say that for the rice sector, a sustained increase in fertilization costs can affect the profitability of producers.
