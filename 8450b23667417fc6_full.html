# Trump's Energy Shock Will Escalate

The fundamental issue is that the crisis is changing in nature – for something much worse

By José Roberto Mendonça de Barros

02/05/2026

This week, the Middle East conflict completed two months with still no prospect of agreement between the parties. There is broad consensus among analysts that discussing the terms of a ceasefire itself will require time, involving many complex points, such as the nuclear question.

Furthermore, President Trump does not have a clear strategic vision, alternating different objectives with the military campaign. This is what we see now, with the determination to impose a blockade on all ships leaving Iranian ports. The agenda for discussions will keep changing over time, prolonging the impasse. The fact that American negotiators are amateurs complicates the situation.

But now it is not just a matter of more time before we see relief in the scenario. The fundamental issue is that the crisis is changing in nature – for something much worse.

In the initial weeks of the conflict, the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz produced an interruption in the flow of petroleum and derivatives, and a sharp increase in energy prices. This raised inflation worldwide, but still without interruption of productive and commercial flows. But this was possible thanks to the use of oil from reserves in consuming countries, including product that was in ships en route to their destinations.

Now, the level of available petroleum is beginning to dangerously dwindle. Several countries are blocking exports of many products to protect their domestic market, as China and Russia have already done with refined products and fertilizers. Worse than that, in many consuming countries, with the end of available petroleum reserves on the horizon, governments will be forced to ration its use, as Sweden has already pointed out.

The signal will be one of physical scarcity and reduced production of many goods, pointing toward a recessionary horizon ahead. In this case, the oil price will take a new leap, reaching the range of $120-150 per barrel, with significant additional inflationary pressures. We will also see a reduction in agricultural production, due to restrictions on the use of fertilizers and fuel to operate machinery.

The current energy shock would change in quality and would be complete: fuels, petrochemicals, fertilizers, and food – which are also a form of energy. Its consequences would be enormous, pushing the world toward a scenario of high inflation and probable recession. It is becoming increasingly difficult each day to avoid a dramatic outcome of the crisis produced by Trump.
