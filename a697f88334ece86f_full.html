# Amstel Targets Gen Z with Low-Alcohol Portfolio and Social Media Strategy

Amstel, a Heineken brand, is reorganizing its communication strategy in Brazil to reach Generation Z consumers—a demographic that, according to the company itself, is more oriented toward moderation in alcohol consumption. The company's response comes on two fronts: expanding its portfolio with low-calorie and alcohol-free products, and adapting brand language to formats native to social media.

On the portfolio side, Heineken Zero leads the alcohol-free beer segment in Brazil and is growing in double digits. Amstel Ultra, a version with fewer calories and gluten-free, is also experiencing accelerated expansion. The company has also launched Sol Zero and Praya Zero as part of the movement. "We offer options for this audience to be present in the way they choose to relate to us," says Fernanda Saboya, marketing director for Heineken in Brazil.

In communications, the brand uses social media as monitors for trends and strategy testing. Focus groups and online behavior tracking guide campaign development. It was in this context that a vertical novel distributed on TikTok emerged—a format chosen for its adherence to Generation Z's content consumption and for allowing a more immersive narrative than traditional 15 or 30-second pieces.

The influencer strategy is treated as part of brand positioning, not merely as media. "We seek to bring influence not just as a point of contact, but as part of the brand's DNA," says Saboya, citing concerns about diversity in the profile of hired creators. Amstel positions itself as a broad-appeal brand within Heineken's portfolio, but the growing focus on young people reflects intensifying competition in the sector.

Competitors are also investing in zero labels and reformulating their communications in response to a generation that, according to research cited by the executive, drinks less and demands more from the products they consume. According to the Brazilian Beer Industry Association, sales of the country's most consumed alcoholic beverage fell between 6.5% and 7% in 2025 compared to the previous year. National and international research also shows declining interest among young people in this habit.
