# Superintendencies Must Be Technical Bodies, Not Political: Billy Escobar, Former Superintendent of Corporations

Billy Escobar Pérez closes a long and atypical cycle leading the Superintendency of Corporations. In the midst of one of the worst recent business crises—the post-pandemic period—his administration focused on changing the supervisor's role to be less judge and more accompanying. The balance he leaves combines recovery figures, technological advances, and a vision in which the State cannot arrive late nor use sanctions as a first response. However, he also launches forceful warnings: improper practices in insolvency, institutional weakness in regions, and actors who still view the system as a business.

In an interview with EL TIEMPO, he presented a balance that is not self-congratulatory. While he recognizes important advances, he also admits that the country must still strengthen ethics and institutional coordination to protect its business fabric.

**Why do you step aside at this moment?**

Like any public official, I always have my resignation at the President's disposal. But beyond that, I feel I have completed a cycle. In fact, I have been the superintendent with the most continuity in recent years. But there comes a time when one understands that the mission is accomplished and it is time to open other stages. I believe I fulfilled my duty to the country, to society, and to business.

**How long were you in office?**

Four years and eight months. I started on January 1st of the last year of (Iván) Duque's government and continued during the current administration until today.

**What is the greatest change you leave at the Superintendency?**

Changing the way we view companies. We broke with that vision in which the Superintendency was distant, centralized, and in many cases disconnected from the territory. We understood something basic: the superintendent serves all companies in the country, not just large ones or those in Bogotá. That's why we took the entity to much of the regions like never before.

**Did that transform the relationship with the business sector?**

Undoubtedly. Before they saw us as the police or the judge. Today they see us as a helping hand, as an entity that accompanies. And that was key because we arrived at the worst moment: the post-pandemic period. We received a business sector that was battered, in crisis, and our response could not be punitive. It had to be supportive.

**How do you leave Colombian business today?**

It must be said clearly: micro, small, and medium-sized enterprises are the country. They represent about 40 percent of GDP, 90 percent of companies, and 80 percent of employment. In many regions, they are practically 100 percent of economic activity. That shows there is a living business fabric, but also a very vulnerable one.

**What do those companies need?**

Real accompaniment. Not just supervision. They need financing, institutional coordination, and State presence in their territories. The entrepreneur in regions is often alone. And that's where the State needs to arrive to provide support.

**Does centralism still weigh heavily?**

Yes. There is still a tendency to view business from the capitals. And that is a mistake. Regional entrepreneurs have maturity, have capacity, and should be treated as equals.

**How damaging is the vision of the Superintendency as a police entity?**

It's a contradiction. We are a technical body, not politicized, whose purpose is for companies to be viable and sustainable. It doesn't make sense for the language to be coercive. It should be preventive, pedagogical, and accompanying. In any risk system—and superintendencies must be technical bodies, not political.
