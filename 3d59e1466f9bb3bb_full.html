# Cost to Import Gasoline Rises 61% Since Start of Iran War, Says ANP

The cost of importing gasoline has risen 61% since the start of the war in Iran, according to an estimate by the ANP (National Agency of Petroleum, Natural Gas and Biofuels). According to market assessment, any eventual reduction in tax on the product would open space for price adjustments at Petrobras refineries.

The state-owned company has not yet changed gasoline prices since the conflict began, but the sector expects an announcement of price increases soon after a possible tax cut, as occurred with diesel, which rose R$ 0.38 per liter at refineries after PIS/Cofins exemption. The increase was slightly larger than the R$ 0.32 per liter that the government gave up by zeroing federal taxes on the fuel.

In an interview on Thursday (23rd) about using extraordinary revenues obtained from high oil prices to reduce taxes on gasoline and ethanol, government representatives did not discuss specific amounts.

The import parity price for gasoline calculated by the ANP rose 60% between the week before the first attacks by the United States and Israel on Iran and last week, rising from R$ 2.45 to R$ 3.95 per liter. The import parity is a simulation of how much it costs to import fuel.

At market opening on Thursday, the average price of gasoline at Petrobras refineries was R$ 1.51 per liter below the import parity measured by Abicom (Brazilian Association of Fuel Importers).

Petrobras did not respond to a request for an interview on the subject. It normally states that its pricing policy does not provide for immediate tracking of international market volatility. Changes are approved, according to the company, when quotations stabilize at new levels.

In the market, there is almost consensus that international prices will remain elevated for the coming weeks, given uncertainties about the direction of the conflict between the United States, Israel and Iran. Even with the end of the war, the scenario tends to take some time to normalize.

According to analysts at Scotiabank, for example, a diplomatic success in ending the conflict would take Brent oil to between US$ 80 and US$ 85 per barrel — on Thursday, the quote was above US$ 100. Even in the scenario predicted by the bank, quotations are higher than the previous projection of US$ 60 to US$ 70 per barrel.

Diesel became a more urgent problem in the first weeks after the war because Brazil imports about 30% of domestic demand. With the risk of private import cuts, the government moved quickly to enable price adjustments with less impact on final prices. The government zeroed federal taxes and created a subsidy program that guarantees R$ 1.52 per liter for companies that import the fuel and sell it below a price cap established by the ANP. The program still raises questions and does not have the presence of major companies in the sector.

Gasoline imports represent only 10% of demand and the product can be replaced by ethanol in case of reduced supply. But the duration of the war is already beginning to pressure Petrobras' margins, as it is the country's main seller of the product. Furthermore, even in years without war, the second quarter is a period of seasonal price increases for gasoline, with the formation of inventories to supply the summer vacation season in the United States.
