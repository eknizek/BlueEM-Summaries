# Can Countries Enrich Themselves by Exporting People Rather than Goods?

Much of Kerala's oil comes from coconuts. Yet the southern Indian state, famous for its idyllic backwaters, aromatic cuisine and laid-back lifestyle, owes much of its prosperity to barrels of foul-smelling crude oil that gushes from the soil in the Persian Gulf and is normally shipped through the Strait of Hormuz.

Since the Middle Eastern oil boom began half a century ago, Keralites have migrated there for work, first as cleaners and construction workers, then as clerks, nurses and salespeople. An estimated 1.7m of them live in the Gulf, equivalent to 5% of the state's population and nearly 11% of its workforce. Gulf oil money has transformed Kerala. KP Kannan and KS Hari, from the Centre for Development Studies (CDS), an Indian think tank, calculated that in the mid-2010s, remittances from the region amounted to about a quarter of the state's output, exceeding both the value added in manufacturing and government spending. This has lifted living standards. Per capita consumption is almost three-quarters higher than the Indian average. Multidimensional poverty, an Indian measure of wretchedness, affects about one in ten Indians, but is virtually non-existent in Kerala.

Some economists argue that enriching oneself through industrialisation and exporting manufactured goods is harder nowadays than when Europe, Japan, South Korea and, more recently, China managed it. For countries like India, capturing a larger export market for manufactured goods in an already saturated world requires eliminating a competitor. When that competitor is China, good luck with that.

Could exporting people instead of goods, as Kerala does, offer an alternative path to prosperity? Many poor regions depend on emigrants. The World Bank estimates that remittances sent by them represent more than a fifth of national income in Honduras, Lebanon, Nepal and Tajikistan. In low- and middle-income countries as a whole, they constitute a third of all capital inflows. In Nepal, they may have reduced poverty rates by 40% between 2001 and 2011. In Mexico, they reduced infant mortality. The effect of remittances on economic growth, however, is less clear. A 2013 study of African, Asian and Latin American countries that are sources of migrants found that a permanent 10% increase in remittances per citizen was associated with a 0.13% increase in per capita GDP. More recent research from 2022 suggested a somewhat less modest effect of 0.66%.

In a new study, Charles Kenny, of the Centre for Global Development, a think tank in Washington, finds practically no relationship between the size of a country's diaspora and the growth of its per capita GDP. After all, emigration can be as much a result of weak economic growth, which prompts people to emigrate in the first place, as a cause of economic acceleration.

Kenny still believes that emigrants can, in some circumstances, be an economic stimulus. It all depends on the nature of emigration's spillover effects. These can be positive if they increase the average human capital in the country of origin and it is leveraged domestically. They can also perpetuate the very problems that lead people to emigrate. A recent study published in the Journal of Economic Perspectives, authored by Gaurav Khanna of the University of California, San Diego, examines waves of Asian migration to the United States. Some of these waves, Khanna argues, facilitated "brain circulation" rather than "brain drain". Many Indians, for example, obtained software engineering degrees in hopes of seizing lucrative opportunities in Silicon Valley. Some were "drawn in the lottery" of immigration and moved to San Francisco, but many others remained in India. This created a new export industry, able to operate not only in person through migration, but also remotely, from locations like Bangalore in Karnataka state. India's IT exports are now worth more than $220bn annually, more than the $135bn the country raises in total from remittances.

In Kerala, by contrast, spillover effects appear scarce. The state outperforms the rest of India in literacy and longevity, thanks to a legacy of enlightened princely rule before independence and large public investments in education and health promoted by left-wing governments subsequently. But potential investors are discouraged by these same governments' anti-capitalist economic policies and the state's excessively powerful trade unions. Like IT workers elsewhere in India, Kerala has many health professionals who failed to emigrate, but many nurses are on strike demanding higher wages. Companies struggle to meet salary expectations inflated by connections to wealthy Gulf employers in the labour market. As a result, firms prefer to locate in more business-friendly places, like neighbouring Tamil Nadu state.

Much of Kerala's windfall from remittances is spent on building houses and buying cars, which raises living standards but not productivity. Investment in children's education, also financed by Gulf money, does make Keralites more productive. But as long as local opportunities to put that productivity to use remain scarce, the brightest graduates will keep trying their luck abroad.

Perhaps the biggest drawback of emigration-driven growth is that its success depends on factors beyond the control of the country of origin. This is true to some extent for any economic activity. But it is easier for manufacturers facing tariffs in one market to find another than for emigrants to move somewhere else when economic or political circumstances in their temporary home change. Consider Kerala again. The number of Keralites in the Persian Gulf has stalled in recent years, as countries in the region reserve certain jobs for their citizens. Their remittances could fall 20% this year as spillover effects from war with Iran damage the region's economies. Emigration almost always benefits emigrants and their families in Kerala. Relying on it for broader growth amid growing fragmentation, protectionism and conflict is risky.
