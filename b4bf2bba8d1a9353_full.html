# 'Art of the Deal': Americans Close $2.8 Billion Rare Earth Deal for Brazilian Lands

While some Brazilian politicians begin discussing the creation of a state-owned rare earth company, Americans have gradually been securing control over the country's first reserve of these minerals. USA Rare Earth announced today an agreement to acquire 100% of the mining company Serra Verde, with operations in Goiás, for $2.8 billion – nearly 14 billion reais at today's exchange rate. The buyer of the Brazilian assets received a $1.6 billion injection from the Trump Government at the beginning of the year – and the United States has gained an equity stake of approximately 10% in the company.

Serra Verde itself had been moving closer to the U.S., with a $565 million loan from a Washington agency, the US International Development Finance Corporation, which also involved an offtake agreement for future production. The Trump administration, eager for alternatives to China in rare earth supply, negotiated an agreement to guarantee the purchase of 100% of the initial production from the Brazilian industry pioneer. This 15-year agreement was signed by Serra Verde with a vehicle capitalized by U.S. government agencies and private investors, according to USA Rare Earth CEO Barbara Humpton. She assessed that the deal "demonstrates the global importance" of the asset.

The CEO also said that the purchase of reserves in Brazil is "a transformational step" in the American company's ambition to become a "global champion" in rare earths. The USA Rare Earth transaction for Serra Verde involves $300 million in cash and the remainder in new shares to be issued by the company, which is listed on Nasdaq. The deal is expected to close in the third quarter, subject to regulatory approvals.

Valued at $4.35 billion before the M&A, USA Rare Earth projects that production in Brazil will reach by the end of 2027 levels sufficient to generate annualized EBITDA of between $550 million and $650 million. Beyond the first phase of its projects, expected to be 100% operational by the end of next year, Serra Verde has "significant growth potential with its phase 2," according to the Americans. USA Rare Earth expects to achieve, with its operations and new reserves in Brazil, annualized EBITDA of $1.8 billion by the end of 2030.

While the Americans do business, Brazil has brought the rare earth issue into the arena of political polarization. The PT caucus, the party of President Lula, presented a bill to create "TerraBras" – a state company to "manage critical and strategic minerals and guarantee national sovereignty over these resources." In a text defending the proposal, the PT said that Lula's likely rival in this year's presidential elections, Flávio Bolsonaro, "signaled the intention to hand over the exploitation of these minerals to the U.S." The PT project would create a mineral exploration sharing model, similar to that used with pre-salt oil, so that the Government is a direct partner of mining companies, receiving part of the production.

Setting aside political questions, the BTG Pactual team is optimistic about the development of a rare earth supply chain in the West, and sees potential for Brazil to become an important player in this market. "We believe this could mark the beginning of a larger wave of transactions in the sector, with early-stage companies increasingly becoming targets for acquisitions, especially as projects advance to production," wrote analysts Leo Correa, Marcelo Arazi, and Rodrigo Gotardo.

For now, Serra Verde is the only active rare earth mine in Brazil, with most new projects expected to begin operating after 2028. Producing since 2024, the pioneering mine in Goiás has received investments from two American private equity firms, Denham Capital and Energy & Minerals Group, and British Vision Blue. Production has been sold to Asia – under a contract that expires at the end of 2026 and will be replaced by the agreement with the Americans.
