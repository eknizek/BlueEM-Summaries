# 'Art of the Deal': Americans Close Billion-Dollar Deal for Brazil's Rare Earths

While some Brazilian politicians begin discussing the creation of a state-owned rare earths company, Americans have gradually been securing control over the country's first reserve of these minerals. USA Rare Earth announced today an agreement to acquire 100% of Serra Verde mining company, with operations in Goiás, for US$ 2.8 billion – nearly R$ 14 billion at today's exchange rate. The buyer of Brazilian assets received a US$ 1.6 billion injection from the Trump Government earlier this year – and the United States now holds approximately 10% ownership in the company.

Serra Verde itself had been moving closer to the U.S., with a US$ 565 million loan from a Washington agency, the US International Development Finance Corporation, which also involved an offtake agreement for future production.

The Trump administration, eager for alternatives to China in rare earths supply, secured an agreement to guarantee purchase of 100% of the Brazilian pioneer's initial production. This agreement, for a 15-year term, was signed by Serra Verde with a vehicle capitalized by U.S. government agencies and private investors, according to USA Rare Earth CEO Barbara Humpton. She assessed that the deal "evidences the global importance" of the asset.

The CEO also stated that the purchase of reserves in Brazil is "a transformational step" in the American company's ambition to be a "global champion" in rare earths. The USA Rare Earth transaction for Serra Verde involves US$ 300 million in cash and the remainder in new shares to be issued by the company, which is listed on Nasdaq. The deal is expected to close in the third quarter, subject to regulatory approvals.

Valued at US$ 4.35 billion before the M&A, USA Rare Earth estimates that production in Brazil will reach by end of 2027 a level sufficient to generate annualized EBITDA of between US$ 550 million and US$ 650 million. Beyond the first phase of its projects, expected to be 100% operational by the end of next year, Serra Verde has "significant growth potential with its phase 2," according to the Americans. USA Rare Earths expects to achieve, with its operations and new reserves in Brazil, annualized EBITDA of US$ 1.8 billion by end of 2030.

While Americans do business, Brazil has brought the rare earths issue into the arena of political polarization. The PT caucus, the party of President Lula, presented a bill creating "TerraBras" – a state company to "manage critical and strategic minerals and guarantee national sovereignty over these resources." In a text defending the proposal, the PT stated that the probable rival of Lula in this year's presidential elections, Flávio Bolsonaro, "signaled the intention to hand over the exploitation of these minerals to the U.S."

The PT project would create a mineral exploration sharing model, similar to that used with pre-sal oil, so that the Government would be a direct partner of mining companies, receiving part of the production. (Are the Americans following this discussion?)

Political questions aside, the BTG Pactual team is optimistic about the development of a rare earths supply chain in the West, and sees potential for Brazil to become an important player in this market. "We believe this could mark the beginning of a larger wave of transactions in the sector, with companies in early development stages increasingly becoming acquisition targets, especially as projects advance toward production," wrote analysts Leo Correa, Marcelo Arazi, and Rodrigo Gotardo.

For now, Serra Verde is the only active rare earths mine in Brazil, with most new projects scheduled to begin operating after 2028. Producing since 2024, the pioneering mine in Goiás received investments from two American private equity firms, Denham Capital and Energy & Minerals Group, and British Vision Blue. Production has been sold to Asia – under a contract expiring at the end of 2026 and to be replaced by the agreement with the Americans.
