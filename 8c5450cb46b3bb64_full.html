# Only four provinces created jobs in January as 100,000 positions lost nationwide

The economic model being shaped by Javier Milei's government is betting that sectors linked to natural resources—agriculture, mining, oil and gas—will generate the dollars needed to buy abroad products that local industry cannot manufacture competitively. While the equation in foreign currency might work out, economists are not so sure the same will be true for employment.

The sectors that must take off do not generate as many jobs nor as quickly to compensate for those being lost in industry, a labor-intensive sector that accounts for nearly 19% of formal employment, the second largest after commerce (20%). On the contrary, those the Government wants to promote—mainly through RIGI (the fiscal benefits regime)—are mining, steel and hydrocarbons, with a participation of less than 7% in registered private employment. Agriculture contributes 5.1%.

"The key question would relate to the spillover capacity of the few vigorous productive sectors to the rest, which we see as unlikely," says consultancy LCG in a report.

According to January data released this week by the Labor Secretariat, in year-on-year comparison, the largest drops in registered wage employment were seen in industry: it lost 39,808 positions, a decline of 3.4%. Textiles, with 13,000, and metalworking, with 9,900 positions, were the hardest hit. Commerce followed, with 13,143 fewer positions, a decline of 1 percent.

Beyond a reformatting of employment by sector, a new map would emerge, with winning and losing provinces. "To illustrate: in the last 12 months there were 7,500 new registered employees in Neuquén, Río Negro and San Juan, against a drop of 100,000 in the rest of the provinces," LCG details.

These three provinces are linked to the boom of the Vaca Muerta gas and oil megafield and mining production.

The provinces that lost the most employment in January 2026 vs 2025 were: Buenos Aires: 23,300; CABA: 23,000; Chubut: 6,400; Mendoza: 6,300; Santa Cruz: 5,100; and Salta: 5,000.

Those that gained employment were: Río Negro: 3,200; Neuquén: 3,000; San Juan: 1,300; and Santiago del Estero: 500.

These three provinces, along with Santiago del Estero, were the only ones that in January managed to increase the amount of registered employment: Río Negro added 3,200 positions; Neuquén, 3,000; San Juan, 1,300; and Santiago del Estero, 500.

On the other hand, those that lost the most employment in absolute terms were Buenos Aires (with a decline of 23,300), CABA (23,000), Chubut (6,400), Mendoza (6,300), Santa Cruz (5,100) and Salta (5,000).

For consultancy LCG, this dual economic dynamic, with winning sectors (generators of foreign currency) and losing sectors (generators of employment) is not "an innocuous 'draw'." "The anti-employment bias of this mix could further erode legitimacy, and without legitimacy, increase 'political risks,' which generates a renewed impact on economic activity," it speculates.

During Milei's administration, five provinces managed to increase employment levels: Río Negro, Corrientes, Chubut, Mendoza and Neuquén, according to work by consultancy CEPA. A second group reduced employment between 0.01% and 5%: Chaco, Misiones, Tierra del Fuego, San Juan, Buenos Aires, Córdoba, Santa Fe, Salta, Entre Ríos, Catamarca, CABA, Jujuy and Tucumán. And the most affected, with declines of more than 5%, were La Rioja, Formosa, Santiago del Estero, Santa Cruz, San Luis and La Pampa.
