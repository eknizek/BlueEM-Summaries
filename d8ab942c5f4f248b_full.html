# After acquiring Empírica, SRM enters debt market and targets R$ 500 million

SRM is launching its debt capital market (DCM) area to diversify its operations beyond investment funds in credit rights (FIDCs) — a segment where the asset manager has reached approximately R$ 2 billion in assets under management. The expectation is to conduct around R$ 500 million in operations in the first year of operation, taking advantage of the favorable moment in private credit, with issuances hitting records in recent years.

"We began to see that many of the clients we work with in receivables were entering the capital markets with another manager, another firm," says Eduardo Siqueira, director of SRM Asset, to NeoFeed. "With some of these clients we've been working for ten years; it should be us doing this."

SRM's DCM vertical will operate with instruments such as debentures, Real Estate Receivables Certificates (CRIs) and Agribusiness Receivables Certificates (CRAs), with operations ranging from R$ 50 million to R$ 200 million. The focus is to serve middle market companies, whose annual revenue ranges between R$ 200 million and R$ 700 million — the same audience SRM serves with its FIDCs, although it is open to serving companies that earn somewhat less or more.

The idea is also to serve the venture capital area, SRM Ventures, which has 16 fintechs in its portfolio. Siqueira says the decision to enter DCM also aims to defend the receivables area, given the assessment that capital market operations reduce how much SRM can earn from its traditional operation.

"If part of the receivables goes to a capital market operation, my volume can decrease," he states.

SRM began structuring the DCM area in the first half of 2025, organically, with some hiring. The vertical gained momentum with the arrival of Empírica, which belonged to Reag and was acquired by SRM last year. Leonardo Calixto, one of Empírica's founding partners, heads the capital markets area as CEO of SRM Empírica.

"We saw the possibility of accelerating the process by buying a manager that knows the capital markets well," says Siqueira. "We had the strategy [to enter the capital markets] and we saw the opportunity with Empírica."

SRM's debut in DCM occurs at a time when the debt market is booming. Data from the Brazilian Association of Financial and Capital Markets Entities (Anbima) show that fixed income again concentrated the largest portion of fundraising and maintained its predominance in 2025, with R$ 737.7 billion, a 3.4% increase. With interest rates at historically high levels, causing investors to look more at fixed income, smaller companies are increasingly accessing the capital markets. Banks, asset managers and investment advisory firms are watching this niche.

Recently, Banco Pine announced its capital markets operation, targeting mid-sized and large companies. Last year, BS2 announced its own vertical, also targeting the middle market, which often gets left aside by major banks due to low issuance amounts.

To stand out, Siqueira says SRM will leverage the reach of its operations to advance its DCM area. The asset manager has 18 branches spread across the country in the Center-West, Southeast and South regions, and 300 employees who originate operations. The manager also has infrastructure for credit analysis and a commercial team.

"We have a structure much more similar to that of a bank than to that of a traditional asset manager," he says. "The branches have commercial managers who work alongside companies daily to originate the receivables we operate with."
