# After Acquiring Empírica, SRM Enters Debt Market and Targets R$500 Million

SRM is launching its debt capital market (DCM) area to diversify its operations beyond credit receivables investment funds (FIDCs), a segment where the asset manager has reached approximately R$2 billion in assets under management. The expectation is to conduct around R$500 million in operations in the first year of activity, taking advantage of the favorable moment in private credit, with issuances hitting record levels in recent years.

"We began to see that many of the clients we work with on receivables were entering the capital markets with another manager, another firm," says Eduardo Siqueira, director of SRM Asset, to NeoFeed. "We've been with some of these clients for ten years; we should be doing this."

SRM's DCM vertical will operate with instruments such as debentures, Real Estate Receivables Certificates (CRIs) and Agribusiness Receivables Certificates (CRAs), with operations ranging from R$50 million to R$200 million. The focus is to serve companies in the so-called middle market, whose annual revenue ranges between R$200 million and R$700 million, the same audience that SRM serves with its FIDCs, although it remains open to serving companies that earn slightly less or more.

The idea is also to serve the venture capital area, SRM Ventures, which has 16 fintechs in its portfolio. Siqueira says the decision to enter DCM also aims to defend the receivables area, given the assessment that operating in the capital markets reduces how much SRM can earn from its traditional operations.

"If part of the receivables goes to a capital markets operation, my volume could decrease," he states.

SRM began structuring the DCM area in the first half of 2025, organically, with some hiring. The vertical gained strength with the arrival of Empírica, which belonged to Reag and was acquired by SRM last year. Leonardo Calixto, one of Empírica's founding partners, heads the capital markets area as CEO of SRM Empírica.

"We saw the possibility of accelerating the process by buying a manager that knows the capital markets well," says Siqueira. "We had the strategy [to enter the capital markets] and we saw the opportunity with Empírica."

SRM's debut in DCM occurs at a time when the debt market is booming. Data from the Brazilian Association of Financial and Capital Markets Entities (Anbima) show that fixed income again concentrated the largest share of fundraising and maintained its predominance in 2025, with R$737.7 billion, a 3.4% increase. Amid interest rates at historically high levels, causing investors to look more toward fixed income, smaller companies are increasingly accessing the capital markets. Banks, asset managers and investment advisory firms are eyeing this niche.

Recently, Banco Pine announced its capital markets operation, looking at medium and large companies. Last year, BS2 announced its own vertical, also targeting the middle market, which ends up being overlooked by large banks due to low issuance amounts.

To stand out, Siqueira says SRM will leverage the reach of its operations to advance its DCM area. The asset manager has 18 branches spread across the country, in the Center-West, Southeast and South regions, and 300 employees who originate operations. The manager also has infrastructure for credit analysis, plus a sales team.

"We have a structure much more similar to that of a bank than to that of a traditional asset manager," he says. "The branches have sales managers who are with the day-to-day operations of companies to originate the receivables we operate."
