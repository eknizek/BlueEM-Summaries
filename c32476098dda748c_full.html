# Wheat prices surge 30% due to Middle East crisis, hitting highs since June 2024

Two months after the outbreak of hostilities in the Middle East and with peace negotiations underway for twenty days, the international scenario continues to be marked by uncertainty. Far from normalization, global trade remains affected by restrictions in the Strait of Hormuz, which remains practically impassable: in the last sixty days, barely 356 ships managed to enter or exit, close to half of the usual weekly transit.

In this context, the rise in energy costs—with Brent above US$107 per barrel—is already fully impacting the global economy and agricultural production costs. The wheat market is one of the most sensitive to this new scenario, according to a report from the Rosario Stock Exchange. It is a crop highly dependent on nitrogen fertilizers, whose prices have skyrocketed in line with energy. This has begun to be reflected in production decisions: in Australia, where planting has already begun, surface reductions of between 4% and 12% are projected, while in Argentina a reduction in area is also anticipated shortly before the campaign begins.

In the United States, the situation adds another pressure factor, the Rosario Stock Exchange added: drought has affected winter wheat for weeks. Currently, only 19% of the area shows no water stress, while 35% of crops are in poor or very poor conditions, 15 percentage points above last year.

All this occurs in a campaign that is already shaping up as the one with the smallest sown area since official records began. This combination radically changed market expectations. From a scenario of global abundance with comfortable stocks, the possibility of supply cuts emerged. The reaction was immediate: wheat in Chicago rose from US$190 per ton at the end of January to US$246 in the last week, a jump of 30% and the highest level since June 2024.

In Argentina, the trend follows but with particularities. Wheat futures advanced between 12% and 17% since January, with the July 2026 contract gaining US$25 per ton. However, the most relevant data is the change in market structure: from an "inverted" scheme to a "carry" one until January 2027. This reflects the expectation of tighter supply in the future, encouraging the hoarding of merchandise.

In parallel, the milling industry faces an increasingly complex scenario. To problems with wheat quality, with shortages of batches suitable for bread-making, are now added difficulties in obtaining volume. During the first four-month period of the 2025/26 cycle, milling reached 1.96 million tons, barely 1% more than the previous year, despite a record harvest of 27.9 million tons, 50% higher than the previous campaign.

"Milling growth is far from reflecting the magnitude of the harvest," warned Diego Cifarelli, who also noted that "it was never projected that, with a historic production, we could face supply problems." According to him, although mills are willing to pay better prices for quality wheat, the merchandise does not appear in the necessary volumes.

By April 15, exports had acquired 14.47 million tons, well above the 9.66 million from the previous year, while milling barely added 2.60 million, practically unchanged year-over-year.

Among the possible causes of this dynamic are mentioned the greater liquidity of producers from corn sales, also with record harvest, and the deterioration of wheat's purchasing power against the increase in inputs.

The prospects ahead are generating concern in the sector. Official projections estimate milling of 7.2 million tons for the 2025/26 cycle, above previous years. However, the industry warns that if current conditions persist, that objective will not be met.

"This means less Argentine work and less added value at origin," Cifarelli summarized.
