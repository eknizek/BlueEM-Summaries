# Luciano Laspina: "More than structural reforms, Argentina needs basic political agreements to build confidence"

For the economist and newly appointed Executive Director of the public policy think tank Cippec, the great challenge is "finding a diagonal that can unite the two Argentinas that have confronted each other throughout our history." He points out that "changes must be made from the foundations and not from the ruins."

**—How does Cippec, a public policy think tank, operate before a Government that believes the main public policy is stability and disdains the rest?**

—The role of think tanks in the world, and of Cippec in particular, is to be at the frontier of discussion to contribute ideas to politics. We believe there is a set of reforms that are urgent but have been postponed over time, about which much has been written and very little has been done, such as pension reform, tax reform, changes to the federal fiscal regime, among others, and which are now on the Government's agenda awaiting execution. Some because there is no fiscal space, others because there isn't sufficient political support. We think that this debate about the present agenda is necessary and is on the table of both the ruling party and the opposition with other visions, even though they have different perspectives. We believe Cippec's role is to lift our gaze somewhat toward the long term and start thinking about the Argentina of the future. The future agenda.

**—Before the future, how do you find present-day Argentina?**

—Argentina is crossed by two forces. One is the stabilization process, which is still underway, and the second is structural reform, moving from a closed, hyper-regulated economy to an open and deregulated one. And that, obviously, generates many tensions in different sectors. The great challenge is finding a diagonal that can unite the two Argentinas that have confronted each other throughout our history, which never agreed with each other, and that diagonal consists of seeing how we transform those enormous comparative advantages we have, starting from winning sectors, like energy, mining and agriculture, and how we bring other sectors that are now losing—particularly industry—aboard that growth train. There are many models in different parts of the world that managed to connect those sectors to the great engines with supplier development, value chains in services and goods downstream. It seems to me that is the path we must try to follow, with public policies that do it efficiently, with the least degree of interventionism possible, without resorting to the simple strategy of raising tariffs to make domestic goods more expensive, because countries that have managed to develop are those that have been able to chain, convert those initial comparative advantages of natural resources into competitive advantages to exploit global demand.

—These are processes that take time and require support, and that doesn't seem to be the case in Argentina...

—The change from the current economic structure to the new economy should be done on the foundations and not on the ruins of the current economy. Because it is much more efficient to build on our companies. The foundations are our companies, our workers, the capacities that have been created, the physical capital that has been accumulated. Everything should be put in service of a productive reconversion focused on exploiting global demand or conquering markets based on our comparative advantages. And it's possible to do it. It takes time, you have to put your head to it, you have to put passion into it, but it's the future agenda we have ahead.

—Can you give examples of countries that achieved this?

—It took Australia two or three decades to gradually open its economy and try to transform industrial sectors into suppliers for its industries, and to try to conquer markets. Ireland is another case. Poland is an economy that integrated into the European Union, which is our new commercial partner, integrated into its value chains, invested in infrastructure and in twenty years doubled its income. There are many examples of country transformations that achieved it. The great challenge is economic, but also political, which is to be able to unite those two Argentinas. And today we have the opportunity to do it, all conditions are in place.

—There's always talk of opportunity, why this time?

—We have a world that demands much of what we produce, there is Argentine talent, and a series of historic trade agreements with the European Union and eventually with the United States, which allow the country to integrate into the world in an intelligent way, combining its natural resources with its business talents, with its human resource talents, to do what the countries that managed to grow sustainably over time did, which is basically to have a fiscally ordered economy, a stable macroeconomy, but above all, an economy prepared to compete with the world. Not only to defend itself from the world, but also to go out and conquer the world.

**—Is it possible with social inclusion?**

—It's necessary. And I want to cite the example of Neuquén, in which behind the Vaca Muerta project the government, unions, companies and civil society aligned behind a dream of progress. We should replicate it for harmonious growth at the federal level, which generates jobs that will allow the inclusion of all of society in a progress project we never had. We never had a common project in Argentina like the one Neuquén has today, which is a very simple case. That dream of progress shouldn't have winners or losers or victors or vanquished, but should include all sectors, in some way. Each one within its possibilities, in a federal process. That's Argentina's challenge.

**—What role do private actors have?**

—Productivity is the responsibility of companies, and another part of the State in its role as provider of the quality public goods and services needed to compete with the world. Infrastructure of global scale, a reasonable tax system, education integrated with production, and a set of reasonable regulations, within the framework of a rule of law that guarantees things Argentina never had, which are quite basic, like contract continuity.

**—In that sense, the past condemns us for breach of contracts...**

—Argentina needs more than reforms, it needs agreements. In the last fifteen years we've had an economic crisis, on average, every two years. Crises generate instability in the rules of the game and, in some cases, contractual breaches. Argentina needs to have, at minimum, basic political agreements that prevent Argentines from suffering a crisis on average every two years. And the conditions are in place. Those agreements should be minimum agreements on three aspects that would prevent a crisis. First, continuity of respect for contracts. Second, commitment to fiscal balance. And third, fundamental, renunciation of monetary financing to the treasury. These three failures have led us, for different reasons, to different types of crises, generating legal uncertainty in investments. If we achieve it, I don't say that growth and development are guaranteed, but we will avoid a crisis and take a great leap in investor confidence.
