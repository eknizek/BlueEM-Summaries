# Agribusiness leaders criticize end of 6x1 work schedule in Uberaba

Agribusiness leaders gathered this Saturday (25) at the opening of Expozebu in Uberaba, in the Triângulo Mineiro region, criticized the proposal by President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's (PT) government to end the 6x1 work schedule in the country.

The main national livestock event, the fair is organized by ABCZ (Brazilian Association of Zebu Breeders), with 22,000 members, and annually holds a livestock exhibition. This year it expects more than R$200 million in negotiations involving cattle across 41 auctions and 11 animal shopping centers running until May 3.

Arnaldo Manuel de Souza Machado Borges, president of the association, stated in his opening speech—which included presidential candidates Ronaldo Caiado (PSD), former governor of Goiás, and Romeu Zema (Novo), former governor of Minas Gerais—that it is necessary to discuss "with due seriousness" issues such as ending the 6x1 schedule, considering "all possible consequences."

"For the proper functioning of Brazil's productive sector economy, we ask our parliamentarians to support opposition to this agenda so harmful to our economy, with serious and unprecedented consequences to our agriculture," he said, receiving applause from the audience of industry entities and livestock farmers.

Tirso Meirelles, president of Faesp (Federation of Agriculture and Livestock of São Paulo State), also questioned the federal government's proposal, criticizing current public policies and stating there are other priorities before debating changes to working hours.

"We collect R$3 trillion in taxes, we spend more than five months paying taxes, and what is brought to society? More taxes. There's a tax reform that will concentrate even more resources in the hands of the federal government, making it difficult for municipalities and governments. And then simply someone goes on TV and says, 'You, simpler society, are working so much that you don't have time for your children.' Fix transportation, provide security, you need to resolve the country's infrastructure first instead of touching the 6x1," he said.

He praised Caiado and Zema for entering the presidential pre-candidacy race, saying they put their names forward during a difficult moment of polarization in Brazil, and stated that it is necessary to create a country project.

"South Korea, in 1965, produced 25% less GDP than Brazil. After 60 years, they grew 700%, Brazil grew 100% [...] I want to thank Caiado and Zema for offering your names so we can listen to you, so we can follow the debates, so we can build the country's structure," he said.

Currently, the government and Congress are disputing details of the text and procedural format of the work schedule reduction proposal in the Chamber. On Wednesday (22), the Constitutional and Justice Committee of the House approved a favorable report for the processing of the PEC (Constitutional Amendment Proposal) for the 6x1 schedule. The PEC is a proposal format with slower processing that cannot be vetoed by the president.

Because of this, the government defends that the proposal be processed as a PL (bill), contrary to the intentions of Chamber president Hugo Motta (Republicans-PB). The text approved on Wednesday does not address the amendment's content, only the constitutionality of the proposal.

Two PECs are being processed together, from deputies Reginaldo Lopes (PT-MG) and Erika Hilton (PSOL-SP), proposing a reduction in weekly working hours from the current 44 to 36 hours. Hilton's proposal also changes the schedule, setting it at 4 working days with 3 days off. This work schedule design is considered outdated by the government, which has been advocating for a 40-hour weekly limit without fixing a schedule regime, to be left for negotiations between categories and businesses.

Reducing weekly working hours is one of the government's bets to improve President Lula's popularity in an election year.

## CATTLE ON THE RISE

The livestock exhibition in Uberaba takes place this year when cattle prices are valued, with an 11.18% increase over one year in the price of an arroba (15 kilos), according to Cepea (Center for Advanced Studies in Applied Economics) from Esalq/USP. On Thursday (23), it was quoted at R$362.40, compared to R$325.95 twelve months earlier.

Held since 1935 in the Minas Gerais city, the fair, considered the world's largest zebu cattle exhibition, reaches its 91st edition expecting to receive 400,000 visitors until May 3, with a program that includes animal judging, dairy competition, commercial area, international business rounds, 11 animal shopping centers, and 41 auctions—two more auctions and two more shopping centers than in 2025.
