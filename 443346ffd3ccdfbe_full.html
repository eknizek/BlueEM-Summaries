Argentina's Economy Between Recovery Hope and Concerns of Fiscal Cliff

# Argentina's Economy Between Recovery Hope and Concerns of Fiscal Cliff

Wall Street banks and local analysts highlight Argentina's financial and exchange rate resilience in a volatile global climate due to the Middle East crisis and domestic political uncertainty. But the fall in tax collection and the need to maintain the fiscal adjustment committed by President Milei generate doubts about what kind of recovery will come.

A report from Itaú bank this week states that the Argentine economy is at the threshold level with the Merval in dollars near 2,000 points. The Brazilian bank highlights this moment even though in the recent past the stock index already touched these levels but amid a political climate of lower uncertainty and larger macroeconomic imbalances as with Mauricio Macri in 2017 or Javier Milei himself practically a year ago. Fundamentally, the bank wonders what would happen to stock valuations without Adornigate.

"What distinguishes this cycle from previous ones is that Argentina is not just a story of reforms but of exports. Unlike the enormous external imbalances that accompanied the country whenever companies and the stock index reached those peaks, this time Argentina arrives with a small current account deficit and being a net oil exporter," says Itaú.

The risk of a financial and exchange rate crisis as the country has registered so many times is now lower, admits Marina Dal Poggetto, an economist critical of the Government. Of course, this does not mean that foreign dollars will immediately flow into the economy. Central Bank statistics on foreign direct investment since Javier Milei took office show a decline of $535 million versus more than $100 billion in investment announcements through RIGI and others for the next five years according to abeceb.com.

Not only is there a clear dissociation there, but moving from 0 to 100 billion in five years would mean almost like transporting yourself to Jeddah, Saudi Arabia in the '70s, as recounted by former Washington Post journalist Paul Blustein in his latest book (Dollar King), and seeing how a country grew from an oil shock that opened the floodgates of a wave of dollars in a short time. Will Argentina manage to be such a hub? Part of the market is hopeful.

Itaú notes that the company Adecoagro "deserves greater attention following the acquisition of Profertil, Argentina's only urea producer, because urea production requires high gas consumption, which links Profertil's profitability to increased domestic gas supply. It is expected," adds Itaú, "that Argentina will represent approximately 50% of Adecoagro's Ebitda in the future, which will increase its exposure to two of the country's strongest sectors: agriculture and energy-driven growth."

One of the greatest surprises of the current energy shock, the most serious since the 1970s, is the resilience shown by emerging and developing economies like Argentina so far. It is estimated that the closure of the Strait of Hormuz removed almost 13 million barrels of oil per day from the global energy supply, and yet stock indices in many countries, from the United States to Brazil and through Korea, have registered new highs. One explanation is that energy dependence adjusted for inflation to generate dollars fell by a third in the U.S. and almost 40% in China since 2000, according to the World Bank and IMF.

Thus, the global shock may have more impact in terms of the real sector than the financial sector for Argentina. While Fiel's chief economist Daniel Artana estimates it would reduce growth by one percentage point, this Tuesday the debt rating agency Fitch improved the country's rating to B-.

Thus, attention is already focused on what comes next. Or in terms of what Itaú says, is Argentina positioned to cross the 2,000 point threshold of the Merval in dollars in this context? It will be key that disinflation stabilizes, something that could occur with the next data (April) the following week. For Martín Rapetti of Equilibra, it will be 2.4% (March was 3.4%). For the Government, the year in terms of inflation is already decided; the Budget target (10%) will not be met, and the idea is maturing that inflation this year will be similar to last year with the consolation that the December monthly figure will start with a one.

Today, what the market is watching most is whether activity rebounds or not, rather than how much inflation falls. For example, the Government decided to grant an extraordinary gas subsidy to the poorest. "Javier Milei is in serious trouble," The Economist said this Tuesday. "Today the economy is depressed or in recession," says Rapetti.

For Artana, "the first quarter in terms of activity was bad because digesting the backlog in people's debt payments takes time. But in the second quarter it will rebound because interest rates have fallen and in the second half the recovery will continue." According to Miguel Kiguel, "in the first quarter activity will end without growth compared to the last quarter of last year; we expect the economy to start growing again in the second quarter."

Reactivation is crucial for people's pockets, something the IMF Board is closely watching so that Washington "continues to provide support." But recovery also has an importance of a mechanical character if you will, something that is more abstract but no less important for politics and that the market pays attention to: if there is more movement, more money on the street, tax collection goes up more (nine consecutive months of decline discounting inflation) and that helps Milei, governors and mayors adjust less.

In the agreement with the IMF, the target is a surplus of 1.4% of GDP. But according to a study by the Capital Foundation, "it will be complex" to sustain that objective given the fall in revenues, because it will require compensating on the spending side. "If realized, public spending would be at its lowest level in the last ten years, which presents a challenge for some items."

The fiscal issue is key because if Milei prides himself on anything, it's the failed stabilization experiences of the past that loosened or flexibilized fiscal adjustment and which, paraphrasing Gerchunoff-Llach, started as an illusion but ended with the disappointment of confirming that it is not possible to sustain the effort to prevent the economy from falling down a waterfall—not Manuel Adorni's waterfall, but the waterfall of our macroeconomy.
