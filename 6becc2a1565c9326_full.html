# BTG raises $1.24bn for reforestation fund, prioritizes Cerrado

BTG has raised $1.24 billion for its forest restoration fund, the Timberland Investment Group (TIG), marking the largest fundraising effort to date for this purpose and exceeding its initial $1 billion target. The funds will be used to restore and conserve approximately 270,000 hectares across Latin America, with the Brazilian Savanna (Cerrado) as a priority biome. Returns on the investment will come from the sale of carbon credits verified by Verra.

The fundraising attracted several development finance institutions, including the Brazilian Development Bank (BNDES), which committed up to R$300 million; the Development Bank of Latin America and the Caribbean (CAF); Germany's development finance institution DEG; the Dutch entrepreneurial development bank FMO; the International Finance Corporation (IFC)—the World Bank's private-sector arm; the Impact Fund Denmark; the Mobilising Finance for Forests program funded by the UK and Netherlands governments; New Zealand's sovereign wealth fund (New Zealand Superannuation Fund); and the San Francisco State University Foundation. Private investors also participated, including Mitsui O.S.K. Lines, Australia's NGS Super fund, insurer Tokio Marine & Nichido Fire Insurance Co., and Brazil's Vale. Green investment organizations GenZero, Green Spark Ventures, and the Hempel Foundation also joined the funding round.

"We designed this strategy to demonstrate that landscape-scale restoration can deliver both environmental and commercial results," noted Gerrity Lansing, head of BTG Pactual TIG, in a statement.

TIG's restoration strategy is managed by Conservation International, which provides support and advisory services to strengthen environmental, climate, and social outcomes. The strategy incorporates IFC performance standards.

TIG has already begun restoring more than 20,000 hectares of degraded pastureland, planting around 29 million trees in Mato Grosso do Sul and Minas Gerais. In addition, it maintains another 20,000 hectares under conservation, according to BTG.

The project, known as "Brazil Cerrado 1," has been certified by Verra and is already eligible to issue 230,120 verified carbon units (VCUs). Over its 20-year certification cycle, the project is expected to remove 7 million tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent from the atmosphere.

Brazil Cerrado 1 was the first project in the world to receive credits under Verra's afforestation, reforestation, and revegetation (ARR) methodology. This methodology is among those approved by the Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM), the governance body for the voluntary carbon market, under its Core Carbon Principles (CCPs), which define criteria for classifying high-integrity carbon credits.

In areas already under conservation, forests are certified under Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) standards or are expected to achieve certification by the end of 2025.

To support these efforts, TIG has mobilized local groups, such as the Native Seed Network of Mato Grosso do Sul, and trained 26 new collectors, gathering 5.6 tonnes of native seeds. According to BTG, seed sales to nurseries and restoration projects have generated income equivalent to about 20% of the average regional household income per collector.

Through its tree-planting and conservation activities, the fund has already supported 518 direct and indirect jobs and, once fully implemented, is expected to support 2,700 such jobs. The bank noted that job creation is significantly higher than under previous land uses, such as cattle ranching.

TIG also partners with the Forest Restoration Laboratory at the Federal University of Viçosa (UFV) in Minas Gerais to test restoration methods—including direct seeding, planting native seedlings, and natural regeneration techniques—across an 81-hectare area.

TIG has already committed to selling 8 million carbon credits to Microsoft in 2024 and at least 1.3 million credits to Meta, the parent company of Instagram.
