# Colombia's Government is Indebting the Country at Rates Double Those Previously Paid to the IMF

Despite President Gustavo Petro having called several times a "foolishness" the credit that Colombia took with the International Monetary Fund (IMF) during the pandemic disaster, today his Government is taking on debt at rates double those of that credit, which has already been paid off.

This Tuesday, at a forum convened by the Ministry of Finance, the minister, Germán Ávila, said that debt had finished being paid. The decision to replace IMF multilateral financing with debt issuance in private capital markets has created a distortion in the cost of credit that now pressures the State's accounts.

According to specialists, the Nation has moved from paying historically low interest rates, protected by the backing of international organizations, to negotiating commercial obligations under conditions that are almost double as costly. The rates being paid for external debt in dollars have increased and have reached 6.5 percent.

"If we had access to that credit at this moment, with the SRI rate, which is the rate handled by the Monetary Fund, plus the spread it charges for this flexible credit line, plus a commission, it gives me that we would pay between 3.3 and 3.4, versus a bond that is at 6.5 percent," detailed Felipe Campos, Investment and Strategy Manager at Alianza Valores and Fiduciaria.

This means current interest rates are double what was subscribed to in the IMF credit. "If today Colombia could take the same IMF loan from 2020, it would pay a rate of 3.4-3.5 percent for 5 years, with principal payments concentrated in years 4 and 5 (in equal quarterly installments). The credit disbursement would be immediate and without any conditionality imposed by the IMF. In contrast, today the country would pay a rate of 6.1 percent for 5 years or 5.5 percent for three years in dollars in international markets," added Andrés Pardo Amézquita, Head of Macro Strategy for Latin America at XP Investments.

In pesos, last week the Government committed to paying up to 13.8 percent in a TES auction. According to Campos, "in pesos it is 12.5 to 13.5 percent. In dollars between 4.5 and 6.5 percent."

For experts, this shift not only alters the country's maturity profile but represents a million-dollar overcost in debt service that limits the room for maneuver for public investment in the coming years.

For Campos, the core of the problem is that the government has "canceled" or allowed the expiration of a benefit that has no comparison in the open market. The expert maintains that if the National Government had not renounced this credit, today it would have the possibility of requesting the same 5 billion dollars that were managed under the previous administration, but paying a rate close to 3.5 percent. In contrast, current emissions in dollars force payment of 6.5 percent.

"We're talking about a difference of 300 basis points that costs the country close to 2 trillion pesos additional per year," emphasizes Campos, underscoring that this money comes directly from Colombian taxpayers' taxes.

One of the debate points is the supposed imposition of structural conditions by the IMF. However, Pardo clarifies that the instrument Colombia possessed was unique in its class. "Colombia had the Flexible Credit Line (FCL), which is like a Platinum credit card, the most luxurious of the Fund."
