# Colombia government borrowing at double the IMF rates, creating annual surcharge

Despite President Gustavo Petro having called the credit that Colombia took from the International Monetary Fund (IMF) during the pandemic a "nonsense" on several occasions, his Government is now taking on debt at rates double those of that credit, which has already been paid off.

This Tuesday, at a forum convened by the Ministry of Finance, the head of that portfolio, Germán Ávila, said that debt had been finished being paid. The decision to replace multilateral financing from the IMF with debt issuance in private capital markets has generated a distortion in the cost of credit that today pressures the State's accounts.

According to specialists, the Nation has moved from paying historically low interest rates, protected by the backing of international organizations, to negotiating commercial obligations under conditions that are almost double as expensive. The rates being paid for external debt in dollars have increased and have reached up to 6.5 percent.

"If we had access to that credit at this moment, with the SRI rate, which is the rate the Monetary Fund handles, plus the spread it charges for this flexible credit line, plus a commission, it gives me that we would pay between 3.3 and 3.4 percent, versus a bond that is at 6.5 percent," detailed Felipe Campos, Investment and Strategy Manager at Alianza Valores y Fiduciaria.

This means current interest rates are double what was subscribed with the IMF credit. "If today Colombia could take the same IMF loan from 2020, it would pay a rate of 3.4-3.5 percent at 5 years, with principal payments concentrated in years 4 and 5 (in equal quarterly installments). The credit disbursement would be immediate and without any conditionality imposed by the IMF. In contrast, today the country would pay a rate of 6.1 percent at 5 years or 5.5 percent at three years in dollars in international markets," added Andrés Pardo Amézquita, Head of Macro Strategy for Latin America at XP Investments.

In pesos, last week, the Government committed to paying up to 13.8 percent in a TES auction. According to Campos, "in pesos it is 12.5 to 13.5 percent. In dollars between 4.5 and 6.5 percent."

For experts, this shift not only alters the country's maturity profile but represents a costly surcharge in debt service that limits the margin of maneuver for public investment in coming years. For Campos, the core of the problem is that the government has "canceled" or allowed the expiration of a benefit that has no comparison in the open market. The expert argues that if the National Government had not renounced this credit, it would today have the possibility of requesting the same $5 billion that was managed under the previous administration, but paying a rate close to 3.5 percent.

In contrast, current dollar emissions require paying 6.5 percent. "We are talking about a difference of 300 basis points that costs the country approximately 2 trillion pesos additional per year," emphasizes Campos, underscoring that this money comes directly from Colombian taxpayers' taxes.

One of the debate points is the supposed imposition of structural conditions by the IMF. However, Pardo clarifies that the instrument Colombia possessed was unique in its class. "Colombia had the Flexible Credit Line (FCL), which is like a Platinum credit card, the most luxurious of the Fund," he explained.
