# The Government is Indebting Colombia at Rates Double Those Paid to the IMF

Despite President Gustavo Petro repeatedly calling the credit Colombia took from the International Monetary Fund (IMF) during the pandemic a "foolishness," his Government is now taking on debt at rates double those of that already-repaid credit.

This Tuesday, at a forum convened by the Ministry of Finance, Finance Minister Germán Ávila announced that the IMF debt had been fully paid. The decision to replace multilateral financing from the IMF with debt issuance in private capital markets has created a distortion in credit costs that now pressures the State's accounts.

According to specialists, the Nation has moved from paying historically low interest rates, protected by the backing of international organizations, to agreeing to commercial obligations under conditions that are nearly double as costly.

External debt rates in dollars have increased and reached 6.5 percent. "If we had access to that credit right now, with the SRI rate, which is the rate the Monetary Fund handles, plus the spread it charges on this flexible credit line, plus a commission, I calculate we would pay between 3.3 and 3.4 percent, versus a bond at 6.5 percent," detailed Felipe Campos, Investment and Strategy Manager at Alianza Valores y Fiduciaria.

This means current interest rates are double what was subscribed to with the IMF credit.

"If today Colombia could take the same IMF loan from 2020, it would pay a rate of 3.4-3.5 percent over five years, with principal payments concentrated in years 4 and 5 (in equal quarterly installments). The credit disbursement would be immediate and without any conditions imposed by the IMF. In contrast, today the country would pay a rate of 6.1 percent over five years or 5.5 percent over three years in dollars in international markets," added Andrés Pardo Amézquita, Head of Macro Strategy for Latin America at XP Investments.

In pesos, last week the Government committed to paying up to 13.8 percent in a TES auction. According to Campos, "in pesos it is 12.5 to 13.5 percent. In dollars between 4.5 and 6.5 percent."

For experts, this shift not only alters the country's maturity profile but represents a million-dollar surcharge in debt service that limits room for maneuver for public investment in coming years.

For Campos, the core of the problem is that the government has "canceled" or allowed the expiration of a benefit with no comparison in the open market. The expert argues that if the National Government had not renounced this credit, it would today have the possibility of requesting the same 5 billion dollars that were managed under the previous administration, but paying a rate close to 3.5 percent.

In contrast, current dollar issuances require paying 6.5 percent. "We are talking about a difference of 300 basis points that costs the country approximately 2 trillion pesos annually," Campos emphasizes, underlining that this money comes directly from Colombian taxpayers.

One point of debate is the supposed imposition of structural conditions by the IMF. However, Pardo clarifies that the instrument Colombia possessed was unique in its class. "Colombia had the Flexible Credit Line (LCF), which is like a Platinum credit card, the most luxurious of the Fund's products," he explained.
