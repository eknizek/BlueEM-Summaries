# Study Warns That Bottlenecks in Electrical Infrastructure Threaten Renewable Energy Expansion

Accelerating the expansion of electrical networks and guaranteeing regulatory stability are key to ensuring projects advance at the required pace.

The World Energy Issues Monitor 2025 delivered a forceful diagnosis: Latin America possesses first-class renewable resources, but faces bottlenecks in infrastructure, financing, and governance that put its competitiveness at risk over the next decade. On a global scale, volatility in commodity prices such as oil and gas remains the greatest source of uncertainty. Armed conflicts, protectionist policies, and restrictions on critical minerals destabilize markets. For Latin America, this means the challenge of simultaneously managing the energy transition and energy costs that impact households and industries.

In this scenario, electrical transmission networks have consolidated as the top priority globally for the second consecutive year. The message is clear: without modern networks, renewable energy cannot be integrated on a large scale. This would increase systemic costs, limit supply reliability, and slow the electrification of transportation and industry throughout the region.

The World Energy Council (WEC) report also identifies two factors that are systematically underestimated globally but are especially critical for Latin America. Social license emerges as a recurring blind spot: ignoring local perceptions can delay or block strategic initiatives, especially in contexts of high socio-environmental conflict. Additionally, the circular economy remains off the radar for many decision-makers. The transition cannot be limited solely to changing the energy source but requires production and consumption models that minimize waste and restore ecosystems.

"Latin America has a historic opportunity in the energy transition, but the challenge is no longer potential, but execution," stated Daniel Díaz, executive director of WEC Colombia.

In that sense, he considers it fundamental to accelerate network expansion, guarantee regulatory stability, and strengthen community participation in processes. Otherwise, he warned, there is a risk that projects will not advance at the pace required by decarbonization. "The region's competitiveness will depend on structural decisions made today," Díaz added.

These challenges will be at the center of the agenda for the 9th Latin American Renewables Meeting and Fair, which will take place from April 22-24, 2026 in Barranquilla. Companies, investors, authorities, and experts will gather to address issues ranging from electrical network expansion to social licensing processes.
