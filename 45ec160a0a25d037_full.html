# Future interest rates close sharply higher on oil surge

Future interest rates closed sharply higher on Monday (4th), pressured by soaring oil prices above $110 per barrel amid deteriorating investor risk perception in the face of tensions between the United States and Iran, which have moved the countries further away from a diplomatic agreement to end the war in the Middle East.

In a context of high volatility in fixed income markets, the increase in commodity prices worsens market prospects for inflation and monetary policy, resulting in an addition of risk premiums to global interest rate curves.

At the close of business, the rate of the Interbank Deposit (DI) contract maturing in January 2027 rose from 14.14%, from the previous adjustment, to 14.21%; the DI rate for January 2028 advanced from 13.79% to 13.95%; the DI rate for January 2029 posted a strong increase from 13.665% to 13.85%; and the DI rate for January 2031 jumped from 13.73% to 13.86%.

In the United States, the Treasury bond market also showed a relevant movement, considering that volatility in American fixed income is much lower than in Brazilian fixed-rate yields. The two-year T-note yield advanced from 3.892% to 3.962%, while the ten-year T-note yield jumped from 4.379% to 4.442%.

The perception that there is a stalemate in diplomatic negotiations between Iran and the United States was accentuated today by conflicting reports from the two countries about ship passage through the Strait of Hormuz and the entry of American warships into the channel. Early in the morning, Washington said it had escorted two vessels, which was denied by Tehran. The Iranian government stated it had prevented American destroyers from entering the strait, contradicting the White House.

With this, investors are now pricing in a more lasting impact of the war on oil prices and, consequently, its inflationary effect. According to a market participant, Brent crude with delivery scheduled for December of this year is already being traded above $90 per barrel, when the most common price since the start of the war had been oscillating between $80 and $85.

When asked if local news could be contributing to the addition of risk premiums to the curve, such as through the announcement of the Desenrola 2.0 program, this operator was categorical: "everything happening in the market today is related to the war."

The expectation that the conflict in the Middle East could affect inflation for longer may explain, even, the sharp pressure on the longer vertices of the forward curve. In recent episodes of stress due to oil price increases, rates at the so-called "belly" of the curve, with intermediate terms, concentrated the advances in future interest rates. Today, on the other hand, long-term rates came close to leading the negative movement in local fixed income, before reducing the increase and closing trading with a more contained advance compared to the belly of the curve.

Regarding the outlook for monetary policy, the probability that the Central Bank pauses the Selic rate cuts cycle at the next Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) decision on June 17 has increased. In the Copom digital options market, the probability that the BC's basic interest rate remains at 14.50% rose from 31% to 40%, while the chance of another 0.25 percentage point cut fell from 54.5% to 50%. The percentage for a more aggressive 0.5 percentage point cut fell from 12% to 10%, establishing itself as the least likely scenario for the next Copom.

Gabriel Leal de Barros, chief economist at ARX Investimentos, maintains his base scenario and forecasts a Selic rate of 12.50% at the end of the monetary easing cycle. He, however, reveals there is a tendency to increase that estimate to 13% and considers that the size of the cuts budget will depend on the evolution of the war.

"We could have a very intense war, but of short duration, and then the effect on inflation is marginal. But if [the conflict] has lower intensity and long duration, the pass-through to inflation is greater," says the economist.

For now, he still sees incentives for the United States and Iran to end the war as soon as possible, especially on the American side, since President Donald Trump's popularity has fallen significantly and could result in loss of majority in the House and Senate in this year's legislative elections.

"When we look at the political scenario [in the U.S.], everything points to an end or shorter duration of the war. If there is a resolution and oil returns, given the way the exchange rate is, the BC delivers a larger cycle of cuts than what is priced into the curve," evaluates Leal de Barros.

Currently, the forward interest rate curve estimates a Selic around 14% at the end of 2026, which would constitute a cycle of only 1 percentage point of monetary easing — already counting the two 0.25 percentage point cuts in the Copom meetings in March and April.
