# 2026 Elections: The Next Government Would Not Slow Teacher Spending

Education in Peru faces serious structural challenges associated with infrastructure and equipment, teacher performance, and access to quality higher education, among others. Fuerza Popular (FP), Juntos por el Perú (JPP), and Renovación Popular (RP) recognize the urgency of reforms in the sector. However, their proposals omit budgetary restrictions and lack concrete execution strategies, in a context where high ministerial turnover in the sector has weakened the continuity of educational policies.

## Critical Infrastructure

By the end of 2025, 27,000 educational facilities (48% of the total) require complete replacement due to representing a high risk of collapse for 1.2 million students. This problem is more severe in the poorest localities, where up to 60% of students are at risk, double that in wealthier districts. According to the Ministry of Education, the total infrastructure gap amounts to S/ 172 billion, 20 times the annual investment budget for 2026.

In this context, FP proposes the "Safe Schools" program to rebuild schools with damaged infrastructure and the creation of 3,000 new schools. The latter is an inefficient measure that would disperse the already limited resources of the sector: less than 1% of school absenteeism is explained by the lack of a nearby educational center. Meanwhile, JPP proposes reducing the gap by one-fifth by 2031 by raising investment to 6% of GDP. This goal is unattainable considering the current level is only 0.8%; and, without proper planning, would require resources that would leave other urgent needs of the country without budget or would exert pressure on fiscal sustainability. Finally, in the presidential debate, RP mentioned that private investment would be used for school repairs. However, there was no further detail and its government plan does not include any formal strategy on the subject.

## Unrestricted Salary Increases

Salary increases for teachers explain half of the increase in education spending between 2021 and 2025. In particular, their remuneration grew 5.1% annually on average during that period, more than that of any other public official, pressuring the already weakened fiscal balance. However, these increases have not been linked to any improvement in learning: according to the Ministry of Education's sample evaluations, in 2024, only three out of every ten fourth-grade students achieved satisfactory performance in mathematics or reading, with no significant advances compared to previous years.

Despite this, FP, RP, and JPP would not place any brake on growing spending on salaries and pensions. The most serious is JPP, which proposes hiring 90% of teachers and ensuring none earn less than 1 UIT (S/ 5,500). While FP and RP have not expressly promised to increase salaries, recently, along with JPP, they voted in favor of approving by insistence the increase in pensions for retired and laid-off teachers, which would have a permanent cost of at least S/ 8 billion annually, according to the Fiscal Council.

## Inadequate Equipment

Equipment in public schools is the main concern for families. According to the National Institute of Statistics and Informatics (INEI), 22% of Peruvians believe these are bad or very bad, more than infrastructure (18%), provision of materials (12%), or quality of teaching (8%). In response, FP proposes the acquisition of 6 million laptops or tablets. However, without an adequate maintenance plan, many will become inoperative: according to the 2025 School Census, one out of every five computers or laptops in public schools does not work. JPP and RP have not included any proposals to improve equipment in schools in their government plans.

## Higher Education at Risk

In 2024, three out of every 10 Peruvians had completed higher education (university, technical, or postgraduate), an advance compared to the early 2000s, when it barely exceeded 21%, according to INEI. Despite this, in the last two decades, the proportion of university graduates working in occupations that do not require higher education has increased, especially among young people: in the 25 to 35 age group, it rose from 46% in 2004 to 57% in 2024, while in the 35 to 45 age group, from 42% to 51%.

These results occur in a context of institutional weakening of Sunedu following the approval of Law No. 31520 in 2022, supported by FP, RP, and the teachers' bloc, which has promoted the creation of public universities. Despite the questions, the three political groups analyzed recently voted in favor of the creation of two public universities (in Loreto and Puno). In this context, it is crucial to strengthen the quality of existing universities. In this regard, FP and JPP propose the implementation of a quality assurance system for higher education that monitors and evaluates educational institutions. Meanwhile, RP does not include any proposal on the subject. Also, only FP and JPP propose the creation of an entity that aligns educational supply with labor market needs. Tools such as Ponte en Carrera (2015) and Mi Carrera (2025) currently exist, so the challenge lies in strengthening coordinated work between the Ministry of Labor and the Ministry of Education to integrate and keep information updated, among other pending tasks included in the National Qualifications Framework.

## Scholarships and Student Support

Higher education offers significantly greater returns in terms of labor income, especially when studies are completed. While completing secondary education increases labor income by around 13%, finishing a university degree raises it by about 50%. In this context, scholarships have been a key instrument for expanding access to this educational level.
