# Global Economy: Damage Far from Temporary

For some time now, the month of April in Washington has been characterized by several particularities. The most well-known is the blooming of the cherry blossoms that surround several of the city's most emblematic monuments, a spectacle that occurs when winter ends and temperatures begin to rise. However, due to global warming, the event now happens earlier. For example, this year the natural phenomenon that adorns the U.S. capital took place at the end of March.

Although much less visible, another notable fact is the spring meetings of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, which convene central bankers and finance ministers from the 191 countries that make up these institutions. The meeting used to be described as a summit of global finance where, in addition to examining the state of the economy, contacts were also made and deals were closed. But that was not the case, at least in 2026. Those who have been attending the meeting—which began this week and formally ends today—speak of a much smaller audience than in the past. The script has been the same as always and the venues attract people, including high-level officials, but the contrast with what was usual stands out.

Two factors explain what could be described as relative apathy. Many prefer to maintain distance from the place where Donald Trump's administration operates, as happens with its usual allies, now mistreated. Europeans and Canadians, for example, sent smaller delegations than when fraternal feelings were the norm on both sides of the Atlantic. The other is that there is an ongoing war in the Middle East, which not only makes travel from different latitudes more difficult for security reasons, but also translates into disruptions when taking a flight. For example, in Asia hundreds of flights have been cancelled because aviation fuel inventories have decreased, and it is still unclear how they will be replenished.

**The Possibilities**

Furthermore, it is not always attractive to travel thousands of kilometers to hear bad news. Because the central message from experts is that the crisis that began with the bombing of Iran at the end of February is already causing serious impacts, which could be more or less severe depending on whether the hydrocarbon supply routes now closed can be reopened and repairs to damaged facilities begin. But none of that is assured, even though negotiations are underway and there are promises that there is light at the end of the tunnel.

For this reason, the IMF presented three scenarios showing how things can range from bad to worse. The base case—or reference scenario—is one of a short-duration conflict that would raise energy input prices for a few months, which would then begin to decline. If so, global economic growth that before the hostilities seemed headed for 3.4 percent annually would be 3.1 percent. However, if the disputing parties do not reach agreement soon and oil continues to hover around $100 a barrel in the second half, the consequences would be more profound. Higher gasoline prices will not only make it more difficult for the family economies of billions of people, but will raise transportation costs and cause a significant inflationary rebound. The worst scenario suggests that next year the supply of gas, crude oil, and derivatives will not have normalized.

Efforts by authorities to control prices will include higher interest rates that in turn will negatively influence consumption and investment. Under this reading, global gross domestic product would increase barely two percent and high prices will return to be a headache. These forecasts led to repeated statements in Washington that the current threat is the most serious for the global economy since the pandemic emerged.
