# Global economy: lasting damage

The spring meetings of the International Monetary Fund and World Bank confirmed this week that the global economy is already feeling the blow of the war in the Middle East.

For some time now, April in Washington has been characterized by several particularities. The most well-known is the blooming of the cherry blossoms that surround several of the city's most emblematic monuments, a spectacle that occurs when winter ends and temperatures begin to rise. However, due to global warming, the event now happens earlier. For example, this year the natural phenomenon that adorns the U.S. capital took place in late March.

Though far less spectacular, another notable fact is the spring meetings of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, which convene central bankers and finance ministers from the 191 countries that make up these institutions. The gathering used to be described as a summit of global finance where, in addition to examining the state of the economy, contacts were made and deals were closed. But that was not the case this year, at least not in 2026.

Those who have been attending the meeting—which began this week and formally ends today—speak of a much smaller public than in the past. The script has been the same as always and the venues attract people, including high-level officials, but the contrast with what was usual stands out.

Two factors explain what could be described as relative apathy. Many prefer to keep their distance from where the Donald Trump administration operates, as do his usual allies, now mistreated. Europeans and Canadians, for example, sent smaller delegations than when fraternal feelings were the norm on both sides of the Atlantic. The other factor is that there is a war underway in the Middle East, which not only makes traveling from different latitudes more hazardous for security reasons, but also affects the ability to book flights. For example, in Asia hundreds of flight frequencies have been canceled because aviation fuel inventories have decreased, and it is still unclear how they will be replenished.

Besides, it is not always attractive to travel thousands of kilometers to hear bad news. Because the central message from experts is that the crisis that began with the bombing of Iran in late February is already causing serious damage, which could be more or less severe depending on whether the hydrocarbon supply routes that are now closed can be reopened and repairs to damaged facilities can begin. But none of that is assured, even with negotiations underway and promises that light can be seen at the end of the tunnel.

For this reason, the IMF presented three scenarios showing how things can range from bad to worse. The baseline scenario is one of short-duration conflict that would raise energy input prices for a few months, which would then begin to decline. If so, global economic growth that before the hostilities seemed headed toward 3.4 percent annually would settle at 3.1 percent. However, if the parties in dispute do not reach agreement soon and oil continues hovering around $100 per barrel in the second half, the consequences would be more profound. More expensive gasoline will not only make life harder for billions in their household economies but will raise transportation costs and cause a significant inflationary rebound. The worst-case scenario points to next year when the supply of gas, crude oil, and derivatives will not have normalized. Efforts by authorities to control prices will include higher interest rates, which in turn will negatively influence consumption and investment. Under that reading, global gross domestic product would increase by barely two percent and inflation will be a headache once again.

Such forecasts led officials in Washington to repeatedly say that the current threat is the most serious for the global economy since the pandemic emerged.
