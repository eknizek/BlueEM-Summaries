# From Buying Tompkins Estate to Launching Film Production: Texas Entrepreneur Charlie Clark's Ventures in Chile

American businessman Charlie Clark first came to Chile in 2016 on an urgent personal mission. He needed to do post-production work on his film Green Ghost, a superhero movie in which he also stars as one of the protagonists. The work had been delayed too long in his native United States, where it would have cost him dearly: at minimum $2 million, at most five times that amount. It was complicated.

Then Chilean actor Marko Zaror, a martial arts expert who also participated in the film, suggested he try Santiago. He told Clark that the work was done well there and would be much cheaper. "And that's how it was, I paid a fifth of what they would have charged me in the United States," says Clark, speaking via Zoom from Texas, where he lives. The film, in any case, had to be practically remade entirely. "As it was, it was a mess. We had to burn a lot of brain cells on that," he acknowledges. What should have taken a couple of months ended up taking four years. But the entrepreneur, who made his fortune in the automotive business he operates on the border between the United States and Mexico, says that during that time a whole universe opened up to him: he began traveling throughout Chile, from San Pedro de Atacama to Torres del Paine, and became fascinated with the country's diverse nature—and the people who were rescuing his movie.

The charm was so great that in 2017 he did not hesitate to buy the Reñihué estate, which Kristine Tompkins had for sale and which had been the first property she and Douglas—her husband, who died in 2015—had acquired here. Clark would not stop there; he would return to Chile again and again with different projects in mind. Like in 2022, when he created his film production company Trío de la Luz, with which he plans to create audiovisual products that showcase the nature of this corner of the planet. Including the sky, he emphasizes. And that's not a small detail: this eccentric entrepreneur is a UFO fanatic.

## A Texan Falls in Love with Chile

Charlie Clark was born in McAllen, in southern Texas, the American state bordering Mexico. He has recounted that his parents—who worked in automobile sales—traveled frequently and he was left in the care of his Mexican nanny, Aurora Aguirre. They loved each other like mother and son, and it was not unusual for her to take him to her house and even cross the border with him and settle in Reynosa, on the Mexican side. There Clark learned about her culture, her food, her celebrations; he watched Chavo del Ocho on television. And in the process, he learned the Spanish that he speaks perfectly to this day. He studied Communications at Texas Christian University, had jobs in different parts of the country, but decided to settle in his native McAllen and start his own automotive business. "I didn't inherit anything from my parents, I started from zero," he clarifies.

His first major success came in 2001 when he started working as a Nissan dealer. Today he has three dealerships of that brand in Texas: in Harlingen, Brownsville, and El Paso. And he has just opened two Hyundai dealerships in the same state. Business is good. In a very good month, he says, one of his Nissan locations can sell up to 400 new cars. "Last year, my store in El Paso ranked number five in sales among all Nissan dealerships in the United States, which must be around 1,200. Another of my stores, the one in Harlingen, was seven or eight. In other years, for one or two months, two of my locations have been number one. That's noteworthy, because we compete with big cities like Miami, New York, Los Angeles, Dallas. How is that possible? Because of my people."

"My people" for Clark is that whole world he got to know during those happy Mexican days at his nanny Aurora's house. As soon as he entered the Nissan dealership business, he realized that the best customers to attract were Latinos, a segment that other dealerships in the area overlooked. He didn't, and he had the advantage of knowing their customs, their emotions. With the participation of his nanny—who died at ninety in 2022—he created innovative advertising directed at them. In their language, using their humor. And it's the tone that even prevails today within his business: he says that of his current 700 employees, barely three are American. "The rest are Latinos, they are my people," he emphasizes.

Since he had always enjoyed audiovisual work, to support his business he set up a series of shows on local television where he brought to life his alter ego: a bumbling American superhero—Green Ghost—who joined much more skilled Latino superheroes. "Basically, I was making fun of American heroism," he notes. He made more than 30 episodes, which contained the idea from which his film was born—a film he produced and financed himself; the same film that brought him to Chile and that, in a way, gave him another destiny in the south of the world.

"Many things happened to me there in Chile, mystical things even. Most of all it was a great connection with the earth. I traveled through the north, through the south. It opened doors to another universe for me, because until then I was only about work, my business, the movie. I had experiences with ayahuasca, with ancestral medicine, I felt other connections," he recounts.

While immersed in nature, someone mentioned to him that Kristine Tompkins wanted to meet him. "She had heard that I, fascinated with this country, was looking for land to buy in Chile," Clark explains.

## Tears at Reñihué

But there was a not insignificant matter of numbers. "I was looking for a place that would cost me around $1.5 million. And Kristine was selling for $11 million plus taxes. I told the person who mentioned it to me that there was no way, that I didn't have that money. But he said she insisted on meeting me," he recalls.

So he went to meet Kristine at the Reñihué estate in the Los Lagos Region, facing Michimahuida volcano. "When I got there, my feet touched the earth and I started to cry. I felt so much emotion. I thought: 'I don't know how, but I have to buy this place.' I went back to ancestral medicine seeking messages from the earth. And I got them: it told me to buy, but without forgetting all the families of Latinos who work with me in Texas and depend on me. Not to do it in a stupid way. But it was clear: 'Let go, sacrifice all your personal wealth,' it told me."

The parties agreed that the purchase price would be $9 million. Clark says he had $3 million in the bank at the time. And he committed to paying the rest divided into two equal parts over the following two years. "I had to work hard for that, it was a risk, but I managed it," he says.

Reñihué was the first estate the Tompkins bought in Chile, in 1991. It is a 708-hectare valley surrounded by two rivers, next to the famous Pumalín Park. Clark recounts that when he first met Kristine Tompkins, "she began to cry, because she told me she couldn't be in this place without her husband, and that it was important to her who would buy it. I told her: 'I promise with all my soul that I will protect this place, I will try to continue on the path of conservation that you began here.'"

That 2017, Kristine Tompkins told the press that Reñihué could not be in better hands... That was a great honor for me.
