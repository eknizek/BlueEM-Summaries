# Itaú Optimus Bets on Falling Rates and Dollar Amid Geopolitical Tensions

The Optimus fund family at Itaú Asset Management is betting on falling interest rates in Brazil and forecasting dollar depreciation against the real, amid market position reductions due to the Iran war.

With DI futures contracts climbing amid geopolitical uncertainties, the manager decided last month to take a position that profits from falling interest rates in Brazil — also called an applied position. The assessment is that prices were already "discounted," according to Pablo Salgado, manager of Itaú Optimus.

"We decided midway through to apply rates and we remain applied," Salgado said in an interview to Bloomberg News in São Paulo. "We weren't positioned. The curves began to reprice strongly and we entered already at a displaced price. We entered a bit early."

Current exposure runs at around 50% of the funds' risk limit.

Before the Middle East war, some local funds held this same position, which benefits from falling rates, betting on a monetary easing cycle by the Central Bank, which was operating at one of the world's most restrictive rates. However, the oil surge raised global inflationary pressures and brought strong volatility to future interest rate contracts, triggering a stop-loss movement in the market, with some managers zeroing positions in Brazil to limit losses.

Salgado assesses that if the war resolves soon, central banks of developed economies tend to absorb the shock without responding with rate increases. The manager maintains applied positions in markets such as Canada, the United Kingdom, and Europe.

In Brazil, a 0.25 percentage point cut in the basic interest rate by the Central Bank next week is "guaranteed," even in an adverse scenario, according to him. For Salgado, before elections there is still the possibility that the monetary authority accelerates the pace of cuts.

At the end of the monetary easing cycle, the interest rate in Brazil should converge to around 12%, depending on the economic policy directions of the next government.

The result of betting on falling rates amid the Iran war caused the fund's quota to fall initially, but it recovered shortly after, Salgado noted. The Optimus funds are multi-market with allocation in rates, currencies, and stocks and have approximately R$ 18 billion under management.

"Resolving this war, the external scenario is extremely benign for emerging markets. The US doesn't seem to attract capital as unquestionably as it did previously," said Salgado, emphasizing that the disinflationary momentum of the Brazilian economy is still present. "I think Brazil's scenario is very good, both for equities, currency, and longer-term rates on the interest curve."

## Weakened Dollar

The manager also adjusted its currency strategy. Before the war, it operated the dollar tactically, but in the last month, it adopted a short position in the currency, betting on dollar depreciation against the real. The assessment is that the real should continue as a high-carry currency, favored compared to emerging market peers, especially given that Brazil is a major oil exporter.

"We share this thesis of portfolio rebalancing and dollar weakening due to American institutional deterioration," said Salgado.

According to him, the potential for real appreciation should not exceed around 10%. "This would be a best-case scenario — and it's not the hypothesis we're working with — that would require a confluence of positive factors," he said.

The manager also views the real position not only for its standalone value but as a hedge for the applied position in rates. If falling rates in Brazil are less than expected, this tends to increase the attractiveness of the real — and in that scenario, gains would come from the currency position.
