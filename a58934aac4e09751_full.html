# Vports seeks to prove that port privatization works in Brazil

Brazil's only privatized port authority wants to prove that concessions of port authorities can be a good business in the country. Vports – created four years ago from the privatization of the former Companhia Docas do Espírito Santo and treated as a pilot project by then-Minister Tarcísio de Freitas – operates three ports in Espírito Santo (Vitória, Vila Velha, and Barra do Riacho). Results are already showing.

Since the concession, cargo movement has grown approximately 15%, from just over 7 million to more than 8 million tons, with the ports gaining traction in imports, containers, and higher value-added cargo. The company generated net revenue of R$ 312 million in 2025 – doubling in size compared to the period before the concession. EBITDA, in turn, rose 240% to R$ 220 million.

"Operating as a private port allows us much more agility and flexibility in building partnerships," CEO Gustavo Serrão told Brazil Journal.

Although common in countries like the United Kingdom and Australia, port authority concessions have always stalled in Brazil, especially due to doubts about the legal model to follow and other regulatory concerns, including the risk of conflict between port administrators and operators within it. For this reason, the country has advanced for decades in leasing specific terminals, but not in the complete privatization of port management. In almost all cases, administration remains in state hands.

According to the CEO, Vports functions as a kind of logistics "shopping mall." "The port authority is like a shopping mall – we organize the space and the partners are the stores that serve the end customer," Serrão said.

Since the concession, there have already been R$ 1.5 billion in contracted investments – in a model that combines Vports' own capital and partner capital, depending on each project's needs. The expectation is to increase capacity by up to 60% over the next five years as new projects come into operation and attract more cargo.

One of these moves happened this week. VLI and Multilift – a company operating in port operations, storage, and road transport – announced a partnership with Vports to build a "railway hopper" within the Vitória port, as well as reactivate and modernize the railway network connecting the dock to the country's major railways. (Glossary: A hopper is a funnel-shaped structure that allows direct unloading of cargo from railway cars or trucks onto conveyor belts or silos)

In total, the investment amounts to approximately R$ 130 million in railway and port infrastructure, plus additional contributions from Multilift. Operations begin in the second half: the first trains should run in July, with shipments already scheduled for August.

According to Serrão, the objective is to create an integrated logistics flow. Currently, a significant portion of cargo (mainly pig iron) reaches the port in a fragmented way: the train stops before the dock, cargo is unloaded, stored, and then transported by truck to the loading area. With the new structure, this process disappears.

"You eliminate a stage of the logistics process. Before, the train stopped outside the port. Now it goes straight in and unloads right there," Serrão said, who believes that Espírito Santo has a strategic position that can take business away from other ports. "We believe this solution will attract cargo that is currently on less efficient routes."
