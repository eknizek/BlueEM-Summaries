# Vports Aims to Prove Port Privatization Works in Brazil

Brazil's only privatized port authority wants to prove that the concession of port authorities can be a good business in the country. Vports – created four years ago from the privatization of the former Companhia Docas do Espírito Santo and treated as a pilot project by then-Minister Tarcísio de Freitas – operates three ports in Espírito Santo (Vitória, Vila Velha, and Barra do Riacho). Results are already showing.

Since the concession, cargo movement has grown approximately 15%, from just over 7 million to more than 8 million tons, with the ports gaining traction in imports, containers, and higher-value cargo. The company achieved net revenue of R$ 312 million in 2025 – doubling in size compared to the period before the concession. EBITDA, in turn, rose 240% to R$ 220 million.

"Operating as a private port allows us much more agility and flexibility in building partnerships," CEO Gustavo Serrão told Brazil Journal.

Although common in countries like the United Kingdom and Australia, the concession of port authorities has always stalled in Brazil, especially due to doubts about the legal framework to follow and other regulatory concerns, including the risk of conflict between those who manage the port and those who operate within it. For this reason, the country has advanced for decades in leasing specific terminals, but not in the complete privatization of port management. In almost all cases, administration remains in state hands.

According to the CEO, Vports functions as a sort of logistical "shopping mall." "The port authority is like a shopping mall – we organize the space and the partners are the stores that serve the final customer," said Serrão.

Since the concession, there have already been R$ 1.5 billion in contracted investments – in a model that combines capital from Vports itself and partners, depending on each project's needs. The expectation is to increase capacity by up to 60% over the next five years as new projects become operational and attract more cargo.

One of these moves happened this week. VLI and Multilift – a company operating in port operations, storage, and road transportation – announced a partnership with Vports to build a "rail hopper" within the Port of Vitória, as well as reactivate and modernize the rail network that connects the dock to the country's main railways.

In total, the investment amounts to approximately R$ 130 million in rail and port infrastructure, plus additional contributions from Multilift. Operations begin in the second half: the first trains are expected to run in July, with shipments already scheduled for August.

According to Serrão, the goal is to create an integrated logistics flow. Today, a significant portion of cargo (mainly pig iron) arrives at the port in a fragmented manner: the train stops before the dock, cargo is unloaded, stored, and then transported by truck to the loading area. With the new structure, this process disappears.

"You eliminate a step in the logistics process. Before, the train stopped outside the port. Now it enters directly and unloads there," said Serrão, who believes that Espírito Santo has a strategic position that can draw cargo away from other ports. "We believe that this solution will attract cargo that today is on less efficient routes."
