# Vports seeks to prove that port privatization works in Brazil

Brazil's only privatized port authority wants to prove that port authority concessions can be a successful business in the country. Vports – created four years ago from the privatization of the former Companhia Docas do Espírito Santo and treated as a pilot project by then-minister Tarcísio de Freitas – operates three ports in Espírito Santo (Vitória, Vila Velha, and Barra do Riacho). Results are already emerging.

Since the concession, cargo movement has grown approximately 15%, from just over 7 million to more than 8 million tons, with the ports gaining traction in imports, containers, and higher-value cargo. The company achieved net revenue of R$ 312 million in 2025 – doubling compared to the period before concession. EBITDA, in turn, jumped 240% to R$ 220 million.

"Operating as a private port allows us much more agility and flexibility in building partnerships," CEO Gustavo Serrão told Brazil Journal.

Although common in countries like the United Kingdom and Australia, port authority concessions have always stalled in Brazil, especially due to doubts about the legal framework to follow and other regulatory concerns, including the risk of conflict between port administrators and those operating within it. For this reason, the country has advanced for decades in leasing specific terminals, but not in complete port management privatization. In almost all cases, administration remains in state hands.

According to the CEO, Vports functions as a kind of logistical "shopping mall." "The port authority is like a shopping mall – we organize the space and the partners are the stores that serve the end customer," said Serrão.

Since the concession, there have already been R$ 1.5 billion in committed investments – in a model combining Vports' own capital and partner capital, depending on each project's needs. The expectation is to increase capacity by up to 60% over the next five years as new projects come online and attract more cargo.

One such move happened this week. VLI and Multilift – a company operating in port operations, storage, and road transportation – announced a partnership with Vports to build a "rail hopper" inside the Port of Vitória, as well as reactivate and modernize the railway network connecting the dock to the country's main railways.

The total investment amounts to approximately R$ 130 million in railway and port infrastructure, plus additional contributions from Multilift. Operations begin in the second half of the year: the first trains should run in July, with shipments already scheduled for August.

According to Serrão, the goal is to create an integrated logistics flow. Currently, a significant portion of cargo (mainly pig iron) arrives at the port in a fragmented manner: the train stops before the dock, cargo is unloaded, stored, and then transported by truck for shipment. With the new structure, this process disappears.

"You eliminate one step from the logistics process. Before, the train stopped outside the port. Now it comes directly inside and unloads there," said Serrão, who believes that Espírito Santo has a strategic position that can take market share from other ports. "We believe this solution will attract cargo that today follows less efficient routes."
