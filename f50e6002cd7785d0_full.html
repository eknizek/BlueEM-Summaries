# Regulation and Storage: Mexico's Pending Fracking Challenges

An updated regulatory framework, a robust supervision system, as well as an increase in storage capacity will be necessary to ensure the proper use of unconventional gas resources; otherwise, the country's energy security will continue to be vulnerable.

Sector experts recognized the need for water in hydraulic fracturing, as well as the possibility of aquifer contamination; therefore, they consider extensive technical, geological, geophysical, hydrogeological, and even seismic knowledge of the subsoil necessary, so that well construction adheres to the most appropriate methodologies.

During the conference "Fracking or No Fracking: Dialogue with Engineers," Rubén Chávez Guillen, coordinator of the Groundwater Subcommittee of the Mexican Civil Engineers Association (CICM), stated that China is the country with the largest unconventional gas reserves in the world, followed by the United States in second place, Argentina in third, while Mexico is the fourth country, so the production potential is significant.

For the expert, the development of these projects is important, especially to reduce import dependence, as this "exposes us to the possibility that eventually its supply could be significantly reduced by increased demand in the United States, by conflict in the Middle East, or for political reasons."

Mexico has significant natural gas potential in unconventional deposits located in the Burgos, Sabinas, and Tampico-Misantla basins. However, he acknowledged that Mexico's gas storage capacity is less than one week, so this infrastructure must be developed in parallel and be part of a strategic plan. Otherwise, energy security, even with fracking, will remain highly vulnerable.

## Supervision

Jesús Campos, president of the CICM Board of Directors, said that "regulation without supervision is dead letter," so a serious technical evaluation of hydraulic fracturing with new low-impact technologies is necessary.

Sergio Aceves, coordinator of the CICM Energy Committee, agreed on the issue, stating that to provide society with confidence about the development and exploitation of unconventional deposits, it is necessary to have a robust supervision and monitoring system that guarantees best practices for these projects, to which must be added the guarantee that they will be profitable.

"We need to think about bankable contract models; the country today requires private initiative to attract investment; we need to generate attractive contract models."

## Opposition

The Mexican Alliance Against Fracking demanded a ban on fracking, insisting that this practice is not sustainable, as there is scientific evidence of its environmental, social, and health impacts.

"Converting the current dependence into a bet on more fossil extraction, more gas infrastructure, and more territorial sacrifice is not energy sovereignty. It is deepening the same fossil model that has contaminated territories, compromised water sources, imposed risks to entire communities, and delayed real and just energy transition. It is not about seeking new technical pretexts to prolong Mexico's fossil dependence, but about making decisions to leave it behind."
