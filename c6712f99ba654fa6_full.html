# Tork Capital closes funds amid hostile environment for stock managers

Tork Capital announced the closure of its operations last week. The firm had approximately R$ 600 million in assets under management, divided among long-only funds, equity hedge funds (recently launched), and pension funds — an insufficient level to keep the business running profitably.

"It's the story of other managers that should close their doors in the next 6 to 12 months," a fund industry professional told Brazil Journal.

Founded in July 2018 when the Selic rate stood at 6.5%, Tork emerged to conduct value investing with some macro bias, combining the expertise of founders Marcelo Magalhães and Murilo Arruda. Magalhães was an analyst at Investidor Profissional (now IP) and M Square (now Velt Partners), two managers specialized in evaluating companies on a fundamentalist basis. (Velt, incidentally, recently decided to change strategy, citing market shrinkage). Before founding Tork, Magalhães spent seven years at JGP, where he was a portfolio manager and refined his macro vision. Arruda was an analyst at M Square and Verde, and left Tork in 2021. He was a portfolio manager at Luis Stuhlberger's firm and in 2024 founded Morada Capital, an equity fund manager with R$ 34 million in AUM today.

"Some managers managed to save themselves with long & short strategies to try to profit from relative bets, but that wasn't the case with Tork," said this professional, citing the launch of three equity hedge funds in 2025 — which together raised less than R$ 20 million.

Tork's demise comes at a time when the Ibovespa rose 50% over the past 12 months — but the rally was fueled by the appetite of foreign investors buying the Brazilian stock exchange mainly through indices, and did not interrupt the wave of withdrawals from equity funds. From January to March, withdrawals from FIAs totaled R$ 6.4 billion; in 12 months, they reached R$ 25 billion. Meanwhile, the participation of foreigners in the stock exchange's trading volume, which was 45% in 2019, rose to 62% this year. The share of locals, individual and institutional investors, fell from 50% to 35% in this period.

Most funds failed to anticipate what the foreign flow would do to the market — and fell behind. In 12 months, only 45% of long-only funds and 17% of long bias funds outperformed the Ibovespa, according to a survey by Outliers Advisory. But underperformance goes back further. In three and five-year windows, only 34% of long-only funds had returns greater than the Ibovespa. In the case of long bias funds, 16% and 26% respectively, according to Outliers.

Tork's main funds ranked in the third and fourth quartiles in 12, 24, 36 and 60 months. "It's a long period in which few managers were capable of beating the index, but it's important to remember that it was atypical," says an equity fund manager.

One example: Petrobras, which has relevant weight in the Ibovespa, rose after Lava Jato and also when the risk of intervention in the Lula government decreased — and few managed to anticipate that. "There was no way to run that risk."

Critics say that if managers are unable to anticipate scenarios, it's better to invest via ETFs, which charge much lower fees. "It's good to remember that the Ibovespa is a very poor representation of the Brazilian market. It's concentrated in few sectors whose growth is not exponential, unlike what we see in the US. Investing in the index may have paid off, but thinking that will always work is a mistake," says this manager.

On its LinkedIn page, Tork stated that it is coordinating the closure of the funds with administrators and that shareholder meetings will be held if necessary.
