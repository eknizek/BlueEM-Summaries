# Petrobras Advisor Criticizes Losses from Fuel Price Controls

A member of Petrobras's board of directors appointed by non-controlling shareholders is warning that the state-owned company is losing billions from selling fuel at below-market prices and risks suffering reputational damage. Petrobras has accumulated between $2 billion and $3 billion in losses from selling diesel below international prices since the beginning of the Iran war, according to estimates by Francisco Petros, who was elected for a fifth term on April 16.

Petrobras's pricing policy requires the company to protect consumers from short-term volatility. Petros's position, while not shared by the majority of the board, reflects investor concerns that Petrobras may once again be absorbing losses to keep fuel costs low—a politically sensitive issue in Brazil that has historically weighed on the company's balance sheet.

His opinions carry weight because he represents a considerable bloc of non-controlling investors and can act as a counterweight to the government majority on the board. "Unfortunately, a problem that is structurally economic can be used for political purposes," Petros said in an interview with Bloomberg News.

Petrobras did not comment. The government controls six of the 11 board seats. Petrobras has increased wholesale diesel prices once since the conflict caused a spike, while wholesale gasoline prices have remained unchanged and are approximately 30% below international levels, by his calculation.

Petros, who is a lawyer and economist, warned Petrobras that postponing price adjustments could harm compliance with its own policy—an important market concern given the company's history of debt fueled by subsidies. "Gasoline has a more direct impact on the government's popularity. Therefore, it seems to me that this constraint may ultimately serve some other interest," he said. "Demonstrating that this is not the case has to be concrete—which means raising prices."

Although Petros does not see the same "rigorous political interference" in Petrobras's fuel prices as in the past, he said the company should begin adjusting them gradually to demonstrate its independence. The energy inflation fueled by the war puts CEO Magda Chambriard in a position where she must navigate between the company's finances and the government's efforts to protect consumers. "For the first time under President Magda's administration, the pricing policy will be put to the test," he said.

The Iran war has reignited the debate over the pursuit of refining self-sufficiency. President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva recently stated that Petrobras will attempt to repurchase a refinery in Bahia sold to Mubadala Capital. For Petros, the company should only reacquire assets that make strategic and financial sense. "I have doubts whether Petrobras's former refineries, now privatized, would meet both criteria," he stated.
