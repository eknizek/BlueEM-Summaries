# For Arriazu, unemployment in Greater Buenos Aires is the biggest political risk for the Government

The economist warned that the current change process is producing more job destruction than creation. "This situation can generate political noise and translate to the electoral arena," he said.

The word of economist Ricardo Arriazu is the bible for the business world. In an exclusive web discussion with sophisticated investors convened by the asset management fund Black Toro, Arriazu warned that the greatest challenge for the economic program lies in the labor market of the conurbano (Greater Buenos Aires area).

In his view, we are facing a profound structural change, but "the current change process is producing more job destruction than creation. This situation can generate political noise and translate to the electoral arena. The challenge is next year's election in Greater Buenos Aires."

Here are his other key points:

"In my life I have never seen this new dynamic of capital-intensive sectors like energy, mining, and agriculture that generate a large amount of foreign currency but absorb little labor.

"Industry, construction, and commerce are in decline and maintain their epicenter in Greater Buenos Aires.

"Business owners are not to blame for having invested where they did; they were following the incentives that governments provided. People are also not to blame for having moved to Greater Buenos Aires; the Government provided those incentives too. Now the rules of the game are changing and some are left very damaged.

"Argentina has an artificial productive structure, the product of nearly a century of penalizing the sectors in which we have comparative advantage and subsidizing sectors where we don't have comparative advantage.

"Vaca Muerta is probably the world's best shale formation. At minimum, equal to Permian in the United States. Argentina's breakeven point should be at $42 per barrel. Argentina is in full development process, it has more gas than oil. Production is growing, it was lacking infrastructure.

"By 2030, Argentina could reach energy exports of $32 billion, driven by the development of Vaca Muerta, the expansion of transport infrastructure, and increased production of liquefied natural gas. This year 135 million barrels of oil will be exported and 225 million next year.

"Agriculture, in the event that export taxes are eliminated, will contribute an additional $20 billion.

"These benefits are not yet fully reflected in current activity and the transition process entails bottlenecks that must be managed.

"At this moment, in my opinion, the Central Bank should lower interest rates. It's the same as in 2024. The Fund said rates should go up, most said they should go up, we said they should go down. This time we believe they should go down because people think in dollars. Banks are afraid because their loan portfolio irregularities have increased.

"The economic program currently has a 50% probability of success; last year I thought it would be 30%, but it is conditioned on the ability to pass the next electoral test without major social deterioration."
