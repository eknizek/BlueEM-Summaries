Agriculture sector accused of 'passivity'; barriers to livestock, tomatoes, and strawberries

# Agriculture Sector Accused of 'Passivity'; Barriers to Livestock, Tomatoes, and Strawberries

The Mexican agricultural sector is experiencing high tension ahead of the review of the Trade Agreement between Mexico, the United States, and Canada (USMCA), as various producers face unjustified sanitary barriers and dumping accusations from the U.S. government.

In an interview with Excélsior, Juan Carlos Anaya, director of the Agricultural Markets Consulting Group (GCMA), warned that Mexico "has been guilty of passivity" in the face of U.S. commercial attacks, losing key opportunities to defend a solid trade surplus.

The expert pointed out that the most concerning point regarding the USMCA review is the border closure to Mexican live cattle, under the argument of screwworm infection. Despite the fact that exporting states such as Sonora, Chihuahua, Coahuila, and Durango have no positive cases, the border has been operating under intermittent closures since late 2024.

"According to the free trade agreement, there is the regionalization issue that allows operation. We do this with their poultry when they have avian flu; if not, they wouldn't be exporting to us. There is no reciprocal treatment," Anaya denounced.

GCMA data indicates losses for Mexico of 1.872 billion dollars for beef cattle production. Additionally, this blockade not only affects local ranchers but has also driven meat prices in the United States to historic levels. According to the GCMA director, the impact for U.S. feedlot operators is massive, as they have stopped producing meat with Mexican cattle for more than 500,000 tons.

Added to this is the fact that the horticultural and fruit sector also lives under threats from the Donald Trump administration, mainly due to dumping accusations against tomatoes and strawberries, which have reduced production.

"We have taken a cooler approach. Not protesting has impacted the tomato sector due to uncertainty, and now we are living the consequences: the price of tomatoes is what is most impacting consumers today," the expert said.

## Lack of Strategy

Juan Carlos Anaya noted the lack of pressure measures from the current government, recalling that in the past tariffs were responded to with reciprocal taxes, which forced the United States to negotiate.

The analyst emphasized that Mexico has a strong card it is not using: its position as the principal buyer of corn, wheat, soy, beans, pork, chicken, and milk for the United States.

"For them we are strategic, that dependence should be the basis for firmer negotiation," he mentioned.

Facing this situation, the GCMA proposes a long-term vision to prevent agriculture from becoming hostage to electoral politics: a regional customs and sanitary union.

"We should work toward a customs and sanitary union between Mexico, the United States, and Canada, where there are fewer procedures and barriers at the border."

The prospects for Mexico are an agro-food trade surplus close to 14 billion dollars; however, the detected decrease in the pace of exports in volume and value in early-year reports is a warning sign. In the first two months of 2026, the value of agro-food exports reached 8.235 billion dollars, 9.3% less than in the same period of 2025.
