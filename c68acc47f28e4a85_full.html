# Agriculture Sector Accused of 'Passivity'; Trade Barriers on Livestock, Tomatoes, and Strawberries

The Mexican agricultural sector is experiencing high tension as it approaches the review of the trade agreement between Mexico, the United States, and Canada (T-MEC), as various producers face unjustified sanitary barriers and dumping accusations from the U.S. government.

In an interview with Excélsior, Juan Carlos Anaya, director of the Agricultural Markets Consulting Group (GCMA), warned that Mexico "has been guilty of passivity" in the face of U.S. trade attacks, losing key opportunities to defend a solid trade surplus.

The expert pointed out that the most concerning issue ahead of the T-MEC review is the closure of the border to Mexican live cattle under the argument of screwworm infection. Despite exporting states such as Sonora, Chihuahua, Coahuila, and Durango showing no positive cases, the border has been operating under intermittent closures since late 2024.

"According to the free trade agreement, there is the matter of regionalization that allows operations. We do it with their poultry when they have avian flu; otherwise, they wouldn't be exporting to us. There is no reciprocal treatment," Anaya denounced.

GCMA data shows losses for Mexico of 1.872 billion dollars for beef cattle production. Additionally, this blockade not only affects local ranchers but has also driven meat prices in the United States to historic levels. According to the GCMA director, the impact on U.S. feedlot operators is massive, as they have stopped producing meat with Mexican cattle for more than 500,000 tons.

To this is added the fact that the horticultural sector also lives under threats from the Trump administration, mainly due to dumping accusations against tomatoes and strawberries, which have reduced production.

"We have taken a more 'cool-headed' position. Not complaining to them has impacted the tomato sector due to uncertainty, and now we are living the consequences: tomato prices are what is most impacting consumers today," said the expert.

## Lack of Strategy

Juan Carlos Anaya questioned whether pressure measures are lacking from the current government, recalling that in the past, tariffs were answered with reciprocal taxes, which forced the United States to negotiate.

The analyst stressed that Mexico has a strong card it is not using: its position as the main buyer of corn, wheat, soybeans, beans, pork, poultry, and milk for the United States.

"We are strategic for them; that dependence should be the basis for firmer negotiation," he mentioned.

Facing this scenario, the GCMA proposes a long-term vision to prevent agriculture from becoming hostage to electoral politics: a regional customs and sanitary union.

"We should work toward a customs and sanitary union between Mexico, the United States, and Canada, where there are fewer procedures and barriers at the border."

The outlook for Mexico is for an agri-food trade surplus close to 14 billion dollars; however, the slowdown in the export pace in volume and value detected in early-year reports is a warning sign. In the first two months of 2026, the value of agri-food exports reached 8.235 billion dollars, 9.3% less than in the same period of 2025.
