# Mexican Economy: Energy Shock and Weak Growth

The problem is not only that oil prices, gas, jet fuel, and some grain prices have risen due to geopolitical deterioration in the Middle East. The most delicate point is that this shock appears when national productive activity has been showing signs of weak dynamism, particularly in services, commerce, and investment. The February data confirms this fragility. The Global Indicator of Economic Activity barely advanced 0.1% monthly with seasonally adjusted figures, after a decline of 0.7% in January and another marginal contraction in December. In annual terms, activity decreased 0.3%. The reading is clear: the economy not only grows little, but started the year with a weak trajectory, insufficient to dispel risks of stagnation.

Sectoral composition helps explain the problem. Industry had a small monthly respite, supported by manufacturing, mining, and construction. However, this advance was offset by setbacks in services and primary activities. Within services, declines concentrated in entertainment, hotels and restaurants, business support, information, and wholesale trade. That is, weakness is not limited to an isolated sector: it cuts across components associated with consumption, business services, and commercial activity. This is why the first quarter looks particularly vulnerable. With available information, a quarterly GDP contraction of 0.6% is estimated. Although recovery in subsequent quarters could still occur, that recovery would depend on several assumptions: greater public spending execution, moderation of uncertainty, better private investment performance, export stimulus, and a less restrictive monetary policy. None of these factors is guaranteed.

In this context, the international energy shock functions as additional pressure. Brent was positioned near $105 per barrel and Mexican Mix around $94.65. Gas references also rose in Europe and Asia, while jet fuel registered a significant increase. Added to this is the price increase of wheat and corn, in an environment where urea remains at elevated levels. The energy channel, therefore, affects not only fuels; it can also transmit to transportation, food, fertilizers, and logistics costs.

For Mexico, the oil price increase has ambiguous effects. In principle, a higher price can favor oil revenues. But this reading is incomplete. An economy that imports gasoline, with production chains sensitive to transportation and with food pressures, also faces higher costs. Furthermore, when general inflation remains around 4.5% annually and underlying inflation near 4.3%, any persistent energy or food shock can complicate the disinflation process.

The dilemma for economic policy is evident. If the energy shock persists, the central bank would have less room to accelerate a neutral monetary stance. At the same time, a weak economy demands less restrictive financial conditions and more effective fiscal policy. This combination—low growth, still-elevated inflation, and greater external volatility—requires avoiding mechanical responses. It is not enough to celebrate higher oil prices; their net effect on costs, expectations, and activity must be evaluated.

The external sector offers no automatic exit either. Oil exports could improve due to price, but manufacturing exports face an uncertain environment. Imports of intermediate and consumer goods could increase if activity recovers, but also reflect higher external costs. The trade balance could show partial relief without implying structural economic improvement.

The current situation demands a sober reading. Mexico does not face a crisis, but rather an uncomfortable combination: weak internal activity, global energy risks, resistant inflation, and dependence on the U.S. cycle. The immediate challenge consists of preventing the external shock from amplifying the slowdown. For this, the priority should be accelerating productive spending, sustaining investment confidence, protecting price stability, and avoiding further deterioration of purchasing power from energy and food price increases. Expensive oil may improve some revenues, but it does not substitute for a growth strategy.
