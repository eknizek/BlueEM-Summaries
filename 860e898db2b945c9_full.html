# Mexican Economy: Energy Shock and Low Growth

The problem is not solely that oil, gas, jet fuel, and certain grain prices have spiked due to geopolitical deterioration in the Middle East. The most delicate point is that this shock appears when national productive activity had been showing signs of low dynamism, particularly in services, commerce, and investment. The February data confirms this fragility. The Global Indicator of Economic Activity advanced only 0.1% monthly with seasonally adjusted figures, after a decline of 0.7% in January and another marginal contraction in December. In annual terms, activity decreased 0.3%. The reading is clear: the economy not only grows little, but started the year with a weak trajectory, insufficient to dispel stagnation risks.

The sectoral composition helps explain the problem. Industry had a small monthly respite, supported by manufacturing, mining, and construction. However, this advance was offset by retreats in services and primary activities. Within services, declines concentrated in entertainment, hotels and restaurants, business support, information, and wholesale commerce. That is, weakness is not limited to an isolated sector: it cuts across components associated with consumption, business services, and commercial activity. This is why the first quarter looks particularly vulnerable. With available information, a quarterly GDP contraction of 0.6% is estimated. Although recovery could still occur in subsequent quarters, that recovery would depend on several assumptions: greater execution of public spending, moderation of uncertainty, better performance of private investment, export momentum, and less restrictive monetary policy. None of these factors is guaranteed.

In this context, the international energy shock functions as additional pressure. Brent positioned near $105 per barrel and the Mexican Mix around $94.65. Gas references also rose in Europe and Asia, while jet fuel registered a relevant increase. Added to this is the price increase of wheat and corn, in an environment where urea remains at elevated levels. The energy channel, therefore, affects not only fuels; it can also transmit to transport, food, fertilizers, and logistics costs.

For Mexico, the oil price increase has ambiguous effects. In principle, a higher price can favor petroleum revenues. But this reading is incomplete. An economy that imports gasoline, with productive chains sensitive to transport and with pressures on food, also faces higher costs. Furthermore, when general inflation remains around 4.5% annually and core inflation near 4.3%, any persistent energy or food shock can complicate the disinflation process.

The dilemma for economic policy is evident. If the energy shock persists, the central bank would have less room to accelerate a neutral monetary stance. At the same time, a weak economy demands less restrictive financial conditions and more effective fiscal policy. This combination—low growth, still-elevated inflation, and greater external volatility—requires avoiding mechanical responses. It is not enough to celebrate higher oil prices; one must evaluate their net effect on costs, expectations, and activity.

The external sector offers no automatic exit either. Petroleum exports could improve through price, but manufacturing exports face an uncertain environment. Imports of intermediate and consumer goods, meanwhile, can increase if activity recovers, but also reflect higher external costs. The trade balance could show partial relief without implying structural improvement of the economy.

The current situation demands a sober reading. Mexico does not face a crisis, but rather an uncomfortable combination: weak internal activity, global energy risks, resistant inflation, and dependence on the U.S. cycle. The immediate challenge is to prevent the external shock from amplifying deceleration. For this, priority must be accelerating productive spending, sustaining investment confidence, protecting price stability, and preventing the price increase of energy and food from further deteriorating purchasing power. Expensive oil can improve some revenues, but it does not substitute for a growth strategy.
