Livestock producer in Santa Fe battles to save animals as 5,000 hectares flood

21 April 2026 · 04:21 PM

# "It's a Disaster": She Has 5,000 Hectares Under Water in Santa Fe and Works Against the Clock to Save Her Animals

Heavy rains over the past week have left a desolate panorama in northern Santa Fe: more than one million hectares flooded, entire fields under water, and livestock producers working frantically to remove animals from affected areas and move them to higher ground. The hardest-hit departments are 9 de Julio, General Obligado, and Vera, with Vera being the most compromised since it is an area of southern lowlands where all water falling to the north or west ends up depositing on its territory.

It is for this reason that in Vera—which has a cattle herd of 700,000 head and where livestock is the main agricultural activity—most fields are flooded. "This is already an agricultural disaster," Josefina Saliva, a producer from the department, told Clarín Rural. Her operation of 5,000 hectares is "under water." This situation forced Josefina and her employees to remove animals from the field, starting with the smaller livestock. "Overnight the field becomes unproductive and you have to get all your livestock out. We've already done it with the breeding cattle, which is the first thing you try to save because it's at the most risk, and we do it by herding, because there are no roads," she said.

Most of Vera's fields are in a zone of sub-meridional lowlands. "So we go from one field to another through wooded areas by herding, which already represents a risk, because moving a large quantity of livestock—calves, cows, bulls—means you lose animals along the way, because transport isn't easy," Saliva explained.

But the problems don't end there: "Then, if you want to sell, it also becomes complicated because you have no way to get production out by truck. We have to manage to find a field that's on a paved road, have someone lend us the land, and get there with the animals by herding so we can arrange a sale. In other words, everything is very complicated, because you're cut off from communication, roads are closed, and you don't have phone service because electricity keeps going out, especially when there are storms, so you can't talk to your staff."

Searching for Fields

Josefina, her brother, and her employees have managed to move 700 head to higher ground, but the vast majority of her livestock still remains on her property. "We have some elevated areas built to hold out for a while, but the outlook is to get them all out. We're looking for fields that we'd have to rent, but it's complicated because there aren't any or because people are asking outrageous prices, taking advantage of the situation. We're searching and all the producers in the area are too," she explained.

For Saliva, the option of keeping the animals on the field until the water recedes is hardly feasible. "We have sleeping areas built so they can go up to sleep at night and come back down. But the thing is, the water has covered most of the pasture and they have nowhere to eat. And besides, that grass rots, so you have no choice but to leave," she said.

Moreover, the probability of a quick solution is also uncertain. "All that water isn't going down in a few hours. This takes days, months even. If it didn't rain anymore, it could stay and maybe we could stay with at least the larger animals. But what's happening is a weather pattern called El Niño is forecast, so this is just a prelude to what's coming."

No Infrastructure

While the rains were very heavy, reaching 400 mm in specific areas and averaging 200 to 300 mm in the region, the fact is that the lack of adequate infrastructure only makes the problem worse. "We've been producing however we can for 40 years, under the same conditions. The complaints are always the same: it rains and we're left in the same situation. The sub-meridional lowlands are an area that could be much more productive because it has great potential, but there's no infrastructure. So every time something like this happens, we have to leave with everything, with enormous losses and animal deaths," Saliva commented.

That is why, through the Vera Rural Society, of which Josefina is a member, they are working to secure a declaration of agricultural emergency for the region. "We need it to come out as soon as possible, it needs to be declared now, because it's a disaster for those of us in this situation and we need both the Province of Santa Fe and the Nation to be aware and be able to start managing things," Saliva concluded.
