Santa Fe Livestock Producer Battles Against Flooding of 5,000 Hectares as Agricultural Emergency Declared

21 April 2026 · 04:21 PM

# "It's a Disaster": 5,000 Hectares Underwater in Santa Fe as Livestock Producer Races to Save Animals

Heavy rains over the past week have left a desolate panorama in northern Santa Fe: more than one million hectares flooded, entire fields submerged under water, and livestock producers working against the clock to remove animals from affected areas and move them to higher ground. The hardest-hit departments are those of 9 de Julio, General Obligado, and Vera, with Vera being the most compromised since it is a zone of meridional lowlands where all water falling to the north or west ultimately collects.

This is why in Vera—which has a cattle herd of 700,000 head and where livestock is the primary agricultural activity—most fields are flooded. "This is already an agricultural disaster," Josefina Saliva, a producer in the department, told Clarín Rural. Her operation of 5,000 hectares is "underwater."

This situation has forced Josefina and her employees to remove animals from the field, starting with the smallest livestock. "Overnight the field becomes unproductive and you have to get all your cattle out of there. We've already done it with the breeding stock, which is what you try to remove first because it's the most at risk, and we do it by herding because there are no roads," she said.

Most fields in Vera are located in a zone of sub-meridional lowlands. "So we go from one field to another through wooded terrain by driving, which itself is a risk because moving a large quantity of cattle—calves, cows, bulls—you end up losing animals along the way because the transport isn't easy," Saliva recounted.

But the problems don't end there: "Then, if you want to sell, it's also complicated because you have no way to get your production out by truck. We have to find a field that's on a paved road, get someone to lend us the land, and drive the animals there by herding to arrange a sale. In other words, everything is very complicated because you're cut off from the world, roads are blocked, and you don't have phone service because the power tends to cut out continuously, especially during storms, and you can't communicate with your managers."

Seeking Fields

Josefina, her brother, and her employees have managed to move 700 head to higher ground, but the vast majority of her livestock remains at her establishment. "We have some elevated areas built so we can hold out a bit longer, but the plan is to get them all out. We're looking for fields that we have to rent, and it's complicated because there either aren't any or they're asking exorbitant prices, taking advantage of the situation. We're in that search, and all the producers in the area are too," she explained.

For Saliva, the option of keeping animals on the field until the water recedes is hardly feasible. "We have sleeping areas built so they go up to sleep at night and then come down. But what's happening is that the water has covered most of the pasture and they have nowhere to eat. And besides, that pasture rots, so you have no choice but to leave," she said.

And the likelihood of a quick solution is also uncertain. "All that water won't recede in a few hours. This takes days, months even. If it didn't rain anymore, it could stay there and maybe we could manage, at least with the bigger animals. But what's happening is that an El Niño has been forecast, so this is just a preview of what's coming."

Lack of Infrastructure

While the rains were very heavy, with peaks reaching 400 mm in specific zones and an average of 200 to 300 mm in the region, the truth is that the lack of adequate infrastructure only makes the problem worse. "We've been producing however we can for 40 years under the same conditions. The complaints are always the same: it rains and we're in the same situation. The sub-meridional lowlands is an area that could be much more productive because it has great potential, but there's no infrastructure. So every time something like this happens, we have to leave with everything, with enormous losses and animal deaths," Saliva commented.

For this reason, the Rural Society of Vera, of which Josefina is a member, is working to obtain a declaration of agricultural emergency for the region. "We need it declared as soon as possible because it's a disaster for those of us in this situation, and we need both the Province of Santa Fe and the Nation to be aware and start managing things," Saliva concluded.
