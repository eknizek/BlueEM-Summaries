# Latin America's True Innovation Challenge: The Competitiveness Paradox

What is Latin America's true challenge in a world where innovation defines competitiveness? Experts warn that the region's lag is not in what it produces, but in its inability to innovate.

Latin America has not failed because of what it produces, but because of how it produces. This was one of the central messages from William Maloney, chief economist for the region at the World Bank, during the presentation of his book "Reclaiming the Lost Century: Growth Toward Learning Economies in Latin America and the Caribbean," at an event that brought together business and academic leaders in Colombia.

The diagnosis is striking: the region failed to consolidate itself as a learning economy—that is, it has been unable to adopt, adapt, and scale technologies in a sustained manner. The result, according to the author, is the loss of an entire growth cycle.

The panel, moderated by Marcela Meléndez, director of Fedesarrollo, raised a key question: how accurate is this diagnosis based on business and sectoral experience in Colombia?

For Ana Fernanda Maiguashca, president of the Private Council for Competitiveness, the problem is not new, but it runs deeper than it appears. "It's not a stock problem, it's a flow problem. The gap cannot be closed because the pool drains faster than it fills," she stated. In her view, one of the most critical factors is the lack of trust in Colombian society. She cited recent studies showing high levels of polarization and "insular trust," where people only interact with close circles. "Eight out of ten people are not willing to genuinely interact with others. It's impossible to build economies of scale, learn, or innovate that way," she explained.

This phenomenon, she added, limits the capacity for collaboration and knowledge flow—essential elements in a learning-based economy. She even warned that Colombia could be in a more critical situation than the Latin American average.

From the productive sector, Germán Bahamón, manager of the National Federation of Coffee Growers, agreed that the central challenge lies in technology adoption. "We need to learn, learn, and learn, but above all translate that learning into productivity," he noted. The executive acknowledged that although coffee remains one of Colombia's main economic sectors, the industry faces innovation lags, especially in the field. "We must reduce costs, improve productivity, and further transform our raw materials. That's the key to being competitive," he said.

Bahamón also raised a structural challenge: the lack of appeal of rural areas for new generations. "Today it's more attractive to be a content creator or motorcycle taxi driver than a coffee farmer. We need to sell hope to young people again," he stated, insisting on the need for relevant education that connects technology with rural development.

Meanwhile, Luis Javier Arango, president of PCP Plastic Parts and Components, emphasized another obstacle: mistrust among economic actors. "In Colombia, many times when you enter a business you think the other person will cheat you. That limits partnerships and, therefore, learning," he asserted. However, Arango was more optimistic about opportunities opened by technology, especially artificial intelligence. In his view, this tool can level the playing field between countries, provided there is adequate training. "Today a young person in Colombia can compete with one in the United States if they have access to the right skills," he explained.

The forum also addressed one of the book's key concepts: the "innovation paradox," which raises the question of why, if innovation is profitable, more isn't invested in it. For Maiguashca, the answer lies in a structural failure of the education system. "We educate for obedience, not for creativity or agency. We become accustomed to following instructions, not to questioning or proposing," she noted. This, she explained, limits companies' capacity to innovate sustainably, as there is no culture of experimentation. Additionally, she criticized the tendency to delegate responsibility for innovation to the State. "We think someone else has to solve the problems. That lack of agency holds us back," she stated.

From business experience, Arango added that one of the biggest obstacles is the low quality of technical support and lack of internal capacities. "Many times programs exist, but they don't have the level needed to truly transform companies," he said. In this sense, he highlighted the importance of investing in talent and training.

The forum reached a clear conclusion: beyond policies or diagnoses, Latin America's challenge is to build a culture of learning, trust, and innovation. Without these elements, any growth strategy will be insufficient. As Maloney summarized, the challenge is not just to grow, but to learn to grow better.
