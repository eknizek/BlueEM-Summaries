Oil and the Zombie Tax

04 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Oil and the Zombie Tax

In the field of economics, "zombie ideas" are proposals that have been repeatedly refuted by evidence but stubbornly refuse to disappear from public debate. From this perspective, Brazilian tax policy looks more like a depressing cave of crystallized ideas. The most recent zombie to resurface is the Export Tax (IE) on oil.

The measure had already been used three years ago, when the Government issued a provisional measure (MP) imposing a 9.2% rate on the commodity. On that occasion, both the Judiciary and the Legislature worked to bury the tax: the Federal Court of Appeals (TRF-2) postponed its validity to the following year and Congress tacitly rejected it that same year.

Now, the Executive branch has decided to exhume the proposal with MP 1.340/2026, which again institutes, with immediate effects, the IE on petroleum – this time with the higher rate of 12%.

Both episodes share the same backdrop: pressured by shocks in diesel prices caused by geopolitical events – and, as a result, by the incumbent's popularity in an election year – the government grants subsidies and tax breaks for fuel. The problems with this policy from an economic perspective are widely known: low efficiency and high cost, around R$ 30 billion according to official estimates.

Perhaps considering it appropriate that a zombie intervention be financed with an equally haunted tax, the Executive decided to pass the bill for its electoral generosity to companies that invest tens of billions of reais to produce oil in the country, in a typical cross-subsidy.

At first glance, taxing oil exports could seem intuitive: with the barrel above $100 since the beginning of the conflict in Iran, the tax supposedly would increase domestic supply, reducing prices. Reality, however, is not generous with this narrative.

For starters, the country does not have the capacity to absorb even half of what it produces. Unlike national production, which has grown exponentially over the past 25 years, refining capacity has expanded very timidly. Even ignoring the technical barriers that prevent them from refining certain types of oils extracted in our territory, the fact is that, according to ANP data from 2025, Brazilian refineries processed only 52% of the total produced in the country.

This creates another problem: the national fuel matrix is still heavily dependent on imports. Moreover, much of the other costs incurred in the production chain – such as logistics and insurance, which have skyrocketed since the beginning of the conflict – is sensitive to international dynamics. All this makes the impact on domestic prices inevitable and the taxation of exports incapable of isolating Brazil from the shock of petroleum derivative prices.

None of this is, of course, news to the government, which never intended to use the IE on oil as a regulator of the fuel market. In a rare moment of institutional honesty, the Statement of Reasons for MP 1.340/2026 confesses its real motivation: to collect up to R$ 17.4 billion in four months, a value presented as compensation "to offset the reduction" of PIS/Cofins on diesel and as a mechanism to "capture and transfer to society a portion of the extraordinary gain" that would have been generated by the rise in oil prices.

If there was still anyone who claimed there was no legal link between the IE charge and the fuel subsidy, the economic team announced this week a bill that transforms into legal norm what had already been admitted in writing and in public statements by authorities such as former Minister Fernando Haddad.

The exclusively revenue-collecting purpose undermines its own legitimacy. The IE was designed by the Constitution as a regulatory tax, an instrument for the execution of exchange and foreign trade policies. At most, even if converted to law – which seems unlikely – the MP could only produce effects starting in 2027. This was signaled by Minister Alexandre de Moraes when deciding the case of the IOF increase in 2025: exceptions to the constitutional rule that tax increases only apply in the following fiscal year do not apply when the Executive uses regulatory taxes for openly revenue-raising purposes.

The restoration of this guarantee is especially relevant in a market where contracts are closed months in advance in relation to the shipment of the product, preventing the tax from being charged on sales completed before the beginning of the conflict in the Persian country.

The rhetoric of "collectivizing extraordinary gains" is also fallacious. As happened in 2023, the IE is levied on gross revenue, not on export profits. It is therefore incapable of capturing the increase in production chain costs that accompanied the rise in the commodity and indifferent to the peculiarities of each company – those that exploit mature fields, for example, operate under narrower margins and lower profitability. Moreover, the State already has more than sufficient means to participate in any "extraordinary gains" of the sector: royalties, special participations, and corporate profit taxes.

The resurgence of the IE on oil exposes our chronic inability to formulate effective responses to the global effects of events that tend to become increasingly frequent in an ever less stable world. It is up to civil society to expose these zombie ideas to public debate; and up to the Public Power to bury them in a way that they never return to haunt us.
