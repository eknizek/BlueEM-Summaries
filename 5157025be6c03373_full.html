Oil and the Zombie Tax

04 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Oil and the Zombie Tax

In the field of economics, zombie ideas are those proposals that have been repeatedly refuted by evidence but stubbornly refuse to disappear from public debate. From this perspective, Brazilian tax policy looks more like a depressing cave of crystallized ideas. The most recent zombie to resurface is the Export Tax (IE) on oil. The measure had already been used three years ago, when the Government issued a provisional measure (MP) imposing a 9.2% rate on the commodity. At that time, both the Judiciary and the Legislature buried the tax: the TRF-2 postponed its effectiveness to the following year and Congress rejected it tacitly that same year.

Now, the Executive has decided to exhume the proposal with MP 1.340/2026, which once again institutes, with immediate effects, the IE on oil – this time, with the higher rate of 12%.

The two episodes share the same background: pressured by shocks in diesel prices caused by geopolitical events – and, in turn, by the incumbent's popularity in an election year – the government grants subsidies and tax breaks for the fuel. The problems of this policy from an economic perspective are widely known: low efficiency and high cost, around R$ 30 billion according to official estimates.

Perhaps considering it appropriate that a zombie intervention be financed with an equally haunted tax, the Executive decided to pass the bill for its electoral generosity to companies that invest tens of billions of reais to produce oil in the country, in a typical cross-subsidy.

At first glance, taxing oil exports could seem intuitive: with the barrel above US$ 100 since the beginning of the conflict in Iran, the charge supposedly would expand domestic supply, reducing prices. However, reality is not generous with this narrative.

To begin with, the country does not have the capacity to absorb even half of what it produces. Unlike national production, which has grown exponentially over the past 25 years, refining capacity has expanded very timidly. Even ignoring the technical barriers that prevent them from refining certain types of oils extracted in our territory, the fact is that, according to ANP data from 2025, Brazilian refineries processed the equivalent of only 52% of the total produced in the country.

From this stems another problem: the national fuel matrix is still heavily dependent on imports. Furthermore, many of the other costs incurred in the production chain – such as logistics and insurance, which have skyrocketed since the beginning of the conflict – are sensitive to international dynamics. All this makes the impact on domestic prices inevitable and the taxation of exports incapable of insulating Brazil from the shock of petroleum derivative prices.

None of this, of course, is news to the government, which never intended to use the IE on oil as a regulator of the fuel market. In a rare moment of institutional sincerity, the Explanatory Memorandum of MP 1.340/2026 confesses its real motivation: to collect up to R$ 17.4 billion in four months, a value presented as a counterpart "to compensate for the reduction" of PIS/Cofins on diesel and as a mechanism to "capture and transfer to society a portion of the extraordinary gain" that would have been generated by the rise in oil prices.

If there was still anyone who claimed there was no legal link between the IE charge and fuel subsidies, the economic team announced this week a bill project that turns into legal norm what had already been admitted in writing and in public statements by authorities such as then-minister Fernando Haddad.

The exclusively tax-collecting purpose puts into question its own legitimacy. The IE was conceived by the Constitution as a regulatory tax, an instrument for the execution of exchange rate and foreign trade policies. At most, even if converted into law – which seems unlikely – the MP could only produce effects from 2027 onwards. This is what Minister Alexandre de Moraes signaled when deciding the case of the IOF increase in 2025: the exceptions to the constitutional rule that tax increases only apply in the following fiscal year do not apply when the Executive uses regulatory taxes for openly tax-collecting purposes. The reestablishment of this guarantee is especially relevant in a market where contracts are closed months in advance in relation to product shipment, preventing the tax from being charged on sales concluded before even the beginning of the conflict in the Persian country.

The rhetoric of collectivizing "extraordinary gains" is also fallacious. As had occurred in 2023, the IE applies to gross revenue, not to export profits. It is therefore incapable of capturing the increase in supply chain costs that accompanied the rise in the commodity and indifferent to the peculiarities of each company – those that operate mature fields, for example, operate under narrower margins and lower profitability. Furthermore, the State already has more than sufficient means to participate in any "extraordinary gains" in the sector: royalties, special participations, and taxes on corporate profit.

The resurgence of the IE on oil exposes our chronic inability to formulate effective responses to the global effects of events that tend to become increasingly frequent in an increasingly unstable world. It is up to civil society to expose these zombie ideas to public debate; and to the Public Power to bury them in a way that they never return to haunt us.
