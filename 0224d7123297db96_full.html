# Spoofing: How Scammers Falsify Phone Numbers

Brazilians receive more than 1 billion abusive calls per month, according to a study by Idec (Consumer Defense Institute). In 2025 alone, 161.16 billion short calls of up to six seconds were recorded, many of them used by robots to validate active numbers before new commercial ventures or fraud attempts.

The government of Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) will implement a system that will allow call verification against telephone fraud to prevent scams.

One of the techniques used by criminals is call spoofing, which occurs when the number displayed on the cell phone does not correspond to the true caller. In practice, the scammer manages to make the call appear to come from a trusted source, such as companies or known contacts.

Data from cybersecurity company Kaspersky show that in the last six months, approximately 70% of Brazilians received unwanted calls, and about 15% were fraud attempts.

The technique typically uses VoIP technology (Voice over Internet Protocol), which converts voice into digital data and allows calls to be made through online systems without directly depending on a traditional telephone line. This gives flexibility to companies but also opens loopholes. According to Kaspersky, this type of system allows the caller ID to be configured manually. That is, whoever makes the call can choose which number will appear on the recipient's screen.

This is how criminals manage to simulate real numbers of companies or banks, use local area codes to increase the chance of answering, and replicate numbers very similar to the victim's (a technique known as "neighbor spoofing"). In this latter case, the numbers share the same initial digits of the victim's phone, creating a false sense of proximity and security.

The use of VoIP and even number customization is not, in itself, illegal. Call center companies, delivery services, and even public agencies use this resource to centralize customer service or standardize contact with clients. Professionals such as journalists may also hide numbers for security reasons.

The problem begins when criminals use the technology to deceive people. Upon answering, the victim is induced to provide sensitive information such as Social Security number, passwords, or codes, or to make transfers. According to Kaspersky, this type of attack generally involves social engineering techniques—psychological manipulation to lead the victim to act without suspicion. Criminals exploit human behavior such as fear, urgency, or trust in known institutions.

In some cases, criminals induce the victim to install remote access applications—such as AnyDesk or TeamViewer—to take control of the phone and access bank accounts.

## Most Common Types of Spoofing Scams

According to Kaspersky, the scripts used by criminals follow recurring patterns. Among the most common are:

- False bank representative, with alerts of "suspicious activity" or imminent account lockdown

- False operator center or technical support, requesting data or device access

- Scams involving social security or "proof of life," such as alleged pending issues or proof of life

- Calls about online purchases, with warnings of unrecognized transactions or blocked deliveries

- Silent calls, used to identify active numbers and map the victim's habits

These approaches frequently simulate urgency or serious problems to pressure the victim to act quickly. In many cases, the criminal already possesses basic information about the victim—such as full name or Social Security number—which makes the contact even more convincing.

## What is the Difference Between Spoofing, Phishing, and Vishing?

Although related, the terms are not synonymous. According to antivirus company Eset:

- **Spoofing:** is the technique of falsifying identity (phone, email, website)

- **Phishing:** is the scam that uses "bait" to steal data

- **Vishing:** is phishing done through a phone call

In practice, spoofing is usually the initial step—the way to make the contact appear trustworthy. Phishing and vishing are the execution of the scam, when the criminal actually tries to obtain information or money.
