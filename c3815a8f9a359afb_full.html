# Flávio Dino's Decision on CVM Fiscal Fee Could Transform Securities Regulator

A decision by Minister Flávio Dino could change the situation at CVM, providing a significant increase in budget allocation and supervisory capacity for the capital markets watchdog, after years of meager funding and a depleted workforce. Following an extensive public hearing yesterday with regulatory and market bodies, from the Federal Police and Coaf to Anbima and Abrasca, Dino signed an injunction on Tuesday ordering the Union to stop retaining the fee that should finance CVM — and gave the government 20 days to present a concrete restructuring plan for the agency.

In the 2023-2025 three-year period, CVM collected R$ 3.17 billion from the Securities and Stock Market Supervision Tax. It received back R$ 845 million, approximately 27%. The remaining R$ 2.29 billion went straight to the National Treasury. Dino's decision requires that everything CVM collects, minus the Revenue Untying limit (DRU) of up to 30%, stays with the agency. In practice, this could represent more than R$ 800 million per year that currently goes to the federal budget with no specific allocation.

This is a discussion that has dragged on for years, a demand from different CVM directors over time, who have faced fiscal and political barriers in enforcing the original tax allocation. One of the Union's concerns was that other agencies and bodies, which also see their fees go directly to the National Treasury, would follow the same path.

For lawyer Marcelo Barbosa, former CVM president who participated in the hearing representing Anbima, this is a historic decision. "Minister Flávio Dino's decision has transformative potential for CVM. Banks, advisors, fund managers, companies and other regulated entities pay this fee expecting CVM to do its job with full conditions, but for many years this collection has not been converted into funding for system modernization, employee training, expanded supervision and enforcement," says Barbosa. "It took all this strain we're seeing for the issue to gain relevance."

The minister's injunction comes in the wake of the Banco Master case, Operation Carbon Hidden, and an accumulation of capital market investigations into money laundering. Coaf president Ricardo Saadi said in yesterday's hearing that funds, fintechs and brokerages are increasingly being used for money laundering. The Federal Police pointed in the same direction.

The internal situation at CVM is critical. The agency went 15 years without a public tender. The Collegiate Board, the body that judges and applies sanctions, operates with only two of five members. In 2026, until yesterday, it had not held a single judgment. The backlog of open cases reached 804. Daniel Valadão, superintendent of the agency, summarized at the hearing: "CVM appears to be designed to fail."

The injunction requires the Union to present within 20 days an Emergency Plan for 2026, with measurable targets in four areas: repression of backlogged cases, personnel and technology recomposition, financial intelligence with Coaf and PF, and preventive supervision of the fund industry. In 90 days, a medium-term plan for 2027 onward, including a public tender and salary review. Dino notified the President of the Republic and the President of Congress about the incompleteness of the Collegiate Board.

The discussion is part of ADI 7.791, filed by the Novo Party, which argues that either the tax is unconstitutional or it must be applied to the purpose for which it was created. The minister's decision still goes to the Plenary for ratification. If confirmed, it could finally resolve CVM's financial crisis, which regulates R$ 50 trillion in assets with a discretionary budget of R$ 35 million.
