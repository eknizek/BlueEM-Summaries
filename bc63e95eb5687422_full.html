# The Next Decade of E-Commerce in Latin America Hinges on Delivery

When discussing e-commerce today, the debate almost always revolves around future technologies: AI agents, real-time personalization, social commerce and other very relevant trends we are incorporating. But there is something more structural, in the present, that deserves attention: Latin America still operates with digital retail penetration of 14% – less than half the levels of China (30%) and the United States (25%). This gap is not a symptom of delay; it is the largest structural opportunity in global retail in the next decade. And it will not be captured solely with the levers of the future, but by levers of the present, such as building trust through the speed and reliability of delivery, expanding offerings in everyday categories and financial inclusion.

The gross merchandise value (GMV) of e-commerce in the region should reach $310 billion in 2029, a 53% increase from current values, according to Morgan Stanley. In Brazil, the central engine of this advance, the bank also projects that online retail penetration will reach 19% in the next four years – which would represent the incorporation of tens of millions of new digital shoppers to the market.

For this potential to materialize, the sector still needs to advance on three structural fronts:

**The first is logistics.** Latin America is a continent of continental dimensions and deep regional inequalities. The World Bank's Logistics Performance Index – a global ranking of logistics efficiency in 139 countries – places Brazil in 51st place and Mexico in 66th. Still, the behavior of Latin American consumers has already evolved toward an immediacy pattern comparable to global markets: they want speed and predictability. Same-day or 48-hour delivery is no longer premium – it is the new minimum standard. In Brazil, where Mercado Libre already has 28 fulfillment distribution centers and will reach 42 by year-end, the consolidation of own logistics networks has been the decisive factor in advancing on this front.

**The second is assortment.** E-commerce in Latin America is still associated in consumer imagination with electronics and fashion. But the next growth frontier lies in everyday categories – supermarket and medications. Digital penetration of food retail in the region still operates in single-digit percentages, representing one of the largest offline-to-online migration opportunities still available in the global market. The same applies to the pharmaceutical segment: in Brazil, 13% of municipalities have no physical pharmacy, creating an access bottleneck that e-commerce is structurally capable of solving. The logic of these categories goes beyond volume: recurring purchases of essential items create habits. And habit is the most valuable asset in digital retail – it is what transforms an occasional buyer into an engaged consumer who returns frequently. In Q4, Mercado Libre registered nine purchases per unique active users in the quarter, a 15% year-over-year increase, but still falls short of the level reached by more developed markets. In the United States, for example, between 34% and 41% of consumers shop online weekly.

**The third – and perhaps the most transformative – is financial inclusion.** The future of online retail cannot be projected without addressing banking exclusion. According to the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB), approximately 45% of Latin Americans do not have a bank account and 80% do not have access to a credit card – figures driven mainly by Mexico and Argentina, where banking penetration is still in its infancy. Brazil presents a different scenario: it is the largest and most advanced market for digital financial services in the region, but access to credit remains deeply unequal. This is where e-commerce enters as a vector of inclusion: by democratizing access to credit through transaction data-based scoring models, it allows classes C and D to expand their participation in the digital market.

The retail of the future will be defined by the ability to orchestrate the invisible – such as logistics, assortment and credit – while delivering what the consumer sees: speed, trust and increasingly personalized and engaging experiences, from social commerce to agentic AI. But the future does not replace the present. It will be built upon it. The sector's commitment should be to continue transforming retail into a development tool, reducing barriers to entry for entrepreneurs and formalizing the economy through technology. What we will see in the coming years is not just the growth of platforms, but the maturation of an ecosystem that places the Latin American consumer at the center of a world-class consumption infrastructure.
