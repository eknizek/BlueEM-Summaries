# Nearly one-third of Brazilians have no emergency fund, Anbima/Datafolha survey shows

At the end of last year, almost one-third (31%) of Brazilians said they had no financial reserves, while another 32% claimed to have less than the minimum emergency fund recommended by experts—enough to cover three to five months of expenses. The data comes from the 9th edition of the Investor Profile survey, conducted by Anbima (Brazilian Association of Entities in Financial and Capital Markets) in partnership with Datafolha. The survey interviewed 5,832 people, all aged 16 or older, about their relationship with investments in 2025. The margin of error is one percentage point up or down, within a 95% confidence level.

The survey also segmented the data by social class and generation. Almost half (48%) of class D/E said they had no financial reserves. This percentage drops to 30% when the sample includes Brazilians from class C and to 13% among those in classes A and B. Among generations, Generation X (born between 1965 and 1980) leads in lack of reserves—37% of its members said they save nothing. Millennials (between 1981 and 1996) come next with 28%. Generation Z (between 1997 and 2012) and Boomers (1946 to 1964) are tied at the lowest rate, with 17% each.

"It's important to note that Generation Z has extremes. Some are just starting university with minimal financial independence, while others are already working and saving money," said Marcelo Billi, superintendent of Sustainability, Innovation and Education at Anbima, in an interview.

The importance of maintaining an emergency reserve is emphasized by experts as one of the first steps for those wanting a more organized financial life. The size of this fund depends on each person's profile. The ideal is to save the equivalent of about six months of fixed expenses to avoid hardship from unexpected events, from home repairs to job loss.

The survey also asked respondents about the main financial products they use. Savings accounts lead: 22% of the population said they maintain this investment and 20% plan to continue using it in the coming years. Private bonds and investment funds follow with 7% and 5%, respectively. Stocks, public bonds, and private pensions are at the bottom of the ranking with 2% each.

The survey also reveals something curious: for Brazilians, saving, investing, and investing in financial products are not the same thing. In 2025, 33% said they had saved, 24% claimed to have invested, but only 10% reported applying money to financial products. The difference makes sense when understanding how Brazilians define investment. For many, it is "anything in which I put my money expecting some return," says Billi, which includes cosmetic procedures, children's education, travel, cars, and real estate. Since the survey uses spontaneous responses without guiding respondents to choose between options, this distinction is evident in the numbers.

Restricting the view to only financial products, Anbima and Datafolha count approximately 60.6 million investors in the country, about 36% of the population—and the projection for this year is that another 8.7 million will begin investing.
