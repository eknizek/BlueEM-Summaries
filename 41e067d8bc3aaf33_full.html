Latin American Aviation Faces Its Toughest Test: Soaring Costs, Weak Policies, and a Historic Opportunity, Says LATAM Group

Roberto Alvo, manager of the group, says the region carries structural debts in public policies, connectivity and incentives that must be addressed.

The air industry faces one of its greatest pressures in years, but the real challenge is not just the rise in fuel costs, but the tasks that Latin America continues to postpone regarding inconsistent public policies, underutilized infrastructure, and a lack of strategic vision that limits its potential.

"The cost increase is gigantic," summarizes Roberto Alvo, General Manager of Latam Airlines Group, who participated in the IATA Wings of Change Americas in Santiago, Chile, in assessing the impact of the current context. Airlines consume approximately 300 billion dollars in jet fuel, and a scenario of doubled prices implies another 300 billion additional in an industry that barely aspired to 4 percent margins.

However, the executive maintains a pragmatic outlook. He proposes an optimistic scenario with prices stabilized between 100 and 120 dollars per barrel, although he does not rule out levels of up to 150 dollars if global tensions persist. "I prefer to be optimistic, but the industry will have to adapt," he notes.

Still, aviation is not starting from scratch. "The industry is accustomed to high prices, but not to such a rapid and violent increase," he warns, underscoring that the difference now is the speed of the shock rather than its magnitude.

That cost shock forces difficult decisions, and in that scenario the transfer to fares appears as something inevitable. "This increase has to be passed on to prices," the executive affirms, anticipating that if the situation persists, there will also be adjustments in supply. However, demand continues to resist. "We don't see a real impact on people's willingness to travel," the executive maintains, which opens a window of stability amid uncertainty.

But that resilience also has limits. If costs remain high, the balance of the system will depend on something deeper than market dynamics: it will depend on the structural conditions in which the industry operates.

Structural Debt

That is where the diagnosis becomes more critical, because what worries is not the current situation but the lack of a solid foundation to sustain growth. "We haven't known how to communicate the value of the air industry," Alvo acknowledges. And that failure has consequences, such as public decisions that, instead of boosting the sector, end up making it more expensive or restricting it.

The contrast within the region is evident. Countries with consistent policies have achieved greater air penetration, while others, with similar income levels, remain lagging. The difference, he argues, lies in basic elements, among which openness, infrastructure, and regulatory stability stand out. "There are countries where the tax burden is greater than the ticket price. That can't be good for anyone," warned the Latam spokesman.

Absence of Strategy

The problem is not minor, because aviation is a central gear of the economy. "We are one of the main arteries: if the air industry stops, the world stops," he stated. The impact goes far beyond flights. For each direct job, multiple indirect positions are generated in tourism, commerce, and services. However, that multiplier effect continues to be underestimated in policy design.

Concrete examples illustrate the cost of that disconnect. In Peru, for example, measures that make connectivity more expensive can end up affecting tourist arrivals. "How many people will stop flying because of that?" the businessman asked. There are airports that are not used efficiently more due to management than lack of investment.
