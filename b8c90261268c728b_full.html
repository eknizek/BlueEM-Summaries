AmCham warns US investment in Colombia is 'cooling': rhetoric alone won't sustain growth

13 April 2026 · 08:54 PM

# AmCham Warns US Investment in Colombia is "Cooling": "Growth Cannot Be Sustained with Rhetoric Alone"

According to the Central Bank, fresh capital fell approximately 27 percent, totaling 5.408 billion dollars in 2025.

According to the Colombian-American Chamber of Commerce (AmCham Colombia), the beginning of 2026 carries a warning that should not go unnoticed: while bilateral trade with the United States shows strong dynamism, the arrival of new investment shows signs of caution that the country cannot ignore.

Between January and February 2026, exports to the United States reached 2.669 billion dollars, representing growth of 22.5 percent and accounting for 32 percent of Colombia's total external sales. But the significance of this figure lies not only in volume but also in what it reveals about the structure of Colombian exports.

Seventy-one percent of sales to the United States corresponded to non-mining-energy goods, which totaled 1.891 billion dollars and grew 24 percent. For AmCham Colombia, the signal is clear: the United States not only continues buying more Colombian products but remains the most important platform for diversifying the country's export basket.

The depth of this relationship is also reflected in the business base. On average, more than 3,000 Colombian companies export annually to the United States, equivalent to approximately one-third of the country's total exporting companies.

Regarding imports, approximately 70 percent of goods from the United States correspond to products not produced in Colombia that are essential inputs for industry and the country's productive chains. In January 2026, these imports totaled 1.240 billion dollars, a decline of 7.1 percent compared to the same period last year.

However, in investment, the message is different and more demanding. The United States remained in 2025 as the principal foreign investor in Colombia, with flows of 3.375 billion dollars, equivalent to 29.4 percent of the country's total foreign investment received.

But beyond the total amount, what concerns AmCham Colombia is the composition of that investment. Fresh capital fell from 7.427 billion to 5.408 billion dollars, a reduction of approximately 27 percent. Meanwhile, reinvestment of profits rose from 4.818 billion to 5.112 billion dollars, according to information from the Central Bank.

AmCham Colombia asserted that this contrast should not be minimized because the figures make clear that those already in Colombia continue to bet on the country, but the arrival of new capital is cooling. This reflects a signal of caution regarding the investment environment. Therefore, it warned that Colombia cannot normalize this message or let it be lost among good trade results.

"This situation should be a wake-up call for the country and especially for those aspiring to govern it. Colombia needs to recover competitiveness and confidence as an investment destination," said María Claudia Lacouture, president of AmCham Colombia.

She also stated that it is not sufficient to claim that "the country remains attractive; it is necessary to demonstrate it with legal certainty, regulatory stability, coherent institutional messages and confidence in execution. Investment is not sustained by rhetoric, but by clear rules."

More concerning still is that this signal comes at a moment when the regional context is actually showing greater investment dynamism. According to Cepal, Latin America and the Caribbean received 188.962 billion dollars in foreign direct investment, growth of 7.1 percent.
