# Trade War with Ecuador: Colombia to Raise Tariffs on Coffee, Shrimp, Rice and Plastics from May

The commercial relationship between Colombia and Ecuador is going through one of its most critical moments. The Ministry of Commerce has published a draft decree that escalates the tariff response to measures adopted by the Ecuadorian government. As of May 1, 2026, a wide range of products from the neighboring country will face levies at three levels: 35 percent, 50 percent, and 75 percent, coinciding with the date Ecuador would activate its own 100 percent increase.

This dispute is based on non-compliance with the Cartagena Agreement, the foundation of the Andean Community (CAN). Colombia argues that the tariff imposed by Ecuador is a prohibited levy between member countries. The tension has escalated to the point that President Gustavo Petro suggested that these measures could mean the end of the Andean Pact for Colombia, proposing a possible shift toward deeper integration with Mercosur.

## Products Affected by the Increase

The draft decree establishes a tiered structure according to the type of good, impacting both the basic basket and the industrial sector:

**75 percent tariff (maximum impact):** Includes sensitive products such as coffee in all its forms, rice, sugar cane, cocoa powder, palm and sunflower oil. It also affects plastic manufactures, petroleum-derived fuels, steel and aluminum profiles, and appliances such as refrigerators and vacuum cleaners.

**50 percent tariff:** Directed at industrial goods such as fish preparations, fats, oils, automobile and heavy vehicle tires, and industrial papers.

**35 percent tariff:** Applies to shrimp, langostinos, beans, fresh plantains, medicines for human use, and animal feed.

## The Millionaire Cost of the Commercial Dispute

The decree not only establishes the new taxes but also quantifies the projected economic damage for both nations if the conflict continues. According to 2025 figures, the impact would be devastating for binational trade flow:

- Colombian exports to Ecuador would be reduced by 79 percent, representing a decline of 1.452 billion dollars.

- Imports from Ecuador would fall 75 percent, equivalent to 640 million dollars less for its economy.

This "trade war" scenario threatens to dismantle years of regional integration. The measures proposed by the Colombian government will be subject to review by the Customs Affairs Committee within two months following their entry into force. For now, the business sector in both countries is watching May 1 with concern—a date that could mark a before and after in the commercial exchange of the Andean region if no prior diplomatic agreement is reached.
