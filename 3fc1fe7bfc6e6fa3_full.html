# Trade War with Ecuador: Colombia to Raise Tariffs on Coffee, Shrimp, Rice and Plastics from May

The commercial relationship between Colombia and Ecuador is going through one of its most critical moments. The Ministry of Commerce has published a draft decree that escalates the tariff response against measures adopted by the Ecuadorian government. Starting May 1, 2026, a wide range of products from the neighboring country will face duties at three levels: 35 percent, 50 percent, and 75 percent, coinciding with the date Ecuador would activate its own 100 percent increase.

This dispute is based on non-compliance with the Cartagena Agreement, the foundation of the Andean Community (CAN). Colombia argues that the tariff imposed by Ecuador is a prohibited duty among member countries. The tension has escalated to the point that President Gustavo Petro suggested that these measures could mean the end of the Andean Pact for Colombia, raising the possibility of a shift toward deeper integration with Mercosur.

## Products Affected by the Increase

The draft decree establishes a tiered approach depending on the type of good, impacting both the basic basket and the industrial sector:

**75 percent tariff (maximum impact):** Includes sensitive products such as coffee in all its forms, rice, cane sugar, cocoa powder, palm and sunflower oil. It also affects plastic manufactures, petroleum-derived fuels, steel and aluminum profiles, as well as appliances like refrigerators and vacuum cleaners.

**50 percent tariff:** Directed at industrial goods such as fish preparations, fats, oils, automotive and heavy vehicle tires, and industrial papers.

**35 percent tariff:** Applies to shrimp, langoustines, beans, fresh plantains, medications for human use, and animal feed.

## The Millionaire Cost of Commercial Dispute

The decree not only establishes new taxes but quantifies the projected economic damage for both nations if the conflict continues. According to 2025 figures, the impact would be devastating for bilateral trade flows:

Colombian exports to Ecuador would be reduced by 79 percent, representing a loss of 1,452 million dollars. Imports from Ecuador would fall 75 percent, equivalent to 640 million dollars less for its economy.

This "trade war" scenario threatens to dismantle years of regional integration. The measures proposed by the Colombian Government will be subject to review by the Committee on Customs Affairs within two months following their implementation. For now, the business sector of both countries watches with concern May 1, a date that could mark a before and after in the trade exchange of the Andean region if a prior diplomatic agreement is not reached.
