# Brazil leads: Ibovespa surges nearly 30% in dollar terms this year, outperforming S&P 500 and peers

The Brazilian stock market is consolidating itself in 2026 as the main destination for capital in Latin America, with performance that far exceeds that of its regional peers and Wall Street, in a scenario marked by global rotation toward emerging markets and the search for commodity exposure due to the war in the Middle East.

Year-to-date, the Ibovespa has recorded a gain of 29.55% in dollars, compared to 4.57% for the S&P 500, while the Peruvian stock exchange advances 17.21% and the Colombian, 14.71%.

The difference is paralleled by a sustained flow of foreign capital that has repositioned Brazil as the axis of strategy in emerging markets. This movement is reflected in the iShares MSCI Brazil ETF, which received $1.53 billion this month, its largest inflow since 2009, according to Bloomberg data. The flow is a sign that international demand for Brazilian assets is accelerating amid restructuring of global portfolios.

Bank of America specialists indicate that Latin America continued to receive capital flows despite the conflict between the United States and Iran, and that Brazil remained the main recipient. Institutional investors are maintaining their positions in a scenario of weak dollar and favorable terms of trade, which has led analysts to question whether the Latin American giant can be considered "the new gold."

This week, BofA analysts raised their projection for the Ibovespa from 180,000 to 210,000 points, in a scenario of reduced global tensions.

"We believe that an eventual scenario of détente could favor the continuation of investment flows in emerging markets, thanks to the reduction of inflationary pressures and greater scope for central banks to cut interest rates," the analysts said.

## Global flows

The position on Brazil reflects a broader shift in global asset allocation. JPMorgan analysts highlight that investors are demonstrating "a slightly more optimistic tone regarding Latin America, especially Brazil," thanks to greater allocation in commodities and the fact that preparation for global disruptions is gaining relevance in institutional strategies.

This shift is based on structural factors that reinforce the investment thesis. Pedro Grimaldi and Ben Laidler, analysts at Bradesco BBI, state that "Brazilian stocks have 'nine lives,' benefiting in an almost unique way from a confluence of factors related to emerging markets, geopolitics, global and local macroeconomics, which we believe still have potential for growth." For the experts, this contrasts with previous cycles marked by deterioration in public finances or external shocks.

The flow to Brazil is also explained by accumulated underweighting in exposure to emerging markets. Grimaldi and Laidler highlight that "there is enormous underweighting by investors in emerging markets," which opens space for gradual portfolio restructuring, with Brazil as one of the main beneficiaries due to its size, liquidity, and sectoral diversification.

In the foreign exchange market, the appreciation of the Brazilian real reinforces this trend. David Beker and Natacha Pérez, Bank of America analysts, state that "investors remain comfortable maintaining their exposure to the Brazilian real and Brazilian stocks," in a scenario where dollar devaluation is considered an essential factor for the continuation of resource flows to emerging markets.

Year-to-date, the Brazilian currency is the most appreciated in Latin America, with a gain of around 10%, which also positions it as the most appreciated among emerging markets.

## What explains the gains

Brazil's performance can be explained by a combination of macroeconomic and sectoral factors. The UBS team, which includes analyst Alejo Czerwonko, highlights that "Brazil stands out among emerging markets, supported by solid fundamentals, cuts in local rates, and its role as a major commodity exporter," in a context of elevated energy prices that favor producing economies.

The weight of commodities in the index reinforces this dynamic. Bank of America highlights that oil companies are responsible for approximately one-third of the contribution to the index's gains this year, in a market where rising oil prices drive both the trade balance and corporate profits.

In parallel, the monetary cycle introduces an additional catalyst. Grimaldi and Laidler note that "Brazil began cutting rates starting from 15% last month, in a unique, comprehensive cycle with dual impact," with effects on both valuations and domestic flows, which could reverse years of outflows to the fixed-income market. The combination of high rates and potential cuts creates an attractive differential compared to other markets.

For Bank of America, "returns on local rates are attractive," although the perception persists that inflation may limit the pace of monetary easing. The political issue adds an additional factor. JPMorgan notes growing investor interest in the electoral process, in a context where "Brazil could fare well in any scenario," while Bradesco BBI considers there is "optionality in the October elections," in a market with high exposure to state-owned companies and sensitivity to fiscal decisions.

## Valuations and risks

Despite the gains, debate over valuations remains open. Bank of America warns that "Brazilian stocks are no longer cheap in valuation terms," although it maintains an optimistic view based on profit growth and cash generation in key sectors. The market presents relatively moderate multiples and high dividend yields.

Grimaldi and Laidler, contrary to JPMorgan, observe that "Brazil remains one of the cheapest stock markets in the world," with a forward P/E ratio near 10.2x and an estimated dividend yield of 5.3%, which maintains interest from global investors seeking value.
