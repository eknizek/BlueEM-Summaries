# Celsia to sell 6 of its hydroelectric plants to Grupo Luz y Fuerza Colombia: What's behind this deal?

Celsia is about to close the sale of six of its hydroelectric plants to Grupo Luz y Fuerza Colombia (GLF), a company that is part of a multinational with more than 32 years of experience in the energy generation sector. Grupo Luz y Fuerza Colombia is willing to pay more than 90 billion pesos for these plants, which would be added to the three self-generation assets it had already acquired from Cementos Argos in 2018.

Specifically, the agreement includes six small hydroelectric plants (PCH) that operate under run-of-river technology in the Valle del Cauca department.

The plants that are part of this deal are:

- Río Cali I: 0.9 megawatts (Cali)

- Río Cali II: 0.9 megawatts (Cali)

- Río Frío I: 1.69 megawatts (Riofrío)

- El Rumor: 2.5 megawatts (Tuluá)

- Nima I: 2 megawatts (Palmira)

- Nima II: 4.7 megawatts (Palmira)

These hydroelectric plants have a combined installed capacity of 12.7 megawatts. This figure represents only 0.6 percent of Celsia's total portfolio, which reaches 2,066 megawatts in Colombia and Central America.

Celsia justified the sale of these assets to Grupo Luz y Fuerza Colombia as a strategic move to concentrate its efforts and capabilities on larger-scale projects. For the company, the focus is now on initiatives with a cost structure and operations that allow levels of competitiveness and profitability that these small plants no longer offer.

Celsia explained to EL TIEMPO that this operation with Grupo Luz y Fuerza Colombia has three main objectives.

First, a strengthening of its capital structure. The resources the company will receive will be allocated entirely to debt reduction, a strategic move with which the company projects an increase of 7 billion pesos in its consolidated net profit.

Beyond the financial aspect, the operation responds to a rebalancing of operational focus. Celsia seeks to concentrate its human and technical talent in the management of larger assets. Specifically, in large reservoirs and small hydroelectric plants with capacities exceeding 10 megawatts, where Celsia already has proven competitive advantages.

Finally, this asset rotation aims to provide the company with greater agility in facing environmental challenges. By divesting from these plants, Celsia seeks to leverage a growth model that is, above all, profitable and sustainable in the long term.

"With this move, Celsia reaffirms its commitment to constantly evolving, adapting to market and business needs to continue leading the energy transformation in Colombia," the company assured EL TIEMPO.
