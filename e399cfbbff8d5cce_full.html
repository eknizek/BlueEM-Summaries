Northern Santa Fe faces million-hectare flooding; livestock sector hit hardest

20 April 2026 · 06:25 PM

# "The losses are in the millions": An estimated one million hectares flooded in northern Santa Fe

The abundant rains recorded last week in northern Santa Fe have resulted in one million hectares flooded in the departments of 9 de Julio, General Obligado, and Vera, with losses in the millions for the livestock sector, the main economic activity in the region. While all three districts were affected by precipitation reaching up to 400 millimeters in some areas, the Vera department was hit hardest, with producers requesting that an agricultural emergency be declared and requesting a postponement of the foot-and-mouth disease vaccination campaign deadline.

The Vera department is a zone of southern lowlands, which facilitates water accumulation, not only from local rainfall but also because it receives runoff from surrounding regions. In an interview with Clarín Rural, the president of the Vera Rural Society, Carlos Chamorro, stated that "until last week there were districts where more than 400 mm fell and in those areas the situation is alarming. The average for the department was between 200 and 300 mm, so the accumulated total is significant."

"The three departments are in difficult situations, but especially ours, because it is a zone of southern lowlands where all the accumulated rain from the west flows here," said Chamorro, who noted that the region's natural drainage is the Paraná River to the north and the Salado River to the south, which is currently overflowing due to rains in Tucumán and Salta.

The livestock sector, the primary activity in the area, is the hardest hit. "People have been moving animals to higher ground for several days. Trucks cannot circulate, so livestock must be herded on foot. And whatever little harvest was possible could not be done. That's why the losses are in the millions in every sense," Chamorro added.

According to a report submitted to the Confederation of Rural Associations of Santa Fe (Carsfe) to request an agricultural emergency declaration from the provincial and national governments, Vera, a livestock department par excellence, currently has approximately 700,000 head of cattle. Due to flooding, the impact on the sector results in loss of body condition in herds, restricted access to quality water, feeding and logistical difficulties, and health problems such as hoof diseases and parasites. While some animal mortality has been recorded, Chamorro noted these are still isolated cases.

The damage extends beyond production: "There are isolated villages today, schools not holding classes because they cannot be reached, northern towns without electricity... The situation is very complicated."

The rains and flooding have also exposed infrastructure problems facing the agricultural sector, with rural roads being the main obstacle to production. "The complaints we are making today are the same ones we have been making for 30 years: we have very little road infrastructure, which today is impassable, and all this is aggravated by the season—heading into winter—and by the scarce pasture available, which with all this water simply does not exist," Chamorro concluded.
