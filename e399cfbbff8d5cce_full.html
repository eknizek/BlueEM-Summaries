Estimated one million hectares flooded in northern Santa Fe, losses mount

20 April 2026 · 06:25 PM

# "The losses are in the millions": Estimated one million hectares flooded in northern Santa Fe

The abundant rains recorded last week in northern Santa Fe resulted in one million flooded hectares in the departments of 9 de Julio, General Obligado, and Vera, with millions in losses to livestock activity, the main production in the area. Although the three districts were affected by rainfall that accumulated up to 400 millimeters, the most affected was Vera, whose producers are calling for an agricultural emergency to be declared in the department and for postponement of the completion of the foot-and-mouth disease vaccination campaign.

The Vera department is a low-lying meridional zone, which promotes water accumulation, not only from local rainfall but also because it receives accumulated water from neighboring regions. In conversation with Clarín Rural, the president of the Vera Rural Society, Carlos Chamorro, indicated that "until last week there were districts where more than 400 mm fell, and in those areas the situation is alarming. The department average was between 200 and 300 mm, so the accumulated total is significant."

"The three departments are complicated, but especially ours, because it is a low-lying meridional zone where all the accumulated rain from the west flows here," said Chamorro, who noted that the natural drainage in the region is the Paraná River to the north and the Salado River to the south, which is now overflowing due to rains in Tucumán and Salta.

Livestock activity, the main activity in the area, is the most affected. Given this situation, the local rural society leader considers the situation "more than complicated." "People have been moving animals for several days to higher ground. Trucks cannot circulate, so livestock is moved by herding. And what little had to be harvested could not be done. That is why losses are in the millions in every sense," Chamorro added.

According to a report submitted to the Confederation of Rural Associations of Santa Fe (Carsfe) to request an agricultural emergency declaration from the province and nation, Vera, a quintessentially livestock department, currently has approximately 700,000 head of cattle. Due to flooding, the impact on the activity results in loss of herd body condition, restricted access to quality water, difficulties in supplementation and food logistics, and health problems such as foot diseases and parasitism.

While animal mortality has been recorded, Chamorro noted that cases are still isolated. But the damage is not only productive: "Today there are isolated settlements, schools that are not holding classes because they cannot be reached, towns in the north without electricity... The situation is very complicated."

The rains and flooding have also exposed infrastructure problems facing the agricultural sector, with rural roads being the main obstacle to production. "The claims we are making today are the same ones we have been making for 30 years: we have very little road infrastructure, which today is impassable, and all this is worsened by the season it is occurring in, entering winter, with the scarce pasture that exists, which with this water is simply gone," Chamorro concluded.
