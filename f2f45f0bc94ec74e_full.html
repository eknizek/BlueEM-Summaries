# USA and Canada Will Learn That the Best Fintechs Come From Latin America, Says Dan Herman

Dan Herman, a specialist in geopolitics and innovation, will be a speaker at São Paulo Innovation Week

An expert in innovation policy, professor and Canadian documentarian, Dan Herman affirms that the recipe for creating successful startups in any country is to encourage them to be globally competitive. Herman cites the case of Nubank, created in Brazil, which is taking its first steps in the United States after obtaining a license to operate as a bank in the country at the beginning of this year.

"In particular in finance, American and Canadian companies have always felt secure. Now, we will learn that the best technologies, especially in fintechs, come from Latin America. Whether it's open banking in Uruguay or innovations in Brazil, the world is about to realize the potential that comes out of São Paulo and the region," he states.

Herman is co-founder of the Centre for Digital Entrepreneurship and Economic Performance (DEEP Centre), a think tank dedicated to digital entrepreneurship and economic performance, and serves as a professor at the Munk School of Global Affairs & Public Policy at the University of Toronto. According to him, the world is at a moment where innovation can come from anyone and anywhere, including countries that were not previously known for creating global or disruptive businesses.

For Herman, companies and countries should accelerate investments in innovation to find new business partners given the turbulent geopolitical scenario. "Although this period of uncertainty always causes an immediate effect on balance sheets, with companies worried about ensuring they don't lose their markets in the short term, they will fundamentally need to find new business partners due to trade difficulties and geopolitics," he says.

Herman will be one of the international speakers at São Paulo Innovation Week, which takes place from May 13-15 at Pacaembu and the Fundação Armando Alvares Penteado. The festival is an initiative of Estadão in partnership with Base Eventos.

## Key Interview Excerpts

**You study innovation around the world. How do you see startups emerging in Global South countries? Can they compete on equal footing with the rest of the world?**

What I see happening in places like Brazil, Colombia, Argentina and Uruguay, and in emerging markets around the world, in Africa, Latin America and Southeast Asia, is the development of startups and ecosystems that are as competitive, technically competent and, in some cases, as transformational as any other. However, if you live and work in North America or Europe, you tend to think that nothing like this could come from those places.

Let me share a very good example: Nubank from Brazil is arriving in North America and the United States. I talk with fintech companies from Colombia, Mexico and Africa that are coming to North America because they want this market. I believe this is part of an absolutely transformational shift that has occurred over the last ten years in technology, where suddenly these places that were not on the global innovation map are now present and, in some cases, will redraw the map.

**Will the world invest less in innovation given the unstable global geopolitical scenario we have today?**

I believe it will be the opposite. Although this period of uncertainty always causes an immediate effect on balance sheets, with companies worried about ensuring they don't lose their markets in the short term, they will fundamentally need to find new business partners due to trade difficulties and geopolitics.

Canada, for example, is a country and economy that for almost 70 years has sent about 80% of everything it produces economically to a single market, the United States. We always knew this was a problem, but it was only in the last 18 months that we realized we need new friends. So our companies need to travel the world and find new partners. But in doing so, they need to be the best. Often, in all markets, we think that if we're number one in Brazil, we'll be number one in Canada, but often we don't know what's happening in those markets. So for companies around the world, this period of uncertainty means they should invest more in innovation.

**You are an entrepreneur. You recently wrote on social media that successful startups are not created without social policies that develop these founders from an early age. Is there a formula for countries to prepare founders of successful startups?**

I believe there is. Like every policymaker trying to stimulate, the recipe for building successful startups is creating businesses that are globally competitive. Anyone working with economic policy wants to create companies that will eventually go from São Paulo to Toronto or New York and bring capital back to São Paulo, and vice versa for Canadian companies. To do this, it's not just about technology or research and development, but about building a cohesive vision of how the world works. It's about having deep engagement and trust relationships in those markets, so people know they can trust you when doing business, whether in Brazil or Toronto.

Innovation needs to meet the realities of how humans interact and how we build trust and social engagement. From the Canadian perspective, since we've been so dependent on the United States, we now need to build engagement everywhere else. Think about the connections between Latin America and Africa, between Africa and Southeast Asia or the Gulf. Before, we considered all these places as small markets. Now, they are huge growing markets with which we need to build social engagement to establish long-term trust relationships.

**How can a country measure the effectiveness of a public innovation policy?**

We dealt with this question during my years in government. We like to think we'll see an immediate impact from an innovation investment policy on company growth and consequently on per capita GDP or productivity. Ultimately, these are the numbers we want. We want people in our countries to feel more prosperous and have more resources at their disposal. For this to happen, we need companies selling something that others want to buy. So the immediate measure of innovation policy is the success rate of our companies. How many of them are becoming global, how many are exporting and how many are creating jobs internally. In the medium term, we can measure these companies' contribution to productivity data, but how multifactorial productivity, which combines technology and labor, and fundamentally how this changes people's income. That's what matters. If people don't have enough for their basic needs or don't feel prosperous, then the policy hasn't worked.
