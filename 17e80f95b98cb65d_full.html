Court suspends 12% tax on oil exports

08 April 2026 · 10:42 PM

# Court Suspends 12% Tax on Oil Exports

Federal Judge Humberto de Vasconcelos Sampaio of the 1st Federal Court of Rio de Janeiro granted an injunction suspending the 12% tax rate on crude oil and bituminous mineral exports. The taxation was instituted by Provisional Measure 1.340/2026.

The suspension was requested by TotalEnergies, Spanish-Chinese Repsol Sinopec, Portuguese Petrogal, British Shell, and Norwegian Equinor. Together, they produced 791,000 barrels of oil in February, equivalent to 20% of national production. The volume is almost entirely destined for the international market and exceeds Petrobras's average exports in 2025, which reached a record 765,000 barrels per day.

The measure halts tax collection on all operations by these companies since the provisional measure took effect on March 12. The tax was created to support the 10 billion reais the government projected spending on the first diesel subsidy of 0.32 reais per liter. The sector calculates the tax will collect approximately 30 billion reais.

The judge recognized that the government's measure has a revenue-raising character and is not merely a rate adjustment, as the Union claims. This is clear, according to the decision, from the information that the tax will be destined for urgent fiscal needs. According to Sampaio, this provision rules out any interpretation that the tax serves to regulate foreign trade or exchange policy.

For the government, the prevailing zero rate represented an incentive policy rather than an acquired right. The oil companies argue that the measure places them at a competitive disadvantage in the international market. The tax increases the price of exported oil and would also violate principles of legal certainty, equal treatment, free competition, and tax capacity.

In his decision, the judge stated he saw "legal plausibility in the thesis that immediate collection of the export tax, instituted for revenue-raising purposes, violates the principle of prior notice." The injunction is justified, he says, because "the immediate tax requirement could cause irreversible harm to the petitioners, affecting their international competitiveness and financial capacity."

At an event in Rio de Janeiro this Wednesday, representatives of the companies that filed for the injunction questioned the tax and stated that fiscal instability could affect investment decisions in Brazil. "For every three barrels of oil produced in Brazil, two are subject to tax burden. In the United States, only one. That's why we emphasize the need not to increase the tax burden," said Shell's president in Brazil, Cristiano Pinto da Costa.

"Brazil is recognized and has a tradition of respecting contracts, and that is important," said Veronica Coelho of Equinor. "But over recent years we have had some surprises in fiscal changes that increase this risk and make it more difficult to make decisions about new investments."

Also present at the event, Minister of Mines and Energy Alexandre Silveira defended that the tax transfers extraordinary oil profits from oil companies to the Brazilian consumer. "Extraordinary times demand extraordinary measures," he stated.
