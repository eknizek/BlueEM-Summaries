Jet Chocolate Bar Sales Increase Following Price Reduction to 1,000 Colombian Pesos

08 April 2026 · 10:05 PM

# What Has Happened with Jet Chocolate Bar After Its Price Was Reduced to 1,000 Colombian Pesos?

Seven weeks after the Jet chocolate bar reduced its price to 1,000 pesos, the results are consolidating as one of the most relevant recent movements in mass consumption in Colombia. The decision, driven by Compañía Nacional de Chocolates, sought to respond to pressure on household spending while simultaneously energizing the traditional channel, where a significant portion of daily purchases are concentrated.

The data shows an immediate market reaction. On average, a store that previously sold 50 units monthly when the price was 1,200 pesos now commercializes approximately 84 units after the adjustment, representing a 67% growth in volume. This increase not only reflects greater demand but also a change in purchase frequency: consumers are acquiring the chocolate bar more regularly, reintegrating it into their daily consumption.

From the company's perspective, the balance is positive. Regina Malo, marketing manager of Chocolates from Grupo Nutresa, noted that behavior at points of sale evidences a transformation in commercial dynamics. According to her explanation, when the product reaches this price level, it becomes a traffic driver in stores, increases rotation, and generates more purchase occasions throughout the day. In other words, not only is more sold, but the cash flow of small businesses is also activated.

This effect is especially visible in the traditional channel, which groups over 400,000 small shopkeepers and businesses in the country. There, the price reduction has had a direct impact on sales volume and customer relationships. Consulted shopkeepers indicate that buyers have increased the quantity of units per purchase, driven by greater product accessibility.

"Since the Jet chocolate bar returned to 1,000 pesos, the change in my business has been immediate. Rotation skyrocketed; before a customer would ask me for two chocolate bars, but now, since the price dropped, they take four or even five at once. It's been a very positive impact on my daily sales," said Luz Dary Meneses, a traditional channel shopkeeper.

The phenomenon also replicates in other sales formats. In Novaventa, commercialized units multiplied 3.8 times compared to the average of the six campaigns prior to the measure. Additionally, the number of effective customers grew 17%, evidencing that the strategy not only increased volume per buyer but also expanded the consumer base.

This behavior confirms a clear trend in the Colombian market: high price sensitivity. In a context of adjusted incomes and more careful spending decisions, small changes can have significant effects on demand. The chocolate bar, which for decades has been an icon in the country, thus manages to recover spaces in everyday moments such as after lunch, a coffee break, or as a low-cost gift.

Brand representatives specifically highlight that return to everyday life. Jet spokespeople note that consumers have reincorporated the product into different moments of the day, reinforcing its historical positioning and emotional closeness with Colombians. This factor, combined with the price, has been key to explaining the upturn in rotation.

In terms of implementation, adoption of the new price advances unevenly across channels. While in large supermarkets, wholesalers, and self-service stores it exceeds 90%, in the traditional channel it approaches 40%, though with a sustained growth trend week by week. This lag responds, in part, to the dynamics of distribution and restocking in smaller-scale stores, the company explained.

At the regional level, sales growth has been generalized, though with greater dynamism in the southwestern part of the country, followed by the Santanders region, Antioquia, and Bogotá. This suggests that the strategy has had national reach, with variations responding to consumption characteristics of each area.

Overall, the results show that the price reduction to 1,000 pesos not only increased chocolate bar sales but also strengthened the commercial ecosystem surrounding it. For Grupo Nutresa, the measure translates into an opportunity to consolidate its leadership in the category and reaffirm its connection with consumers in a challenging economic environment, where price again becomes a decisive factor.
