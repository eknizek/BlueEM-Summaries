# Buffett Returns After Retirement: Highlights Apple Success and Berkshire Successor

The annual Berkshire Hathaway meeting in Omaha returned Warren Buffett to the center of the corporate stage at the beginning of the era under Greg Abel.

The meeting took place after a first quarter in which the company reported profits of US$10.106 billion, nearly double that of a year earlier, with operating income of US$11.346 billion and a cash position close to US$397 billion.

For the first time in decades, Buffett did not occupy the central seat as CEO during the meeting, but instead sat among the attendees, reflecting the effective transfer of responsibilities. It was Abel who conducted the session and yielded the floor, when the investor intervened with a brief message to shareholders.

In that intervention, Buffett validated the change in executive leadership. "He is doing everything I did and more, and he is doing it better in all cases," said Buffett, referring to Abel.

The executive assumed control of the conglomerate this year, after the founder announced his departure as CEO, though he continues as board chairman. Under the first quarter of this transition, Berkshire reported that net income attributable to shareholders reached US$10.106 billion and rose nearly 120% from US$4.603 billion a year earlier, while operating income rose nearly 18% to US$11.346 billion from US$9.641 million. The company closed the quarter with liquidity close to US$397 billion, adding more than US$58 billion in cash, including US$6.644 billion in its energy and transportation businesses, and around US$339 billion in Treasury bills.

## Apple: Main Position and Leadership Transition

Buffett also used his intervention to highlight the performance of Apple, one of his main investments, weeks after Tim Cook, who was present at the meeting, announced his departure from the Cupertino company.

The company concentrates a substantial part of Berkshire's portfolio, which amounts to nearly US$288 billion and maintains high concentration in a reduced number of companies. "We made our investment and gave him 10% of Berkshire's resources," said Buffett. The operation involved, ten years ago, approximately US$35 billion.

The investor reviewed the transition in the company's leadership following Steve Jobs' era and under Tim Cook's leadership and recalled that when they made their first investment "few American investors" had heard of the businessman. "When Tim Cook took over Apple, the legendary Steve was known to everyone in the United States, but not many knew Tim," he said. "Everyone was wondering who would run Apple when Steve Jobs was no longer there, and probably only a small percentage of American investors had heard of Tim Cook."

During Cook's 15 years, Apple increased its market capitalization by US$3.66 trillion to around US$4.01 trillion, multiplying its value tenfold since US$350 billion in 2011. In parallel, net profit reached US$112 billion in 2025, representing a 699% increase from 2010, amid geopolitical tensions, a pandemic, and supply chain disruptions.

Buffett stressed that today Berkshire's investment is worth around US$185 billion before taxes. "And I didn't have to do absolutely anything. So, as you know, here we really like others to do the work. And it's a success," he concluded.
