# Oil and freight rate increases due to Iran war sustain iron ore prices, says Vale

Rising oil prices and maritime freight rates amid the war in Iran are increasing global iron ore production costs, threatening the supply of less competitive miners and supporting commodity prices, said Vale's Executive Vice President of Commercial and Development, Rogério Nogueira, on Wednesday (29).

At an investor conference, the executive said that average costs in the industry have grown between $5 and $10 per ton. According to Nogueira, a $10 per ton increase in costs puts the production of approximately 50 million tons of iron ore worldwide in a situation of negative cash generation, mainly affecting marginal producers.

"This cost shift upward is, in fact, asymmetric, with some marginal players on the cost curve being impacted by more than $10 per ton, which provides some support to the prices we are seeing today," Nogueira said.

The scenario allowed iron ore prices to rise, said Vale's Executive Vice President of Finance and Investor Relations, Marcelo Bacci, to journalists. "Since there is a certain balance between supply and demand, when costs rise significantly, the price ends up rising because the market needs that volume and needs to incentivize that production to come," Bacci said in a press conference after the investor call. "If the price had not risen $10, there would be 50 million tons that would be generating losses."

Nogueira also acknowledged that, being geographically farther from China than its Australian competitors, the rise in maritime fuel (bunker) prices could leave Vale at a disadvantage. However, Vale executives explained that it was possible to "compensate much of this disadvantage" with a hedging program that provides protection for Brent oil prices above $80 per barrel for approximately 70% of its bunker demand in 2026, bringing greater predictability and stability to cash generation.

The company has also reduced its exposure to the bunker spot market to less than 5% this year, down from between 25% and 30% previously, and has increased the level of freight contracting for its fleet to almost 100%, according to executives.

Regarding Vale's operations in the Middle East, Bacci explained that production at Oman's pelletizing plants was interrupted in mid-March for scheduled annual maintenance. This shutdown was brought forward due to regional difficulties. "We decided to bring forward some maintenance and plant adaptation work that would have happened later in the year," he said, noting that the initiative does not affect the annual pellet production forecast.

"It just shifted the schedule between quarters a bit. And we believe there will be no problem supplying the pellet market," he said, noting that the pellet feed (ultrafine ore particles) initially destined for Oman will be redirected to Tubarão plants, where there is idle capacity.

## Neutral impact on supply

In this context, the Iran war had a neutral impact on global iron ore supply, since steel production remains stable. "In the Middle East specifically, steel production is also stable because they are maintaining production based on scrap and pellet inventories. This occurs despite a contraction in Iran's crude steel production," the executive said, emphasizing that Iranian steel production has been interrupted. "But the rest of our customers in the region continue producing," he added.

Vale still expects the pellet market to remain stable, considering the net balance between supply and demand for high-grade pellet feed. "Thus, in the next quarter, we expect pellet premiums to remain stable or even show a slight increase," it said.

In China, Nogueira said that crude steel production is stable, according to data from independent institutes. Blast furnace utilization, according to the executive, is at about 90%, which Vale considers "extremely positive and elevated," he said. "We still believe and see the continuity of annualized steel exports [by China] at a level of 100 million tons in 2026. Infrastructure and manufacturing compensate for a weak real estate sector, which continues to be, in fact, a challenging sector for China," he said.

Outside of China, Nogueira added that Vale sees a generally stable market, despite variations by region. "Overall, we see balance between supply and demand, and the price outlook is also balanced," he concluded.
