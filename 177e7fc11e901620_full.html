# After takeoff, Bre-B's big challenge is making salaries and remittances also become 'immediate'

Its director, Ana María Prieto, explains how the central bank's platform seeks to transform daily payments and the heart of the financial system.

Ana María Prieto, Director of the Payment Systems Department at the Central Bank of Colombia, explains what the next step for the platform will be.

**By Carlos Arturo García Mahecha, Economic Journalist**

**April 7, 2026 | Updated: 4:46 PM**

If Bre-B, the immediate payments platform developed by the Central Bank of Colombia, managed in six months to get millions of Colombians to stop relying on cash for their daily payments, what comes next is to fully enter the terrain where the economy really moves.

It's no longer just about paying for lunch or sending money to a family member. The next step is for companies to pay their employees' salaries in seconds, for digital commerce to integrate immediate payments, and for sending remittances from abroad to be as simple as scanning a code or using a "digital key."

Converting Bre-B into the backbone of payments in Colombia is the true scope of the project led by the Central Bank of Colombia, according to Ana María Prieto, Director of the Payment Systems Department at the Central Bank, who in an interview with EL TIEMPO not only takes stock of the first six months—with striking figures—but also reveals the steps that will mark the next phase of this transformation.

**Beyond the assessment, what really changes for Bre-B at this new stage?**

What changes is the scale and nature of the flows we will handle. In the first six months we focused on stabilizing operations and achieving adoption in person-to-person payments, which was the natural step to introduce the system. Today we already have 630 million transactions and nearly 100 trillion pesos processed, which demonstrates that the infrastructure works and that users incorporated it into their daily lives.

But the turning point comes now when we move from daily payments to more structural ones in the economy. That is, when Bre-B stops being just a tool for small transfers and starts participating in processes like payroll, business payments, or mass collections. That's where the financial system truly transforms.

**When you talk about business payments, what do you mean in practical terms?**

We're talking about incorporating companies as active players within the system, not just as indirect recipients of payments. That means a company can pay its payroll through Bre-B, but also pay suppliers or receive customer payments in real time. Today many of these processes still depend on slower systems, with restricted hours or intermediations that generate costs and friction. What we seek is to eliminate those barriers and bring the logic of immediacy to those flows.

**What impact could this have on company operations?**

Several impacts. The first is operational efficiency: payments that previously could take hours or even days are now executed in seconds. The second is liquidity management. For a company, being able to access money immediately or pay in real time changes how it manages its cash. And the third is traceability. By having digital and immediate operations, visibility improves over financial flows, which can facilitate access to credit or other services.

**When might we see that change reflected, for example, in payroll payments?**

We are working on the regulation that enables these cases. The idea is to publish the regulatory package for business payments around mid-year. That includes payroll, but also supplier payments and collections. Once that framework is defined, financial entities and companies will be able to start implementing it.

**Another key front is e-commerce...**

Today, many online purchases depend on processes that are not completely immediate or that require additional intermediaries. With Bre-B, the idea is that payment is made directly from the user's account, in real time, which improves the experience and reduces friction for both the customer and the merchant.
