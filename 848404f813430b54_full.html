# Pinheiro family surpasses 50% in Hapvida and will sell southern operations

The Pinheiro family has increased its stake in Hapvida, now surpassing 50% of the company's capital. The family was already the practical controllers of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares, but are now also the formal controllers through a derivative arrangement with BTG Pactual bank.

The increase was aimed at consolidating control and attempting to contain the drop in the company's stock price, but there is no intention to close the capital, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

In an effort to stop criticism and improve financial performance, the healthcare operator has already made changes to its administration and has now decided to sell its southern region operations. BTG was hired to find a buyer for the region's assets, according to Pipeline reporting.

The southern operation comprises assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho, plus three hospitals built by the company. The total includes eight hospitals, 21 clinics, and 490,000 lives. If segregated, it would be the ninth-largest operator in the market and the second-largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure, paying R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

The sale of the southern operation was highlighted as one of the alternatives in an extensive letter sent by investment firm Squadra last week, which described Hapvida as "the greatest destruction of shareholder value in the Brazilian capital market." The letter suggested the divestment as a way to simplify the company's structure and reduce leverage.

According to a source close to Hapvida, the divestment was already being debated on the board before Squadra's letter, sent on April 1st. On Monday the 6th, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also issued a letter to the market, offering a more optimistic review of the company's recent history. "I recognize, however, that recent financial results fell short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he evaluated.

Pinheiro is stepping down as president and CEO and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future demands new skills, new perspectives, and a new pace," commented Pinheiro, who remains on the board.

The company has also made changes to other strategic management positions, bringing in executives from the market. On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
