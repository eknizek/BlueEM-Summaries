# Pinheiro Family Moves Above 50% Stake in Hapvida and Will Sell Southern Operations

In recent weeks, with Hapvida's stock falling and its guidelines questioned by Squadra, the Pinheiro family has increased its stake in the company, moving above 50% of capital. The Pinheiros were already Hapvida's controlling shareholders in practice, as the largest shareholders with 42% of the shares — but now they also hold this formally, through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The increase was to consolidate control and attempt to contain the fall in stock prices, but there is no intention to close the company to the public, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

To try to stop criticism and improve financial performance, the healthcare operator has already made changes to management and has now decided to sell its operations in the South of the country. BTG has been hired to find a buyer for the regional assets, Pipeline has found. The Southern operation is composed of assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho and three hospitals that the company built. In total, there are eight hospitals, 21 clinics and 490,000 lives — if segregated, it would be the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure. It paid R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020, and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

In the extensive and harsh letter written by manager Squadra last week — which described Hapvida as the "greatest destruction of shareholder value in the Brazilian capital market" — the sale of the Southern operation was pointed out as one of the company's alternatives to simplify its structure and reduce leverage. According to a source close to Hapvida, the divestment was already under debate on the board before Squadra's letter, sent to the company on April 1st. Last Monday, April 6th, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also released a letter to the market, offering a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I recognize, however, that recent financial results fell short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he said.

He is stepping down as chairman and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives and a new pace," said Pinheiro, who remains on the board. The company has also made changes to other strategic management positions, bringing executives from the market. On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
