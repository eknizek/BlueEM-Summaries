# Pinheiro Family Crosses 50% at Hapvida and Plans to Sell Southern Operations

In recent weeks, with Hapvida's stock falling and its guidelines questioned by Squadra, the Pinheiro family has increased its stake in the company, surpassing 50% of capital. The Pinheiros were already practical controllers of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares — but now they are also on screen through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The move was to consolidate control and try to contain the fall of the shares, but there is no intention to take the company private, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

In an attempt to stem the criticism and improve financial performance, the health operator has already made changes to its administration and has now decided to sell its operations in the South of the country. BTG was hired to find a buyer for the region's assets, Pipeline learned.

The Southern operation is composed of assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho and three hospitals built by the company. The total includes eight hospitals, 21 clinics and 490,000 members — if segregated, it would be the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure. They paid R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

In the harsh and extensive letter written by manager Squadra last week — which described Hapvida as the "greatest destruction of shareholder value in the Brazilian capital market" — the sale of Southern operations was pointed out as one of the company's alternatives to simplify its structure and reduce leverage. According to a source close to Hapvida, the divestment was already under board discussion before Squadra's letter, sent to the company on April 1.

Last Monday, April 6, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also issued a letter to the market, giving a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I recognize, however, that recent financial results fell short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he assessed.

He is stepping down as president and director and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "Luccas's selection as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives and a new pace," commented Pinheiro, who remains on the board. The company has also made changes in other strategic management positions, bringing executives from the market.

On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. In 12 months, the decline is 67%.
