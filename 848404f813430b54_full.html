# Pinheiro Family Crosses 50% Stake in Hapvida and to Sell Southern Operations

In recent weeks, with Hapvida's stock falling and its management questioned by Squadra, the Pinheiro family increased their stake in the company, surpassing 50% of capital. The Pinheiros were already practical controllers of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares — but now they are also formally controllers through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The move was to consolidate control and attempt to stem the stock decline, but there is no intention to take the company private, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

In an effort to address criticism and improve financial performance, the healthcare operator has already made administrative changes and has now decided to sell its Southern region operations. BTG was hired to find a buyer for the regional assets, according to Pipeline's reporting.

The Southern operation comprises assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho plus three hospitals built by the company. The total includes eight hospitals, 21 clinics, and 490,000 beneficiaries — if segregated, it would be the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure. It paid R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

In the lengthy and harsh letter written by manager Squadra last week — which described Hapvida as the "greatest value destruction in the Brazilian capital market" — the sale of the Southern operation was pointed out as one of the company's alternatives to simplify its structure and reduce leverage. According to a source close to Hapvida, the divestment was already being debated on the board before Squadra's letter, sent to the company on April 1st.

Last Monday, the 6th, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also issued a letter to the market, offering a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I recognize, however, that recent financial results fell short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he assessed.

He is stepping down as CEO and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives, and a new pace," commented Pinheiro, who remains on the board.

The company has also made changes to other strategic management positions, bringing in executives from the market. On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
