# Pinheiro Family Increases Stake to Over 50% in Hapvida; Plans Southern Operations Sale

In recent weeks, with Hapvida's stock declining and management guidelines questioned by Squadra, the Pinheiro family increased their position in the company, surpassing 50% of capital. The Pinheiros were already de facto controllers of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares—but now they are also formally in control through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The increase was aimed at consolidating control and attempting to contain the stock decline, but there is no intention to take the company private, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

To also attempt to stem criticism and improve financial performance, the health operator has already made administrative changes and has now decided to sell its Southern operations. BTG was hired to find a buyer for the region's assets, according to Pipeline.

The Southern operation consists of assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho and three hospitals built by the company. The total comprises eight hospitals, 21 clinics, and 490,000 members—if segregated, it would be the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure. It paid R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

In the lengthy and strongly-worded letter written by manager Squadra last week—which described Hapvida as the "greatest destruction of shareholder value in the Brazilian capital markets"—the sale of the Southern operation was pointed out as one of the company's alternatives to simplify its structure and reduce leverage. According to a source close to Hapvida, divestment was already under discussion on the board before Squadra's letter, sent to the company on April 1st.

Last Monday, April 6th, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also addressed the market with a letter, offering a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I recognize, however, that recent financial results fell short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he assessed.

He is stepping down from the position of chief executive officer and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives, and a new pace," commented Pinheiro, who remains on the board. The company has also made changes to other strategic management positions, bringing executives from the market.

On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
