The Role of Former Central Bank Presidents and Directors in Pushing the Financial Autonomy PEC

# The Role of Former Central Bank Presidents and Directors in Pushing the Financial Autonomy PEC

After the president of the Central Bank (BC), Gabriel Galípolo, called for "help" at the Organized Crime CPI for the Senate to vote on the proposed constitutional amendment (PEC) for financial and administrative autonomy of the monetary authority, former presidents and former directors have intensified pressure on senators and mobilized public opinion so that the text is finally approved in the House.

In the most striking example of this movement, former BC president Roberto Campos Neto has been calling senators from both opposition and government parties in search of support for the PEC's approval. At the request of the rapporteur, Plínio Valério (PSDB-AM), he has already spoken with closer parliamentarians, acting behind the scenes and in a reserved manner.

The PEC, authored by senator Vanderlan Cardoso (PSD-GO), has been pending in the Senate since 2023, when Campos Neto still presided over the monetary authority. After several difficulties, the rapporteur presented his new opinion a week ago to the Senate's Constitutional and Justice Committee (CCJ), awaiting for the committee's president, Otto Alencar (PSD-BA), to schedule the vote and the rapporteur to read his opinion.

"What I said was for him to talk to some senators he knows to be able to put it to a vote in the CCJ," Valério told NeoFeed. "Roberto Campos [Neto] has been following up and talking with friendly senators."

When contacted, Campos Neto did not respond to the report's contact attempts.

In Valério's view, who has spoken with some of them, especially former BC presidents have adopted a more emphatic stance in recent days, in publications or making more frequent public statements in defense of the proposal, in a posture of "concern" with the future of the agency.

To NeoFeed, former BC president and former Finance Minister Henrique Meirelles said he understands this is a moment for leadership and authorities with experience in managing the monetary authority to take a stand and request the PEC's approval.

"I write articles, I've been in Senate hearings and whenever I have the opportunity to talk with senators I have done so. But not just former BC presidents, everyone who has the ability to influence and understands the importance of this for Brazil must have active participation for this to happen now," said Meirelles. "If necessary, I could eventually go to Brasília again [to talk with senators]."

Meirelles reports that the BC has already been showing difficulties in managing major demands such as the PIX system and even maintaining its workforce with migration to the private sector.

"It's difficult because it's an election year, and the subject is being discussed in Congress by parliamentarians who will be strongly focused on their bases. But it's possible and I hope the PEC will be approved this year," says Meirelles, who headed the agency from 2003 to 2011. "An independent BC is the solution in practically all relevant countries," he adds.

At the end of last year, Meirelles signed a manifesto initiated by the National Association of Auditors of the Central Bank of Brazil (ANBCB), along with several former directors, such as Aldo Luiz Mendes and Luiz Fernando Figueiredo and entities such as the Brazilian Federation of Banks (Febraban).

Beyond former presidents, those who have served in the Central Bank's management have also been mobilizing public opinion in favor of the PEC's approval. In a recent article on his CNN Brasil blog, former director Tony Volpon launched the slogan "approve PEC 65 now" and warned that the monetary authority is experiencing a "personnel blackout due to a shameful lack of budget resources."

"We may be politically polarized, but I don't know anyone who doesn't like PIX. Perhaps this question is enough for our political class to finally act on this necessary improvement to our institutional framework," he emphasizes.

Luiz Fernando Figueiredo, who was also a director of the agency, stated that not moving forward with the financial and administrative independence of the Central Bank, and also of the CVM, greatly increases "the risk of ending up with the ability to ensure that we have both a healthy financial system and capital market in Brazil."

"We have to move this PEC forward!" he told NeoFeed. "Whoever is against the financial autonomy of these entities either doesn't understand their importance as central entities for the financial health of our country or wants to keep Brazil in the 1970s."

Senator Tereza Cristina (PP-MS), leader of the PP in the Senate, who opposes the government and is in favor of the PEC, admits that the issue has been gaining strength in Senate corridors in recent weeks and that many senators have been approached to debate the subject. She defends that the PEC advances to the plenary, despite the election year.

"Regardless of the PEC, the Central Bank and agencies like the CVM [Securities and Exchange Commission] need more structure to oversee the financial system in Brazil. So we need to approve it. I've always been in favor of this PEC," said Tereza to NeoFeed.

## The Opposition

The obstacles to the PEC were already expected in a shorter election calendar year, but the Master Case scandal has awakened urgency in the debate of the subject, which has actually been dragging on for practically two and a half years.

However, the government's resistance and PT factions continue to cause uncertainties for the proposal to advance. As NeoFeed showed last week, just like the former Finance Minister, Fernando Haddad, the new Finance Minister, Dario Durigan, has also signaled opposition to the PEC, according to rapporteur Plínio Valério. There is still no prediction of when the proposal will be scheduled in the CCJ and its new report will be put to a vote.

If it passes through the committee, the text would have to go to plenary, where Senate president Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP) has already signaled he will schedule promptly. After that, the PEC still needs to pass through the Chamber.

But opposition pressure does not come only from government factions. Within the civil service body, there is also resistance. After the release of the rapporteur's most recent opinion on the PEC, the National Union of Central Bank Employees (Sinal) reinforced its opposing position to the PEC. According to the union, the proposal does not guarantee strengthening to the BC, "but rather a potential increase in risk to the integrity of the technical workforce."
