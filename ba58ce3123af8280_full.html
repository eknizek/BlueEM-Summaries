Former Central Bank Presidents and Directors Act Behind Scenes to Push PEC on Financial Autonomy

24 April 2026 · 08:44 PM

# Former Central Bank Presidents and Directors Act Behind Scenes to Push PEC on Financial Autonomy

After Central Bank President Gabriel Galípolo called for "help" in the Organized Crime CPI for the Senate to vote on the constitutional amendment proposal (PEC) on financial and administrative autonomy for the monetary authority, former presidents and directors have intensified pressure on senators and mobilized public opinion so that the text is finally approved in the House.

In the most notable example of this movement, former Central Bank President Roberto Campos Neto has been calling senators from both opposition and government sides seeking support for the PEC's approval. At the request of the rapporteur, Plínio Valério (PSDB-AM), he has already spoken with parliamentarians closer to him, acting behind the scenes and in a reserved manner.

The PEC, authored by Senator Vanderlan Cardoso (PSD-GO), has been in the Senate since 2023, when Campos Neto still presided over the monetary authority. After several difficulties, the rapporteur presented a new opinion a week ago in the Senate's Constitutional and Justice Commission (CCJ), awaiting the commission's president, Otto Alencar (PSD-BA), to schedule the vote and the rapporteur to read his opinion.

"What I said was for him to talk to some senators he knows so they could put it to a vote in the CCJ," Valério told NeoFeed. "Roberto Campos [Neto] has been following up and talking with friendly senators."

When contacted, Campos Neto did not respond to the report's contact attempts. In Valério's view, who has talked to some of them, particularly former Central Bank presidents have adopted a more emphatic stance in recent days, through publications or more frequent public statements in defense of the proposal, in a posture of "concern" about the future of the agency.

To NeoFeed, former Central Bank President and former Finance Minister Henrique Meirelles said he understands this is a moment for leaders and authorities with experience in managing the monetary authority to take a position and request the PEC's approval.

"I write articles, I've been in Senate hearings and whenever I have the opportunity to talk to senators I have done so. But not just former Central Bank presidents, everyone who has the possibility to influence and understands the importance of this for Brazil needs to have active participation for this to happen now," said Meirelles. "If necessary, I could eventually go to Brasília again [to talk to senators]."

Meirelles reports that the Central Bank has already been demonstrating difficulties in managing large demands such as the PIX system and even maintaining its workforce with migration to the private sector.

"It's difficult because it's an election year, and the subject is being discussed in Congress by parliamentarians who will be strongly focused on their constituencies. But it's possible and I hope the PEC is approved this year," says Meirelles, who headed the agency from 2003 to 2011. "An independent Central Bank is the solution in practically all relevant countries," he adds.

At the end of last year, Meirelles signed a manifesto at the initiative of the National Association of Central Bank Auditors of Brazil (ANBCB), together with various former directors, such as Aldo Luiz Mendes and Luiz Fernando Figueiredo, and entities such as the Brazilian Federation of Banks (Febraban).

Besides former presidents, those who have served on the Central Bank board have also been mobilizing public opinion in favor of the PEC's approval. In a recent article on his CNN Brasil blog, former director Tony Volpon launched the slogan "approve PEC 65 now" and warned that the monetary authority is experiencing a "personnel blackout due to shameful lack of budget resources."

"We may be politically polarized, but I don't know anyone who doesn't like PIX. Perhaps this question is enough for our political class to finally act on this necessary improvement in our institutional arrangement," he emphasizes.

Luiz Fernando Figueiredo, who was also a director of the agency, stated that not advancing with the financial and administrative independence of the Central Bank, and also of the CVM, greatly increases "the risk of ending up losing the capacity to ensure that we have both a healthy financial system and capital market in Brazil."

"We have to move this PEC forward!" he told NeoFeed. "Whoever is against the financial autonomy of these entities either doesn't understand their importance as central entities to the financial health of our country or wants to keep Brazil in the 1970s."

Senator Tereza Cristina (PP-MS), PP leader in the Senate, who is in opposition to the government and is in favor of the PEC, admits that the theme has been gaining strength in Senate corridors in recent weeks and that many senators have been approached to debate the issue. She defends that the PEC advances to the plenary, despite the election year.

"Regardless of the PEC, the Central Bank and agencies like the CVM [Securities Commission] need more structure to supervise the financial system in Brazil. So we need to approve it. I've always been in favor of this PEC," Tereza told NeoFeed.

## Against

The obstacles to the PEC were already expected in a shorter election year calendar, but the Master Case scandal has awakened urgency in the debate of the topic, which in fact has been dragging on for practically two and a half years.

However, government resistance and PT factions continue to cause uncertainties for the proposal to advance. As NeoFeed showed last week, like the former Finance Minister, Fernando Haddad, the new Minister of Finance, Dario Durigan, has also signaled in a contrary manner to the PEC, according to rapporteur Plínio Valério. And there is still no forecast for when the proposal will be scheduled in the CCJ and its new report will go to a vote.

If it passes through the commission, the text would have to go to plenary, where Senate President Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP) has already signaled he will schedule promptly. After that, the PEC still needs to pass through the Chamber of Deputies.

But opposing pressure does not come only from government factions. Within the civil servants' body, there is also resistance. After the disclosure of the rapporteur's most recent opinion on the PEC, the National Union of Central Bank Employees (Sinal) reinforced its opposition stance to the PEC. According to the union, the proposal does not guarantee strengthening of the Central Bank, "but rather a potential increase in risk to the functional integrity of the technical body."
