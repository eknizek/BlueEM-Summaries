# Former Central Bank Presidents and Directors Work Behind the Scenes on Financial Autonomy Constitutional Amendment

After the president of the Central Bank (BC), Gabriel Galípolo, called for "help" at the Organized Crime CPI for the Senate to vote on the proposed constitutional amendment (PEC) for financial and administrative autonomy of the monetary authority, former presidents and directors have intensified pressure on senators and mobilized public opinion for the text to finally be approved in the House.

In the most notable example of this movement, former BC president Roberto Campos Neto has been calling senators from both opposition and government parties in search of support for the PEC's approval. At the request of rapporteur Plínio Valério (PSDB-AM), he has already spoken with closer parliamentarians, acting behind the scenes in a reserved manner.

The PEC, authored by senator Vanderlan Cardoso (PSD-GO), has been in the Senate since 2023, when Campos Neto still presided over the monetary authority. After several difficulties, the rapporteur presented his new opinion a week ago to the Senate's Constitutional and Justice Commission (CCJ), awaiting Senate president Otto Alencar (PSD-BA) to schedule the vote and the rapporteur to read his opinion.

"What I said was for him to speak with some senators he knows to put it to a vote in the CCJ," Valério told NeoFeed. "Roberto Campos [Neto] has been following up and talking with friendly senators."

When contacted, Campos Neto did not respond to the news outlet's attempts at contact. In Valério's view, former BC presidents have adopted a more emphatic stance in recent days, through publications or more frequent public statements in defense of the proposal, in a posture of "concern" about the future of the agency.

To NeoFeed, former BC president and former Finance Minister Henrique Meirelles said he believes this is a moment for leaders and authorities with experience in managing the monetary authority to take a stance and request approval of the PEC.

"I write articles, I've attended Senate hearings and whenever I have the opportunity to speak with senators I do so. But not just former BC presidents—everyone who has the ability to influence and understands the importance of this for Brazil must take active participation for this to happen now," Meirelles stated. "If necessary, I could eventually go to Brasília again to speak with senators."

Meirelles reports that the BC has already been experiencing difficulties managing large demands such as the PIX system and even maintaining its workforce with migration to the private sector.

"It's difficult because it's an election year, and the subject is being discussed in Congress by parliamentarians who will be strongly focused on their constituencies. But it's possible and I hope the PEC is approved this year," says Meirelles, who headed the agency from 2003 to 2011. "An independent Central Bank is the solution in practically all relevant countries," he adds.

At the end of last year, Meirelles signed a manifesto at the initiative of the National Association of Central Bank Auditors of Brazil (ANBCB), along with various former directors, such as Aldo Luiz Mendes and Luiz Fernando Figueiredo, and entities such as the Brazilian Federation of Banks (Febraban).

Beyond former presidents, those who have served on the Central Bank board have also been mobilizing public opinion in favor of the PEC's approval. In a recent article on his CNN Brasil blog, former director Tony Volpon launched the slogan "approve PEC 65 now" and warned that the monetary authority is experiencing a "personnel blackout due to shameful lack of budgetary resources."

"We may be politically polarized, but I don't know anyone who doesn't like PIX. Perhaps this issue is enough for our political class to finally act on this necessary improvement to our institutional arrangement," he emphasizes.

Luiz Fernando Figueiredo, who was also a director of the agency, stated that not moving forward with the financial and administrative independence of the Central Bank, as well as the CVM, greatly increases "the risk of ending up with the inability to ensure that we have both a healthy financial system and capital market in Brazil."

"We need to get this PEC moving!" he told NeoFeed. "Whoever is against the financial autonomy of these entities either doesn't understand their importance as central entities to the financial health of our country or wants to keep Brazil in the 1970s."

Senator Tereza Cristina (PP-MS), leader of the PP in the Senate, who is in opposition to the government and is favorable to the PEC, admits that the issue has been gaining momentum in Senate corridors in recent weeks and that many senators have been approached to debate the topic. She argues that the PEC should advance to the plenary, despite the election year.

"Regardless of the PEC, the Central Bank and agencies like the CVM [Securities Commission] need more structure to oversee the financial system in Brazil. So we need to approve it. I've always been in favor of this PEC," Tereza told NeoFeed.

**Against**

Obstacles to the PEC were already expected in a shorter election calendar year, but the Master Case scandal has sparked urgency in the debate on the topic, which has actually been dragging on for practically two and a half years.

Resistance from the government and PT factions, however, continues to create uncertainties for the proposal to move forward. As NeoFeed showed last week, like the former Finance Minister Fernando Haddad, the new Minister of Finance, Dario Durigan, has also signaled opposition to the PEC, according to rapporteur Plínio Valério. There is still no forecast for when the proposal will be scheduled in the CCJ and its new report will go to a vote.

If it passes through the commission, the text would have to go to plenary, where Senate president Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP) has already signaled he will schedule promptly. After that, the PEC still needs to pass through the Chamber.

But resistance does not come only from government factions. Within the civil service, there is also resistance. Following the release of the rapporteur's most recent opinion on the PEC, the National Union of Central Bank Employees (Sinal) reinforced its opposition to the PEC. According to the union, the proposal does not guarantee strengthening of the BC, "but rather a potential increase in risk to the technical integrity of the workforce."
