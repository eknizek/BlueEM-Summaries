Wall Street Posts 3% Weekly Gains on US-Iran Ceasefire, Best Week Since November

10 April 2026 · 09:47 PM

Wall Street closed mixed on Friday but posted solid weekly gains in its major indices of around 3%, its best week since November, marked by the ceasefire between the United States and Iran, on which investors remain attentive to its fragility and its possible effects on oil prices.

At the close of the New York stock market, the Dow Jones lost 269 points or 0.56%, down to 47,916.57; the S&P 500 selective index fell 0.11%, down to 6,816.89; and the tech-heavy Nasdaq advanced 0.35%, up to 22,902.89 units.

On a weekly basis, the Dow Jones gained 3%, the S&P 500 advanced 3.6%, and the Nasdaq rose 4.7%. These weekly gains were driven by partial relief of geopolitical tensions and expectations of certain stability in energy supply, which favored risk appetite in markets, especially in the technology sector.

The United States reached a two-week ceasefire agreement with Iran on Tuesday in exchange for Tehran allowing the transit of ships through the Strait of Hormuz. However, doubts continue about the durability of the truce and restrictions still in effect for ship traffic in the strait. Thus, markets oscillated throughout the week due to crude volatility, according to analysts, amid doubts about the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz following the agreement.

U.S. benchmark West Texas Intermediate (WTI) oil remained near $100 per barrel and ended with a 13% weekly plunge, closing today at $96.57 per barrel.

Investors also evaluated U.S. March Consumer Price Index (CPI) data, which showed a 0.9% monthly increase and 3.3% year-over-year increase, driven by higher energy costs. However, underlying inflation was more moderate.

Regardless, analysts warn that if crude remains near $100 per barrel in the coming weeks, inflationary pressures could increase and complicate the Federal Reserve's monetary policy, which maintained caution among investors despite the week's positive balance.

"The Federal Reserve will do everything within its reach to overlook any data it receives corresponding to March and April," expert Tim Holland tells CNBC. Although Holland believes the war with Iran will "subside," he points out that investors will be watching for possible inflationary effects if oil prices continue trading "around $100 per barrel between early and mid-June."

On the corporate front, the technology sector led weekly gains, driven by semiconductor manufacturers. Nvidia advanced around 6.3% for the week, while Broadcom surged nearly 19%, AMD rose around 11.3%, and Intel approximately 22.9%.

However, the big loser of the week was tech company Palantir, with declines of around 14%, despite support shown to the company by U.S. President Donald Trump, who praised its artificial intelligence capabilities applied to defense and military matters.

In other markets, at Wall Street's close, the yield on the 10-year U.S. bond stood at 4.31% on Friday, gold declined to $4,765.5 per ounce, the euro appreciated against the dollar and traded at $1.17, and bitcoin rose 2.05%, up to $73,270.76.
