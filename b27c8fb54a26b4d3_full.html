# After acquiring Adecoagro and Profértil, stablecoin giant Tether invests in Argentine app

Tether, the company that issues USDT, the world's largest stablecoin, is deepening its expansion in Latin America through an investment in Argentine crypto platform Belo. The firm led a Series A funding round for Belo worth $14 million, according to a statement that did not include details about the startup's valuation. Titan Fund, The Venture City, Mindset Ventures, and G2 also participated in the round, according to Bloomberg.

The digital wallet Belo is expanding across South America, a region where USDT already dominates cryptocurrency flows. A 2025 Artemis Analytics report shows that USDT represents the largest share of stablecoin use in Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru.

"Tether is looking for distribution and sees Belo as an actor that can strongly drive the use of USDT, both at the customer level and at the infrastructure level," said Belo CEO Manuel Beaudroit in an interview.

Stablecoins are a type of cryptocurrency linked to the price of an established asset, typically the U.S. dollar, and are gaining increasing popularity as a means of payment. The total volume of stablecoin transactions increased 72% to reach $33 trillion in 2025, according to Artemis.

Belo, which has reported three consecutive years of profitability, recently expanded to Brazil and plans to enter seven more countries this year, including Peru and Bolivia. It will use the Series A funds to integrate payments, savings, and cryptocurrencies into a single application, as well as to hire staff that will increase its workforce by 20%.

The company projects reaching one million Brazilian users before the end of 2027 and will continue deploying LUX—its 100% digital international card on the Visa network—as a central piece of its proposition in each new market where it operates.

Tether's regional expansion goes beyond the crypto ecosystem. Last year, the firm acquired a controlling stake in agricultural company Adecoagro, expanding a series of investments ranging from brain implants to sports equipment. Later, through Adecoagro, the company paid $1.2 billion for fertilizer company Profértil.
