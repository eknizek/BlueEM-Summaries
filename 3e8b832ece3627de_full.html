# Ecuador Tariffs Put Colombian Agricultural Sectors at Risk

The escalating tariff situation between Ecuador and Colombia continues to generate disruptions, not only in bilateral trade but also in the economies of both countries, employment, and various productive sectors.

The decision by Ecuador's president to raise tariffs to 100% on Colombian products, followed by Colombia's reciprocal tariff response at the same level, is creating significant challenges for sectors such as agroindustry and partly energy.

In general terms, the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex) recalled that energy goods were the first to be sacrificed amid the bilateral trade war.

## Energy Exports Decline Sharply

According to DIAN data, between January and February of this year, Colombia's electrical energy exports to Ecuador fell 77.1% year-over-year, declining from US$110.3 million in the same months of 2025 to approximately US$25.2 million in the current period.

Although Colombia limited external energy sales to the neighboring country from the first tariff announcement—which corresponded to a 30% tariff—the cessation of exports represents a loss of a complementary business for the country.

Alejandro Castañeda, president of the National Association of Generating Companies (Andeg), indicated that Colombian energy exports to Ecuador represent a complementary business worth between US$130 million to US$300 million, depending on climate factors and whether generation is thermal or hydroelectric.

"The capacity of the link with Ecuador is 450 megawatts. That is roughly 3% of what Colombia produces. The priority is to serve Colombia 100%, and that additional percentage is what is commercialized to the neighboring country and is what is halted today," said Castañeda.

The sector leader emphasized that Colombia's energy sector does not suffer greatly from the interruption of the export market with the neighboring country, but "a complementary business is lost, with which energy has been sold to Ecuador for just over 20 years."

## Agriculture Sector Affected

Another sector facing disruption from the trade closure between the two countries is agroindustry, especially the sugarcane sector.

Claudia Calero, president of Asocaña, stated that the measure of raising tariffs to 100% hits the sector, considering that Ecuador is the fifth export market for Colombian sugar and generates more than US$40 million annually in foreign currency.

"Ecuador has historically been a key market for our sugar exports," emphasized the sector leader.

The loss of this commercial flow represents a direct blow to the sector's competitiveness and introduces greater pressure on other destinations that do not necessarily have the same absorption capacity.

It is worth noting that so far this year (January and February), sugarcane exports showed a 26% decline to the neighboring country. Specifically, according to DIAN figures, external sales of this product fell from US$7.6 million between January and February 2025 to US$5.6 million in the same period this year.

According to former Agriculture Minister Andrés Valencia, of the 2025 exports to Ecuador totaling US$1.846 billion, approximately US$336 million corresponded to agricultural and agroindustrial products.

"We are talking about 18% of external sales to Ecuador coming from the agriculture sector. In fact, of those US$336 million, approximately US$31.2 million is sugar, followed by instant coffee and other confectionery products. For sugarcane agroindustry, the effect could be significant," Valencia stated.

It is important to note that Analdex president Javier Díaz confirmed that with the tariff measure decision, "foreign trade between the two countries practically closed" and that Ecuador's willingness to negotiate would come after August.

## Other Affected Sectors

Based on Analdex's analysis, other major sectors caught in the trade war include human use medications, which went from US$6.5 million in 2025 to US$8.3 million in 2026 during the year to date, representing a 27.3% increase. Insecticides also showed positive dynamics during this period, increasing 143.3%, from US$1.7 million in January-February 2025 to US$4.2 million in the same period of 2026. Additionally, jet fuel (carburoreactants) showed an 88.3% increase in the same period analyzed by the trade association.
