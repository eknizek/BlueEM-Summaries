# Ecuador Tariffs Risk Agricultural Sectors

The escalating tariff situation between Ecuador and Colombia continues to generate disruptions, not only in bilateral trade but also in the economies of both countries, employment, and problems in different productive sectors. The decision by Ecuador's president to raise tariffs to 100% on Colombian products, and consequently Colombia's retaliatory tariff measure against Quito at the same level, is creating challenges for sectors such as agroindustry and partially the energy sector.

In general terms, the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex) recalled that energy goods were the first to be sacrificed amid the bilateral trade war.

## Energy Exports Plummet

According to data from DIAN, between January and February of this year, Colombia's electrical energy exports to Ecuador fell 77.1% year-over-year, dropping from US$110.3 million in the same months of 2025 to approximately US$25.2 million in the current period.

Although Colombia limited external energy sales to the neighboring country from the first tariff announcement, which corresponded to a 30% tariff, the non-exportation means a loss of complementary business for the country. Alejandro Castañeda, president of the National Association of Generating Companies (Andeg), indicated that Colombian energy exports to Ecuador represent complementary business that could be in the range of US$130 million to US$300 million, depending on climate factors and whether generation is thermal or hydroelectric.

"The capacity of the link with Ecuador is 450 megawatts. That is roughly 3% of what Colombia produces. The priority is to serve Colombia 100% and that additional percentage is what is commercialized to the neighboring country and is what is currently halted," said Castañeda.

Additionally, the industry leader highlighted that the Colombian energy sector does not suffer greatly from the interruption of the export market with the neighboring country, but "we are losing complementary business through which we have sold energy to Ecuador for just over 20 years."

## Agriculture Affected

Another sector facing disruption from the trade closure between the two countries is agroindustry, especially the sugarcane segment. Claudia Calero, president of Asocaña, stated that the measure of increasing tariffs to 100% hits the sector, considering that Ecuador is the fifth export market for Colombian sugar and generates more than US$40 million annually in foreign currency.

"Ecuador has historically been a key market for our sugar exports," emphasized the industry leader. The loss of this commercial flow represents a direct blow to the sector's competitiveness. Furthermore, it introduces greater pressure on other destinations that do not necessarily have the same absorption capacity.

It is worth noting that year-to-date (January and February), sugarcane exports showed a 26% decline to the neighboring country. Specifically, according to DIAN figures, external sales of this product dropped from US$7.6 million between January and February 2025 to US$5.6 million in the same period this year.

For his part, former Agriculture Minister Andrés Valencia assured Portafolio that of 2025 exports to Ecuador, which totaled US$1.846 billion, approximately US$336 million corresponded to agricultural and agroindustrial products.

"We are talking about 18% of external sales to Ecuador coming from the agricultural sector. Even of those US$336 million, some US$31.2 million is sugar, followed by instant coffee and other confectionery products. For sugarcane agroindustry, the effect could be significant," said Valencia.

It is important to mention that Analdex president Javier Díaz assured that with the tariff measure decision, "foreign trade between the two countries was practically closed" and that Ecuador's willingness to negotiate would come only after August.

## Other Affected Sectors

Based on Analdex analysis, other major sectors caught in the trade war include human-use medications, which year-to-date increased from US$6.5 million in 2025 to US$8.3 million in 2026, representing a 27.3% increase. Insecticides have also shown good dynamics in this period, rising 143.3%, going from US$1.7 million in January-February 2025 to US$4.2 million in the same period of 2026. Additionally, jet fuel appeared with an 88.3% increase in the period analyzed by the industry group.
