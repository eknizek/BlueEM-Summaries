# Ecuador's Tariffs Threaten Colombian Agricultural Sectors

The tariff escalation between Ecuador and Colombia continues to generate disruptions, not only in bilateral trade but also in the economies of both countries, employment, and problems in different productive sectors. The decision by Ecuador's president to raise tariffs to 100% on Colombian products, and consequently Colombia's reciprocal tariff response to Quito at the same level, is creating a series of challenges for sectors such as agroindustry and partly energy.

In general terms, the National Foreign Trade Association (Analdex) recalled that energy goods were the first to be sacrificed amid the bilateral trade war.

## Energy Exports Decline 77.1%

With data from the Colombian tax authority (Dian), between January and February of this year, Colombia's electrical energy exports to Ecuador fell 77.1% annually, dropping from US$110.3 million in the same months of 2025 to approximately US$25.2 million in the current period. Although Colombia limited external energy sales to the neighboring country since the first tariff announcement at 30%, the non-exportation represents a loss of a complementary business for the country.

Alejandro Castañeda, president of the National Association of Energy Generating Companies (Andeg), indicated to this outlet that Colombian energy exports to Ecuador are a complementary business that could be in the range of US$130 million to US$300 million, depending on climate factors and whether generation is thermal or hydroelectric.

"The capacity of the link with Ecuador is 450 megawatts. That is roughly 3% of what Colombia produces. The priority is to serve Colombia 100%, and that additional percentage is what is commercialized to the neighboring country and is what is currently halted," Castañeda stated.

Additionally, the industry leader highlighted that the Colombian energy sector does not suffer as much from the interruption of the export market with the neighboring country, but "if we lose a complementary business with which energy has been sold to Ecuador for a little over 20 years."

## Agriculture Sector Affected

Another sector facing disruption from the commercial closure between the two countries is agroindustry, especially the sugarcane sector. Claudia Calero, president of Asocaña, stated that the measure of increasing tariffs to 100% hits the sector, considering that Ecuador is the fifth export market for Colombian sugar and generates more than US$40 million annually in foreign currency.

"Ecuador has historically been a key market for our sugar exports," emphasized the industry leader.

The loss of this commercial flow represents a direct blow to sector competitiveness. Additionally, it introduces greater pressure on other destinations that do not necessarily have the same absorption capacity. It is worth noting that so far this year (January and February), sugarcane products experienced a 26% drop in exports to the neighboring country. Specifically, according to Dian figures, external sales of this product fell from US$7.6 million between January and February 2025 to US$5.6 million in the same period this year.

For his part, former Agriculture Minister Andrés Valencia assured Portafolio that of 2025 exports to Ecuador, which totaled US$1.846 billion, approximately US$336 million corresponded to agricultural and agroindustrial products.

"We are talking about 18% of external sales to Ecuador corresponding to the agricultural sector. Even of those US$336 million, approximately US$31.2 million are sugar, followed by instant coffee and other confectionery products. For the sugarcane agroindustry, the effect could be significant," Valencia stated.

It is important to mention that the president of Analdex, Javier Díaz, assured that with the tariff measure decision, "foreign trade between the two countries was practically closed" and that Ecuador's willingness to negotiate would come only after August.

## Other Affected Sectors

Based on Analdex's analysis, other main sectors caught in the trade war include human-use medications, which in the year to date went from US$6.5 million in 2025 to US$8.3 million in 2026, an increase of 27.3%. Similarly, insecticides showed good dynamics in this period, increasing 143.3%, rising from US$1.7 million in January-February 2025 to US$4.2 million in the same period of 2026. Additionally, there are aircraft fuels with an 88.3% increase in the same period analyzed by the association.
