# German giant Merck is optimistic about Brazil and will have a 'bioprinting' hub in Rio

On his first visit to Brazil since taking over the Life Science division of German conglomerate Merck about a year ago, Frenchman Jean-Charles Wirth predicts that the country "will be one of the largest pharmaceutical markets in the world within five years." After strengthening its logistics infrastructure in Brazil, the CEO wants to deepen ties with the local scientific community — including a new 3D "bioprinting" research center in Rio.

The executive has a privileged vantage point. He heads the highest-revenue division within a group worth nearly R$ 300 billion on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange and which manufactures everything from medications to crystals for smartphone screens. The company is 358 years old — it was founded before Bach was even born, for example. The Life Science arm sells products such as water purification systems for laboratories and even genome editing tools. Last year, it generated €9 billion in revenue.

Latin America represents only 4% of the division worldwide, but it is the fastest-growing region: it advanced 8.2%, double the global segment. According to Wirth, Brazil accounts for about half of the region's results — meaning Merck would have generated something like R$ 1 billion with Life Science in Brazil in 2025.

"Brazil is a strategic country for us, and we have operated here for over a century, since 1923. It will be one of the largest pharmaceutical markets in the world. You have strong universities, qualified scientists, necessary production capabilities, and you are advancing in segments like biosimilar medicines. That is why we will continue to invest in Brazil going forward," says the CEO.

According to IQVIA, one of the industry's leading consultancies, Brazil was the tenth largest pharmaceutical market in the world in 2024. Wirth doesn't give a number, but is convinced that the country will tend to gain positions.

Over the past two years, Merck invested €20 million to double the size of its logistics distribution center in Cajamar (São Paulo). According to the CEO, the infrastructure is "one of the five best" the company has around the world. Wirth says that after the logistics shocks of the pandemic and given new tariff barriers, Merck adopted a "region by region" strategy, and thus investments in assets like this gained importance.

"If you think about the global logistics ecosystem, today we are better able to deal with geopolitical conflicts precisely because we adopted this regionalized strategy. And that is why we invested these €20 million in logistics in Brazil," he explains.

Another leg of the strategy for Brazil is collaboration with academia. In the coming months, the company will invest in a 3D bioprinting hub within UFRJ's startup incubator. The idea is to enable collaboration between industry and academia in areas such as bio-inks and drug testing in organoids — technology that creates miniaturized versions of human organs in the laboratory from stem cells. The strategy is to use bioprinting and organoids to develop pharmaceuticals and therapies.

The hub will be open to startups, industries, and research groups working in the bioprinting field. Merck has been betting heavily on this type of technology. Early last year, it closed the purchase of Dutch company HUB Organoids, one of the pioneers in this sector worldwide. The initiative with UFRJ will provide virtual courses with HUB Organoids experts.

"This acquisition is allowing us to expand the types of development and prototyping for tissue engineering, bioprinting, and 3D cell culture. It is fantastic technology; scientists love it because it accelerates research. Our strategy is to expand these technologies from small European startups to the rest of the world. Given the solid foundation of science in Brazil, we sought to establish this partnership in Rio," says the CEO.

Cariocas have had a close relationship with Merck since the 1930s, when the group opened its first factory in the country in the Andaraí neighborhood. Fifty years ago, production moved to a new complex in Jacarepaguá — which remains its Brazilian factory to this day and even became the name of a BRT station. The unit, however, falls outside the Life Science division, being dedicated to the manufacture of medications.
