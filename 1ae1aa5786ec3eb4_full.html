# At Vivara, the disputed 1%

The compensation package proposed by Vivara for Paulo Kruglensky — a former CEO and current vice chairman of the company — has sparked debate in the market, with some managers and analysts criticizing how it was structured. Vivara is proposing a stock options plan that would give the board member 1% of the company's capital over three years, arguing that it wants to keep Kruglensky "aligned with the company's interests."

The plan — described on page 80 of the administration's proposal for the shareholder meeting, a 172-page document — has a three-year vesting period. The proposal does not explicitly state the strike price, but the company told Brazil Journal that it is around R$ 20-25 (according to the company, the calculation approved by the board is a weighted average of the last 60 trading sessions with a discount of up to 25%). In practice, Kruglensky would gain the right to buy shares at that price, benefiting from the variation between the strike price and the value at which the stock is trading at the end of three years.

Investors' criticism relates to the fact that the plan is exclusive to a board member without executive functions, and is funded by all shareholders through the issuance of new shares, rather than by the family. Kruglensky is a cousin of Marina Kaufman, Vivara's chairman and daughter of founder Nelson Kaufman. In mid-2024, Kruglensky sold his 4% stake in Vivara to Nelson at a significant discount, according to his own account to managers at the time.

The situation is now being compared to the compensation of the former CEO. "When Nelson wanted to give 1% of the company to Icaro [Borello, the company's former CEO], he did it with his own shares, not by dividing the cost with minority shareholders. He was even more correct than he needed to be," said an investor. "Now, if the family wants to do the same thing to attract Paulo back, they should use their own shares."

The stock options plan for Kruglensky still needs to be approved at Vivara's shareholder meeting, scheduled for the 27th. At least two foreign investors — who together account for the majority of free float — told Brazil Journal they intend to vote against the proposal. It is unclear whether the family will vote, given the potential conflict of interest. The Kaufman family holds 47.6% of Vivara's capital, with Marina holding around 14% of the shares. The remainder still belongs to Nelson, who stepped away from the business due to health issues almost a year ago.

One investor said that if the proposal is approved, it will create a "governance Frankenstein," with the company's chairman receiving no compensation, the vice chairman receiving this package, and the other three independent board members receiving less than the market average (according to a manager with access to the company, each board member receives compensation of R$ 30-40 thousand per month). "If he were the company's CEO, I would see no problem. But paying this compensation to a board member without executive function doesn't make sense," said another manager.

The criticisms are not unanimous. One of the analysts Brazil Journal spoke with said he sees no problem with the stock options plan given Paulo's potential contributions to the company and his knowledge of the business. "I don't think it's a major red flag, but they certainly could have thought about it more carefully, especially being a family member," said another analyst. "If it were a board member who everyone understands adds a lot of value, nobody would criticize, but Paulo is not a consensus."

The proposal comes after recent CEO and CFO changes at Vivara. The previous CEO, Icaro Borello, lasted only a year in the position. It is also the second questioning of Vivara's governance since 2024, when Nelson's turbulent return to the business also generated friction with the market, although many investors later praised the changes the founder made.

A source close to the family said that the request to create greater alignment of Paulo with the company came from the market itself. "Paulo has a very strategic role at Vivara. He was the company's CEO and has extensive knowledge of the market and the company's stakeholders," said this source. "He has the potential to generate significant value and will be dedicating considerably more hours than other board members."

According to this source, Marina invited Paulo to the board to help with strategic decisions, "to help think about what this company will be like in 3, 4 years." A manager uncomfortable with the situation said that "the point here is not even the value of Paulo's compensation itself, but the preferential treatment for a board member who is not the chairman. Paulo is entering this almost in an advisor role, and because something is arranged within the family."

Marina Kaufman declined to comment on the matter.

Vivara is worth R$ 6.4 billion on the stock exchange. The stock is up 41% in the last 12 months.
