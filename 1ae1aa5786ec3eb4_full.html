# At Vivara, the 1% Discord

The compensation package proposed by Vivara for Paulo Kruglensky — a former CEO and current vice chairman of the company — has sparked debate in the market, with some fund managers and analysts criticizing how it was structured. Vivara is proposing a stock options plan that would give the board member 1% of the company's capital over three years, arguing it wants to keep Kruglensky "aligned with the company's interests."

The plan — described on page 80 of the administration's proposal for the shareholders' meeting, a 172-page document — has a three-year vesting period. The proposal does not explicitly state the strike price, but the company told Brazil Journal that it is around R$ 20-25 (according to the company, the calculation approved by the board is a weighted average of the last 60 trading sessions with a discount of up to 25%). In practice, Kruglensky would gain the right to buy shares at that price, benefiting from the variation between the strike price and the value at which the stock is trading after three years.

Investor criticism centers on the fact that the plan is exclusive to a board member without executive functions, and that it is funded by all shareholders through the issuance of new shares, rather than by the family. Kruglensky is a cousin of Marina Kaufman, Vivara's chairman and daughter of founder Nelson Kaufman. In mid-2024, Kruglensky sold his 4% stake in Vivara to Nelson at a significant discount, according to his own accounts to fund managers at the time.

The situation is now being compared to the compensation of the former CEO. "When Nelson wanted to give 1% of the company to Icaro [Borello, the company's former CEO], he did it with his own shares, and not by dividing the cost with minority shareholders. He was even more correct than he needed to be," said one investor. "Now, if the family wants to do the same thing to attract Paulo back, they should use their own shares."

Kruglensky's stock options plan still needs to be approved at Vivara's shareholders' meeting, scheduled for the 27th. At least two foreign investors — who in aggregate account for the majority of the free float — told Brazil Journal they intend to vote against the proposal. It is unclear whether the family will vote, given the potential conflict of interest. The Kaufman family owns 47.6% of Vivara's capital, with Marina holding approximately 14% of shares. The remainder still belongs to Nelson, who stepped away from the business due to health issues nearly a year ago.

One investor said that if the proposal is approved, it will create a "governance Frankenstein," with the company's chairman receiving no compensation, the vice chairman receiving this package, and the other three independent board members receiving less than the market average (according to a manager with connections to the company, each board member receives approximately R$ 30-40 thousand per month).

"If he were the CEO of the company, I would see no problem at all. But paying this compensation to a board member without executive function makes no sense," said another manager. The criticism is not unanimous. One of the analysts Brazil Journal spoke with said he sees no problem with the stock options plan given the contributions Paulo can make to the company and his knowledge of the business. "I don't think it's a major red flag, but certainly they could have thought about it more, especially since it's a family member," said another analyst. "If it were a board member that everyone understood adds significant value, nobody would criticize it, but Paulo is not a consensus."

The proposal comes following recent changes in Vivara's CEO and CFO positions. The previous CEO, Icaro Borello, held the position for only one year. This is also the second governance question for Vivara since 2024, when Nelson's troubled return to the business also generated friction with the market, although afterward many investors praised the changes the founder made.

A source close to the family said that the request to create greater alignment of Paulo with the company came from the market itself. "Paulo has a quite strategic role for Vivara. He was the company's CEO and has considerable knowledge of the market and the company's stakeholders," said this source. "He has the potential to generate significant value and will dedicate much more hours than other board members." According to this source, Marina invited Paulo to the board to help with strategic decisions, "to help think about what this company will be in 3, 4 years."

A manager uncomfortable with the situation said that "the point here isn't even the value of Paulo's compensation itself, but the preferential treatment for a board member who isn't the chairman. Paulo is entering this almost in an advisor role, and because there's something arranged within the family."

Marina Kaufman declined to comment on the matter. Vivara is valued at R$ 6.4 billion on the stock exchange. The stock has risen 41% over the last 12 months.
