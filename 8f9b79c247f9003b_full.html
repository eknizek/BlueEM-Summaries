# USA, Venezuela, El Niño and the Future in Grupo Argos' Sights; Interview with New President

Juan Esteban Calle completed a month at the helm of the materials, infrastructure and energy conglomerate. The transition in his group coincides with that of the country.

Juan Esteban Calle, new president of Grupo Argos, navigates times of transition both for the businesses he leads and for the country. In this interview, Calle discusses how the moment of political changeover relates to central issues for his company: the future in energy, infrastructure, housing and investment in general.

**How do Grupo Argos' activities align with the current moment of government decisions and transition?**

We are very aware that opportunities need to reach many more people in Colombia. But the way they arrive has been proven to be through the private sector, in alliance with the public sector. There are many examples: what has happened in infrastructure, the speed at which essential projects for the country and regional competitiveness are really being built. I'll give just one example: the Eastern Tunnel in Medellín. It is an exemplary work that improves competitiveness and contributes significantly to a region's economy. And you see the concessions, and it's the same. And at this moment of fiscal stress for the Government, the only way the country continues advancing with what it needs is through public-private partnerships.

But let me address smaller issues that communities understand much better. Visit a PDET or ZOMAC municipality where works-for-taxes projects have arrived, and they are examples that come directly from feedback from people in the territories. You go to a place like Dabeiba, in Antioquia, and talk with the people and they were waiting for decades for solutions like those that works-for-taxes have brought, and they really cannot believe that the works can be built so fast with the quality they are being delivered and below budget. What you feel directly is the infinite gratitude and hope of the populations when these kinds of infrastructures that they have been waiting for decades are built. The private sector has a capacity for execution and managing high-impact issues that are necessary to solve Colombia's problems, which really makes it essential for envisioning the country's future.

**Specifying the above by sectors, what would be the direction in infrastructure?**

We must support the public-private partnership model and a number of projects that are already structured and ready and that the country needs. One example: the expansion of El Dorado Airport. It became too small and Colombia's competitiveness depends on airports, ports, roads, and there are many projects that are ready, waiting for investment conditions, but additionally support at the Central Government level to move forward. In moments of fiscal constraints where the government doesn't have to put in a single peso. Really, we private sector actors are willing to make the investment and those projects when in operation mean higher revenues for the national treasury.

**In construction, such as housing?**

It pains us that thousands of families do not have access to the homes they have been dreaming of for years. We must reactivate the housing sector, thinking especially about social interest housing. The deficit between quantitative and qualitative housing in the country is 5 million families, and they are waiting for solutions to arrive. Programs that were working well should continue in the future.

**In energy?**

The country cannot afford to black out. Having all the potential to be a power in energy generation because it has all really clean sources: hydroelectric generation, the gigantic opportunity to continue growing in solar, wind energy. It has gas, which is a fundamental fuel for the energy transition, and it cannot be that Colombians are paying much higher rates because conditions are not being created for the sector to continue growing and there to be more investment.
