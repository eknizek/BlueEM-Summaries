# Banco da Amazônia's Plan to Diversify Revenue and Gain 1 Million Customers

Banco da Amazônia, a financial institution controlled by the federal government, has a well-defined plan for 2026: to make its revenue more diversified, without depending almost entirely on its credit portfolio. The goal is that within two years, this segment moves from its current 85% to around 55%. The remaining 45% should be occupied by insurance, capitalization, credit card acquiring, and credit cards. All of this without reducing the volume of the core sector but rather expanding the overall revenue pie.

To achieve this, the company decided to look at its own customer base of approximately 1.2 million people to offer additional services, especially insurance. In this case, the goal is not to venture into "open waters" to avoid competing directly with the country's major banks.

"We've been restructuring over the last two years so the bank can operate in all business possibilities. During this period, we're also growing in development policy, commercial banking, and digital advancement," says Luiz Lessa, CEO of Banco da Amazônia, in an interview with NeoFeed. "In 2023, 95% of the bank's revenue came from credit. That's not good and carries a very high level of risk within the results. What we did was create, starting last year, these new avenues of growth with more products."

According to Lessa's assessment, only the insurance sector has significant growth potential to represent at least 20% of results, precisely because of the avenues of opportunities with its own customers. In the case of Bradesco, for example, insurance accounts for 41% of the total group's net profit. For the Amazon bank, it currently represents 5%.

The options, which began being offered recently to account holders, include life insurance, corporate insurance (to protect assets), employee insurance, lender insurance, and credit payment guarantee insurance.

"We only had this option, and it wasn't even well exploited. Our plan starts from our customer base. We'll grow from our customers. We want to attract entrepreneurs, their employees, their suppliers, and their customers," Lessa states.

In this sense, the CEO of Banco da Amazônia is aware that competing with leading market banks is not the best option, since there is still a long way to go within a universe that, in his view, has not yet been explored.

"I'm not going into open waters to compete with major retail banks, because we're not that size. We're a mid-sized institution. That's why we want a customer acquisition cost that's appropriate to our characteristics," the executive says. "Today I finance equipment with my credit line, for example, but I don't offer insurance to this customer who came looking for this resource. That's what we're going to change from now on."

In the line of service diversification, Banco da Amazônia also wants to grow geographically through technology. Today, the company has 124 branches in the nine states that make up the Legal Amazon (Acre, Amapá, Amazonas, Mato Grosso, Pará, Rondônia, Roraima, Tocantins, and part of Maranhão).

## Digital Arm in Progress

At the end of the first semester, the bank launches its digital arm, with a superapp responsible for account opening, which previously was only possible at a physical branch. The estimate is to gain one million new customers in the first 12 months after starting operations. In practice, it wants to almost double the volume of new account holders and users of financial services.

"This new channel will complement the action of expanding new products and services and, with that, attract more customers. We want to board several platforms in this app and make us a more national bank," Lessa explains.

And, unlike the insurance wallet implementation, when the bank doesn't intend to compete with Brazil's largest, in the case of the app he recognizes that the intention is to enter an avenue that already has consolidated digital banks. "The market is too large. In this case, it's open water, and I want to conquer a space. There are opportunities in this digital area for many institutions. Technology is something important for our growth," Lessa states.

According to the CEO, the company made investments of approximately R$ 800 million to implement this digital transformation in the last year. "Anyone from São Paulo or from Chuí can't open an account without coming to a state where we operate. Now, we'll be able to sell credit cards or open checking accounts for anyone anywhere in Brazil," he explains.

Even with the established plan to expand products and services, Banco da Amazônia ended 2025 with a 2.4% drop in net profit compared to the previous year, at R$ 1.1 billion. According to the CEO, this slight reduction is directly linked to the increase in default, which reached 4.7%.

"This is directly related to changes in legislation dealing with credit provisioning and losses. We had to change the limit and increase this account. And that affected our profit," Lessa states.

According to him, the bank recorded increased expenses, but related to more investments precisely in digital transformation. "It's an expense that, down the road, will generate revenue and reduce costs," he explains.

In any case, even with the new avenues underway, the trend for the bank is that the credit division continues to grow. In 2025, the bank reached a credit volume of R$ 66.8 billion, an increase of 20.4% over the previous year's result. The largest increase was in the commercial portfolio, which surpassed 37% growth, reaching R$ 3.5 billion. Most of this volume comes from legal entities (companies) and businesses from the Northern Region, the area where the bank operates. And in this line, the bet is on advancing service for small and medium-sized enterprises.

"The idea now is to refine the approach in credit release, monitoring this customer's journey before they might become delinquent. We'll check if the customer who took resources for their plantation with a one-year grace period, for example, is advancing in their production stage," he explains.

The bank's shareholder equity grew 9.7% and closed 2025 at R$ 7.2 billion. The return on average shareholder equity (ROAE) reached 16.2%.
