Latin American inflation to rise due to oil, warns Goldman Sachs: most affected countries

02 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Latin American Inflation to Rise Due to Oil, Warns Goldman Sachs: Most Affected Countries

The rise in oil following disruptions in the Middle East is increasing inflationary pressures in Latin America and forcing upward revisions of forecasts for 2026, according to a Goldman Sachs report.

The document stems from a relevant shift in the baseline scenario for crude. The firm raised its projections and now anticipates "Brent relaxing only to US$90 for December 2026 (previously US$80) and to US$80 for December 2027," implying a higher price environment for a longer period. This adjustment responds to a more prolonged disruption in energy flows, as production recovery is also slower.

This new price level changes how oil impacts inflation. The report estimates that "a 10% shock in oil prices raises inflation in Latin America by approximately 30 basis points," establishing a direct relationship between energy and consumer prices. This link is amplified because the impact extends beyond fuel to fertilizers, chemicals, and other industrial inputs.

## Higher Inflation and Widespread Price Pressure

The main adjustment in the report focuses on inflation expectations. Goldman Sachs raised its forecast for the region and now projects inflation will reach 8.0% in 2026, compared to the previously estimated 7.8%, with a subsequent moderation to 5.8% in 2027.

The revision is not uniform. Argentina leads the increase with a projection of 29.0%, followed by Brazil with 5.0%, Chile with 4.2%, and Colombia with 6.5%. Ecuador also registers an upward adjustment to 2.7%, while Mexico remains at 4.5% and Peru rises to 3.6%.

This pattern reflects both oil exposure and internal price policies. The report notes that "risks to consumer prices are broadening beyond fuels," introducing a more persistent dynamic. As the energy shock filters through production chains, inflation stops being a punctual phenomenon and acquires a more structural character.

This scenario reinforces the idea of more persistent inflation in the region, at a time when central banks have yet to consolidate the disinflation process. A central element is the degree to which international prices are passed on to consumers. In countries like Chile and Peru, where local prices more closely follow global references, the increases have been more pronounced.

In contrast, economies like Brazil, Colombia, and Mexico have used subsidies or fiscal mechanisms to cushion the impact, which partially contains inflation in the short term but generates fiscal distortions.

**A widespread increase across the region:**

- Argentina: rises to 29.0% (+100 bp)

- Brazil: rises to 5.0% (+20 bp)

- Chile: rises to 4.2% (+40 bp)

- Colombia: rises to 6.5% (+20 bp)

- Ecuador: rises to 2.7% (+30 bp)

- Mexico: unchanged at 4.5% (0 bp)

- Peru: rises to 3.6% (+40 bp)

- Latin America (average): rises to 8.0% (+28 bp)

In Argentina, inflationary pressure is amplified by fuel behavior. The bank states that gasoline prices have risen slightly less than 30% so far this year and around 20% since the start of the Middle East conflict. The bank estimates that if international prices remain at current levels, gasoline could still rise an additional 10%, continuing to transfer pressure on inflation in coming months.

## Winners and Losers: The Role of Being an Exporter or Importer

The effect of oil on growth introduces a clear divergence between countries. Goldman Sachs highlights that "the impact on growth depends on whether a country is a net exporter or importer of oil," establishing the main axis of regional differentiation.

In this context, Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, and Ecuador benefit from higher crude prices, as increased export revenues offset part of the inflationary pressure. In contrast, Chile, Mexico, and Peru face an adverse effect, given that higher energy costs deteriorate their terms of trade.

This contrast explains why the adjustment in growth is moderate at the aggregate level but more visible in specific economies. The report projects that the region will grow 1.9% in 2026, slightly below the previous 2.0%, with more marked cuts in Mexico, whose estimate drops to 1.3%, and in Chile, which declines to 1.9%.

The transmission channel is not limited to foreign trade. The document warns that "a tightening of 10 basis points in global financial conditions reduces growth in Latin America by approximately 10 basis points," linking the energy shock to the international financial environment. Adding to this is the impact of global slowdown, which also spills over to the region.

## Risks Ahead

Faced with this scenario, central banks' response appears conditioned by the nature of the shock. Goldman Sachs expects "most central banks to look beyond the oil-driven supply shock," provided inflation expectations remain anchored.

However, maneuver room is limited in some countries. In Brazil, the deterioration of inflation expectations led to raising the Selic rate projection to 13.25% toward the end of 2026, reflecting a more cautious stance. This adjustment responds to an environment where inflation remains elevated and economic activity shows resilience.

The report also introduces a risk scenario in which the energy shock is prolonged or intensified. In that case, "a longer and deeper oil shock could shift policy focus from inflation to growth," suggesting a more complex dilemma for monetary authorities.

The future trajectory will depend on the evolution of the Middle East conflict, the recovery of energy supply, and market reaction. Meanwhile, the increase in oil is already redefining inflation expectations in Latin America and shifting the balance of regional economies.
