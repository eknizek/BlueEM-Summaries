# Meat Giants Face U.S. Justice Department Scrutiny Following Price Surge

The U.S. Department of Justice has confirmed it is investigating potential antitrust violations in the meat processing industry amid rising beef prices in the domestic market and declining cattle herds. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche refused to provide additional details about the investigation but stated that the agency will seek to advance rapidly.

"Much work has already been done and much still needs to be done," said Blanche, who took over the helm of the Department of Justice after President Donald Trump fired his first attorney general last month. "We are acting as quickly as possible."

Bloomberg News reported at the end of last month that the Department of Justice had opened a criminal investigation into how meat processing plants, including JBS and Tyson Foods, purchase cattle from ranchers.

This investigation, which also involves a separate inquiry conducted by the department's civil prosecutors, began after Trump ordered the Department of Justice in November to investigate the meat processing industry amid record-high beef prices.

Spokespeople for JBS, Tyson, Cargill, and National Beef did not immediately respond to requests for comment.

Blanche refused to answer questions about how the current investigation differs from one initiated during Trump's first term and ended by the Department of Justice last year.

The use of so-called alternative marketing agreements between cattle feedlots and meat processors, rather than open auctions, has long been a target of criticism. Such contracts are based on disclosed spot market prices, but consolidation in the sector—with four companies controlling about 85% of cattle purchases in the U.S.—has generated concerns among ranchers that these base prices may be distorted, according to the Department of Agriculture.

However, the severity of the current cattle shortage has raised prices so significantly that meat processors are losing money on each animal processed, according to HedgersEdge data. Live cattle futures contracts in Chicago reached a record high last week, and U.S. beef imports have increased to meet domestic demand.

At a press conference on Monday, Blanche and Peter Navarro, senior White House adviser for trade and manufacturing, said the Department of Justice had reached a separate agreement to resolve an antitrust lawsuit against data provider Agri Stats, based in Indiana. The Department of Justice sued Agri Stats in 2023 for allegedly helping poultry and pork processors conspire over wholesale prices they set for sales to major supermarkets. The trial was scheduled for the end of this month. Agri Stats refused to comment.
