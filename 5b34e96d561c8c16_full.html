The mirage of platform work: study reveals more informality, lower income, and uncertain future

# The Mirage of Platform Work: Study Reveals More Informality, Lower Income, and Uncertain Future

While thousands of Colombians increasingly find in digital applications an alternative to generate income for their families, a recent study from Universidad del Rosario reveals that behind the promise of autonomy and flexibility lies a labor model that deepens informality and deteriorates the economic conditions of those who depend on these platforms.

The research, led by sociologist Óscar Javier Maldonado and revealed on the occasion of Labor Day celebrations on May 1st, estimates that between 200,000 and 400,000 people work today on digital platforms in Colombia, many of them as their primary source of sustenance. However, far from consolidating as a solution to unemployment, this type of occupation reproduces and amplifies structural inequalities in the labor market.

"Platforms thrive in scenarios of inequality, capitalize on informality and promote precariousness," warns Maldonado, who emphasizes that this model responds to a logic in which "profitability takes precedence over worker well-being."

One of the most compelling findings of the study is the relationship between working time and income. Data cited from Fedesarrollo show that a delivery person earns on average 867,000 monthly for 35 weekly hours, while a driver can reach close to 1.2 million for 44.6 hours. However, reality is often more critical, with cases of people working more than 70 weekly hours without reaching minimum wage. This situation confirms a paradox: although platforms present themselves as a form of individual entrepreneurship, in practice they function as high labor demand schemes with low remuneration and without basic guarantees.

The study itself points out that this model allows companies to "create wealth" by relying on informal work, avoiding labor costs such as social security or benefits. This translates into a transfer of economic risk to workers.

## Rising Informality

This precariousness in the labor force of millions of people in the country is gaining strength with recent figures revealed by the National Administrative Department of Statistics (Dane) showing that labor informality in Colombia reaches levels close to 55.6 percent, a figure that the platform model contributes to deepening. Added to this is the growth of self-employment. According to the official statistics entity, in March 2026, 10.3 million people were in this condition, representing 42.4 percent of the employed, with a significant increase compared to the previous year.

These data show, moreover, that formal employment is not growing at the same pace as the employed population, which pushes millions toward more precarious forms of subsistence. In this context, digital platforms appear as a pressure valve for those who find no opportunities in the formal market, but also as a mechanism that consolidates that same precariousness.

## Labor Risks

But beyond income, the study warns about the physical and social risks associated with these activities. 36.8 percent of delivery workers have suffered traffic accidents and 33.3 percent work-related illnesses, in an environment where most lack health or occupational risk coverage. About 10 percent of workers are approaching retirement age without guarantees of pension protection.
