# Petrobras Resumes Scientific Research Activities Across 37 Sedimentary Basins for Oil and Gas

By Gabriela da Cunha (Broadcast) | May 2, 2026

Petrobras, the state-owned oil and gas company, has resumed research, development, and innovation (RD&I) projects in 37 Brazilian sedimentary basins to update maps that reconstruct the geological history and evolution of basins on the surface and subsurface. These maps are known as stratigraphic charts. The data from onshore (land exploration) and offshore (ocean) basins should facilitate new geological research.

"The project was previously coordinated by Petrobras in two earlier editions, from 1994 to 2007, serving as an educational base for students, researchers, and the industry. This is not an exploratory project with commercial objectives, but a scientific effort to update and standardize information," the company stated.

In 15 of these basins, there is cooperation between Petrobras, researchers from universities in different regions of the country, and the Geological Survey of Brazil (SGB), one of the scientific institutions hired through a public call. "This partnership will make it possible to integrate decades of subsurface data collected by the company with surface mapping and mineral resource assessment, which is the SGB's specialty," explains Cleide Regina Moura da Silva, SGB researcher and head of the Sedimentary Basins Division (DIBASE).

"This is important work for a range of products that will be available to society as a whole, such as geological maps and paleontology and geophysics databases. This benefits various studies, from more basic minerals like sand and limestone to research on critical minerals needed for energy production and transition," the researcher explains.

The project is in its initial phase, compiling already-published data, before moving on to field campaigns. Areas of activity include the Bananal basins, located between Goiás and Tocantins, and the Marajó basin in Pará.

Although it was an area of interest for oil discovery in the 1950s, the Marajó Basin has not registered significant discoveries in the 18 wells drilled through 1989, which led to the abandonment of activities, according to Francismar Ferreira, research coordinator at the Institute for Strategic Studies on Oil, Natural Gas, and Biofuels (Ineep).

"Since 1998, there have been no block offerings or contracts in the region. The basin was forgotten in terms of oil and gas exploration, as were other smaller terrestrial basins that became less attractive given the exploratory success in other regions, especially in the pre-sal," Ferreira explains.

According to the SGB, work on stratigraphic charts in basins without petroleum potential helps understand the geology of other nearby areas with possible new accumulations.
