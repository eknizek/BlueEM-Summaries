# Villar Reaffirms Constitutional Mandate of Banco de la República Amid Pension Decree Tension

Central Bank Manager highlighted support received at Asofondos Congress; called for clarity and timelines on key pension decisions.

Leonardo Villar Gómez, General Manager of the Banco de la República, during his presentation at the 19th Annual Asofondos Congress, which concludes this Friday in Cartagena.

Amid growing discussion over the Government's decision to issue Decree 0415, which orders the transfer of approximately 25 trillion pesos from private pension funds (AFP) to Colpensiones, Leonardo Villar Gómez, manager of the Banco de la República, sent a message of institutional caution and defense of the Central Bank's constitutional mandate. His statements came ahead of his presentation at the Annual Asofondos Congress, concluding this Friday in Cartagena, where he was received with prolonged applause from the audience.

Villar interpreted this gesture not as personal recognition, but as endorsement of the institution's role.

"I understand this clearly as support for an institution, for the role it plays as the development of Colombia's Constitution," he affirmed, emphasizing that the recognition is directed toward the Bank and its employees.

Villar avoided delving into specific pension sector topics, despite being at a key venue like Asofondos. However, he did refer to the general context of pension reform and the uncertainty still surrounding its implementation, particularly awaiting a pronouncement from the Constitutional Court.

In this regard, he reiterated that the Banco de la República is prepared to act according to what the Court determines, but insisted on the need for adequate timelines to make required technical adjustments.

"We trust that the decision made will give us the necessary time to adjust," he stated.

One central point is the potential participation of the Bank in administering the Contributory Pillar Savings Fund (FAPC), contemplated in the reform. Villar explained that although the entity advanced in preparations, these have been halted by lack of regulatory definitions and suspension of the process while the constitutionality of the law is resolved.

"We were ready to have it running, but the Court's decisions stopped that process," he indicated.

As an example, he mentioned that it is still not possible to appoint the fund's board of directors, since legally this does not exist while the law has not come into force.

Therefore, the manager proposed that, should the Court approve the reform, at least a three-month period be granted to complete necessary institutional and operational adjustments.

Beyond the pension debate, Villar centered his presentation on the report submitted to Congress, addressing key topics such as inflation evolution, economic expectations, and monetary policy decisions. He reiterated that the Bank's strategy is guided by an explicit constitutional mandate: maintaining low and stable inflation while contributing to the maximum sustainable level of employment in coordination with general economic policy.

In a context marked by high-impact government decisions and tensions surrounding pension reform, Villar's message aimed to reinforce the Banco de la República's technical autonomy and the need to preserve clear rules to guarantee the country's economic stability.
