# Zena Cucina Targets 20% Annual Growth Through Recurring Revenue and Customer Loyalty Strategy

For years, the American TV series Cheers explored the idea of a place where "everybody knows your name" and to which customers always return. At Zena Cucina, located in the Jardins neighborhood of São Paulo, this logic has moved from fiction to become a business model. Many loyal customers return to the restaurant several times in a single week. This recurrence, built over 17 years, has become part of the foundation upon which chef Carlos Bertolazzi and businessman Dudu Pereira sustain the restaurant's growth, which has navigated different cycles in the restaurant market without losing relevance and with consistent growth in recent years.

"There are people who have lunch here three or four times a week," Bertolazzi said in an interview with Bloomberg Línea. "You create a bond with the customer," Pereira added.

Customer loyalty is built on a close relationship, with recognition of habits and participation in important moments in clients' lives. This connection reduces dependence on trends and contributes to more stable demand over time. This recurring customer base is considered one of the business's main assets.

The differentiator lies in the frequency at which customers return. Revenue growth does not depend solely on price increases, but on expanding consumption per customer. "Ticket increases don't come just from price; they come from being able to sell an appetizer, a wine, an experience," said Bertolazzi.

To achieve this, Zena invested in staff qualification, hiring professionals from high-end restaurants who have greater capacity to guide customers and conduct the experience. "The team has more expertise and this generates better sales," said Pereira.

This reflects part of the restaurant's natural evolution over nearly two decades. When it opened in 2009, Zena functioned as a café with flexible operations, combining full meals, snacks, and quick-consumption products throughout the day. "We opened Zena with a more informal identity," said Pereira. "Time and customers themselves showed us that we had potential to move toward being a restaurant," he added.

The transition was accompanied by improvements in staff, menu, and structure, eventually reaching its current positioning as a restaurant focused on complete meals and experiences. Since 2022, the establishment has registered annual growth between 18% and 20%, with an average ticket of around 160 Brazilian reais and an average of 4,000 to 4,200 customers served per month. According to the partners, this performance reflects a combination of operational and strategic decisions adjusted over time, rather than an abrupt repositioning. "Long-lasting companies always adapt to the market and mature," said Pereira.

One of the pillars of the business is balance between quality and price, according to Pereira and Bertolazzi. The restaurant works with inputs comparable to those of more expensive establishments but seeks to maintain a lower average ticket to expand its audience. "We buy inputs as good as restaurants with higher tickets, but we work hard to maintain lower prices to be attractive," said Bertolazzi.

In an environment of cost pressures, the strategy involves absorbing some of these increases and compensating with efficiency. "When prices rise, we try not to pass on 100% of the cost, otherwise the business won't be able to continue attracting customers," he said.

Operational management is central to this process. The restaurant invests in constant equipment updates, reduced energy and water consumption, and supplier negotiations to preserve margins. The goal is to treat the restaurant as a structured operation in which efficiency gains compensate for the limitation of passing prices to consumers.

Unlike much of the sector, the restaurant is not betting on expansion through new locations. The strategy has been to extract more revenue from the current asset, a large property that is still considered underutilized. "Every centimeter of this space needs to generate revenue," said Pereira.

This move led to the creation of new fronts within the operation itself, such as the Il Covo bar, installed in a previously unused space. In recent years, a new avenue for growth has been the expansion of the events area. Zena has begun hosting weddings, corporate events, and gastronomic experiences, often occupying slower hours in the main dining room. The strategy allows for increased revenue without depending exclusively on traditional lunch and dinner traffic.

The operation was also adjusted to changes in consumer behavior that intensified in recent years. The traditional late-night service practically disappeared, and dinner hours were moved earlier. "Today at 11 p.m. the restaurant is already much emptier," said Bertolazzi. Adapting to these changes has become part of the business's routine.

A significant portion of the results is continuously reinvested in the restaurant, in renovations, improvements, and structural updates, according to the partners. This practice seeks to avoid the stagnation common in older establishments and maintain the standard of experience over time.
