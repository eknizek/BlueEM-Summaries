Colombian Export Base Expands in Latin America While Contracting in Key Markets

26 April 2026 · 04:50 PM

# Colombian Export Base Expands in Latin America While Contracting in Key Markets

The Colombian export sector during 2025 and in the present year has faced a series of disruptions that have put foreign trade in a precarious position. However, its business fabric has remained at the forefront in diversifying and growing the destinations that are partners for Colombian products, while also working to sustain others despite the international situation.

According to an analysis by the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex), during 2025, Colombia's export fabric was made up of 9,246 companies, representing a contraction of 1.0% compared to 2024, when 9,339 companies were registered. "This result shows a slight reduction in the country's export base, mainly explained by the decline in lower-scale segments," the business group highlighted.

In terms of structure, the report shows that high concentration remains in the lower export ranges. The segment between $1,000 and $100,000 concentrated 49.4% of total companies, followed by the range between $100,000 and $1 million with a participation of 31.5%. Meanwhile, intermediate segments showed positive behavior, as companies with exports between $1 million and $10 million grew 6.4%, reaching a participation of 14.2%, while the range between $10 million and $100 million registered a slight increase of 1.3%, with a participation of 4.2%.

In contrast, the number of companies with sales exceeding $1 billion decreased by 40%. Additionally, the lower-scale segment ($1,000 to $100,000) fell 4%, which would largely explain the overall reduction in the number of exporters. In this regard, the business group details that the behavior of business segments shows there is a need to strengthen mechanisms for entry and permanence in the country's export activity. "Especially to prevent the exit of companies in lower-scale segments, which are the most vulnerable to external shocks and internationalization costs," they point out.

The document also states that the figures revealed seek to identify changes in the country's business base, in concentration levels, and in signals of strengthening or reduction in different export segments.

## Destinations

Looking in detail at the behavior of the business fabric by destination countries, if analyzed in terms of growth in the number of exporting companies, the greatest advances were recorded in relatively smaller-sized markets. Cuba led the expansion with an increase of 13.9%, followed by Uruguay with 12.5% and Argentina with 10.6%. Guatemala and Nicaragua also stood out, both with increases of 3.9%, as well as Honduras with 3.7% and Venezuela with 4.3%.

In contrast, Bolivia presented the largest decline in business fabric with a contraction of 10.2%, followed by Paraguay with -2.7%, the United States with -2.2%, Costa Rica with -0.9%, and Chile with -0.8%.

Despite these variations, the United States remains the main destination in terms of breadth of the export fabric, although in 2025 it registered a slight reduction. This market continues to be characterized by high concentration in small-scale companies: 48.3% export between $1,000 and $100,000, and 30.3% between $100,000 and $1 million.

A similar behavior is observed in Ecuador, where the number of companies reached 2,569, with a slight contraction of 0.3%. There, 60.1% of exporters are in the range between $1,000 and $100,000, while 30% are concentrated between $100,000 and $1 million.

Panama, meanwhile, showed marginal growth of 0.9%, reaching 1,733 companies. The export base continues to be dominated by small-scale companies, representing 69.4% in the lowest range.

Regarding the European Union, the export fabric grew 0.3%, with a structure where 52.4% of companies export between $1,000 and $100,000 and 28% between $100,000 and $1 million. Unlike other destinations, this market showed strengthening of intermediate segments, with growth of 10.5% between $1 million and $10 million.

Peru also reflected a dynamic of strengthening in medium scales, with total growth of 0.9% and significant growth in segments between $1 million and $10 million (12.8%) and between $100,000 and $1 million (7.4%). However, the lower-scale segment fell 3.6%.

Mexico and Brazil showed moderate increases of 1.3% and 2%, respectively, with similar patterns: high concentration in small companies, as more than 50% are in the range between $1,000 and $100,000. Furthermore, they registered growth in intermediate segments but with sharp declines at higher levels, such as a reduction of 90.9% in Mexico and 63.6% in Brazil in the range above $100 million.

Markets with the largest declines reflect more structural weaknesses. Bolivia not only reduced its number of companies but also presented contractions in intermediate segments of 23.8% and the disappearance of large-scale exporters. Paraguay, although it fell in number of companies, showed a recomposition toward higher ranges, with growth of 27% and 62.5% in intermediate segments.

Finally, Analdex argues that the ranking shows high concentration in traditional markets such as the U.S., Ecuador, and Panama, which continue to lead in number of exporting companies. "In this context, the challenge lies in diversifying destinations and deepening insertion into markets with greater growth potential," they concluded.
