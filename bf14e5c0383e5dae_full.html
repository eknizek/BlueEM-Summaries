# Trafigura Prepares Entry into Energy Trading in Brazil

Trafigura, one of the world's largest commodity trading companies, is setting up an electricity trading operation in Brazil. The move by the Singapore-based group draws attention not only for bringing a relevant new player to the market, but also because it occurs at a turbulent moment — many independent energy trading companies are facing financial crises and even judicial recoveries.

With total revenues of US$ 240 billion in 2025, Trafigura is one of the world's largest traders of oil and metals, with offices in more than 50 countries. It also operates in electricity, gas, renewables and carbon, in addition to holding stakes in various companies.

In Brazil, the electricity trading market involves more than 500 companies. Among the leaders in volumes are companies linked to banks, such as BTG Pactual and Santander, and major utilities, such as Auren, Cemig, Copel, Eneva and Engie. The segment is also composed of hundreds of independent traders, and already has participation from some international commodity trading firms, such as Danish company Danske and Vitol.

"Brazil has many opportunities in the energy sector," a source with knowledge of Trafigura's strategy told Brazil Journal, adding that the company has "big plans."

Trafigura has not yet officially commented on the operations. "They are setting up the structure. It's not meant to have a result in trading this year; it's long term. But they know the way," said a second source close to the company.

Trafigura's head of electricity trading in Brazil will be Pedro Vidal, former Director of Trading at Light Com. He has previous stints at Lightsource, part of BP, as well as Energisa, Mercurio and Eneva. Vidal will face the challenge of putting Trafigura in the game amid volatile energy prices and low liquidity in the electricity market that has broken many smaller trading companies.

Volatility in short-term prices, which can vary more than 2,000% in a single day, stems from the growing participation of variable-generation energy sources in the energy mix, such as wind and solar plants. The lack of liquidity is associated in the market with the strategy of large generator groups holding electricity sales in long-term contracts, due to prospects of high spot market prices and credit risk in dealings with smaller trading firms.

In the sector, the scenario divides opinions. Some sources say independent trading companies are living through "the biggest crisis in history." Other players see a "cleansing" of the segment.

For an experienced market consultant, Trafigura could bring "international experience" and new products to the electricity market, "compatible with the sector's new reality," which is more volatile. "There is a squeeze on trading companies. And every crisis generates improvements, changes. Large trading firms from abroad can support the development of our market," said a sector executive.

In Brazil, Trafigura already has offices in Rio de Janeiro and Minas Gerais, operating in sectors such as oil and mining. The group closed in 2024 a leniency agreement with Brazilian authorities in which it accepted paying R$ 435 million, admitting involvement in bribes for business dealings with Petrobras — the irregularities came to light in Operation Lava Jato.
