Pyrrhic Victory? Enel's de-licensing process in São Paulo could take two years

07 April 2026 · 08:13 PM

# Pyrrhic Victory? Enel's de-licensing process in São Paulo could take two years

In a historic decision, the board of Aneel (National Electric Energy Agency) unanimously approved on Tuesday, April 7, the opening of an administrative process to evaluate the forfeiture of the concession against energy distributor Enel SP. According to the board, the concessionaire may present a written defense within 30 days and, after analysis of any appeal, Aneel may or may not recommend to the Ministry of Mines and Energy (MME) the forfeiture of the company's concession contract.

In practice, the regulatory agency tends to recommend that the MME break the company's concession contract before the deadline – which ends in 2028 – due to serious and repeated failures in the distribution service of the concessionaire in its area of operation, the capital and 23 other municipalities in the Metropolitan Region of São Paulo. If the recommendation is later accepted by the MME, the government will have the option to define the transition of concession operations in São Paulo through a provisional operator until the end of the contract in 2028 – for which Enel had filed a renewal request, which should be ignored if forfeiture is decreed – or preparation of a new tender.

But this could be a Pyrrhic victory, an expression used to refer to a triumph so costly that it ends up having an effect almost equal to a defeat. The term comes from King Pyrrhus of Epirus, who defeated the Romans in 279 BC but lost so many soldiers and resources that he supposedly said something like: "One more victory like this and I will be lost."

The term may make sense because, according to experts heard by NeoFeed, the procedural process at Aneel and the MME to confirm forfeiture is long and could extend, strictly speaking, until 2028 or beyond. That is, even defeated, Enel will continue to operate the concession until the end of its contract.

Enel São Paulo serves approximately 18 million people in the Metropolitan Region of São Paulo, according to official data from the concessionaire itself. In addition, the Enel group has two other energy distribution concessions in the country: Enel Rio de Janeiro and Enel Ceará, the latter also facing much criticism.

## The Vote

The majority in favor of opening a process to evaluate the forfeiture of the São Paulo concession was formed at Aneel after the reading of the votes by directors Gentil Nogueira and Agnes Maria da Costa. Two other directors of the regulatory agency – Fernando Mosna and Willamy Moreira Frota – subsequently confirmed the decision. Aneel's director-general, Sandoval Feitosa, had already recommended forfeiture in a vote given previously.

The decision of Aneel's directors took into account three criteria. The first involved recurring blackouts and long interruptions between 2023 and 2025, leaving millions of consumers in the Metropolitan Region of São Paulo without power for long periods. The most serious case occurred in December 2025, when an extratropical cyclone left more than 500,000 properties without electricity, with slow restoration considered far inferior to that of other distributors in similar situations. In March, when he cast his vote, secretary-general Feitosa cited "interruptions exceeding 147 hours (more than six days) in 2025, characterizing extreme events of long duration, similar to those verified in the November 2023 and October 2024 events."

Another argument from the regulatory agency were the structural failures of the concessionaire. Director Gentil Nogueira was very harsh in his vote. He cited the continuation of these problems, complaining about the insufficiency of the measures adopted and the severity of the episodes: "The failures and transgressions were not remedied in a 'structural and definitive' manner, with the persistence of relevant deficiencies in service provision."

Finally, the directors cited the regulatory crisis throughout the process generated by these problems, with judicialization and loss of institutional confidence. Enel tried to suspend the process in court, alleging violation of due process, which increased tension with Aneel and the Court of Accounts of the Union (TCU). These episodes motivated strong political pressure on the company from São Paulo's Mayor Ricardo Nunes (MDB) and Governor Tarcísio de Freitas (Republicans).

In parallel, municipal and state authorities reported insufficient teams, poor service quality and prioritization of profits over investments. The sum of these factors – technical failures, recurring blackouts, political pressure and legal questions – led Aneel to consider forfeiture as a possible measure to guarantee service continuity and quality.

## Two Phases

Italian multinational Enel supplies energy to 14 million customers in 274 Brazilian cities. It also operates in generation with 27 hydroelectric plants, 14 wind energy parks and seven solar parks in the country. The Italian government holds 29% stake in the company.

Enel assumed the electricity concession of the São Paulo metropolitan region in 2018 by acquiring 73.38% of AES Eletropaulo's shares for R$ 5.55 billion in an auction held at B3. The acquired concession drew attention for being the largest in Latin America, with almost 8 million consumer units and about 18 million people, in one of the continent's most densely populated urban regions.

Strictly speaking, Enel's management in São Paulo can be divided into two parts. The first, between 2018 and 2023, was marked by reduced investments, with cuts to much of the workforce of the former operator, failing to meet Aneel's minimum quality targets in 2021 and 2022. From 2023 onwards, however, the company paid the price for the negligence of previous years by suffering repeated extreme weather phenomena passing through São Paulo since then – intense storms, including an extratropical cyclone last December, which knocked down trees and poles – which only reinforced its bad reputation.

Enel São Paulo has accumulated R$ 374 million in fines applied by Aneel and Arsesp since 2020, but has paid only R$ 29 million – about 8% of the total. The remainder, approximately R$ 345 million, is under litigation or in the appeal phase.
