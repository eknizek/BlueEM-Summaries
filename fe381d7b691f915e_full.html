Pyrrhic Victory? Enel's Potential 'Switch-Off' Process in São Paulo Could Take Two Years

07 April 2026 · 08:13 PM

# Pyrrhic Victory? Enel's Potential 'Switch-Off' Process in São Paulo Could Take Two Years

In a historic decision, the board of Aneel (National Electric Energy Agency) unanimously approved, on Tuesday, April 7, the opening of an administrative process to evaluate the forfeiture of the concession against the energy distributor Enel SP. According to the board, the concessionaire may present a written defense within 30 days, and after analyzing any appeal, Aneel may or may not recommend to the Ministry of Mines and Energy (MME) the forfeiture of the company's concession contract.

In practice, the regulatory agency tends to recommend to the MME that it break the company's concession contract before the deadline – which ends in 2028 – due to serious and repeated failures in the energy distribution service of the concessionaire in its operating area, the capital and 23 other municipalities in the Metropolitan Region of São Paulo.

If the recommendation is later accepted by the MME, the government will have the option to define the transition of concession operations in São Paulo through a temporary operator until the end of the contract in 2028 – for which Enel had filed a renewal request that should be ignored if forfeiture is decreed – or preparation of a new bidding process.

But this could be a Pyrrhic victory, an expression used to refer to a triumph so costly that it ends up having an effect almost equal to a defeat. The term comes from King Pyrrhus of Epirus, who defeated the Romans in 279 BC, but lost so many soldiers and resources that he allegedly said something like: "One more victory like this and I will be lost."

The term may make sense because, according to specialists heard by NeoFeed, the procedural process at Aneel and the MME to confirm forfeiture is long and could extend, strictly speaking, until 2028 or beyond. In other words, even defeated, Enel should continue operating the concession until the end of its contract.

Enel São Paulo serves approximately 18 million people in the Metropolitan Region of São Paulo, according to official data from the concessionaire itself. Furthermore, the Enel group holds two other energy distribution concessions in the country: Enel Rio de Janeiro and Enel Ceará, the latter also facing many criticisms.

The majority in favor of opening a process to evaluate the forfeiture of the São Paulo concession was formed at Aneel following the reading of the votes of directors Gentil Nogueira and Agnes Maria da Costa. Two other directors of the regulatory agency – Fernando Mosna and Willamy Moreira Frota – subsequently confirmed the decision. Aneel's director-general, Sandoval Feitosa, had already recommended forfeiture in a previously issued vote.

The decision of Aneel's directors took into account three criteria. The first involved recurrent blackouts and long interruptions between 2023 and 2025, leaving millions of consumers in the Metropolitan Region of São Paulo without power for long periods. The most serious case occurred in December 2025, when an extratropical cyclone left more than 500,000 properties without electricity, with a slow restoration considered much inferior to that of other distributors in similar situations. In March, when issuing his vote, secretary-general Feitosa cited "interruptions exceeding 147 hours (more than six days) in 2025, characterizing extreme events of long duration, and similar to those verified in the events of November 2023 and October 2024."

Another argument by the regulatory agency was the concessionaire's structural failures. Director Gentil Nogueira was extremely harsh in his vote. He cited the continuation of these problems, complaining about the insufficiency of adopted measures and the severity of episodes: "The failures and transgressions were not remedied in a 'structural and definitive' manner, with the persistence of relevant deficiencies in service provision."

Finally, the directors cited the regulatory crisis generated throughout the process by these problems, with judicialization and loss of institutional trust. Enel attempted to suspend the process in court, alleging violation of due process, which increased tensions with Aneel and the Federal Audit Court (TCU). These episodes motivated strong political pressure on the company from São Paulo's mayor, Ricardo Nunes (MDB), and governor Tarcísio de Freitas (Republicans). In parallel, municipal and state authorities denounced insufficient teams, poor service quality, and prioritization of profits over investments. The sum of these factors – technical failures, recurring blackouts, political pressure, and legal challenges – led Aneel to consider forfeiture as a possible measure to guarantee service continuity and quality.

## Two Phases

The Italian multinational Enel supplies energy to 14 million customers in 274 Brazilian cities. It also operates in generation with 27 hydroelectric plants, 14 wind energy parks, and seven solar parks in the country. The Italian government holds a 29% stake in the company.

Enel assumed the electrical energy concession for the São Paulo metropolitan region in 2018 by acquiring 73.38% of AES Eletropaulo's shares for R$ 5.55 billion in an auction held at B3. The acquired concession drew attention for being the largest in Latin America, with almost 8 million consumer units and approximately 18 million people in one of the continent's most densely populated urban regions.

Strictly speaking, Enel's management in São Paulo can be divided into two parts. The first, between 2018 and 2023, was marked by reduction in investments, with cuts to much of the former operator's staff, failing to meet Aneel's minimum quality targets in 2021 and 2022. From 2023 onwards, however, the company paid the price for the negligence of previous years by suffering repeated extreme weather phenomena passing through São Paulo since then – intense storms, including an extratropical cyclone in December last year, which knocked down trees and poles – which only reinforced its bad reputation.

Enel São Paulo has already accumulated R$ 374 million in fines applied by Aneel and Arsesp since 2020, but has paid only R$ 29 million – about 8% of the total. The remainder, approximately R$ 345 million, is in litigation or under appeal.

For Rosemeire Costa, president of the National Council of Electric Energy Consumers (Conacen) – a non-profit association representing consumers in concession areas – the consumer is the ultimate beneficiary of Aneel's strict oversight.
