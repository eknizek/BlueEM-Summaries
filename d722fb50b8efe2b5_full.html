# Sabesp seeks to transform Emae into subsidiary with purchase of remaining shares

Sabesp informed investors Friday evening (April 24) that it is evaluating purchasing all remaining shares of Emae and transforming it into a subsidiary. Emae operates in energy generation and water resource management in São Paulo state.

Currently, Sabesp holds 98.04% of Emae's ordinary shares, which carry voting rights. The sanitation company began the process of assuming control of Emae in October of last year when it acquired 74.9% of the ordinary shares held by Phoenix Água e Energia, owned by businessman Nelson Tanure. On that occasion, Sabesp also purchased 66.8% of the preferred shares held by Eletrobras, now Axia Energia. The entire transaction cost R$ 1.13 billion.

The acquisitions, made amid a dispute between debenture holders and Phoenix Água e Energia, were questioned by Tanure. However, in January, Cade (Administrative Council for Economic Defense) and Aneel (National Electric Energy Agency) approved the purchase.

In the statement released to investors Friday, Sabesp informed that, if the incorporation of the shares is approved by the administrative bodies of the companies and their shareholders, holders of Emae shares will receive, in exchange, Sabesp shares, according to an exchange ratio still to be defined.

"The intended incorporation of shares aims at simplifying and optimizing the corporate structure of the companies, consolidating their shareholding bases into a single company and reducing operational costs," the statement says.

A remnant of the Eletropaulo privatization, Emae was the last energy company managed by the São Paulo state government. The company operates a hydraulic system and electric power generator located in São Paulo state, consisting of reservoirs, canals, and plants. Its infrastructure includes the Guarapiranga and Billings reservoirs, which supply a portion of the water provided by Sabesp. The company has five power generation plants, three of which are hydroelectric, one small hydroelectric, and one photovoltaic. Altogether, these plants have the capacity to generate 961 MW (megawatts)—the largest being the Henry Borden complex with 889 MW.
