# Bradesco Seguros uses 'creative engineering' in pension plans without focus on retirement or succession

Bradesco Vida e Previdência has launched a product called "Proteção a Dois" (Protection for Two), which combines death and disability coverage with financial reserve accumulation for two people in a single contract. Unlike traditional PGBL/VGBL plans, it is not designed for retirement income, but rather to protect policyholders in case of claims, functioning more like insurance. It allows withdrawals at any time with fixed returns of IPCA + 2.5%.

The product is more affordable, costing 30% to 40% less than contracting two separate life insurance policies individually. It targets childless couples, which represent 24% of Brazilian households, as well as other relationships with mutual financial dependence, such as small business partners.

Private pension plan gross collections fell R$ 157 billion over 12 months through January, according to Fenaprevi data, following regulatory changes to VGBL products. Bradesco is seeking to diversify its pension offerings, moving away from the exclusive association with retirement and succession planning, positioning it instead as a tool for various medium and long-term financial objectives.

According to Estevão Scripilliti, director of Bradesco Vida e Previdência, the market today is heavily concentrated in accumulation products with more financial than actuarial characteristics, like PGBLs and VGBLs. However, in the 1980s and 1990s, pension products had more of a risk coverage focus, aimed at death benefits and pensions.

"Our intention is to increasingly complement PGBLs and VGBLs with other solutions within pensions. Risk coverages with the most creative engineering possible to meet needs that go beyond retirement and succession," the executive states.

The product targets a broader audience than the classic image of couples with children planning retirement or succession. It specifically targets DINKs—double income, no kids—a profile that is growing in Brazil. According to the latest Census released by IBGE, childless couples represented 24% of Brazilian households, or 13.9 million families, and have become the fastest-growing household composition since 2000.

"If we can capture 10% of this addressable market, that would already be significant. And we still believe this family profile can grow over time," Scripilliti affirms.

The pension plan extends beyond couples to any relationship of mutual financial dependence, such as parents and children, siblings, friends, and primarily small businesses with two partners.

The launch is also a response to market pressure to find new sources of growth at a time when VGBL has lost momentum—representing about 90% of the pension market. Following a 5% IOF tax on large VGBL contributions, the industry has entered "monument robbery mode," with competition centered more on portability and migration of existing assets rather than new contributions.

Bradesco understands that Proteção a Dois will not offset VGBL losses in the short term, as that is a mature product with substantial capital reserves. However, it represents the beginning of a diversification strategy the insurance company wants to pursue.
