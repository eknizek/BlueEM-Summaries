# Bradesco Seguros Uses 'Creative Engineering' in Pension Plans Without Focus on Retirement or Succession

Private pension plans have always been known for their tax advantages in accumulating resources for retirement. However, following regulatory changes in VGBL, gross collections in the sector fell by R$ 157 billion in 12 months through January, according to Fenaprevi data. Looking for a new avenue for growth following these changes, Bradesco Vida e Previdência is launching a pension product called Proteção a Dois.

It combines death coverage with financial reserve formation for two people within a single contract. Unlike PGBL/VGBLs, the proposal is not retirement income, but rather to protect policyholders in case of loss, functioning more like insurance and not requiring all accumulated value to be withdrawn for protection. At the same time, it offers the possibility of withdrawing accumulated value at any time, according to the contracted plan rules. Accumulation contributions can be made monthly or in a lump sum. The product is not tied to investment funds and has fixed returns of IPCA+2.5%.

"The beauty of the product is that it is about 30% to 40% cheaper than individually contracting two separate life insurance policies where one covers the other. Plus it also builds up a financial reserve like a rescindable insurance," says Estevão Scripilliti, director of Bradesco Vida e Previdência.

The structure departs from the traditional model that dominates the sector. According to Scripilliti, the pension market today is very concentrated in accumulation products with more financial than actuarial characteristics, like PGBLs and VGBLs. But according to him, it wasn't always this way. In the 1980s and 1990s, pensions had more of a risk coverage appearance, with products focused on death benefits and pension payments. Only later did the market concentrate on accumulation plans.

"Our intention is to increasingly complement PGBLs and VGBLs with other solution possibilities within pensions. Risk coverages, with the most creative engineering possible, to meet needs that go beyond retirement and succession," says the executive.

The product serves a broader audience than the classic image of couples with children who plan to retire or have succession focus that marked much of private pension expansion in Brazil in recent years. It targets what in the United States is called DINK, an acronym for double income, no kids — couples without children. A profile that is growing in the country.

Data from the latest Census released by IBGE show that couples without children represented 24% of Brazilian households, or 13.9 million families, and became the family composition that has grown the most since 2000. "If we can capture 10% of this addressable market, that would already be quite something. And we still believe this family profile can grow over time," affirms Scripilliti.

The pension plan goes beyond couples to any relationship of mutual financial dependence, such as parents and children, siblings, friends, and especially small businesses with two partners.

At the same time, the launch is a response to a market need to find new originations at a time when VGBL has lost momentum — since it represents about 90% of the pension market. As NeoFeed showed, the 5% IOF tax on large VGBL contributions interrupted the industry's expansion cycle and put pensions in "robbing Peter to pay Paul" mode, with competition more focused on portability and migration of existing assets.

Bradesco understands that Proteção a Dois will not compensate for VGBL losses in the short term, a mature product with substantial capital reserves. But it is the beginning of a diversification that the insurer wants to pursue.
