# Bradesco Seguros Uses "Creative Engineering" in Pension Plans Without Focus on Retirement or Succession

Bradesco Vida e Previdência launched the product "Proteção a Dois" (Protection for Two), which combines death and disability coverage with financial reserve formation for two people in a single contract. Unlike PGBL/VGBL plans, it is not intended for retirement, but offers protection in case of loss and allows withdrawals at any time, with fixed returns of IPCA + 2.5%. The product is more accessible, being 30% to 40% cheaper than contracting two life insurance policies separately. It aims to serve childless couples, representing 24% of Brazilian households, as well as other financial dependency relationships, such as partners in small businesses. Bradesco is seeking to diversify its pension offerings, moving away from exclusive association with retirement and succession, and positioning it as a tool for various financial objectives of medium and long-term duration.

Private pension plans have always been known for their tax differential advantage when accumulating resources for retirement. However, after regulatory changes in VGBL, gross collections in the sector fell R$ 157 billion in 12 months through January, according to Fenaprevi data. Looking for a new avenue for growth after these changes, Bradesco Vida e Previdência is launching a pension product called Proteção a Dois. It combines death coverage with financial reserve formation for two people within a single contract. Unlike PGBL/VGBLs, the proposal is not retirement income, but to protect policyholders in case of loss, functioning more like insurance and not requiring the entire accumulated value to withdraw the protection amount. At the same time, it has the possibility of withdrawing the accumulated value at any time, according to the plan's rules. Accumulation contributions can be made monthly or as a lump sum. The product is not tied to investment funds and its returns are fixed at IPCA+2.5%.

"The beauty of the product is that it is about 30% to 40% cheaper than individually contracting two life insurance policies, where one covers the other. In addition to also forming a financial reserve like a redeemable insurance," says Estevão Scripilliti, director of Bradesco Vida e Previdência. The structure departs from the traditional model that dominates the industry. According to Scripilliti, the pension market today is heavily concentrated in accumulation products with more financial than actuarial characteristics, such as PGBLs and VGBLs. But according to him, it wasn't always this way. In the 1980s and 1990s, pensions had more of a risk coverage appearance, with products aimed at paying benefits and pensions. It was only later that the market began to focus on accumulation plans.

"Our intention is increasingly to complement PGBLs and VGBLs with other solution possibilities within pensions. Risk coverages, with the most creative engineering possible, to meet needs that go beyond retirement and succession," says the executive. The product serves a broader audience than the classic image of couples with children who intend to retire or have a succession focus that marked much of the expansion of private pensions in Brazil in recent years. It targets what in the United States is called DINK, an acronym for double income, no kids, which are childless couples. A profile that has been growing in the country. Data from the latest Census released by IBGE show that childless couples represented 24% of Brazilian households, or 13.9 million families, and became the composition that grew the most since 2000.

"If we can get 10% of this addressable market, it would already be quite significant. And we still believe that this family profile can grow over time," Scripilliti says. The pension plan goes beyond couples to any relationship of mutual financial dependency, such as parents and children, siblings, friends, and especially small businesses with two partners. At the same time, the launch is a response to a market need to find new originations at a time when VGBL has lost momentum — since it accounts for about 90% of the pension market. As NeoFeed showed, the 5% IOF taxation on large VGBL contributions interrupted the cycle of industry expansion and put pensions in "robbing from Peter to pay Paul" mode, with competition more focused on portability and migration of existing assets. Bradesco understands that Proteção a Dois will not compensate for VGBL losses in the short term, an already mature product with significant capital reserves. But it is the beginning of a diversification that the insurer wants to undertake.
