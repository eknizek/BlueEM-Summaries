# Brazil-Mexico Football 'Legends' Game Tests Cashless System Ahead of World Cup

The match bringing together football "legends" from Brazil and Mexico, scheduled for this Sunday (19th), will be the acid test for the cashless system at Banorte Stadium (formerly Azteca Stadium) ahead of the 2026 World Cup, according to one of the companies behind the new cash-free payment ecosystem.

The stadium's reopening, at the friendly match between Mexico and Portugal on March 28th, drew more than 81,000 people and caused a network overload that temporarily prevented card payments and forced the venue to resume accepting cash for food and beverage purchases.

The explanation comes from Arturo Halgraves, CEO of Puntored in the country, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea. The executive mentioned that at subsequent matches, such as Club América against Cruz Azul, with 46,000 fans, there were no complications. But the game that will bring together famous former players like Ronaldinho Gaúcho, Kaká, Adriano, Lúcio, Júlio César and Djalminha, as well as Mexicans Cuauhtémoc Blanco, Rafael Márquez and Antonio Naelson, "Sinha", is expected to attract nearly double the crowd.

"On Sunday we have a Mexico-Brazil match that will also be full. The expectation is for more than 80,000 spectators. So this could be the acid test," Halgraves said.

The executive stated that the card payment intermittency recorded in March was resolved and expressed confidence that something similar will not happen again.

In a few weeks, the former Azteca Stadium will host five matches of the 2026 FIFA World Cup, including the tournament's opening ceremony on June 11th.

## From Cash to Card

Puntored is a Colombian fintech that, in partnership with the KeepCash platform, is behind the infrastructure of the newly reformed stadium's cashless ecosystem, where Banorte acts as the acquiring bank. The company also provides a prepaid card in partnership with the Mexican bank, which allows fans without digital payment methods — such as credit or debit cards — to deposit cash onto the card, with an initial value of 300 Mexican pesos (approximately R$ 86), to use for purchases.

The cards can be reloaded, and remaining balance cannot be withdrawn in cash, but can be used on a future stadium visit or at partner commercial establishments.

Halgraves stated that the implementation of the cashless system streamlined operations within the stadium for both fans and food and beer vendors — who traditionally circulated through the stands with cash between their fingers and now carry payment terminals.

"They had to constantly close out their registers, go to specific deposits, count the money and only then could they return to selling," the executive explained. "By eliminating all of that, you avoid discrepancies in those sales and also allow them to sell much more — which is good for everyone."

Revenue is also growing. Halgraves revealed that at the Mexico-Portugal match, Banorte Stadium recorded a record food and beverage sales of 15 million Mexican pesos (approximately R$ 4.3 million) — a 50% increase from the previous maximum.
