Itaú reports R$12.3 billion Q1 profit with 24.8% return on equity, maintains guidance

06 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

Itaú Unibanco (ITUB4) closed the first quarter of 2026 with recurring managerial profit of R$12.3 billion, up 10.4% over 12 months, and return on equity (ROE) of 24.8% on a consolidated basis, a level that remains among the highest in the global banking system. Itaú maintained its unchanged projections (guidance) for 2026, which forecast total credit portfolio growth between 5.5% and 9.5%, expansion of customer financial margin between 5.0% and 9.0%, credit cost between R$38.5 billion and R$43.5 billion, and growth in non-interest expenses between 1.5% and 5.5%.

Compared to the previous quarter, however, results were practically stable, with a slight decline of 0.3%, reflecting the early distribution of dividends made at the end of 2025. Adjusted for this effect, quarterly growth would have been 3.2%.

The credit portfolio reached R$1.5 trillion, with expansion of 9.0% over 12 months excluding currency effects. The highlight was residential credit for individuals (+11.2%), private payroll-deductible loans, which grew 63% in the period, and credit cards (+8.2%). In the corporate segment, growth was driven by government program lines targeting micro, small and medium-sized enterprises, precisely the segment showing the most sensitive delinquency normalization cycle.

"We begin 2026 in a scenario that requires caution and discipline in credit," said Milton Maluhy Filho, the bank's CEO, in a statement. "In recent cycles, we have anticipated adjustments to protect our customers in the most complex moments. It is this preventive vision that gives us confidence today to continue supporting families and businesses," he added.

Delinquency above 90 days remained at 1.9% for the fifth consecutive quarter, a stability celebrated by the bank as proof of origination quality. However, there were distinct movements by segment. In Brazil, the micro, small and medium-sized enterprise delinquency indicator rose to 1.9%, an increase of 0.1 percentage point in the quarter and over 12 months, amid the end of grace periods for government programs—the same segment the bank pointed to as the driver of portfolio growth in the period.

Short-term delinquency, from 15 to 90 days, also showed signs of pressure: the consolidated index advanced 0.1 percentage point in the quarter, and that for individuals in Brazil jumped 0.23 percentage point, closing at 3.0%. The bank attributes the movement to typical seasonality at the beginning of the year, when families concentrate their spending, combined with a calendar with fewer business days in the quarter. Since much of the bank's financial margin is accrued on a daily basis (interest accrues daily on balances), shorter quarters mechanically yield less revenue without any change in the business itself.

NPL Creation, which measures the entry of new delinquent credits, rose 25% over 12 months, from R$7.7 billion to R$9.7 billion. In the same period, write-offs—the accounting deduction of credits considered unrecoverable—grew at practically the same rate, reaching R$9.7 billion in the quarter, up 17.4% from the fourth quarter. Since write-offs remove problematic credits from the active portfolio, part of the stability of the aggregate indicator is mechanical rather than operational.

In Brazilian retail, short-term delinquency (15 to 90 days) for individuals advanced 0.23 percentage point in the quarter.

Customer financial margin grew 4.5% over 12 months, sustained by portfolio growth and better product mix. Looking at the fourth quarter, the margin was nearly stable (-0.7%), from R$31.7 billion to R$31.5 billion, with calendar effects weighing on it. Market margin declined 11.2% in the annual comparison, pressured by higher hedge costs for the capital index. Service and insurance revenues rose 5.3% year-on-year, with insurance advancing 17.2%, but recorded a 5.7% decline in the quarter, in a typical seasonal movement at the beginning of the year.

On the expense side, the efficiency ratio in Brazil reached 34.9%, the best level in history for a first quarter. Non-interest expenses totaled R$16.2 billion, up 4.8% over 12 months, driven by technology and the effects of collective labor agreements. The bank closed 162 branches in the quarter and reduced headcount by 925 employees.

"The results reflect consistent execution of the bank's strategy," highlighted Gabriel Amado de Moura, the institution's CFO. "We maintained comfortable levels of capital and liquidity, supported by rigorous risk management."

The core capital ratio (CET1), the safety cushion against losses, fell to 12.0%, compared to 12.3% in December and 12.6% a year earlier, at the lowest level in a year. The Basel ratio declined to 14.8%, from 15.7% in the same period of 2025. The reduction is partly explained by regulatory changes related to credit and operational risks, but also by interest payments on equity and share buybacks.
