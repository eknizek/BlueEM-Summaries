# Squadra Criticizes Hapvida, Suggests Board Changes and Southern Divestiture

Squadra Investimentos intensified its criticism of Hapvida and formalized a movement to attempt to reconfigure the company's board of directors at the assembly scheduled for April 30. In a letter sent this Tuesday, the asset manager — which holds 6.98% of voting capital — requested the adoption of multiple voting and nominated three names for the board, in a clear signal of dissatisfaction with business management and, above all, with the company's governance.

The document is harsh. Squadra states that Hapvida has undergone "one of the greatest destructions of value in the history of the Brazilian capital market" since its IPO in 2018. During this period, the stock fell 85%, while the Ibovespa rose approximately 120%.

The central criticism concerns capital allocation decisions and execution. The asset manager points out that significant acquisitions diluted exposure to the original business and that asset integration — especially following the merger with NotreDame Intermédica — was poorly executed, with an estimated loss of value of R$ 80 billion.

There are also questions about recent operational deterioration, even in a context of sector recovery, in addition to increased leverage and worsening risk perception of the company, reflected in debt spreads that reached CDI + 9% per year. The letter also highlights episodes that, in Squadra's view, evidence governance failures — such as the restatement of financial statements, late recognition of regulatory liabilities, and loss of beneficiaries in regions where the market grew.

Another sensitive point is compensation. The asset manager criticizes both the CEO's package — which totaled approximately R$ 110 million in 2023 and 2024 — and the board's incentive structure, which would have received high bonuses despite the destruction of value. For the 2026 assembly, the administration proposed the complete reelection of all nine board members, without renewal, as well as the reduction of independent members. The compensation projected for the board is R$ 57 million — approximately 20% of estimated profit for the year and one of the highest on the Ibovespa in relative terms.

In Squadra's assessment, the sum of these proposals reveals a board "out of step" with the company's financial situation.

The asset manager also questions the succession process underway, which provides for the migration of the current CEO — a member of the controlling family — to a leadership position on the board with executive functions.

Beyond the governance agenda, the letter brings a strategic thesis: the need to simplify the business. Squadra suggests that Hapvida evaluate divestitures, especially in Southeast and South operations, where the turnaround has not advanced. The sale of these assets, according to the asset manager, could reduce leverage, unlock value, and allow greater focus on more profitable operations.

To enable the change, Squadra nominated three independent candidates to the board: Tania Chocolat, Bruno Magalhães, and Eduardo Parente. According to the asset manager, the trio brings together experience in capital allocation, restructuring, and governance — competencies considered critical for the company's current moment.

In the end, the letter makes clear that the dispute will be public. Squadra requests immediate disclosure of the document to the market and states that it will also make the content available to its investors. In practice, it is the beginning of a battle for influence within one of the country's largest health operators — and a relevant test for minority shareholder engagement in Brazil.
