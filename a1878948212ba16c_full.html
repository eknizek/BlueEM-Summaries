# Gonzalo Said on Daughter's Death: "Faith Helps You Learn to Live with Grief"

Gonzalo Said, president of Embotelladora Andina, vice president of Sofofa, and director of the Grupo de Empresas Said Handal holding company, says that as a businessman two ideas compel him: achieving unity and maintaining a common vision. Sitting in the spacious meeting room on the eighth floor of a Las Condes building where his family office operates, he explains that the important thing to face this challenge is not to remain in discourse, "because words are carried away by the wind," but to convert it into action, into a concrete way of operating.

Enthusiastic about the topic, he says: "That's something you build. What matters most there is corporate governance, because that's where you set the tone, define how things are done. And that builds the culture from the top down. And there's another factor you always need to keep in mind: when you're making decisions, you must put people at the center. The human dimension is important. You sometimes get stuck on strategy, on the numbers, but what happens? All of that within the company is managed by people. You can't forget that. Less PowerPoint and more power reality: walk around the company, greet people, see what's happening."

"You've even spoken about the role affection plays in companies..."

"Undoubtedly. And I think there's a lack of knowledge on both sides, both from businessmen and from society. That creates prejudices that we need to break down. Businessmen should let ourselves be known better, because ultimately we're human beings, we have the same common problems, with our children, with school, with tutors."

"That's probably why many say you're an approachable, human businessman. Have you worked on that or does it come naturally?"

"I'd say the latter. It's something inherent to the personality you have and also the way my parents raised me."

## "I'm Truly Grateful"

Two years ago, in April 2024, his eldest daughter, Carolina, died. She lived in Seattle, United States, with her husband and young daughter. She was 32 years old and seven months pregnant. She died while sleeping. "For me, in that moment it was like the world stopped," says Gonzalo Said, her father, emotional. He was in Mexico with Francisca Llona, his partner of 16 years, one of his children who lived there, and his sister Marisol. They had attended a nephew's wedding. From there, the four of them traveled as quickly as they could to Seattle. He says he had one objective in mind: to bring his daughter back to Chile. "I felt that as my mission."

"When what happened to your daughter occurred, you had the chance to receive that affection and empathy that you've defended so much in the business world. What did you feel?"

"It was impressive solidarity. There were many calls offering help. In that moment there were no barriers, no ranks or anything; just people accompanying you. And when I got here, to Santiago, there were many people at the funeral, who accompanied me, some talking to me, others in silence. All of that moved me deeply and I'm truly grateful. That's why I wrote the column 'Thank You' just days after my daughter's death."

"Did you feel accompanied in Seattle as well?"

"Yes. All the Chilean friends who were studying there with my daughter, who was in that city with her husband and my granddaughter, functioned as if they were family. Impressive. During those days when this happened they took turns bringing breakfast, others lunch, others dinner, others taking my granddaughter for walks, while we were dealing with all the paperwork. I didn't know them well. I'd been there a couple of times, knew some of them."

"In your column 'Thank You' you wrote: 'Facing this tragedy we were never alone.'"

"We were never alone. Afterward, people came to see me at the office, they went to the house. What I always did with people was talk about this, about what had happened. That's very healthy."

"Difficult and painful too..."

"You put on a shell, because you say: 'I suffered so much, I don't want to suffer anymore.' But you have to dare to open up and be able to share it, because besides, you help others. Furthermore, my family had already lived through something like this. I'm the youngest of six siblings, but we were seven. Right between the three oldest and three youngest, a sister died, Cecilia. She was 3 years old, from pneumonia. I didn't get to know her, but I saw how my father continued to remember her, had her on his nightstand, traveled with a photo of her. At the same time he kept working, kept moving forward."

## "Life Doesn't End Here"

"In your column you said faith was another support you had to face this hard blow. How did it help you?"

"I was raised in an environment of faith, by my father and mother. Not just Sunday mass, but faith lived and felt."

"But how did it help in the specific case of your daughter's death?"

"Faith doesn't answer the question of why this happened... So how did faith help me? In not continuing to ask myself why. I stopped. I didn't want to keep investigating anything. I stopped questioning myself what would have happened if I'd done this or that, because if I hadn't, I would have entered into depression. Faith makes you say that these are the mysteries of life; and besides it makes you know that she is in a better place than where we are, since we're only passing through here. Life doesn't end here, there's an afterlife."

"There's a quote from Saint Paul that you say resonated with you in those days: 'When I am weak, then I am strong.'"

"Yes. That's about lowering your guard and accepting. Saying, with humility, that this happened to me."

"And convincing yourself also that, despite everything, life goes on?"

"Exactly. I said: I have more family, children, grandchildren, and I need to be well."

## "Without Getting Angry, Without Questioning"

"What do you remember about those days?"

"I remember thinking: 'I'm not going to get angry at life, I'm not going to get angry at God, I'm a man of faith. There are things that have no explanation, these are life's mysteries.' And from then on I never got angry, never complained. I resigned myself."

"After two years of grief, how do you live and feel this loss today?"

"The sorrow never goes away, it's always there. You learn to live with the sorrow, faith helps you. Also letting go, letting it go. I think one of the things I did was let go quickly. Without getting angry, without questioning. On the other hand, I'm grateful. There were 32 years of her life here. She lived it fully. I did everything with her, there were no loose ends. And the other thing that also sustains me and that I'm grateful for is the immense gift she left me, which is my granddaughter."

"Do you find something like consolation? Or is it about learning to live, however you can, with that wound?"

"The wound never heals. There's no consolation in the world that heals that wound. You're left with an open wound, but it transforms: at first into pain, then you carry it as a companion and you learn to manage it. But in these things there's no recipe, each person has their own way of living their grief. There are people who get stuck, who don't get over it and obviously it affects them psychologically. That's why I said: 'I'm not going to get sick, there's nothing more I can do.' If you look rationally at what happened, it doesn't make sense, it's unnatural for a father to have to bury a child, but from faith, as I was telling you, these are life's mysteries..."
