Gonzalo Said and the death of his daughter: 'You learn to live with the sorrow, faith helps you'

25 April 2026 · 01:51 AM

# Gonzalo Said and the Death of His Daughter: "You Learn to Live with Sorrow, Faith Helps You"

Gonzalo Said, president of Embotelladora Andina, vice president of Sofofa, and director of the Grupo de Empresas Said Handal holding company, says that as a businessman, two ideas challenge him: achieving unity and having a common vision. Sitting in the spacious meeting room on the eighth floor of a building in Las Condes, where his family office operates, he explains that the key to facing this challenge is not to remain in discourse, "because words are carried away by the wind," but to convert it into action, into a concrete way of acting.

Enthusiastically addressing the topic, he says: "That is something that is built. What is most relevant there is corporate governance of the company, because that is where you set the tone, define the way of doing things. And that builds the culture downward. And there is another factor you must always keep in mind: when you are making decisions, you must put the person at the center. The human dimension is important. One sometimes gets stuck in strategy, in the numbers; but what happens? All of that within the company is managed by people. You must not forget that. Less PowerPoint and more power reality: walk through the company, greet people, see what is happening."

"You have even gone further and spoken about the role that affection plays in companies…"

"Without a doubt. And I believe there is a lack of understanding on both sides, both from businesspeople and from society. That creates prejudices that we must break down. As businesspeople, we should make ourselves better known, because ultimately we are human beings with the same common problems—with our children, with school, with the educational psychologist."

"That is probably why many agree that you are an approachable, humane businessman. Have you worked on this or does it come naturally?"

"I would say the latter. It is something inherent to my personality and also how my parents raised me."

## "I am truly grateful"

Two years ago, in April 2024, his eldest daughter, Carolina, died. She lived in Seattle, United States, with her husband and young daughter. She was 32 years old and seven months pregnant. She died in her sleep.

"For me, at that moment it was like the world stopped," says Gonzalo Said, her father, visibly moved. He was in Mexico with Francisca Llona, his partner of 16 years, one of his sons who lived there, and his sister Marisol. They had attended the wedding of a nephew. From there, the four of them traveled as quickly as possible to Seattle. He says he had one objective in mind: to bring his daughter back to Chile. "I felt that as my mission."

"When your daughter's situation occurred, you had the opportunity to receive that affection and empathy that you have so defended in the business world. What did you feel?"

"It was impressive solidarity. There were many calls offering help. In that moment there were no barriers, no titles or anything; just people accompanying you. And when I got here to Santiago, many people came to the funeral, they accompanied me, some talking to me, others in silence. All of that moved me deeply and I am truly grateful. That is why I wrote the column 'Thanks' a few days after my daughter's death."

"Did you feel accompanied in Seattle as well?"

"Yes. All the Chilean friends who were studying there with my daughter, who was in that city with her husband and my granddaughter, functioned as if they were a family. Impressive. Those days, they took turns bringing breakfast, others lunch, others dinner, others taking my granddaughter for walks, while we were dealing with all the procedures. I didn't know them well. I had gone a couple of times, knew some of them."

"In your column 'Thanks,' you wrote: 'Facing this tragedy, we were never alone.'"

"We were never alone. Afterward, people came to see me at the office, they came to the house. What I always did with people was to talk about this, about what had happened. That is very healthy."

"Difficult and painful too…"

"You put on a shell, because you say: 'I suffered so much, that I don't want to suffer more.' But you must dare to open up and be able to share it, because besides, you help others. Additionally, my family had already lived through something like this. I am the youngest of six siblings, but we were seven. Between the three eldest and the three youngest, a sister, Cecilia, died. At 3 years old, from pneumonia. I didn't get to know her, but I did see how my father continued to remember her, kept her on his nightstand, traveled with a photo of her. At the same time, he continued working, he continued forward."

## "Life does not end here"

"In your column, you said that faith was another support you had to face this hard blow. How did it help you?"

"I was raised in an environment of faith, by my father and mother. Not just Sunday mass, but lived and felt faith."

"But how did it help you in the specific case of your daughter's death?"

"Faith doesn't answer the question of why this happened… So, how did faith help me? In not continuing to ask myself why. I stopped. I didn't want to keep investigating anything. I stopped questioning myself about what would have happened if I had done this or that, because if I didn't, I was going to fall into depression. Faith makes you say that these are the mysteries of life; and besides, it makes you know that she is in any case in a better place than we are, because here we are just passing through. Life does not end here, there is an afterlife."

"There is a quote from Saint Paul that you say resonated with you those days: 'When I am weak, then I am powerful.'"

"Yes. That is lowering your guard and accepting. Saying, with humility, that this happened to me."

"And convincing yourself too that, despite everything, life continues?"

"Exactly. I said: I have more family, children, grandchildren, and I have to be well."

## "Without getting angry, without questioning"

"What do you remember from those days?"

"I remember thinking: 'I am not going to get angry with life, I am not going to get angry with God, I am a man of faith. There are things that have no explanation, these are the mysteries of life.' And from there, I never got angry, never complained. I accepted it."

"After two years of grief, how do you live and feel this loss today?"

"The sorrow never goes away, it is always there. You learn to live with the sorrow, faith helps you. Also, to let go, to release. I think one of the things I did was let go quickly. Without getting angry, without questioning. On the other hand, I am grateful. There were 32 years of her life here. She lived it to the fullest. I did everything with her, nothing was left pending. And the other thing that also sustains me and for which I am grateful is the immense gift she left me, which is my granddaughter."

"Do you find something close to consolation? Or is it about learning to live, however you can, with that wound?"

"The wound never heals. There is no consolation in the world that will heal that wound. You are left with an open wound, but it transforms: at first into pain, then you carry it as companionship and you learn to manage it. But in these matters there is no recipe, each person has their own way of living their grief. There are people who get stuck, who don't overcome it and obviously it affects them psychologically. That is why I said: 'I am not going to get sick, there is nothing more I can do.' If you look rationally at what happened, it makes no sense, it is unnatural for a father to have to bury a son, but from faith, as I told you, these are the mysteries of life."
