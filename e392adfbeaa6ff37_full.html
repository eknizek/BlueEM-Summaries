Oscar Lería, Maratué Founder: 'This Is a Project for the Middle Class, the Great Forgotten'

03 May 2026 · 01:02 AM

# Oscar Lería, Maratué Founder: "This Is a Project for the Middle Class, the Great Forgotten"

More than a week ago, businessman Óscar Lería Chateau published a video on Instagram and, as he himself explains, it was the first time. "I've wanted to be there for a while. Upon realizing that we have to coexist with fake news, lies, misinformation. It doesn't seem fair to me. One has the right to say things as they are. And the only way was to do it on a personal basis," he explains.

In his post, he discusses Maratué, the mega real estate project that he leads with his children in the municipality of Puchuncaví, and which involves an investment of US$ 2 billion to build approximately 14,000 homes over some 40 years on a plot of about 1,024 hectares located between Maitencillo and Horcón, in the Valparaíso Region, in an area described as urban according to territorial regulations.

The project, the largest real estate initiative under development in Chile, received approval on Monday, April 13, when it was approved by the Ministers' Committee, which enables its implementation after a decade of environmental processing. Aware that there are groups that will persist in their rejection of the initiative and that will likely resort to legal proceedings, Lería details that he plans to make five Instagram capsules explaining what happens with the cliffs, the belloto trees, etc.

"One has to step up and face the music. I don't want to send anyone to say what we're going to do. We've handled this very personally. My daughter Claudia and I have been personally with the Housing Committees, which are very relevant to me. Our project has been approved by three administrations, Piñera, Boric, and now Kast, in the Ministers' Committee. So when you hear opponents saying that everything is wrong, you become convinced that this is a political issue, not a technical one. And it's regrettable. Because when I listen to Salvemos Quirilluca, we think exactly the same way, we want to protect the same things, the cliffs, and we're doing it. They're worried about the cormorants and we're also very concerned about the cormorants. We did tremendous work and we moved 1,500 meters away from the coast. The wetlands: we said we would protect them, long before they were declared by law. We have 50% green space," says Óscar Lería in this interview granted along with one of his daughters, Claudia Lería Scarneo (CL), the executive director of Maratué.

Here they explain that the project will nevertheless proceed, because any legal action does not invalidate the Environmental Qualification Resolution (RCA).

"This is a first-home project for the middle class, the great forgotten ones of this country. And it has subsidizable homes, because I firmly believe in social integration. And the second home is also middle class. Toward Horcón, you find illegal subdivisions, sale of rights, plots without water and sewage. A disaster. And people who have money, Guanay, Maitencillo, Cachagua, Zapallar, those are three guys in this country who can do it. What does a middle-class person do if they want to vacation on the central coast? Where do they go? The Valparaíso Region has nowhere to grow. There's a deficit of 85,000 homes. We have Norman Foster, who are architects for high-end segments, now in a project for the middle class and for people who have even fewer resources, like our housing committees. I'm over 70 years old, I'm turning 74, I have nothing to lose. People have the right to know the truth. My father was a worker, he didn't have a thing. The most beautiful thing about Maratué is that we're going to give people without resources a standard of living as it should be, with a nature sanctuary, with a public beach where they're not segregated. This is for everyone."

**"We didn't want anyone to come and tell us 'we have to squeeze the lemon more'"**

**Is this the legacy you want to leave?**

No. That would be very presumptuous. The legacy that I have worked on and wanted to leave is that my children understand and care about those who have the least. Never look down on anyone and be a contribution by doing business for this country. Ten years ago when we talked about social integration, many of my peers didn't understand what we were doing. We don't have a single partner. We did it ourselves, because we didn't want anyone to come and tell us "we have to squeeze the lemon more."

Lería reports that they have already invested about US$ 50 million, financed so far with a mix of their own resources and banks. They financed, for example, the arrival of water at a cost of 7 billion pesos, which "the family paid," says Lería, for the first 4,000 homes.

They just met at Norman Foster's office in Miami to kick off the second phase of their project, which corresponds to the definition of structural roads, defining the roadways, explains Claudia Lería, to be able to subdivide the urbanization, and thus start making budgets, where the different neighborhoods will go, and look for partners. In this phase they are working with three specialized firms: with Alejandro Pizarro Asociados (APA), from Concepción; with Francisco and Pablo Allard, in territorial architecture and landscaping; and with Pablo Larraín and Klaus Benkel, the architects who make "the implementation" of the Norman Foster team.

"We expect in 90 days to have a Gantt chart with the timeline, to be able to start sector permits (...) We want to start yesterday," add the Lería family.

The project includes housing niches and in the schedule they expect that in the first quarter of 2028 there will be the groundbreaking, starting with social housing, "because they don't have a home," emphasizes Óscar Lería, explaining that he knows and has participated with the housing committees.

"I know the neighborhood. I used to vacation in Quintero when we didn't have anything, in a house without hot water. I know Los Enamorados, Papagayo, El Durazno, and Loncura beaches. We know their needs and clearly they've been waiting a long time," he adds.

**What is the business model you settled on?**

It's always been said that we were going to look for a partner. We came to the conclusion that the urgency that this country has in housing deficit deserves that we also give it urgency. And so, to speed up the process, what we're going to do is partner with different companies in different niches. We're going to participate with these different real estate companies. If this gets fragmented, I don't see how I'm going to be successful in 45 years. That's why we're going to stay inside the business by participating in the different niches. In 30%, 40%, or 50%. We'll be on the board and we'll have voting rights.

**CL: We decided this a month ago.**

**Did an international partner never arrive?**

**CL: Several arrived.**

But we always had the concern that everyone wants to make some change to improve profitability. And we're focused. It's middle class. At most, on the coast for a second home, 8,000 UF. But the bulk of the project is 4,000 UF. There's no luxury housing. What real estate companies lack is land. And we have the land. The novelty is that we don't want to sell the land, we want to be in the business. And one thing that happened to me with possible partners is that they told me "you can't make subsidizable housing, because on the beach people will mix." Well, precisely so. That's one of the perks.

**Will you also partner in subsidizable housing projects?**

More than in any other. At Constructora Aconcagua I built more than 10,000 houses with projects in Lo Prado and La Florida. We were the first to put in green areas.

**"We're not going to wait"**
