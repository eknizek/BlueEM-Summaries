# Oscar Lería, Maratué Founder: "This Project is for the Middle Class, the Greatly Overlooked"

A little over a week ago, businessman Óscar Lería Chateau published a video on Instagram, and according to him, it was his first time doing so. "I've wanted to be there for a while. Upon realizing that we have to coexist with fake news, lies, misinformation. It doesn't seem fair to me. One has the right to say things as they are. And the only way was to do it in a personal capacity," he explains.

In his post, he talks about Maratué, the mega real estate project that he leads with his children in the municipality of Puchuncaví, which involves an investment of US$ 2 billion to build, over approximately 40 years, some 14,000 homes on a plot of about 1,024 hectares located between Maitencillo and Horcón, in the Valparaíso Region, in an area described as urban according to territorial regulations.

The project, the largest real estate initiative in development in Chile, received approval on Monday, April 13, when it was passed by the Ministers' Committee, making its realization viable after a decade of environmental processing.

Aware that there are groups that will persist in their rejection of the initiative and that will probably resort to legal proceedings, Lería details that he plans to make five Instagram capsules that will explain what's happening with the cliffs, the belloto trees, etc.

"One has to step up and face it. I don't want to send anyone to say what we're going to do. We've handled it in a very personalized way. My daughter Claudia and I have personally met with the Housing Committees, which are very relevant to me. Our project has been approved by three administrations: Piñera, Boric, and now Kast, in the Ministers' Committee. So when you hear opponents saying that everything is wrong, you become convinced that this is a political issue, not a technical one. And it's unfortunate. Because when I listen to Salvemos Quirilluca, we think exactly the same; we want to defend the same thing, the cliffs, and we are doing it. They're worried about the boobies and we're also very concerned about the boobies. We did tremendous work and moved 1,500 meters away from the coast. The wetlands: we said we would protect them, long before they were declared by law. We have 50% green space," says Óscar Lería in this interview conducted with one of his daughters, Claudia Lería Scarneo (CL), the executive director of Maratué. Here they explain that the project will proceed anyway, because any legal action doesn't invalidate the Environmental Qualification Resolution (RCA).

"This is a first-home project for the middle class, the greatly overlooked people of this country. And it has subsidizable homes, because I firmly believe in social integration. And the second home is also middle class. Toward Horcón, you find illegal subdivisions, sale of rights, parcels without water and sewage. A disaster. And people who have money—Guanay, Maitencillo, Cachagua, Zapallar—there are only three such places in this country where they can do it. What does a middle-class person do if they want to spend the summer at the central coast? Where do they go? The Valparaíso Region has nowhere to grow. There's a deficit of 85,000 homes. We have Norman Foster, architects for high-end segments, now on a project for the middle class and for people who have even fewer resources, like our housing committees. I'm over 70 years old, I'll be 74, I have nothing to lose. People have the right to know the truth. My father was a worker; he didn't have a penny. The most beautiful thing about Maratué is that we're going to give people without resources a standard of living as it should be, with a nature sanctuary, with public beaches where they won't be segregated. This is for everyone."

"We didn't want anyone to come and tell us 'we need to squeeze the lemon more'"

**—Is this the legacy you want to leave?**

No. That would be too presumptuous. The legacy I have worked on and wanted to leave is that my children understand and care about those who have the least. Never look down on anyone and be a contribution by doing business for this country. Ten years ago, when we talked about social integration, many of my peers didn't understand what we were doing. We don't have a single partner. We did it ourselves, because we didn't want anyone to come and tell us "we need to squeeze the lemon more."

Lería recounts that they have already invested about US$ 50 million, financed so far with a mix of their own resources and bank financing. For example, they financed the arrival of water, at a cost of 7 billion pesos, which "the family paid," says Lería, for the first 4,000 homes.

They have just met at Norman Foster's office in Miami to launch the second phase of their project, which corresponds to the definition of structural roads and defining roadways, explains Claudia Lería, in order to subdivide the development and thus begin to make budgets, decide where the different neighborhoods will go, and seek partners.

In this phase, they are working with three specialized offices: Alejandro Pizarro Asociados (APA), from Concepción; Francisco and Pablo Allard, in territorial architecture and landscaping; and Pablo Larraín and Klaus Benkel, the architects who carry out the "implementation" of the Norman Foster team.

"We expect in 90 days to have a Gantt chart with the timeline, so we can start sector permits (…) We want to start yesterday," add the Lería family.

The project considers different housing segments and in the schedule they expect that by the first quarter of 2028 there will be a groundbreaking, starting with social housing, "because they don't have homes," emphasizes Óscar Lería, explaining that he knows and has participated with the housing committees.

"I know the neighborhood. I used to spend summers in Quintero when we didn't have a penny, in a house without heating. I know Los Enamorados, Papagayo, El Durazno, and Loncura beaches. We know their needs and clearly they've been waiting a long time," he adds.

**—What business model did you settle on?**

There has always been talk that we would seek a partner. We came to the conclusion that the urgency this country has in housing deficit means we also need to give it urgency. So, to speed up the process, what we're going to do is partner with different companies in different segments. We're going to participate with these different real estate developers. If this gets fragmented, I don't see how I'll have success in 45 years. That's why we're going to stay in the business by participating in the different segments. In 30%, 40%, or 50%. We'll be on the board and we'll have voting rights.

—CL: We decided this a month ago.

**—Did an international partner never come along?**

—CL: Several came.

But we always had the apprehension that everyone wants to make some change to improve profitability. And we're focused. It's middle class. At most, on the coast for a second home, 8,000 UF. But the bulk of the project is 4,000 UF. There's no luxury housing. What real estate developers lack is land. And we have the land. What's new is that we don't want to sell the land; we want to be in the business. And something that happened with possible partners is they told me "you can't build subsidizable housing because on the beach people will mix." Well, precisely that's the point. That's one of the merits.

**—Will you also have partners in the subsidizable housing projects?**

More than any other. At Aconcagua construction company, I built over 10,000 homes with projects in Lo Prado and La Florida. We were the first to put in green areas.
