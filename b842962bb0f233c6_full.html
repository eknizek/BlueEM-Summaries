# Food Delivery Company War Escalates with Corporate Espionage and Regulatory Battles in Brazil

The food delivery market war in Brazil has escalated to maximum tension. Mutual accusations of corporate espionage, diversion of confidential information, and disputes at the Administrative Council for Economic Defense (Cade) and in court over alleged illegal and predatory practices are at the center of actions by iFood, 99Food, and Keeta.

The most recent episode in this escalation was a video published by iFood CEO Diego Barreto on his social media, in which he claims that since April of last year, Asian consultancies, especially from China, have been approaching iFood employees via LinkedIn to seek internal company data in exchange for payments. According to the executive, the modus operandi consists of sending messages from supposed executives of these consultancies to teams in various areas of iFood, mainly commercial and technology, asking questions about GMV by region and city, daily orders, average order value, and other sensitive data.

NeoFeed had access to some of these approaches via social media. According to the company, since then there have been more than 170 cases in which company employees have been targeted, dating back to last year—a practice that Barreto says has decreased but has returned with force now. According to the iFood CEO, the approaches have come to involve employees of other companies in the Dutch Prosus group, which owns iFood, precisely in search of confidential information about the Brazilian company.

"This movement by these Chinese consultancies coincides with the arrival in Brazil of 99Food and Keeta, which are platforms from China," the iFood CEO says in an interview with NeoFeed. "I'm seeing a coordinated and systematic action to daily steal data from iFood. This is a very evident and repetitive process. They offer around R$ 3,000 or R$ 4,000 to talk about Brazil, then offer more to talk about the company," he states.

Keeta denies any involvement in this type of approach and says it has never received any notification involving it in the espionage cases. "The company advocates for an open and fair market and, for this, is deeply committed to the highest ethical and legal standards, and operates in compliance with all local requirements," it said in a statement sent to NeoFeed. "Keeta reiterates that it does not hire third parties to approach individuals on its behalf for the purposes described and emphasizes that it has not received any notification," the company added.

According to Barreto, iFood has been unable to take criminal action against these consultancies due to difficulties in accessing Chinese courts. "I've given up on seeking a way to resolve this issue in China. There's no way to break the confidentiality of these companies. I find myself with my hands tied to seek this reparation."

The iFood CEO states that there is another problem involving harassment by Asian companies. According to him, former iFood employees who went on to work at competing companies would have taken confidential information. "For me, it's terrible to have to spend energy on compliance when I could be spending it on product development. And what we've made clear in the company is that the situation will be taken to the police. There are already cases under investigation and we've had to fire employees," Barreto states.

On the other hand, Keeta also claims to be a target of espionage of its operations. According to the company, the Civil Police is investigating reports of coordinated attacks against the company at establishments in Baixada Santista, on the coast of São Paulo. "At least eight local restaurants were approached by individuals presenting themselves as supposed company employees, presenting false credentials, with the objective of obtaining data from the establishments, including accepted and dispatched orders, financial information, consumer preferences and other sensitive data," the company said in a statement.

## Exclusivity Barrier and Predatory Practices

Alongside espionage, the delivery fight also involves accusations of predatory practices and difficulties in breaking through the exclusivity barrier. In August 2025, Keeta went to Cade to demand investigation by the agency against alleged non-compliance with market control measures. It also accused fellow countryman 99Food of anticompetitive practices. Abrasel, which represents bars and restaurants, asked to be party to the process, as it has a direct relationship with the market. Cade opened an administrative inquiry to more rigorously investigate possible anticompetitive practice in the market at the end of March this year. There are no decisions in any of the cases.

In February of this year, Keeta postponed the start of its operations in the Rio de Janeiro market indefinitely, citing difficulties in breaking through the exclusivity barrier of iFood and 99Food.

Another point that demonstrates the escalation in the delivery market also involves accusations of "predatory pricing" practices, with the use of discounts above industry margins. "There's the use of very large amounts of money generated in China that comes here and forces the market to operate at extremely low prices. Discount volume of 50% is a form of pressure. Predatory pricing is different from discounts," says the iFood CEO.

Keeta also denies any anticompetitive practice in the case of discount coupons. "As a newly arrived brand in Brazil, we use coupons strategically to create initial trial, but our focus is to ensure that consumers stay for quality, predictability and experience."

## A Billion-Dollar Market

The backdrop to this war is the conquest of space in a giant, rapidly expanding market. A survey conducted by the market intelligence area of Getnet shows that the food delivery sector billed R$ 79 billion in 2025, an increase of 12.7% over the previous year. In the period, the average ticket was R$ 66.21 per meal, 12% above the figure found in in-person restaurant consumption. Monthly volume reached 150 million orders, an increase of 24%.

According to a 2023 Abrasel study, the market is dominated by iFood, which holds 82% market share. But this doesn't yet account for new competitors. A new study should be conducted in the coming months to gauge the current temperature of the market.

The issue is that, in the midst of this dispute, are the restaurants that need the income from the delivery service.
