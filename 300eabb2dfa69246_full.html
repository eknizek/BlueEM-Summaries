# Global Trade Would Fall if Strait of Hormuz Closes, Worsening Global Economic and Energy Tensions

The volatility represented by tensions in the Middle East, especially in the Strait of Hormuz, is currently placing most indicators in maritime transport, energy, food, and finance in the red due to repeated closures and openings of this strategic passage for global foreign trade. According to international organizations, the cascading effects of the escalation in the strait are being felt strongly, and risks have become more persistent across different productive sectors globally.

Based on data from the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), major productive indicators are already showing signs of impact. For example, since February 26, 2026, ship transit through the passage has decreased by 91.3%. Similarly, in general terms, food prices have increased 8.2%, while crude oil in Europe has become 67% more expensive. Meanwhile, emerging market stock prices have fallen 0.1%.

In this regard, UNCTAD predicts that global merchandise trade will decelerate drastically, moving from growth of around 4.7% in 2025 to between 1.5% and 2.5% in 2026. They also estimate that inflationary pressures will persist due to energy crises, which are raising prices and cost of living. Additionally, financial tension will grow as investors withdraw from developing countries.

## Breakdown by Sector

UNCTAD's new monitoring panel confirms sectoral impacts from the escalation in the Strait of Hormuz in recent months.

**Maritime Transport**: The organization shows that ship transit through the passage has fallen dramatically since January of this year. For example, 118 ships passed through the Strait of Hormuz on the first day of the year, fluctuating gradually until reaching 129 on February 27. However, during one of the escalations where the passage was practically blocked, only 7 ships managed to cross on March 3, 2026. Since then, transit has fluctuated, reaching a peak of 44 ships on April 18, but by the most recent figure of April 28, the indicator dropped again to 11 ships.

**Food Sector**: The FAO price index shows increases since tensions in this part of the Middle East. For example, in January 2024 the indicator was at 94, jumping to 95 in the same month of 2025. For the latest available data in March 2026, the index is already at 102.

**Fertilizer Prices**: Another upward variation shown in UN data concerns fertilizer prices. According to the most recent data, the input price index has reached one of its highest peaks since 2022 when Russia invaded Ukraine, with urea reaching 150 on April 21, 2026 and diammonium phosphate at 118. In percentage terms, this means that since February 26 of this year, urea prices have risen 50% and diammonium phosphate 17.1%.

**Energy Sector**: Based on organizational data, crude oil price increases have been rising since the escalation in the Strait of Hormuz. In Europe, crude has increased 67%, in the Middle East 60.7%, and in the Russian Federation 100.7%. Regarding gas, values have fluctuated depending on the region. In Asia, hydrocarbon prices have risen 57.5%, in Europe 47.6%, but in North America they have decreased 6.7%. Biofuels have also increased 11.4%.

"If interruptions persist or intensify, damage to energy infrastructure could keep prices elevated for longer, prolonging inflationary pressures. Regions most dependent on energy imports from the Middle East, particularly South Asia and Europe, would be most exposed," UNCTAD assures.

The report highlights that the interruption of energy flows, price increases, slowing trade, and tightening financial conditions are generating widespread global economic tension. "As uncertainty increases, resilience also weakens and the risk of a broader debt crisis increases," they mention. They add that if disruptions persist, the situation could become a cascading crisis with far-reaching consequences for development and affect around 3.4 billion people.
