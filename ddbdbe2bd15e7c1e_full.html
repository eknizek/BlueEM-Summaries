# Itaú names new chief economist; Central Bank post remains vacant

Diogo Guillen, the former director of Economic Policy at the Central Bank who ended his tenure in December, will be the new chief economist at Itaú, assuming a position that has been held for almost a decade by Mário Mesquita – another former director of Economic Policy at the Central Bank.

Guillen, who holds a degree in Economics from PUC-Rio and a PhD from Princeton, will assume leadership of the bank's macro analysis team on July 1st, after completing a six-month cooling-off period. He spent the last few months at Stanford as a visiting scholar. Before joining the Central Bank, he had already worked at the group as chief economist at Itaú Asset.

Last November, in his last public event before leaving the Central Bank, Guillen spoke at an FGV seminar about improvements in communication and decision-making processes at the Central Bank. On that occasion, the former director commented on internal discouragement at the institution regarding working conditions, given the loss of technical staff and financial constraints.

"Obviously, it requires motivated people," said the economist. "With ten years without a civil service exam and without salary increases, you don't need to be an HR expert to imagine how motivation is at the Central Bank."

"One issue I pursued quite a bit was financial and administrative autonomy, but without success," he commented. "That would be to bring the Central Bank in line with its peers and to improve the regulatory framework."

The constitutional amendment on the Central Bank's administrative and financial autonomy remains stalled in the Senate – and behind the scenes, it faces fierce opposition from the Government.

Mesquita, who has led the area since July 2016, will step down at the end of the month due to a statutory requirement of the bank. He turned 60 in September, and therefore cannot hold a directorship position. He will remain as a consultant until Guillen's arrival and then plans to take a sabbatical. However, he has not yet defined his professional future.

"What's certain is that I won't stop working," Mesquita told Brazil Journal. "After the sabbatical months, I plan to work in what I like, which is macro and markets."

At the Central Bank, Guillen's directorship remains vacant. The Economic Policy Directorate (Dipec) has been temporarily managed by Paulo Picchetti, director of International Affairs and Corporate Risk Management (Direx). Also vacant is the directorship of Financial System Organization and Resolution (Diorf), which has been temporarily managed by the Regulation director (Dinor), Gilneu Vivan.

Fernando Haddad, before leaving the Finance Ministry, indicated two names for the directorates: Guilherme Mello, a professor at Unicamp who was secretary of Economic Policy at the Finance Ministry, and Tiago Cavalcanti, a professor at Cambridge University. Two weeks ago, Mello assumed the presidency of Petrobras' board of directors.

The appointments at the Central Bank have not advanced, amid a general reshuffling of positions on the Esplanada due to approaching elections. President Lula would also be waiting for the Senate's approval of Jorge Messias's nomination to the Supreme Court.
