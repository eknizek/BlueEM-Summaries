# Coastal El Niño could inflict daily losses of US$86 million across seven Peru regions

The coastal El Niño phenomenon could generate losses of approximately US$86 million daily (S/ 291 million) across seven regions of Peru, according to a report from the Lima Chamber of Commerce (CCL). The Chamber's Institute of Economics and Business Development (IEDEP) sees the impact concentrated primarily in the regions of Tumbes, Piura, Lambayeque, La Libertad, Ica, Arequipa, and Moquegua.

Together, these regions represent 23.3% of the national Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and 27.5% of formal private employment. Approximately 1.9 million people living in poverty reside in these seven regions.

"If immediate mitigation, reconstruction, and support measures are not implemented for the most affected sectors, the economic impact of the phenomenon could escalate beyond initial projections," said Óscar Chávez, head of IEDEP at the CCL.

The report notes that it is critical to rehabilitate roads, bridges, irrigation systems, and basic services to prevent the paralysis of economic activities from prolonging.

According to the report, the sectors that could be most impacted by the coastal El Niño phenomenon are manufacturing, agriculture, commerce, and transport. In general, an impact is anticipated on Peruvian foreign trade due to the phenomenon, especially in activities such as traditional fishing.

Manufacturing alone could report losses of up to S/111.8 million daily (US$33 million), followed by commerce (S/74.9 million or US$22 million), agriculture (S/64.2 million or US$19 million), and transport (S/40.4 million or US$12 million).

According to Scotiabank's daily report, the coastal El Niño phenomenon is in its initial phase and is already generating intense rainfall in different regions, primarily on the northern coast, according to information from Peru's National Meteorology and Hydrology Service (Senamhi).

"The sea surface temperature off Tumbes, Piura, and Lambayeque ranges between 28 and 29 degrees Celsius, conditions that confirm the development of the event," the Scotiabank document indicates.

The impact of coastal El Niño could extend until November, according to the Multisectoral Commission in charge of the National Study of the El Niño Phenomenon (Enfen).

During the El Niño meteorological phenomenon—the most recent were in 2017 and 2023—ocean temperatures remain above normal, which triggers evaporation that feeds heavy rainfall.

The Peruvian government has declared more than 700 districts across the country in a state of emergency due to the downpours generally associated with El Niño.

"We must act with urgency in the face of the emergency caused by El Niño," declared new Prime Minister Denisse Miralles on Tuesday during her first press conference after taking office. "This is our number one priority."

The increase in food costs is the main concern, as the El Niño weather pattern can affect agriculture.

"In fruit shipments alone, the value exceeded US$7.560 billion in 2025, followed by traditional fishing, hydrobiological products, and traditional agriculture, which confirms the high sensitivity of Peruvian foreign trade to climate events," warned Carlos Posada, executive director of the Institute for Research and Development of Foreign Trade (Idexcam).
