# Coastal El Niño could cause daily losses of US$86 million in seven Peruvian regions

The coastal El Niño phenomenon could generate losses of around US$86 million daily (S/ 291 million) in seven regions of Peru, according to a report from the Lima Chamber of Commerce (CCL). The Institute of Economics and Business Development (IEDEP) of that entity sees an impact concentrated mainly in the regions of Tumbes, Piura, Lambayeque, La Libertad, Ica, Arequipa, and Moquegua.

Together, these regions represent 23.3% of the national Gross Domestic Product (GDP) and 27.5% of formal private employment. About 1.9 million people living in poverty inhabit the seven regions.

"If immediate measures of mitigation, reconstruction, and support for the most affected sectors are not implemented, the economic impact of the phenomenon could escalate beyond initial projections," said Óscar Chávez, head of IEDEP at the CCL.

The report notes that it is crucial to rehabilitate roads, bridges, irrigation systems, and basic services to prevent the paralysis of economic activities from prolonging.

According to the report, the sectors that could be most impacted by the coastal El Niño phenomenon are manufacturing, agriculture, commerce, and transportation. In general, an impact on Peruvian foreign trade is anticipated because of the phenomenon, especially in activities such as traditional fishing.

Manufacturing alone could report losses of up to S/111.8 million daily (US$33 million), followed by commerce (S/74.9 million or US$22 million), agriculture (S/64.2 million or US$19 million), and transportation (S/40.4 million or US$12 million).

According to Scotiabank in its daily report, the coastal El Niño phenomenon is in an initial phase and is already generating intense rainfall in different regions, mainly on the northern coast, according to information from Peru's National Meteorology and Hydrology Service (Senamhi).

"The sea surface temperature off Tumbes, Piura, and Lambayeque ranges between 28 and 29 degrees Celsius, conditions that confirm the development of the event," indicates the Scotiabank document.

The impact of coastal El Niño could extend until November, according to the Multisectoral Commission in charge of the National Study of the El Niño Phenomenon (Enfen).

During the El Niño meteorological phenomenon—the last ones were in 2017 and 2023—ocean temperatures remain above normal, which triggers evaporation that feeds the downpours.

The Peruvian Government has declared more than 700 districts throughout the country in a state of emergency due to the downpours generally associated with El Niño.

"We must act with urgency in the face of the emergency caused by El Niño," declared on Tuesday the new prime minister, Denisse Miralles, in her first press conference after taking office. "This is our number one priority."

The increase in food costs is the main concern, since the El Niño weather pattern can affect agriculture.

"In fruit shipments alone, the value exceeded US$7.560 billion in 2025, followed by traditional fishing, hydrobiological products, and traditional agriculture, which confirms the high sensitivity of Peruvian foreign trade to climate events," warned Carlos Posada, executive director of the Institute for Research and Development of Foreign Trade (Idexcam).
