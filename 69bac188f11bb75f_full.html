# Specialized Housing for Seniors Gains Momentum in Bogotá: New Model Seeks Greater Accessibility

The accelerated growth of the elderly population is driving new specialized housing models designed to respond to the needs of this segment with comprehensive solutions for wellness, health, and support. In Bogotá, this trend is beginning to materialize with projects that seek to expand access to services traditionally associated with high-cost schemes.

In this context, Aldeia emerges—a new senior living development located in the Teusaquillo sector, with an opening scheduled for June 20. The initiative aims to offer a housing alternative that combines independence, professional care, and socialization spaces within the same environment.

The model reflects a structural change in housing demand for seniors, driven by the progressive aging of the population. According to figures cited by the project, in the coming two decades, 20% of Colombians will belong to this age group, increasing the need for solutions adapted to this life stage.

Unlike traditional developments focused on high-income segments, this proposal seeks to position itself as a viable option, integrating permanent support services without replicating the elevated costs that have characterized this type of offering in the capital.

The complex will combine two housing modalities: independent and assisted living. In both cases, residents will have access to medical services, physical and cognitive activity programs, as well as group activities aimed at strengthening social interaction. This approach responds to recommendations from the World Health Organization, which highlights the importance of combating sedentary lifestyles in people over 60 to preserve their health and autonomy.

The project also incorporates common use spaces with characteristics associated with high-end developments, such as a cinema, library, sky-bar-style terraces, and BBQ areas. These amenities seek to offer wellness experiences in a community environment without completely transferring the costs of traditional models to the end user.

According to Bernardo Asuaje, the development responds to the need to create more scalable schemes. The executive noted that approximately 500,000 people in the country would have the economic capacity to access this type of solution, opening space for proposals that balance quality and accessibility.

The complex will have capacity for over 220 seniors and will additionally offer complementary services such as temporary stays for post-operative recovery processes and day programs. This scheme will allow users to transition between different levels of care according to their needs without disconnecting from their social environment.

Another component of the project is the incorporation of geriatric architecture principles, a discipline that adapts spaces to the physical and cognitive conditions of older people. In this case, the design prioritizes safety, mobility, and autonomy—key aspects to ensure residents' quality of life.

This is complemented by an interdisciplinary team responsible for providing periodic medical care, continuous supervision, and comprehensive wellness programs. The combination of these elements seeks to consolidate a model that promotes active aging in a structured and supported environment.

With initiatives of this type, the real estate market in Bogotá is beginning to diversify toward segments that until recently had limited offerings, in line with the demographic transformations facing the country and the growing demand for specialized housing solutions for the elderly population.
