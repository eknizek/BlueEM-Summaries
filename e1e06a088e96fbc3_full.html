# Warsh Prepares to Lead a Federal Reserve Increasingly Reluctant to Cut Interest Rates

As Kevin Warsh approaches the presidency of the Federal Reserve, his future colleagues are moving further away from supporting the resumption of interest rate cuts that President Donald Trump hopes for. In their last meeting under the current leadership of Fed Chair Jerome Powell, policymakers agreed on Wednesday by near unanimity to keep interest rates stable. But a deep divide emerged over where rates could go from here, with three officials dissenting on language that still pointed to future cuts.

"If anyone was expecting immediate interest rate cuts with Warsh's arrival, they will be disappointed," said Kevin Flanagan, director of investment strategy at WisdomTree. "It will depend on data from the next six weeks, and even more, for these three dissenters to move from dissenting to actually wanting to resume cutting rates."

In a CNBC interview on April 21, Trump said he would be disappointed if Warsh did not cut rates as soon as he took office. Warsh has long indicated that he sees room to lower rates, but has not explicitly said he will immediately ask his Fed colleagues to do so once he is sworn in. He could end up aligning with the majority of Fed policymakers who have indicated that interest rates are well-positioned for now. It is also possible that a sudden change in the economy will force the committee to act. But absent a clear signal from the data, Warsh will inherit a divided committee that is increasingly resistant to lowering rates.

Since cutting rates three times last fall, the Federal Reserve's post-meeting statement has suggested that additional cuts would eventually be appropriate. But with persistent inflation and the subsequent sharp rise in energy prices following the Middle East war, a growing number of officials has pushed to signal that the next move could just as easily be a rate increase.

"The center is moving toward a more neutral place, and that is more or less what the markets are saying too," Powell told reporters Wednesday after the Federal Open Market Committee's decision to keep its benchmark rate stable in a target range of 3.5% to 3.75%. Powell noted that most officials are not yet ready to signal that. But the dissents showed that supporters of change are growing impatient.

"This message is directed not only to incoming President Warsh, but also to the market and the administration: the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) is a credible and independent democracy," said Priya Misra, portfolio manager at JPMorgan Asset Management. "Warsh will have to convince his colleagues if he wants to lower interest rates."

Powell Stays

Powell's decision to remain at the Fed could also reduce the likelihood of a rate cut in the near term. The outgoing Fed chair said he plans to keep a low profile and does not intend to serve as the main opposing force to the incoming chair. His decision stems rather from concern about "legal attacks on the Fed, which threaten our ability to conduct monetary policy without regard to political factors."

Still, his continued presence as Warsh takes over could influence policy. If nothing else, it prevents Trump from naming a loyalist to the board. Once confirmed, Warsh will replace Governor Stephen Miran, who, since being appointed by Trump last fall, has dissented at every meeting in favor of lower rates than his colleagues preferred.

"Jay's announcement that he will remain in office makes it practically clear that the Federal Reserve is not going to cut interest rates in the near future," said Patrick Harker, former president of the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia. "The committee is clearly in a wait-and-see phase."

Even among policymakers who agreed to maintain the rate-cut bias in their statement, few are eager to lower rates in the near term. Several officials have said they need more time to see how the Middle East conflict will affect the U.S. economy, and some worry that rising energy prices could worsen already elevated inflation.

"The war continues, oil prices have remained elevated, inflation measures are moving, and on the other side, the real side of the economy, we have gotten stronger data," said Loretta Mester, former president of the Cleveland Federal Reserve. "So if before the meeting they thought the next move would be down due to positive inflation developments or if they were worried about the labor market, many of those reasons have gone away," she said.
