# What Drives the New Wave of Brazilian Entrepreneurs in the US, According to Endeavor Research

The United States has long attracted entrepreneurs, investors, and technology executives from around the world willing to establish themselves in the country and experience the world's leading innovation hub. And in this landscape, the presence of Brazilians is gaining increasing momentum.

This is one of the conclusions of "Brazil Tech Diaspora in The USA," a groundbreaking study conducted by Endeavor Brasil. Shared exclusively with NeoFeed, the survey identified 200 Brazilians – of which more than 140 are entrepreneurs – currently living on American soil for this purpose. By compiling a series of public data, the research also established a connection to one of the main factors driving Brazilians to venture into the American technology ecosystem: the artificial intelligence (AI) boom.

"AI dynamics make it faster, leaner in terms of personnel, and cheaper to start and grow a business," says Maria Teresa Fornea, general director of Endeavor Brasil, to NeoFeed. "And this stimulates the desire to start a global business. It became easier to play."

The decision to focus the study on the United States came from the observation that with AI, venture capital has concentrated in the country again, after a period in which these resources began to be directed to other hubs, such as China. Translating this movement into figures, 79% of the $159 billion in global AI company financing in 2025 was directed to companies based in the US, according to Crunchbase. Of that total, more than 75% was invested in California.

"In terms of power, the main destinations would be the US and China," says Fornea. "But in China, there isn't the same level of investment in entrepreneurship. It's a different dynamic. So people want to be in the US and I feel this movement will be more present than in past cycles."

For her, one of the numbers reinforcing the US as the main center of attraction for entrepreneurs from other geographies is the fact that 60% of the leading AI companies based in the country have at least one immigrant founder.

In this scenario, Endeavor's survey shows that AI is also prominent in these Brazilians' projects in the US. According to the study, 49% of these entrepreneurs are developing businesses with this technology at their core. Similarly, the distribution of these entrepreneurs is well-aligned with the AI race, which has reinforced the relevance of an innovation hub in the US – California – which is home to 39.5% of companies run by Brazilians. Of companies with AI at their core, 45.8% are in the region. Other locations are Florida, with 24.5%, and New York, with 12.9%.

On this map, California has more well-known examples, such as Brex, founded by Henrique Dugubras and Pedro Franceschi in 2017 and sold in January this year to Capital One in a transaction worth $5.1 billion. In New York, the American headquarters of more established Brazilian companies like VTEX and Wellhub, there is even a unicorn: YipitData, a market research and data analytics company that has Brazilian Vinicius Vacanti as co-founder and has already attracted nearly $400 million in investments.

Examples are not restricted to these regions. Founded in 2019 by Brazilian Igor Marinelli and American Jim Moran, Tractian, which uses AI to monitor industrial equipment, is based in Atlanta, Georgia, and has already raised over $180 million from funds like General Catalyst and DGF.

At the same time, sectors like healthcare and biotech, both with a participation of 8.1% in ventures run by Brazilians, are also beginning to gain prominence. One of the names contributing to this evolution is OneSkin. Founded in 2016 by Carolina Reis de Oliveira, Alessandra Zonari, Juliana Carvalho, and Mariana Boroni, and based in San Francisco, the biotech startup has raised over $46 million from investors like Prelude Growth Partners and Selva Ventures.

Brazilian Flavor

In this exchange, there is also room for Brazilian flair. These businesses are concentrated primarily in segments where the country excels and has more vocation, such as software and business services, with a share of 48.1%, and software and financial services, with 18.5%.

One example is fintech SellersFi, founded in 2017 by Ricardo Pero, Alessandro Novaes, and Fabio Knijnik, which has raised over $400 million in equity and debt from names like Citi and MUFG Innovation Partners.

"The fintech industry and banking infrastructure in Brazil is much more advanced," says Fornea. "And there are examples like Blip and other Brazilian companies that are working with Meta and American telecos to bring integrations, for example, with WhatsApp, which don't yet exist in the US."

Despite these advances, the study points out that access to capital is still unequal across different fundraising stages for these companies. Resources are more concentrated in the early stage, with 32.9% of fundraising in pre-seed and 24.2% in Series A funding.

"This continuity in other stages needs to evolve more," says Fornea. "But those making things happen don't have capital pain. And in any case, access to resources is bizarrely different compared to other markets."

Fornea also highlights another point of concern amid this diaspora: the worry that this movement is restricted to the American market and doesn't bring positive effects to this ecosystem in Brazil. She points out, however, a favorable factor in this context.

"This movement of a more global scale is less tied to markets. So these entrepreneurs can return to Brazil," she says. "We want to build this bridge so that companies in Brazil have a high standard and not a ruler disconnected from what's happening in the world, especially in AI."
