# Cirkula, the Peruvian App Reducing Food Waste, to Expand Categories and Store Partners—What It's About

Is it possible to save money and combat the amount of food waste in Peru? A Peruvian startup saw the opportunity to create an ecosystem that could make it a reality: Cirkula. Michelle Gomberoff, CEO of the platform, explains that it functions as a sort of marketplace that connects businesses that have surpluses of good quality food—with expiration dates approaching—seeking to offer them at better prices for greater sales options with users willing to buy them and looking for discounted food.

The executive emphasizes that products in the application have a minimum 40% discount and have recommended consumption dates. To date, among the more than 1,000 affiliated businesses are restaurants, cafés, and supermarkets. Gomberoff previews that they will enter more categories, with which they seek this double impact: generating savings and reducing waste in the capital.

"This year we are looking to consolidate the supermarket segment, convenience stores, and distributors within the application. We started little by little with food items, but many of our clients also work with pet food or personal care products that have the problem that the product is expiring, which will generate a negative environmental impact," she states.

Thus, she explains, she saw the opportunity for Cirkula to mobilize these new categories, which are beginning to open this year. "We are exploring some of the categories that make sense for our clients," she notes.

The executive recounts that for distributors, which have warehouses farther from Lima, they created Cirkula's own warehouse model, where brands can dispatch products and from there—in Miraflores—the platform carries out sales of different products. Gomberoff recalls that platform users generally go to pick up products from the point of sale according to proximity. Delivery usage is very minimal, she says.

How frequent is purchasing, on average? Currently it is slightly more than twice a month. "This is what we want to continue promoting, which is why we also expanded categories both in other verticals and in supermarkets and distributors," she mentions.

On the business side, the executive comments that they affiliate, choose hours, and publish their surplus products in real time on the platform. Currently, Cirkula operates in Lima for operational and logistic reasons, as well as to take advantage of growth profitability. For now they don't see the option of going to other cities in the country, but hope to do so at some point. However, they have made the leap to Quito, Ecuador.

"Right now we are focusing on cities that bring us profitability. We are considered an environmental impact startup and that is our purpose," she mentions. In fact, the financing they have been able to raise for model development has been from environmental impact investors from Germany, Norway, Ecuador, and Peru. Later, she confesses, they are also interested in reaching the Bolivian market.

"We have it in mind for a next expansion. What we do is enter the ecosystem one or two years before to get to know other companies, investors, and mentors," she says.

How did the model emerge? Gomberoff recounts that they saw it seven or eight years ago in another country and it matched. They asked themselves how in Latin America and Peru, where gastronomy is so cultural, there wasn't something similar.

"We seek healthy competition to occur and new players to emerge because that generates the formation of legislation for reducing food waste. Or, that what happens in other markets occurs, where businesses or brands have alternative platforms or have to donate," she refers.

The app's beginning was not easy. Not only because they launched it two months before the pandemic began, but when they were able to operate, they had to convince businesses to participate, since many didn't understand the concept. In fact, normally food and supermarket businesses register between 2% and 20% of overproduction or surplus; at that time, it was hard for them to admit it. Cirkula made nighttime visits, talked with business staff, and gathered evidence. That way, they demonstrated the app's good results.

"One of the most adventurous restaurants was Tanta, which was like a seal of confidence. Little by little more stores and brands joined in," she recalls.

By year-end, Cirkula would reach 1,500 affiliated businesses between Peru and Ecuador. They also seek that at least 20% of transactions generated in the application occur through alternative categories—outside of food, restaurants, and cafés—and that the convenience, distributor, and supermarket segment represent 20%. This year they project adding more than S/10 million in savings for their users and 1 million products sold through the app.
