Federal Audit Court orders INSS to suspend new payroll deduction credit contracts

29 April 2026 · 09:41 PM

# Federal Audit Court Orders INSS to Suspend New Payroll Deduction Credit Contracts

The TCU (Federal Audit Court) has ordered the INSS (National Social Security Institute) to immediately suspend new payroll deduction loan contracts in the modalities of payroll credit cards and benefit cards. Additionally, the agency must halt the granting of new payroll deduction loans until the security requirements of the eConsignado system are functioning properly. The decision was made in a collegiate judgment on Wednesday (April 29). The suspension will remain in effect until the agency takes a final position on the matter.

The TCU also determined that Dataprev (the government's technology company) adopt measures to protect beneficiaries' data. The Central Bank must establish forms to oversee this type of contracting. Both INSS and Dataprev will have 45 days after notification of the decision to present a technical report proving the effectiveness of security safeguards to protect data.

In a statement, the Ministry of Social Security affirms that security in payroll deduction credit operations is a central priority of the current administration. Protection of beneficiary data is also a priority. The agency states it has not yet been notified of the decision. The company reports that once notified by INSS, it "will adopt necessary measures to comply with the deadlines set by the court," and emphasizes that it works on "evolution and improvement of systems, focusing on security and data integrity" of beneficiaries.

Payroll deduction credit is a loan with installments discounted directly from retirement or pension payments. The risk of default is practically zero, which is why interest rates are the lowest on the market. Rules are controlled by CNPS (National Social Security Council). The biometrics requirement began being enforced after the TCU ordered the blocking of releases.

The case was brought to the TCU by accounts prosecutor Lucas Furtado in February of this year. The initiative was based on a column by lawyer Rômulo Saraiva in Folha de S.Paulo regarding Congress's investigation through a parliamentary inquiry commission (CPI) of the INSS regarding irregularities in payroll deduction credit.

A TCU audit had already found that in just three years, possible bundled sales of payroll deduction loans would correspond to R$219 billion in discounts. The amount was deducted from retirements and pensions.

Furtado stated to Folha that he decided to approach the accounting body on suspicion that part of the money from Banco Master had been obtained improperly. The money would come from discounts on retirements and pensions of Social Security beneficiaries. "INSS concentrates a lot of money, and this is also an important issue related to Master regarding payroll deduction that leads retirees and pensioners to have improper discounts. That's why I asked the TCU to monitor any eventual illegality. I shot in the dark and ended up hitting," he says.

Furtado stated in his complaint that the inclusion of improper discounts in beneficiaries' benefits occurred in breach of explicit norms. These norms require express authorization from beneficiaries. According to him, there would be no direct damages to public coffers, but to Social Security beneficiaries themselves.

According to the TCU report, the CGU (Federal Comptroller General's Office) identified that 36% of card contracting were not recognized by beneficiaries. Another 25% stated they had not requested the card, and 36% said they did not receive the withdrawal amount back. Many beneficiaries did not even receive card statements (78% of those surveyed), making it difficult to monitor debt.

Among the irregularities found are: contracting without authorization and identity fraud; contracts made after the beneficiary's death; improper use of biometrics, with multiple operations using the same biometric database; loans to minors. Also identified were: absence of authorization; contracts made without documentation being sent by the beneficiary; diversion of funds to accounts that do not correspond to the beneficiary's account; bundled sales; and charging of abusive fees.

In addition to suspending new contracts, the TCU ordered INSS to present alternatives within 30 days to improve oversight of card operations. Currently, oversight is limited by banking secrecy.

Dataprev was instructed to prioritize implementation of eight security demands indicated between November 2025 and March 2026. These include contract validation using AI (artificial intelligence).
