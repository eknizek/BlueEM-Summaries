Genial private banking surpasses R$19 billion AUM via 95% client retention strategy

# The "95% effect": Genial's private banking surpasses R$19 billion AUM via client retention strategy

When Genial launched its private banking three years ago, the goal was to bring the firm's investment banking differentiation to the synergistic business of wealth management. Since then, the vertical has more than tripled its total assets under management, and the wealth and private area has surpassed R$15 billion. Now, the bet is on credit structures for a new leap forward.

Today, structured strategies alone in Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) within the wealth and private area total over R$2 billion. The firm has approximately 20 such funds and a pipeline of at least another ten to be launched in the first semester. The expectation is that this volume could double by year-end, which will contribute to the target of reaching between R$18.5 billion and R$19 billion under management in wealth and private.

"It's a capital solution that pleases both those who need money and those who have resources to allocate in a diversified and low-risk manner," says Cláudio Massari, head of wealth management at Genial, to NeoFeed.

The advancement of FIDCs within Genial gained momentum from two market changes. The first was the alteration in taxation of exclusive and offshore funds, which prompted high-net-worth clients out of inertia. According to Massari, many investors maintained outdated structures with portfolios concentrated in fund-of-funds, multi-market funds, and stocks that were no longer delivering the same performance. However, the regulatory change made them reconsider.

The second was the creation of so-called "95%" funds—multi-market structures that maintain at least 95% of their portfolio in assets such as FIDCs, FIPs, and FIAs and, therefore, preserve the exemption from interim distributions tax (come-cotas). "Practically all of our exclusive fund clients opted for this modality," Massari affirms. "And all of them, in some way, built structures that had some percentage of FIDC to make up that 95% threshold."

It was in this environment that Genial created a product structuring vertical for the high-net-worth public. The strategy is not limited to FIDCs, but credit rights funds ended up becoming the primary product. The area, which started with one dedicated person, already has four professionals and is hiring two more. The expectation is to reach the end of the semester with six people focused on originator selection, thesis analysis, and fund structuring.

The model works through engagement with companies and family businesses that have receivables or credit rights to transform into funds. Originators contribute subordinated quotas, assuming part of the structure's risk. In practice, the FIDC becomes more than an investment product. In some cases, it's a way to address liquidity issues in family companies. The family, which often concentrates liquidity in the individual, can help finance their own company through the purchase of their company's receivables and even share this with the market. In other cases, companies within the same group use the FIDC to connect cash-generating companies with others that need capital, reducing dependence on banks and funding costs.

"One of the FIDC's functions is to address the liquidity issue in companies, at a time when much of these families' liquidity is in the individual and not in the company," says Massari. "This ends up forming a quite interesting virtuous circle. And since we are a large asset manager and administrator, we can do this at very interesting costs."

**Acquisition Mechanism**

This circle has also become a customer acquisition tool. By bringing the originator into the structure, Genial gains not just a product partner. Often, it also gains the originator themselves, their partners, and executives as private banking clients. Massari cites the case of an originator who already had a FIDC at another firm but couldn't grow because they couldn't raise resources for the senior quota. Upon migrating the structure to Genial, the firm successfully raised capital for the fund. At the same time, the 12 quota holders linked to the originator became Genial clients and purchased other platform products.

The strategy also aligns with a change in investor portfolios. With strong demand for exempt assets like CRIs, CRAs, and incentivized debentures, rates became compressed and, in Genial's view, no longer adequately remunerated credit risk in some cases. Throughout the first quarter, the firm reduced positions in exempt assets and increased exposure to FIDCs.

Even with 15% taxation on redemption or amortization, Massari states that FIDC spreads and credit quality compensate for the loss of exemption: "We have been understanding that FIDCs, even paying 15% income tax, more than compensate for the fact that they don't have exemption, also because of the deferral," he says.

The theses finding the most demand involve court-ordered claims, payroll-deducted loans, legal claims, and specific supply chains. The firm has also seen interest from other single and multi-family offices, which have begun buying senior and, in some cases, mezzanine quotas of funds structured by Genial from their clients or externally.

Today, private banking serves clients with assets of at least R$3 million. Wealth management is closer to R$10 million. The difference lies in service sophistication: discretionary management, tax and succession planning, consolidation of multiple custodians, and portfolio analysis maintained at other institutions.

Competition today comes less from large traditional banks and more from investment advisory firms and independent structures that grew in the wake of platforms. Massari says Genial "flies a bit under the radar" of larger firms but has managed to capture clients mainly from larger advisory firms and some banks.

"The market over the last two years has been very much musical chairs," he states. "In this give and take, fortunately we won much more than we lost."
