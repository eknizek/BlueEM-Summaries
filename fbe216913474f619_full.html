# More Lightweight Electric Vehicles in Colombia Would Reduce Cars and Motorcycles on Roads, Following Shanghai and Netherlands Models

Lightweight electric vehicles in the Colombian market have increased by more than 256% since 2022, according to Gabriel Gutiérrez Mejía, founder and general manager of Minca Electric, a Bogotá-based company that has recorded growth of 4.7 times year-over-year. However, the sector's growth comes accompanied by a regulatory dispute over law 2486 of 2025, approved precisely to promote this category of vehicles.

Gutiérrez points out that he identifies deliberate pressure from conventional transportation guilds to equate the requirements for electric scooters and e-bikes with those required for combustion motorcycles. The discussion revolves around article 15 of that law, which the businessman considers explicit in excluding these vehicles from the obligation to obtain SOAT insurance, registration, and license.

Minca Electric currently operates with more than 10 company-owned sales points, over 10 distribution channels, and five specialized workshops in Colombia and Mexico. In this interview with Portafolio, the businessman explains why he considers the regulation contradictory, which countries serve as reference models for Colombia, and what minimum safety conditions the sector is willing to accept.

## What does law 2486 detail?

We are seeing the exploitation of a legal vacuum that exists today, where they want to manipulate law 2486 to suit their interests and generate fear in the market. That fear is that they will equalize us with conventional vehicles and their requirements. At the international level, however, it is evident that this type of transportation, due to its weight and speed, are much safer vehicles. In fact, there are studies in Europe where we see that if people substitute a conventional combustion motorcycle for a lightweight vehicle, we would immediately have a reduction in mortality and accident rates.

Regarding law 2486, it is excellent news for us. It was approved to promote this type of vehicle and gives very clear guidelines and rules of the game to the Ministry of Transportation. The law defines, for example, what helmet users should wear, and for safety reasons at night they should drive with specialized lights—white front light and red rear light—like many other vehicles. Additionally, it defines what can and cannot go on bicycle infrastructure. It also addresses what these vehicles will be in terms of weight and speed to determine whether or not SOAT insurance, registration, and license are required.

Specifically, article 15 of this law is extremely clear that vehicles weighing less than 60 kg or traveling at speeds below 40 km/h, given the nature of their weight or speed, will not require any of these documents.

## Why do you consider establishing many conditions would be overregulation?

We are not reinventing the wheel; the wheel is already invented. There are rules of the game that must certainly be colombianized, understanding our infrastructure, the slopes of our cities, and their nature. There are countries that have been on this path for 15 years. History proves us right in saying that you cannot put a vehicle traveling less than 40 km/h or weighing less than 60 kg to compete with the same incentives as combustion motorcycles.

The idea is not to go to one extreme or the other. We were indeed participants in building this law. But if they want to impose helmets with ISO 9001 certification and a number of requirements to equate it with a 100-horsepower vehicle at speeds of more than 250 km/h, we would be entering overregulation. We must understand that they have not even managed to get more than 70 or 75% of two-wheeled combustion vehicles actively circulating in the country to have current SOAT insurance, and they are already asking for SOAT from other vehicles.

## How has the lightweight electric mobility market grown in Colombia in recent years?

Electric vehicles have grown more than 256% since 2022. This does not indicate that combustion motorcycles have not also grown. This growth is natural. I think that ultimately people's need to move will arise. Our company has been growing since 2021 to date more than 4.7 times year-over-year, and with this type of policy they are trying to establish regulation that actually slows down the growth the industry has had.

## What role can these vehicles play in reducing congestion and emissions?

The car is the most inefficient vehicle for moving people, especially one person at a time. Speaking of what has been a success internationally, if you put any person on a non-motorized bicycle, without the ability to accelerate, the consumer will definitely opt for the most efficient option. But if you give incentives, as has happened in cities like Shanghai or the Netherlands, you are removing a car from the city, or you are removing a motorcycle that has an absurd mortality problem, or you are freeing up space so that people who really need public transportation can actually use it.
