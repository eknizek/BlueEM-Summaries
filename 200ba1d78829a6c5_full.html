# Stablecoins gain ground in Latin America and consolidate as a new avenue for dollar operations

Amid the enthusiasm for cryptocurrencies and blockchain, stablecoins have stopped being an exclusive tool of this ecosystem and are beginning to consolidate as a concrete way to operate in dollars in Latin America. Their use is growing among both individuals and companies, driven by the need to access a stable currency in economies plagued by inflation, currency restrictions, and limitations in the traditional financial system.

Stablecoins are cryptocurrencies whose price is tied to a stable asset. In most cases, their value replicates that of the US dollar in a 1-to-1 relationship. Among the most widely used are USDT (Tether) and USDC (USD Coin). Unlike assets like Bitcoin, which can register sharp variations within hours, stablecoins seek to maintain stability. This characteristic makes them especially attractive for those who need predictability rather than speculation. In practice, they function as digital dollars that are stored in virtual wallets and can be sent or received from anywhere in the world without direct intervention from the traditional banking system.

According to data from the RWA.xyz analytics platform, the stablecoin market surpassed $300 billion in mid-March 2026 and already has more than 236 million users globally, a sign that the phenomenon has ceased to be marginal and has become structural.

"Stablecoins are no longer just a tool of the crypto world: in Latin America they are transforming into a concrete way to operate in dollars, both for saving and for moving money between countries," explained Alejandro Estrin, country manager of Kast.

## Stablecoins in Latin America: regional demand driving adoption

Latin America appears as one of the main growth hubs. The reason is clear: both individuals and companies think in dollars, even though they operate in local economies with high volatility. In Brazil, for example, central bank authorities noted that approximately 90% of flows linked to cryptocurrencies are associated with stablecoins, especially in international payments and purchases.

Argentina reflects one of the highest adoption rates in the region: it is estimated that between 18% and 20% of adults use crypto-assets, including stablecoins. However, their use remains primarily concentrated in value preservation. "What we see in countries like Argentina is that adoption is already massive, but it is still more linked to protecting value than to everyday use. That is the next big step," Estrin noted.

## Simpler transfers without intermediaries

One of the key factors behind the growth is the simplification of international transfers. Unlike the traditional banking system, stablecoins allow money to be sent and settled directly on the network, without intermediaries or restrictive schedules. This reduces timeframes and increases operational predictability. According to the World Bank, sending $200 abroad cost an average of 6.49% in the first quarter of 2025, with costs that could exceed 14% in banking services.

"Stablecoins are not always cheaper, but they do change the logic: they eliminate intermediaries and allow money to move directly, with real-time validation," Estrin explained.

## Stablecoins in Latin America: from digital asset to financial infrastructure

The data reflects a clear trend: in Argentina, stablecoins represent 61.8% of crypto transaction volume, while in Brazil they reach 59.8%, both above the global average. Moreover, they lead entry into the ecosystem: in 2024 they accounted for 39% of crypto-asset purchases in Latin America, showing that many users access instruments linked to the dollar directly.

For Kast, the challenge now is not in adding users, but in improving the experience. The next step is making paying with stablecoins as simple as having them. For that, platforms need to resolve technical complexity in the background and offer a fluid user experience.

In this context, stablecoins are beginning to position themselves as more than a digital asset: a new layer of financial infrastructure that allows converting the demand for dollars into a more agile, global, and accessible system. "Rather than a fad, we are facing a structural change: stablecoins are transforming how people access, use, and transfer value in the region," Estrin concluded.
