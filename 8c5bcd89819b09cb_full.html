# Oxio Targets Movistar in Mexico: How It Seeks to Reduce Costs and Gain Users

Oxio, the company seeking to acquire Movistar in Mexico, aims to optimize costs through its operating model while building user loyalty, according to Nicolás Girard, CEO of Oxio Inc.

"We can optimize costs while building loyalty among subscribers, improving conversion to postpaid services, and delivering innovation," Girard explained to Excélsior.

One of the main challenges the Oxio CEO identifies in the Mexican telecommunications market centers on prepaid users. "One of the primary challenges in the market is the volatility of prepaid customers. For companies, it is difficult to retain a recurring base of prepaid subscribers due to pricing dynamics, affordability, and lack of financial inclusion, heavily influenced by a cash-based economy," the executive detailed.

Therefore, the company does not seek to compete solely on price, although the nature of its technology will make connectivity more accessible while driving digital and financial inclusion. "Oxio's platform enables a more digital and efficient operating model, which creates opportunities to offer greater added value to customers over time," Girard explained.

"This includes more flexible offers, new partnership models, and innovative ways to expand access to connectivity, all while maintaining a robust and reliable customer experience."

## No Spectrum Acquisition

Nicolás Girard announced that they are not seeking to acquire spectrum in Mexico. "Oxio's model is purely cloud-based and asset-light. Our approach in Mexico is to operate under this platform model, not to acquire spectrum," he clarified.

The Oxio CEO assured that they will maintain the AT&T infrastructure usage agreement that expires in 2030. "We have access to all cell towers in Mexico and, under the terms of this agreement, we are prepared to fulfill all obligations of the existing agreement between Movistar and AT&T Mexico."

## Recognized Brand

The Oxio CEO explained that they decided to maintain the Movistar brand because it has been well-positioned in the Mexican market for over 20 years. "Our intention is to revitalize the brand, not rebuild a new one. We seek stability, and a brand change would raise many questions and customers might not understand that it's the same product and service," Girard detailed.

Regarding how Movistar will operate under Oxio's infrastructure, the executive indicated they will migrate to a modern telecommunications stack with full functionality on modern technology rails, operated on the company's cloud. In this way, as a "digitally-enabled operator," Movistar will be managed on a cloud-native platform, software-orchestrated, which allows greater automation, agility, efficiency, and speed to market response.

Regarding operation on Oxio's Telecom as a Service platform, Girard said Movistar will be able to modernize its operating model, simplify technological complexity, and create a more flexible foundation for future services, partnerships, and growth.
