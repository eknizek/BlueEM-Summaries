# Four-Day Week in Brazil: What Pilot Data Reveals About Productivity, Costs, and Infrastructure in 2026

Brazil entered 2026 discussing more seriously the reduction of the weekly work schedule. Part of the topic reappeared in Congress via constitutional amendment proposals seeking to lower the constitutional ceiling on hours worked. The Brazilian difference, however, is that the discussion began to count on local empirical signals: a structured pilot of a four-day week conducted in 2024, in partnership with the global 4 Day Week initiative, brought together companies that tested hour reduction without salary reduction.

## What the Brazilian Pilot Was (and What It Was Not)

The pilot followed the model known as 100-80-100: maintain 100% of salary, work 80% of the time, and pursue 100% of productivity. Important: the publicly released data varies between "worker perception" and "company evaluation" — and this changes the reading.

In coverage of preliminary results, there were well-being indicators (less exhaustion, less stress) and productivity signals perceived by participants. In press reports about the pilot, some numbers point to increased productivity perceived by participants (for example, 71.5%), while company evaluation may appear with different percentages (for example, 61.5% reporting increases). In other words: there is no single "71%" that settles the debate. There are distinct metrics, collected by distinct instruments, in a sample that is not representative of the Brazilian market as a whole.

## The Real Dilemma: The Schedule Itself or the Ability to Operate Well?

One of the most useful readings of the pilot is not "it worked" versus "it didn't work." It is understanding why some companies went through hour reduction with less friction than others. Generally, when the organization already operates with predictable processes and a routine less dependent on urgencies, hour reduction tends to expose fewer bottlenecks. When operations are reactive, with repeated decisions and fragmented communication, "taking a day off" merely accelerates the sense of chaos.

## Why the Topic Gained Strength in 2026: Mental Health Stopped Being an "HR Matter"

Part of the cost debate usually ignores the cost of the status quo. In 2024, Brazil recorded more than 440,000 absences due to mental and behavioral disorders, according to data released by the Ministry of Social Security, a record level in the historical series. This does not, by itself, prove that the four-day week is the solution. But it helps explain why the topic went from being an "aspirational benefit" to being discussed as a policy of productivity, risk, and sustainability.

## Sectors Are Not Equal (and the Law Shouldn't Treat Everything as If It Were)

A frequent objection is valid: continuous care work and industrial work do not behave like knowledge-intensive work. In functions of attendance and presence (cashier, factory floor operation, logistics), reducing hours may require rescheduling and, in some cases, hiring. In knowledge functions (product, marketing, legal, finance), part of the gain usually comes from cutting redundant coordination: too many meetings, slow approvals, rework, and information searching.

The Brazilian pilot, having majority participation from companies willing to experiment, tends to better reflect the second group than the first.

## The Infrastructure Factor: What Changes When the Company Organizes "Invisible Work"

When discussing the reduced week, many organizations start with the calendar ("which day off?"), when in practice the starting point is usually operational:

- Documented processes (who decides what, in how long, with which criteria)

- Less dependence on interruptions (demand queue, clear prioritization)

- More asynchronous communication (decision recorded in document, not in endless meeting)

- Integrated tools for tasks, deadlines, files, and communication, reducing rework

Here, technology helps — not as a "magic solution," but as a way to reduce coordination friction.

## Technology as an Enabler — Not as a Substitute

Digital tools do not create productivity from nothing: automating a bad process only makes the problem faster. But when the company already knows what needs to be done and how to decide, consistent digital infrastructure can reduce time spent on coordination (scheduling, simple approvals, status updates, document searching) and free up hours for main work. Integrated digital platforms (tasks/projects, communication, calendar, and automation) help precisely at this point: less rework and less context switching between tools. In practice, the gain usually appears in small routines that add up to many hours over the week.

## Practical Examples of How Infrastructure Helps

**Marketing agency (15 people, São Paulo):** instead of exchanging loose messages on WhatsApp to approve pieces, the team uses a simple task flow → review → approval. This reduces "back and forth" and avoids rework when the client changes the briefing.

**Accounting office (8 people, interior):** recurring client demands (tax payments, payroll, declarations) enter as standardized tasks with checklist and deadline. When someone is on leave, another person can take over because the information is documented in the task itself.

**Dental clinic (2 units):** confirmations, rescheduling, and document requests no longer depend on individual calls. A simple service funnel and automated messages reduce no-shows and free up reception for exceptional cases.

**Distributor (operations + sales):** orders and pending items become cards with status (separation, delivery, financial). This reduces daily "alignment meetings" because the status is already visible to everyone.
