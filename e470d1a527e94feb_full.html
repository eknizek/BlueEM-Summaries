A Country That Doesn't Trust Itself

20 April 2026 · 09:50 AM

# A Country That Doesn't Trust Itself

In Brazil, improving one's life often doesn't mean accessing better public services. It means escaping them. Health insurance to avoid depending on the public health system, private school to avoid public education, a car to avoid public transportation. Social mobility is frequently measured by the distance one can place between themselves and the State. It's not exactly a citizenship project. It's more of an exit plan.

This isn't just inequality: it's a deeper trait. Brazil functions largely as a low-trust society. The data confirms this intuition. According to a 2022 OECD survey, only 26% of Brazilians declared having high or moderate confidence in the federal government. More revealing: about seven in ten Brazilians believe that public institutions don't function in society's interest. Confidence in parties and public services follows the same pattern. But the most uncomfortable data isn't in Brasília. It's within ourselves.

An Ipsos survey across 30 countries showed that fewer than 15% of Brazilians believe that most people can be trusted, one of the lowest indices in the survey. Latinobarômetro, over decades, records a similar pattern. The problem, therefore, isn't just government. It's society.

But there's a point more relevant than simple perception of corruption. The Brazilian doesn't distrust simply because they believe the State is dishonest. They distrust because they don't believe it functions. And this distinction changes everything.

Distrust of a moral nature is an accusation of character: it points to corruption, deviation, to politicians who steal. Operational distrust is an accusation of capacity: it questions whether the State can deliver, whether policies adapt, whether complaining accomplishes anything. The second form is structurally harder to reverse. It doesn't respond to arrests or punished scandals. It responds to decades of consistent delivery.

The data points in this direction. The same OECD survey indicates that only a third of Brazilians believe the government would change a public policy in the face of popular pressure, and fewer than one in three believe the State would incorporate opinions collected in public consultations. It's not skepticism about the politician's honesty. It's skepticism about the usefulness of participating. The question that organizes this behavior isn't whether there's deviation, but whether it matters.

This transforms the governance problem into an expectation problem. A society that distrusts morally can still be mobilized by indignation, by leaders who promise change. A society that distrusts operationally is harder to reach.

Institutions that aren't trustworthy inevitably become more fragile. Without trust, compliance with rules depends on constant supervision, cooperation becomes the exception, and the cost of implementing public policies increases. The system operates with more friction, more bureaucracy, and less predictability. In this environment, corruption stops being merely a deviation and becomes part of functioning.

In low-trust societies, small deviations tend to be rationalized. Cutting in line, contacting someone you know, speeding up a process aren't seen only as individual failures, but as ways of navigating a system perceived as inefficient or arbitrary. It's not exactly approval of corruption, but something more uncomfortable: the feeling that following the rules might be, in many cases, the worst available strategy.

This is the point where low trust stops being perception and becomes collective behavior. When they can, people leave. The supplementary health sector closed 2024 with more than 52 million beneficiaries, continuous growth even in years of economic crisis. Migration to individual transportation and preference for private education follow a similar trajectory. It's not just about choice, but about self-defense. Citizenship stops being the demand for quality universal public services and becomes the ability to not need them.

The effect on the political system is more profound than it appears. A society that doesn't trust also doesn't hold anyone accountable collectively. Each group seeks its own solution, each individual tries to solve their own problem, and space for collective projects shrinks. Not for lack of ideas, but for lack of social foundation to sustain them.

Politics adapts to this environment. In low-trust societies, the incentive to build collective projects is lower: individual calculation prevails because experience teaches that coordination rarely pays off. The Brazilian political system reflects this logic with precision.

In recent years, the country has witnessed the strengthening of Congress and progressive decentralization of the budget. In theory, this could bring public spending closer to local needs. In practice, it produced fragmentation on a massive scale. Parliamentary amendments, which represented less than 4% of the Union's discretionary spending in 2014, reached almost 29% in 2020 and still account for about 20% in 2024, according to FGV-IBRE data. In absolute terms, they jumped from R$6 billion to more than R$44 billion in the same period.

The effect is structural. When almost half of direct federal transfers to municipalities and states pass through the Legislature, the budget stops being the expression of a national plan and becomes the sum of individual plans. Each parliamentarian operates as an autonomous unit for resource allocation. There's a rationality to this: in an environment of low confidence in central government, legislators have an incentive to directly control resource allocation, reducing dependence on an Executive perceived as untrustworthy. The problem is that this individual logic makes collective coordination impossible. What makes sense for each person makes it harder for what works for everyone.

Polarization completes the picture. If responsibility always lies on the other side, institutions stop being seen as spaces for solutions and start being treated as instruments of dispute. This erodes trust even further and reinforces the cycle. Less trust leads to more fragmentation, which in turn further reduces the capacity for coordination.

A low-trust society isn't just a society that suspects everything. It's a society in which collective success is being replaced by individual survival strategies, both in everyday behavior and in political organization. Democracies need citizens willing to bet on a national plan, to accept temporary losses in exchange for shared future gains, to trust that rules matter even when nobody is watching. In a society where this bet seems naive, democracy doesn't disappear formally. It empties from within. It continues to exist as a procedure, but progressively loses what makes it relevant: the belief that it serves some purpose.
