# Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises Alert Over Food Warning Label Changes

The project to modify provisions on nutritional labeling and front-of-package warning systems in Colombia has raised alarms among micro, small and medium-sized enterprises (MSMEs). The National Observatory of the sector has warned that concerns are emerging regarding the costs and operational challenges that its implementation would entail.

Through a statement signed by its president, Rosmery Quintero Castro, the Observatory expressed its concern regarding the update of resolutions 810 of 2021, 2492 of 2022, and 254 of 2023, regulations that govern nutritional information on food products and warning seals directed at consumers.

Although the organization recognizes the importance of strengthening public policies aimed at improving consumer information, promoting healthy lifestyles, and preventing non-communicable diseases, it emphasizes that any regulatory adjustment must consider the real adaptive capacity of the national productive apparatus.

According to the Observatory, the proposal would impact approximately 51,200 companies in the country, of which 98.5% correspond to micro, small, and medium production units—a key segment of the business fabric and employment generation.

The primary source of concern lies in the costs associated with implementation. Companies would have to assume packaging redesign processes, material reprinting, technical adjustments in production, inventory management, and logistical adaptations. These actions demand financial resources, execution time, and technical support—factors that are not always available to MSMEs, especially in a challenging economic context.

The Observatory warns that these measures are being proposed in a business environment still facing demand deceleration, rising operational costs, financing access difficulties, tax pressure, and insecurity problems. In this scenario, the adaptive and investment capacity of many companies is limited, which could hinder compliance with new regulatory requirements.

Another critical point is the frequency of regulatory changes in short periods, which generates uncertainty and complicates business planning, especially in organizations with narrow margins. The absence of adequate transition periods is also a cause for concern. Insufficient timeframes can affect operational continuity and investment decisions, in addition to creating cost pressures.

According to the Observatory, part of these increased costs could be passed on to the final consumer, with possible effects on prices. In this regard, the entity reiterated the need to achieve a balance between consumer protection and business sustainability. In its view, public health strengthening should be accompanied by regulatory decisions that recognize the reality of the productive sector.

As proposals, it presented to the Ministry of Health recommendations to conduct a prior regulatory impact analysis, establish viable transition periods, design differential measures for MSMEs, and provide technical support.

The debate over front-of-package labeling has gained relevance in recent years as part of strategies to address non-communicable diseases. However, its implementation has also generated discussions about its impact on industry. In this context, the regulatory update reopens the discussion on how to articulate public health objectives with business sustainability, at a time when MSMEs remain key to the national economy.

The front-of-package labeling system in Colombia has been a public policy tool to inform consumers about the content of products high in sugars, sodium, and saturated fats. Its implementation has been accompanied by debates among health authorities, industry, and business groups regarding the scope of measures and their economic effects, especially in the small and medium business segment.
