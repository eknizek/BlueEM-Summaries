# Wall Street rises nearly 3% on US-Iran ceasefire session rebound

The main indicators on Wall Street closed Wednesday with gains close to 3% following a rebound session attributed to a two-week ceasefire between the United States and Iran, conditioned on Tehran reopening the Strait of Hormuz.

At the opening bell on the New York trading floor, the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 2.85%, reaching 47,909 points; the S&P 500 advanced 2.51%, reaching 6,782 units, and the Nasdaq progressed 2.80%, reaching 22,634 points, amid a notable decline in volatility.

The market opened with gains after U.S. President Donald Trump accepted the pause in the conflict on Tuesday, just hours before his ultimatum to Iran expired, generating optimism that particularly affected oil prices today.

Texas barrel futures (WTI) registered their largest daily drop since 2020, exceeding 16%, down to $94.41, despite contradictory information between both countries about reopening the Strait of Hormuz.

Nevertheless, stock exchanges remained in the green and appeared to react with increases to a message posted by Trump on his social network today in which he spoke of "collaboration" with Iran, of "very productive regime change," and of "elimination of nuclear remnants."

By sector, the largest increases were for industrial companies (3.75%), communications (3.4%), and basic materials (3.4%), while the only segment in red was energy (-3.66%).

On the corporate front, Meta soared 6.5% after presenting a new artificial intelligence (AI) model designed to integrate its technology into the company's applications and social networks ecosystem.

Iran said today it had interrupted navigation of oil tankers through the strait in response to massive surprise bombardments that Israel launched today against Lebanon, according to reports from the Fars agency, linked to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard.

White House spokesperson Karoline Leavitt called the reports "false" and stated they had observed "a rebound in strait traffic."

"What is being observed, in reports and physical costs, is not a complete reopening of the Strait of Hormuz but rather a formalization of existing conditions, in which passage continues to depend on coordination with Iranian armed forces and is subject to technical limits," said analyst Janic Shah of Rystad Energy in a note.

In other markets, at Wall Street's close, the 10-year U.S. bond yield fell to 4.305%; gold rose to $4,748 per ounce; the euro appreciated and traded at $1.662; and bitcoin climbed nearly 4%, reaching $71,444.
