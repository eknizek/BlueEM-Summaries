# Petz Cobasi Accelerates Digital Strategy and Targets Merger Synergies

Paulo Nassar spent more than 40 years leading Cobasi with typical owner management, putting into practice the saying that "the owner's eye fattens the ox." Now he faces the challenge of leading a publicly traded company born from a merger with Petz, with approximately R$ 8 billion in revenue and over 500 stores. At this new moment, in addition to delegating more, he wants to double down on digital investments. The move gains even more weight because store traffic this year is expected to be lower. According to the executive, events like the World Cup and elections typically directly affect retail. "We'll have to stop, close the stores and after the games nobody goes out shopping anymore," he said in an interview with Call de Negócios, a NeoFeed program in partnership with CNN Brasil, aired on CNN Money monthly.

According to Nassar, more than 40% of sales from both networks already come from online channels, in an operation that integrates e-commerce and physical stores. The model combines in-store pickup, same-day deliveries, and more aggressive convenience initiatives, with deliveries that can reach up to 1 hour in some regions. "We intend to grow revenue and double our bet on digital sales," said the CEO.

With approximately 10% market share and less than 1% of the sector's stores, Petz Cobasi's greatest competition is not a major player, but rather thousands of small merchants who cut prices emotionally to avoid losing customers and use marketplaces to sell. Even though the company can obtain better prices from suppliers with economies of scale from the merger, Nassar does not intend to compress margins much to outmaneuver small businesses. "The customer wants to enter a store, find what they're looking for and be well served. Price is the third item," said Nassar, who also believes the company serves as a pricing reference for other players.

Beyond a broader assortment, the group bets on offering services to win the preference of pet owners. Today, the company brings together hundreds of grooming centers for bathing and nail trimming — which should be scaled through a franchise model — veterinary clinics, and 24-hour hospitals, forming an ecosystem that increases recurrence.

After growing at rates of up to 30% during the pandemic, the pet market entered a deceleration cycle. Even so, the company remains optimistic. The expectation is to expand revenue, maintain network expansion, and capture operational gains from integration. "We should end the year with basically around 515 stores," said Nassar, considering the opening of new units and the sale of points required by the Administrative Council for Economic Defense (Cade).

For Lucas Esteves, head of retail at Santander, digital advancement should already begin appearing in results, while merger synergies will materialize over time. "Value capture should happen over time, with sequential margin gains," he evaluated. "Already in the first quarter, we expect low double-digit revenue growth, largely driven by digital channels."

Meanwhile, the merger still reveals friction points typical of large integrations. The loyalty programs of both networks remain separate, with no defined timeline for unification, and private labels will continue to coexist, although they will now be sold under both banners.
