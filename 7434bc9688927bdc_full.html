# Argentina's Construction Chamber Says $25 Billion Annual Loss From Poor Infrastructure Maintenance

The Construction Chamber (Camarco) stated that Argentina loses the equivalent of $25 billion annually due to inadequate maintenance of its infrastructure. "Without infrastructure, there will be no growth or sustainable development," the entity said through a statement from its Federal Council.

The organization that groups construction companies issued its "147th Declaration of the Federal Council" on Monday from Rosario, in which it praised the Government's decision to move forward with road concessions as a "positive step," but also expressed its "deep concern" about the "emergency" situation of infrastructure, particularly roads and highways.

The builders also complained about the debt that the national State maintains with companies in the sector. "We express our concern about the infrastructure emergency. Lack of maintenance, paralysis of works, and the absence of a comprehensive plan have generated an infrastructure debt that compromises road safety, logistics competitiveness, and quality of life," said the entity headed by businessman Gustavo Weiss.

"Argentina loses 25 billion dollars a year due to lack of adequate maintenance. Without infrastructure, there will be no growth or sustainable development," added Camarco's statement.

"The OECD has clearly pointed out that lack of investment in infrastructure is one of Argentina's main structural problems. Without solving this deficit, the country will not be able to integrate competitively into international trade or sustain a long-term growth process."

In that context, the entity qualified the start of the road concession system as "a positive step toward modernization of the sector." However, it added, "much work remains to be done for these mechanisms to translate into effective improvements to the road network. In particular, the maintenance of the 30,000 kilometers of roads that are not concessionable must be resolved, and these constitute a vital part of national connectivity."

The Chamber stated that "the historical debt of the National State with construction companies remains unresolved" and that "the lack of definition about the bonus announced for its payment generates uncertainty and worsens the financial crisis in the sector." In that sense, it added, "every day of delay means more companies on the brink of collapse and more jobs at risk."

The Chamber's statement made an "urgent call to all political, social and economic actors" to design and execute a national infrastructure plan that includes:

- Immediate reactivation of paralyzed works

- Recognition and payment of the State's debt with construction companies

- Normalization of the execution of specific funds, such as those allocated to the Integrated Road System (SISVIAL) or the Water Fund

- A federal infrastructure program covering roads, railroads, ports, energy, water and sanitation

- Mixed financing mechanisms that integrate the private sector

- Promotion of necessary measures to activate a mortgage credit program suited to the purchasing capacity of families

- Policies for transparency and efficiency in the execution of works

- Promotion of formal employment and training in construction-related trades

The entity added that the general economic situation "has also impacted private construction, reducing projects and slowing investments." It noted that construction companies in all segments "are going through a profound crisis as the sector that has felt the adjustment the most" and that "the decline in activity directly hits thousands of workers and more than 60 branches of the economy linked to construction."
