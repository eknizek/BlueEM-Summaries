# Cirkula: Peruvian app reducing food waste expands categories and retail partners

Is it possible to save money and combat the amount of food waste in Peru? A Peruvian startup saw the opportunity to create an ecosystem that could make it a reality: Cirkula. Michelle Gomberoff, CEO of the platform, explains that it functions as a sort of marketplace that connects businesses that have surpluses of good quality food—with expiration dates approaching—that seek to offer them at better prices for greater sales options with users willing to buy them and looking for discounted food.

The executive emphasizes that products in the application have a minimum 40% discount and have recommended consumption dates. To date, among more than 1,000 affiliated businesses are restaurants, cafeterias, and supermarkets. Gomberoff advances that they will enter more categories, with which they seek this dual impact: generating savings and reducing waste in the capital.

"This year we are looking to consolidate the supermarket segment, convenience stores, and distributors within the application. We started little by little with food, but many of our clients also work with pet food or personal care products that have the problem that the product is expiring, which will generate a negative environmental impact," she states.

Thus, she narrates, she saw the opportunity for Cirkula to mobilize these new categories, which are beginning to open this year. "We are exploring some of the categories that make sense for our clients," she notes.

The executive explains that in the case of distributors, which have warehouses further from Lima, they created a Cirkula-owned warehouse model, where brands can dispatch products and from there—Miraflores—the platform conducts the sale of different products. Gomberoff recalls that platform users generally pick up products from the point of sale according to proximity. Delivery usage is very minimal, she says.

How frequent is purchasing, on average? Today it is a little more than twice a month. "This is what we want to continue promoting, which is why we are also expanding categories both in other verticals and in supermarkets and distributors," she mentions.

On the business side, the executive comments that these affiliate, choose schedules, and publish their surplus products in real-time on the platform. Currently, Cirkula operates in Lima for operational and logistics reasons, as well as to take advantage of growth profitability. For now, they do not see the option of going to other cities in the country, but hope to do so at some point. However, they have made the leap to Quito, Ecuador.

"Right now we are focusing on cities that bring us profitability. We are considered an environmental impact startup and that is our purpose," she mentions. In fact, the financing they have been able to raise for model development has been from environmental impact investors from Germany, Norway, Ecuador, and Peru. Later on, she confesses, they are also interested in reaching the Bolivian market.

"We have it in mind for a next expansion. What we do is enter the ecosystem one or two years before, to get to know other companies, investors, and mentors," she explains.

How did the model arise? Gomberoff explains that they saw it seven or eight years ago in another country and it clicked. They asked themselves how in Latin America and Peru, where gastronomy is something so cultural, there was nothing similar.

"We seek to have healthy competition and new players to appear because that generates the formation of legislation for the reduction of food waste. Or, what happens in other markets, where businesses or brands have alternative platforms or have to donate," she refers.
