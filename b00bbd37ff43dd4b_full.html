# Brazil set for record wheat imports in 2026

Brazil, historically reliant on wheat imports, is expected to deepen that dependence this year as domestic output is set to decline sharply. The result is likely to be the largest wheat import volume in the country's history, according to industry analysts.

Consultancy Safras & Mercado projects imports of 8.2 million tonnes in the 2026/27 cycle, surpassing the previous record of 7.1 million tonnes in the 2006/07 season. TF Consultoria Agroeconômica also anticipates record imports, albeit at a lower range of between 6.7 million and 8 million tonnes.

According to the National Supply Company (CONAB), Brazil's wheat production is expected to fall 16% this season from the previous crop, to 6.6 million tonnes—well below estimated demand of 13.305 million tonnes for milling. The combination of tighter domestic supply and firm demand is increasing reliance on foreign wheat.

"Brazil is structurally a wheat importer," said Elcio Bento, an analyst at Safras & Mercado. "If we produce less, we need to import more," he added.

The drop in production reflects a mix of factors, including adverse weather, high costs and weaker incentives for farmers. "The agricultural sector is highly indebted, and wheat has lost competitiveness due to falling international prices, which discourages planting," Bento said. In 2025, wheat futures declined on the Chicago exchange for much of the year. In 2026, prices have rebounded, rising nearly 19%, according to Valor Data.

Eduardo Bulgarelli, trading director at Bunge, said the increase in imports also follows market dynamics. "Higher imports are a natural movement, especially during the off-season, when domestic availability is more limited. Imported wheat becomes more relevant to ensure mills' physical coverage until the new crop arrives and to secure market supply," he said.

Production costs are also contributing to the weaker output outlook. "Rising costs, particularly for fertilizers, tend to squeeze farmers' margins, which may lead to reduced planted area and lower investment in technology. This can affect both the volume and quality of wheat produced in Brazil, reinforcing structural import dependence," Bulgarelli added.

Milling companies' storage capacity has historically been limited. "Mills do not hold large inventories. They typically operate with about one month of supply, so they need to import continuously," said Rubens Barbosa, president of the Brazilian Wheat Industry Association (Abitrigo).

Argentina remains Brazil's main source of imported wheat, supported by geographic proximity, Mercosur membership—which grants tariff exemptions—and lower logistics costs. "Argentina is Brazil's main wheat supplier and would only lose that position under very specific circumstances," Bento said.

This season, however, the quality of Argentine wheat has raised concerns among mills. "There is a quality issue in Argentina. The wheat has not fully met the Brazilian industry's requirements, particularly in terms of protein content," he said.

Bulgarelli noted that despite Argentina's large current crop—around 27.8 million tonnes—the wheat "does not fully meet the needs of Brazil's industry." As a result, mills have been seeking alternative origins, "including in the Northern Hemisphere, to meet specific quality requirements."

Wheat sourced from outside Mercosur comes at a higher cost for mills, as imports above 750,000 tonnes are subject to a 10% tariff. According to Luiz Pacheco of T&F Consultoria Agroeconômica, the quality of Argentine wheat has deteriorated to the point that the market is "at a standstill." "Most of the crop is feed-grade wheat, with 10.5% protein. Brazil requires at least 11.5%," he said.

Another concern for the sector is the war in the Middle East, which could affect the supply of Argentina's next wheat crop, scheduled for planting from late May. The Buenos Aires grain exchange has already cut its forecast for planted area in the 2026/27 season to 6.5 million hectares, down 3% from the previous crop. One factor behind the expected decline is higher fertilizer costs linked to the conflict.

According to Barbosa, this could require Brazil to purchase an additional 1 million to 1.5 million tonnes from alternative origins. This shift would increase the share of suppliers such as the United States, Canada, and Russia, while maintaining the relevance of Paraguay and Uruguay within Mercosur.

For the coming months, Bunge expects the market to remain challenging. "Until the new Argentine crop becomes available, Brazil is likely to rely more on Northern Hemisphere origins to meet quality requirements," Bulgarelli said. "The market will operate in an environment that demands greater management of origins, quality, and costs to ensure consistent supply."
