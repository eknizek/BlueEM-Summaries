Domino's plans to 'grow its slice' in Brazil

23 April 2026 · 02:35 PM

# Domino's Plans to "Grow Its Slice" in Brazil

The Domino's pizza chain wants to expand its "slice" of the Brazilian market. Currently operating 220 stores (22 of which are company-owned), the company plans to open 150 additional units over three years — reaching 370 total — with a focus on smaller cities with up to 150,000 inhabitants. At least 35 franchise contracts will be signed and 20 stores will open this year alone.

Controlled by Vinci Compass, the company achieved revenue of R$ 650 million in 2025. With the new strategy, the goal is to exceed R$ 750 million in revenue in Brazil by year-end, representing a 15% increase.

The expansion plan also includes reducing the size of physical units. Until 2023, the strategy was to open larger stores, but these required significant investment. Now, the focus is on ensuring greater profitability for franchisees, starting with units as small as 4.9 square meters (m²) in kiosk format.

"In the past, opening a Domino's store required at least R$ 1 million in investment. With interest rates at this level, I don't have the courage to sell a franchise at that price. It was necessary to change this approach and reduce costs to enable growth. Now, we sell starting from R$ 300,000 in a smaller model," says Orestes Miraglia, CEO of Domino's in Brazil.

Of the new 150 stores planned, at least 10% should be in the most compact format. The majority, around 60%, will be the "mini" model, ranging from 20 m² to 50 m², which can be installed in gas stations, for example. The remaining 30% will be larger restaurants, above 150 m².

The capex budgeted for installing this new package of stores through 2029 will be approximately R$ 70 million, based on an average cost of R$ 450,000. Investment volume comes from the franchisee, with company support.

Beyond reducing store size, the master franchisor of the network in Brazil has also begun nationalizing costs instead of importing equipment from the United States, such as ovens. This reduced expenses for unit opening.

"We started buying this oven in Brazil, which previously had to come from an American supplier. This reduced the cost by four times. Equipment costs dropped by at least R$ 200,000, and this is essential equipment for Domino's without compromising quality," Miraglia states.

In the case of kiosks, importing the oven from abroad would cost R$ 400,000 — more than the entire mini-store installation value. With domestic market options, the franchisee spends R$ 50,000 to install two small ovens.

Domino's first kiosk was opened last year at Novo Rio bus station. By year-end, three more kiosks will open: at Tietê Bus Station in São Paulo; at Campinas bus station; and at Galeão Airport in Rio de Janeiro.

According to the CEO, who previously was a Domino's franchisee in Minas Gerais, the compact format initiative, which he created based on demand for a cheaper and scalable model, should be replicated in other countries where the pizza chain operates.

"I had to prove to the Americans that this model would work and that it was possible to make pizza to serve fresh in five m². And I was able to demonstrate it. This kiosk in Rio de Janeiro generates 25% profit," the CEO says.

Miraglia says the chain won't open a larger number of kiosks because, despite being more affordable, the location must be highly strategic with significant foot traffic, such as airports and bus stations.

In the geographic expansion plan, the strategic goal is reaching municipalities farther from capitals that don't yet have a Domino's store. At least one-third of the new stores in the 150-unit opening plan should be in smaller cities.

"We want to go to Brazil's interior. We're signing store openings in these municipalities with capex of at most R$ 400,000. In Divinópolis (MG), for example, we're arriving with a 50 m² restaurant," he explains.

In smaller formats, the investment payback period, according to Miraglia, ranges between 12 and 36 months.

"I prefer franchisees invest less and earn more. I analyze locations and help entrepreneurs understand the best opportunity for them," he explains.

In his assessment, capitals today already have a significant number of Domino's stores and it wouldn't be healthy to open in already-saturated areas. Of the current 220 stores in Brazil, almost half are concentrated in just São Paulo (47) and Rio de Janeiro (49). There are stores in all state capitals.

"It doesn't make sense to divide the 'filet mignon' and pressure franchisees already generating revenue in these locations. Now, if we open a city, the impact will be significant. In João Pessoa, for example, we have one. The ideal now is to expand into Paraíba's interior," he explains.

Lajeado in Rio Grande do Sul and Pouso Alegre in Minas Gerais are examples of cities that just received Domino's restaurants. The chain will now reach Lagarto in Sergipe and Jequié in Bahia.

"In São Paulo's interior, we're already strong. There's no need to open more. We want to move away from this axis," says the CEO.

## 800 Pizzas Per Hour

With this expansion, the company will quickly surpass the current volume of seven million pizzas produced annually in Brazil stores. This means Domino's sells 20,000 units daily, or 800 pizzas per hour.

To achieve this pizza sales volume, each store's delivery radius cannot exceed one mile (1.6 kilometers). The maximum timeframe for the product to reach customers cannot exceed 25 minutes.

The secret to meeting deadlines, according to Miraglia, is having a proprietary delivery network, even though stores are also registered on delivery platforms like iFood. Using an artificial intelligence digital system, the master franchisor can show store owners that expanding beyond a one-mile horizon would require higher costs.

"We've proven to franchisees that if they expand too much, they won't be able to serve well. Today, 70% of orders are within a 500-meter radius of the store," the CEO states.

According to Miraglia, Domino's in Brazil has grown, considering same-store metrics, for 11 consecutive quarters. The national level exceeds the global scenario of 10 consecutive quarters.
