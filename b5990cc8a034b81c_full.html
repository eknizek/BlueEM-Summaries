# Domino's Plan to Grow Market Share in Brazil

The Domino's pizza chain wants to expand its "slice" of the Brazilian market. Currently operating 220 stores (22 of which are company-owned), the company plans to open 150 additional units over three years and reach 370 locations, focusing on smaller cities with up to 150,000 inhabitants. At least 35 franchise contracts will be signed and 20 stores opened this year alone.

Controlled by Vinci Compass, the company achieved revenue of R$ 650 million in 2025. With the new strategy, the target is to exceed R$ 750 million in revenue in Brazil by year-end, representing a 15% increase.

The expansion plan also includes reducing the physical size of units. Until 2023, the strategy was to open larger stores, but these represented high investment volumes. Now, the focus is on guaranteeing greater profitability for franchisees, starting with units of up to 4.9 square meters (m²) in kiosk format.

"In the past, opening a Domino's store required at least R$ 1 million in investment. With interest rates at this level, I don't have the courage to sell a franchise at that value. It was necessary to change this approach and reduce costs to be able to grow. Now, we sell starting at R$ 300,000 in a smaller model," says Orestes Miraglia, CEO of Domino's in Brazil.

Of the 150 new stores planned, at least 10% should be in the most reduced format. Most, around 60%, will be the "small" model, measuring 20 m² to 50 m², which can be installed at gas stations, for example. The remaining 30% will be larger restaurants, above 150 m².

The planned capex for installing the new package of stores through 2029 will be around R$ 70 million, accounting for an average cost of R$ 450,000. The investment volume comes from the franchisee, with company support.

Beyond reducing store size, the master franchisor for the chain in Brazil has also begun nationalizing some costs, rather than importing equipment from the United States, such as ovens. This has decreased expenses for unit opening.

"We started buying this oven in Brazil, which previously had to come from an American supplier. This reduced the cost by four times. Equipment costs dropped by at least R$ 200,000, and this is fundamental equipment for Domino's, without losing quality," Miraglia states.

In the case of the kiosk, if the oven were to be brought from abroad, the investment would be R$ 400,000, more than the entire value for installing the mini-store. With the option on the national market, the franchisee spends R$ 50,000 to install two small ovens.

Domino's first kiosk opened last year at the Novo Rio bus station. By year-end, three more kiosks will open: at Tietê Bus Station in São Paulo; at Campinas bus station; and at Galeão airport in Rio de Janeiro.

According to the CEO, who was previously a Domino's franchisee in Minas Gerais, the compact format initiative, which he created based on demand for a cheaper and scalable model, should be replicated in other countries where the pizza chain operates.

"I had to prove to the Americans that this model would work and that it was possible to make pizza to be served fresh in a five-square-meter space. And we were able to demonstrate it. This kiosk in Rio de Janeiro delivers 25% profit," says the CEO.

Miraglia notes that the chain won't open a larger quantity of kiosks because, despite being more affordable, the location must be highly strategic with heavy foot traffic, such as airports and bus stations.

In the geographic expansion plan, the strategic plan is to reach municipalities farther from capitals that don't yet have a Domino's location. At least one-third of the new stores from the 150-unit opening plan should be in smaller cities.

"We want to go into Brazil's interior. We're signing store openings in these municipalities with capex of at most R$ 400,000. In Divinópolis, for example, we're arriving with a 50-square-meter restaurant," he explains.

In smaller formats, the investment payback period, according to Miraglia, is between 12 and 36 months.

"I prefer that franchisees invest less and earn more. I analyze the locations and help entrepreneurs understand the best opportunity for them," he explains.

In his assessment, capitals today already have a significant volume of Domino's stores and it wouldn't be healthy to open locations in regions already exploited by the chain. Of the current 220 stores in Brazil, almost half are located only in São Paulo (47) and Rio de Janeiro (49). There are stores in all state capitals.

"It doesn't make sense to divide the prime territory and pressure franchisees already generating revenue in those locations. Now, if we open a city, the impact will be significant. In João Pessoa, for example, we have one store. The ideal now is to expand into Paraíba's interior," he explains.

Lajeado in Rio Grande do Sul and Pouso Alegre in Minas Gerais are examples of cities that just received Domino's restaurants. The chain will now arrive in Lagarto, Sergipe, and Jequié, Bahia.

"In São Paulo's interior, we're already strong. There's no need to open more. We want to move away from this axis," says the CEO.

With this expansion, the company will quickly surpass the current volume of seven million pizzas produced annually in Brazilian stores. This means Domino's sells 20,000 units per day, or 800 pizzas per hour.

To achieve this pizza sales volume, each store's delivery radius cannot exceed one mile (1.6 kilometers). And the maximum timeframe for the product to reach customers cannot exceed 25 minutes. The secret, according to Miraglia, to meeting this deadline is having its own delivery network, even though stores are also registered on delivery platforms like iFood.

With a digital artificial intelligence system, the master franchisor can show store owners that it would require higher costs to deliver beyond a one-mile radius.

"We've proven to franchisees that if they expand too much, they won't be able to serve well. Today, 70% of orders are within a 500-meter radius of the store," says the CEO.

According to Miraglia, Domino's in Brazil has been growing, considering the same stores, for 11 consecutive quarters. The national level exceeds the global scenario, which is 10 consecutive quarters. From the chain's revenue in Brazil, at least 30% comes from delivery.
