# Market Expects Oil Prices to Fall, But Not to Pre-War Levels

By Juan Pablo Álvarez

May 1, 2026 | 01:00 PM

Oil prices show a clear divergence between what the spot market reflects and what futures contracts anticipate. For example, the Brent barrel closed April above US$110, while agreements for December 2026 closed below US$90. Even for April 2027, operations are closer to US$80. This suggests the market expects a gradual reduction in crude prices, though without confidence in a return to pre-Iran conflict levels, when Brent fluctuated between US$60 and US$70.

"U.S. inventories fell 6.23 million barrels, against an expectation of just 230,000, reflecting that the market is reacting to an immediate shortage," said Emanoelle Santos, market analyst at investment app XTB. The spot price, she explained, responds to panic over short-term supply, in a context where the world is drawing on U.S. reserves to compensate for the strait blockade.

"Futures, meanwhile, incorporate the expectation that the conflict will be resolved, the strait will reopen and Gulf supply will return to the market," she noted. However, she warned that this expectation is beginning to deteriorate. "The December contract surpassing US$80 for the first time is a signal that the market is beginning to lose faith in a quick resolution," she indicated.

Diego Barnuevo, market analyst at Ebury, offered his reading of the recent evolution of the spread between spot and futures: "Spot prices continue trading at a premium over short-term futures, reflecting the acute shortage of physical barrels and intense competition to obtain them immediately." Since the conflict began, he explained, futures have shown more contained evolution by discounting a relatively quick resolution and prompt recovery of traffic through the Strait of Hormuz.

"The ceasefire announced three weeks ago caused a sharp fall in spot prices, notably reducing the gap with futures," he clarified. However, in the last week, stalled negotiations and fears of new U.S. attacks have driven new increases in both markets. Still, the spread between spot and futures remains narrower. "This convergence reflects that the market no longer anticipates such an imminent reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. Instead, it assumes that flows will take time to recover and, when they do, it will be gradual, while immediate barrel shortages persist," he concluded.

## Impact on Investments

Regarding impact on investments, Santos held that a scenario with oil sustained above US$80 changes the reading of global markets. "In fixed income, it means that central banks—starting with the Federal Reserve—will have less room to relax monetary policy," she explained.

This pressures long durations and prolongs the validity of inverted or flat yield curves. In equities, the impact is sectoral. Energy and infrastructure benefit, while industrial and discretionary consumption sector margins are affected by higher costs. Technology, she added, also faces pressure from the effect of higher rates on valuations. In commodities, oil price increases act as an additional cost. "It functions as an implicit tax on copper and lithium, compressing margins in a context of slowing global demand," she concluded.

The Ebury executive also noted that the current scenario could provoke falls in sovereign bond prices, especially in the short end of the curve, given expectations of more persistent inflation and more restrictive monetary policy, or elevated interest rates for longer.

In the case of stocks, as long as major damage to oil infrastructure is avoided, the energy sector would be benefited by structurally higher oil and the persistent threat of new disruptions in the strait. Conversely, energy-intensive sectors such as airlines, transportation, or chemicals could see compressed margins. Energy commodities in general would be supported, along with fertilizers and, to a lesser extent, some food commodities.
