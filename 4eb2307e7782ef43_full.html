# La Granja Project Ranks Among World's Largest Copper Deposits with 22 Million Tonnes

The La Granja copper project positions itself as one of the most relevant deposits globally, with approximately 22 million tonnes of contained copper, as highlighted by Rafael Roberto, senior geologist at First Quantum Minerals, during his presentation at proEXPLO 2026.

The specialist emphasized that this volume places the deposit within the world's "top" copper projects, even surpassing other known assets in the country, which reinforces its strategic potential in the context of growing global demand for this metal.

Located in northern Peru, La Granja corresponds to a copper porphyry system with a complex geological configuration. The deposit consists of a telescoped porphyry system and an extensive hydrothermal breccia complex, whose structural control, mainly in northwest directions, is key to understanding the distribution of mineralization.

One of the most relevant aspects of the deposit is its high concentration of hypogene chalcocite, an uncommon characteristic that, according to Roberto's explanation, helps improve the copper grades of the deposit. This particularity differentiates it from other similar projects and increases its economic attractiveness.

The presentation also addressed the presence of arsenic as a critical element in characterizing the resource. The geologist indicated that its distribution is closely linked to veinlets and geological structures, which has allowed the identification of zones with greater or lesser metallurgical penalties. This understanding has been key to refining the geological model of the project.

In this regard, Roberto noted that one of the main challenges has been the reinterpretation of historical drilling data. Many of the old boreholes, of a subvertical nature, generated "noise" in the information by not adequately intercepting the mineralized structures. In response, the team has implemented a new approach that allows for improved representativeness of results.

As a result, the team succeeded in refining the boundaries of geological units, identifying fault zones associated with mineralization, and developing a more robust block model, integrating geological, structural, and metallurgical variables.
