La Granja Project stands out among world's largest copper deposits with 22 million tonnes

04 May 2026 · 09:18 PM

The La Granja copper project stands out as one of the most relevant deposits globally, with approximately 22 million tonnes of contained copper, according to Rafael Roberto, senior geologist at First Quantum Minerals, during his presentation at proEXPLO 2026.

The specialist emphasized that this volume places the deposit within the world's "top" copper projects, even surpassing other known assets in the country, which reinforces its strategic potential in the context of growing global demand for this metal.

Located in northern Peru, La Granja corresponds to a copper porphyry system with a complex geological configuration. The deposit consists of a telescoped porphyry system and an extensive hydrothermal breccia complex, whose structural control, mainly in northwest directions, is key to understanding the distribution of mineralization.

One of the most relevant aspects of the deposit is its high concentration of hypogene chalcocite, an uncommon characteristic that, according to Roberto, contributes to improving the copper grades of the deposit. This particularity differentiates it from other similar projects and increases its economic attractiveness.

The presentation also addressed the presence of arsenic as a critical element in characterizing the resource. The geologist indicated that its distribution is closely linked to veinlets and geological structures, which has allowed for the identification of zones with greater or lesser metallurgical penalties. This understanding has been key to refining the project's geological model.

In this regard, Roberto noted that one of the main challenges has been the reinterpretation of historical drilling data. Many of the old boreholes, which were subvertical in nature, generated "noise" in the information by not adequately intercepting the mineralized structures. Faced with this, the team implemented a new approach that improves the representativeness of the results.

As a result, the geological unit boundaries were refined, fault zones associated with mineralization were identified, and a more robust block model was developed, integrating geological, structural, and metallurgical variables.
