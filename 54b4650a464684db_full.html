# Brazilian Government Bans Polymarket and Kalshi; Blocks Prediction Markets in Brazil

The government blocked access to Polymarket and Kalshi as part of action against so-called prediction market platforms that, according to authorities, violate federal gambling laws. A total of 27 websites were blocked for offering "illegal betting," said Finance Minister Dario Durigan at a press conference on Friday (24th) in Brasília, after the government published a resolution prohibiting prediction markets linked to elections, sports and other events.

Durigan presented the measure as part of a broader effort to protect Brazilians' finances and address rising levels of family debt, a problem that President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva attributes in part to online betting.

"We defend stricter supervision and very careful regulation, which will continue to advance, so that we can contain the negative externalities and social damage that unregulated gambling causes to the Brazilian population," said Durigan.

Polymarket did not immediately respond to a request for comment. A Kalshi spokesperson said the company is analyzing the resolution.

Prediction markets like those offered by Polymarket have grown in popularity in recent years, giving users the chance to trade contracts on sporting events, election results, economic data releases and even the return of Jesus Christ. Kalshi, one of the largest platforms, was co-founded by a Brazilian and recently announced plans to expand to customers in the country.

But the websites also face questions about possible insider information use and widespread access to online betting, and Brazil is among a growing list of countries applying legal scrutiny to these platforms. Countries such as France, Hungary and Portugal have prohibited their citizens from operating on Polymarket in recent years, and regulators state that its bets fall under gambling rules and that the company operated without proper licensing.

State regulators in the United States have also attempted to shut down prediction markets, accusing them of offering unlicensed gambling. But the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, which regulates the sector, has appealed to the courts against the states.

The resolution issued by the National Monetary Council—which brings together government members and the Central Bank—prohibited the trading of derivatives whose underlying assets are linked to sporting events, online gambling and "real or virtual events of a political, electoral, social, cultural or entertainment nature."

However, unlike restrictions adopted in other countries, the resolution left room for certain contracts. Bets linked to economic-financial indices, for example, may be allowed at the discretion of the Securities Commission (CVM).

Brazil's main stock exchange, B3, had considered entering the growing prediction markets space. The exchange has already confirmed the launch of six new contracts on April 27th, linked to the Ibovespa index, the real and bitcoin, and was studying expanding the offering to include contracts based on events. Among the possibilities under analysis were products linked to elections. B3 had previously sought legal advice on whether Brazilian legislation allows contracts tied to election results. These contracts could have been launched before the October presidential election, a close contest between leftist Lula and Senator Flávio Bolsonaro. Recent polls show the two tied in a potential runoff.

The new resolution, however, prohibits this practice. The council assigned the CVM responsibility for issuing additional regulations and overseeing application of the new regulatory framework.
