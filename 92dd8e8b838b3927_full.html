# Congress makes final push to unblock tax exemption bill for data centers

The processing of the Special Tax Regime for Data Center Services (Redata), a government fiscal incentive program designed to attract investments to the segment, has stalled again, this time in the Senate. The main parliamentary fronts of the productive sector in Congress are beginning to organize a political pressure movement to unblock the plenary vote. But the window to vote on a project that doesn't even have a rapporteur in the Senate yet is narrow. The atmosphere is already one of pre-election campaign — several parliamentarians will attempt reelection or support allies for state governments and assemblies as well as for the National Congress itself.

At the end of last year, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's government (PT) issued a provisional measure (MP) that zeroed out taxes such as IPI, PIS/Cofins and import duties affecting items used by data centers, but the Legislature allowed it to expire. The intention was always to increase data processing in Brazil. During the four months the law was in effect, these benefits had an impact. But as the approval deadline expired, the tax reduction ceased to be valid, generating insecurity among investors who were planning to invest billions of reals in the Brazilian market.

After this, the government leader in the Chamber, Deputy José Guimarães (PT-CE), submitted a project in line with the Executive, which was even approved by the Chamber. But since it arrived at the Senate in February, it has "stalled" despite support from parties both in the government base and in the opposition. Even the government leader in the Senate, Randolfe Rodrigues (PT-AP), attempted an agreement to include Redata in the text of another project dealing with tax incentives that was approved in plenary, but the strategy was unsuccessful.

Behind the scenes, the government itself blames Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP), president of the Senate, for having held up Redata's processing in the Senate, a source from the economic team admits confidentially to NeoFeed. "We don't know why it stalled," they say. The government, however, hopes that the bill will be approved at some point this year.

In this context, a coalition of fronts is being mobilized, including the Entrepreneurship Front (FPE) and the Agriculture Front (FPA), to charge Alcolumbre and other Congressional leadership to try one more chance to approve Guimarães' bill.

The president of the Parliamentary Front for Competitive Brazil, Deputy Júlio Lopes (PP-RJ), admits that the electoral backdrop and emerging issues like the war in the Middle East "help to hinder" the vote on the Redata project, which ends up taking a backseat. "The bill is paralyzed, we need to see how it will be resumed, but we have urgency and will pressure the Senate and also the Chamber to unblock the issue," Lopes told NeoFeed. "Businesspeople are crazy to make investments, but projects are paralyzed. In the meantime, Milei [Javier, Argentine president] was faster and Argentina has already attracted billions of dollars in investments. So it's important that we speed up here in Brazil."

The Front held a dinner on Tuesday, April 7, with parliamentarians, authorities from the Finance and MDIC (Development, Industry, Commerce and Services) ministries, as well as businesspeople and technology sector leaders, to discuss the real chances of the project passing in 2026. "We need to guarantee a clear, stable and competitive environment that gives investors confidence. Without it, we run the risk of losing this opportunity," said the vice-president of FPBC, Vitor Lippi (PSDB-SP) on the occasion.
