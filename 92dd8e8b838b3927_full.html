# Congress Makes Final Push to Revive Tax Exemption Bill for Data Centers

Brasília - The processing of the Special Tax Regime for Data Center Services (Redata), a government fiscal incentive program designed to attract investments to the segment, has stalled again, this time in the Senate. The main parliamentary coalitions representing the productive sector in Congress are beginning to organize a political pressure movement to unblock the plenary vote. However, the window to vote on a project that doesn't even have a rapporteur in the Senate yet is narrow. The atmosphere is already one of pre-election campaign - several parliamentarians will seek reelection or support allies in elections for state governments and assemblies as well as for Congress itself.

At the end of last year, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's (PT) government issued a provisional measure (MP) that zeroed out taxes such as IPI, PIS/Cofins, and import duties on items used by data centers, but the Legislature allowed it to expire. The goal has always been to increase data processing in Brazil. During the four months the law was in effect, these benefits had an impact. However, as the approval deadline expired, the tax reduction ceased to be valid, generating uncertainty among investors who had been planning to invest billions of reals in the Brazilian market.

After that, the government leader in the Chamber, Deputy José Guimarães (PT-CE), submitted a bill in the same direction as the Executive, which was even approved by the Chamber. But since it arrived in the Senate in February, it has "stalled" despite support from both government coalition and opposition parties. Even the government leader in the Senate, Randolfe Rodrigues (PT-AP), attempted an agreement to include Redata in the text of another project dealing with tax incentives that was approved in plenary, but the strategy was unsuccessful.

Behind the scenes, the government itself blames Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP), president of the Senate, for holding up Redata's processing in the Senate, a source from the economic team admitted confidentially to NeoFeed. "We don't know why it stalled," they say. The government, however, hopes the bill will be approved at some point later this year.

In this context, a coalition of parliamentary fronts is being mobilized, including the Entrepreneurship Front (FPE) and the Agriculture Front (FPA), to pressure Alcolumbre and other Congressional leaders to try once more to approve Guimarães' bill. The president of the Parliamentary Front for Competitive Brazil, Deputy Júlio Lopes (PP-RJ), admits that the electoral backdrop and emergencies like the Middle East conflict "help to hinder" the Redata bill's vote, which ends up taking a back seat.

"The bill is paralyzed, we need to see how it will be resumed, but we have urgency and we will pressure the Senate and also the Chamber to unblock the issue," Lopes told NeoFeed. "The businessmen are eager to make investments, but the projects are paralyzed. In the meantime, Milei [Argentine President Javier] was faster and Argentina has already attracted billions of dollars in investments. So it's important that we speed things up here in Brazil."

The Front held a dinner on Tuesday, April 7, with parliamentarians, authorities from the Finance and MDIC (Development, Industry, Trade and Services) ministries, as well as businessmen and leaders from the technology sector to debate the real chances of the project passing in 2026.

"We need to guarantee a clear, stable, and competitive environment that gives confidence to investors. Without this, we run the risk of losing this opportunity," said Vitor Lippi (PSDB-SP), vice-president of FPBC, at the time.
