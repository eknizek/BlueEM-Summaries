# Prat-Gay: "Government Plays Wall Street's Favorite Score, But Has No Access to Credit"

Alfonso Prat-Gay, former Minister of Finance and president of the Central Bank, spoke today at the second edition of the Argentine Economic Congress at Expo EFI, describing the country's current economic moment as "uncertain." He stressed that it is necessary to establish "a tangible growth plan," something that according to him is not happening.

Prat-Gay was one of the main speakers at an event that began with the current Central Bank president, Santiago Bausili, assuring that the worst of the credit market delinquency has passed. In his presentation, Prat-Gay was critical of the government: "The economic program has no financial plan. It has no access to the market, nor internal confidence for money demand to recover. Clearly, something has to be done."

"It's not enough to say that inflation will collapse. We need to grow in a sustained and widespread manner, because it's not enough that the mining sector is doing very well," he exemplified, referring to one of the economy's segments that is pulling ahead while industry, commerce, and construction decline.

"The outlook is uncertain. We are in the middle of the river in a process of change, but we have no guarantees it will end well," stressed Prat-Gay. "This government is the one that best plays the score that Wall Street likes most, and yet after two years it has no access to credit. They should all be delighted to be refinancing Argentina and that's not happening. You have to explain better, you don't have to get angry, you have to have a growth program extended to all of society. It's no good if 10% are doing well and 80% are doing poorly," pointed out the former official.

The economist emphasized that the question the market asks itself is "what comes after Milei, because even if he gets reelected, something else will come after, and it's not clear what will happen. It's incredible that we're talking about 'Kuka risk.' It's the government itself transmitting that message of polarization, so outsiders think there's a chance they could return."

"The government's sacred cow, which is the fiscal anchor, is being threatened by lower tax collection. If the government maintains the fiscal anchor, it leads to reduced spending. And that spending reduction negatively impacts the economy's growth prospects. So, it's central that someone in the government is thinking about how we're going to grow," he emphasized.

Looking at the social situation, Prat-Gay criticized the government for its "lack of awareness" about what is happening in different sectors of the population. "You can't ask people to make sacrifices their whole lives. It's essential to improve people's quality of life. The government isn't taking enough note of this shift in social mood," he emphasized.

"It's an economic program that has no financial plan. The plan wins hearts if there's more employment, more exports, more growth. It's quite obvious to think that's the main reason why it's taking so long for the financial market to open," he pointed out.

While he made sure to highlight that Pablo Quirno at the helm of the Foreign Ministry is doing "great work," he was critical of the government's international alignment. "Foreign policy is key. It's the way we relate to the rest of the world, but a foreign policy that has to do with individuals—in Argentina's case with Donald Trump and Benjamin Netanyahu—can never be healthy. The problem is ephemeral and risky personal alliances."
