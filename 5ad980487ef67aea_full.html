# The new dividend chess game: family offices recalculate strategy amid 10% minimum tax

Business families are now doing their 2025 income tax returns with a touch of nostalgia. Next year, with the dividend law, monthly distributions above R$ 50,000 will have 10% withholding at source. And to achieve maximum tax efficiency, wealth managers are running numbers and creating their own calculators to analyze each case individually.

NeoFeed spoke with wealth managers to understand what families have done in these first months with the dividend law already in effect. In most cases, they are still in the calculation, simulation and planning phase. But now, with 2025 profits calculated and income tax being prepared, this discussion is gaining momentum.

The point is that most large business families managed to distribute dividends still last year, tax-exempt, creating a liquidity cushion to get through the first semester and buy time to design a strategy.

"The profit distributed until December was so large that there's a cushion being used in these first months of the year, which allows us to use this time for planning," says Roberto Freitas, head of wealth planning at G5 Partners. "But I have no doubt that in the second half, these conversations will intensify and move from paper to action."

There are still families where the surplus is so large that distribution could occur within the transition period, until 2028. This also suggests that the government may take longer to feel the full impact of the change.

"Those who didn't distribute but prepared the minutes for it have until 2028 to do so tax-exempt. So some families that didn't have cash are now evaluating whether it makes sense to implement a three-year payment plan or if it's better to take on debt now and distribute immediately," says Octavio Arruda, director of wealth services at Andbank.

But in any case, whoever will need liquidity now or later is running the numbers with their financial planners. TAG Investimentos, a multi-family office managing about R$ 17 billion, already has its own calculator and has been guiding clients on the most efficient way to distribute dividends. At NeoFeed's request, the firm ran simulations to try to draw broader conclusions.

The first addresses a question now haunting business families: is it financially more advantageous to leave all dividends for year-end, avoiding early withholding? Under the new rule, if withholding occurs early in the year and there's a refund, the money only returns in the annual income tax adjustment the following year, in May or June.

TAG simulated three paths for a majority partner in a company on actual profit basis with R$ 3 million in dividends available to receive in 2026: distributing R$ 250,000 monthly, concentrating everything in December, or adopting a hybrid strategy with R$ 50,000 monthly from January to November and the balance at year-end.

The numbers show that concentrating everything in December, which seemed like the natural solution to avoid early withholding, is the worst of the three scenarios. In the simulation, the executive's final position is R$ 2.994 million. Monthly distribution ends at R$ 3.015 million. The hybrid strategy reaches R$ 3.057 million.

This happens because not distributing also means leaving money in the company, where taxation of financial returns tends to be less efficient than on the individual level. In the simulation, TAG considered a rate of up to 34% in the legal entity and 15% on the individual, which generated a net return of 12.68% per year for individuals and 9.38% per year for legal entities.

Additionally, taking advantage of the monthly R$ 50,000 limit without withholding has an effect. In the hybrid model, tax falls from R$ 300,000 to R$ 245,000, a savings of R$ 55,000.

But TAG itself emphasizes that the conclusion cannot be made into a general rule. With the same parameters, if annual distribution rises from R$ 3 million to R$ 10 million, the answer changes and December distribution becomes more advantageous.

"Everything has to be weighed: the volume the entrepreneur will distribute, what they'll leave in company cash to earn returns, and also the return rates. In real life, for each case, this changes over time," says Manoela Vargas, head of wealth planning at TAG Investimentos.

According to her, the central point is that leaving money in the company also has a cost. "It's not just a discussion about interest rates. It's the difference in taxation between individuals and legal entities. Keeping money in the legal entity may make sense, but you need to understand that there's a penalty for that cash," says Manoela.

## Between the ideal and the viable

But business families won't always be able to do what's most efficient for the individual. There's the company's cash needs, business seasonality, company investments, and the family's own liquidity.

That's why dividend decisions are increasingly tied to company planning. For Pedro Olmo, partner responsible for wealth management at Sten Multi Family Office, it's the company's cash that will practically determine how much can be distributed.

"If the company has the means to do what would be optimal for the individual, perfect. But if there's a cash flow or seasonality issue, the company's need ends up being sovereign," he says.

In Olmo's view, financial arbitrage needs to be weighed against the reality of the company and family.

"I think you'll have to look case by case: what's that company's cash need, what's the family's liquidity need, and what's the risk of accumulating cash in the legal entity. There's no one-size-fits-all solution anymore," he says.

This change also alters the routine of those advising these families. The dividend decision ceases to be an isolated conversation between partners and accountant and now involves the family office, the company's CFO, lawyers, and those responsible for the individual's financial portfolio.

For Freitas at G5, the new rule brings together two worlds that were previously treated more separately.

"This new system makes personal and company cash more synergistic. You need to plan throughout the year how expenses will be met. And this changes the investment portfolio. Business families' planning has changed," he says.
