# Geopolitics Drives New Plays by Global Mega-Fortunes

Members of the ultra-wealthy global elite are betting on different sectors experiencing valuation changes as a result of the war in the Middle East, showing how increased geopolitical tensions are reconfiguring the focus of private investors.

Jaime Gilinski, Colombia's richest man, has repeatedly increased his stake in independent Latin American oil and gas producer GeoPark since he and his son made a US$107 million investment in March, with the intention of using the company as a vehicle to enter Venezuela's oil sector.

A family office of the heirs of Swedish oil and mining magnate Adolf Lundin allocated nearly 40 million Canadian dollars (US$29.5 million) in March to increase its holdings in copper and diamond mining companies based in Vancouver, in a context where supply chain tensions have driven up natural resource prices.

And investment firms from the dynasties behind Italian automaker Ferrari NV and American media conglomerate Cox Enterprises backed defense startups in recent months, such as Hermeus, which seeks to build the fastest unmanned jet aircraft in history for the U.S. Department of Defense.

The four families have a combined fortune of approximately US$90 billion, with multi-generational patrimonies dating back to 1898, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.

The operations reflect how some of the world's richest investors are positioning their portfolios in response to increased geopolitical tensions, driven largely by President Donald Trump's continued reconfiguration of the global order.

Oil, gold, and other natural resource prices have risen this year due to global trade disruptions and investors' search for safe-haven assets following Trump's efforts to capture Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro, place Greenland under U.S. authority, and attack Iran.

Billionaires like Mexico's richest man, Carlos Slim, are already reaping extraordinary profits from financial crises. Global political uncertainty has also contributed to adding more than US$20 billion this year to the combined fortunes of individuals and families with stakes in publicly traded defense companies.

A JPMorgan Chase survey of 333 investment firms focused on the ultra-wealthy published in February revealed that one in five considers geopolitics as their primary risk, above liquidity and inflation.

"We are operating at a time when the geopolitical and geoeconomic component has never been more important for capital management," said Sara Macedo, managing partner at Emissary Partners, a London-based wealth advisory firm for ultra-high-net-worth individuals and their investment firms. "Families should look beyond the horizon and understand how decisions are made at the highest levels."

Other members of the ultra-wealthy elite changing their allocations include the billionaire family behind Porsche and Volkswagen, which last year reversed its position of financing only civilian-oriented products and created a platform to invest in defense startups.

Flat Capital AB, the investment firm of Klarna Group Plc co-founder Sebastian Siemiatkowski, also expanded its strategy in 2025 toward the defense sector.

Some family offices are also seeking to reduce risks from an increasingly polarized world by expanding into new territories and scaling up their operations to mitigate potential disruptions.

The family office of Belgian real estate magnate Filip Balcaen expanded its private equity team in the U.S. this year, where a wave of Silicon Valley billionaires, including Meta Platforms' early investor Peter Thiel, are establishing private investment firms in Florida to avoid California taxes.

A KPMG report and recruitment firm Agreus Group report, released in September and based on a survey of 585 family office professionals, revealed that nearly half of their employers operate in more than one location, compared to about a third in 2023.

"Wealth structures are being strengthened to ensure that assets can move, adapt, and endure regardless of how geopolitical conditions evolve," said Nigel Green, chief executive of financial advisory firm deVere Group. "Diversification is being rewritten."
