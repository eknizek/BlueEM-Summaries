# Caputo and Pettovello resist Sturzenegger's spending cuts in Economy and Human Capital

Javier Milei's order to "not ease up" resonated within government walls. After more than 63,000 public sector layoffs in two years, the Government advanced new cuts and budget reallocations in an attempt to show that the chainsaw remains active. Federico Sturzenegger sought to take the lead, though he faced some obstacles and friction with areas under the Ministry of Economy and Ministry of Human Capital, headed by Luis Caputo and Sandra Pettovello.

The new downsizing of the National Institute of Industrial Technology (INTI) was one of the decisions that apparently generated internal noise. The Minister of Deregulation celebrated on Thursday the elimination of 1,000 laboratory testing services that could result in 700 layoffs, and thanked Pablo Lavigne, the Production coordinator and person in charge of INTI within Economy. His plan, however, was different. According to sources from the institute, the "colossus" intended to eliminate twice as many services and approximately 1,500 employees. It would have meant sweeping away half of the 2,300-person workforce and dismantling the institute. Production accepted less, but there were still worker protests.

Resolution 42/2026 published in the Official Bulletin affects testing in food and detection of contaminants (Salmonella, Escherichia coli, and Listeria); water quality and industrial effluent studies; critical and toxic contaminants (heavy metals, hydrocarbons, pharmaceutical residues), material resistance in construction; mechanics, plastic containers and industrial materials; electronics and software; automotive safety; fire safety; and fuel analysis, among others.

The regulation states that the cessation of services is based on "low demand" or because "private sector alternatives exist." INTI stated that services "overlapped with those provided by the private sector," while Sturzenegger assured in an extensive tweet that it offered services at a "very low" price, which "was a subsidy to certain companies financed by all of us." He argued that the "move" returns activity to the private sector and eliminates a "bottleneck."

The measure signed by INTI authorities—lacking experience in industry or technology—could have unforeseen costs. The institute's sub-managers already warned that it not only puts 700 jobs at risk, but also production and "population safety." Since no private company performs all of INTI's testing in totality, plastic container manufacturers used to preserve foods and beverages in products that reach store shelves, for example, will need to conduct dozens of analyses in different laboratories, and still might find it impossible to replace the organization. Reciclar, one of Coca-Cola's bottle suppliers, depended on state testing.

Sturzenegger's advance could also add obstacles to organizations under the Agriculture Secretariat, headed by Sergio Iraeta and under Caputo's control. Government officials acknowledge that there are several tests that INTI performed that no one else covers, many related to food and container registration that companies later present to register with Senasa, the body responsible for overseeing and certifying animal and plant health.

The shadow of the deregulation minister loomed again in Economy's hallways in recent weeks. Alejandro Tamer, a loyal official, was controlling Caputo's directors and examining the "details" after budget cuts and area mergers. When employees asked the Vice Minister of Economy, José Luis Daza, about the latest 31 layoffs, he said it was done "for the country and the poor," that temporary leave "doesn't exist in other countries," and that adjustments "are being studied by Sturzenegger's people." "This doesn't end here, there's a government guideline that we adhere to," added the Undersecretary of Administration, Gabriel Crugeiros.

The Labor Secretariat is another arena where there were sparks weeks ago. With labor reform approved in February, Deregulation advanced in March on its plan to remove the State from its intervention in labor relations, with the exception of guidelines and caps on wage negotiations that Julio Cordero signs but Caputo establishes. The idea was to eliminate four regional directorates and 43 territorial agencies, responsible for handling employment procedures, providing job training, and assisting workers, unions, and employers. Internal resolutions that estimated savings of $1,407,713,491 per year generated worker protests and assemblies. But they never saw the light of day. Twelve of the agencies slated for elimination are in Buenos Aires province, where territorial organizer Sebastián Pareja has a strong presence. Even so, the downsizing would have been suspended by the team of Human Capital Ministry chief Sandra Pettovello. Those close to the minister made no comments. In other offices they acknowledge that "it wasn't the ideal moment," amid layoffs in industry and construction and with inflation accelerating. Those who know Pettovello say she is very protective of her structure. One of the few areas she decided to preserve is professional training, from where she promotes virtual courses so former beneficiaries of the Return to Work program use their vouchers for training, since she eliminated the monthly $78,000 allocation in April.

Though not listed on the organizational chart, since February, the undersecretary has been run by Atilio Catelli, a member of the Institute for Growth, a foundation chaired by Miguel Ponte, a former Mauricio Macri official and former Techint group member. The former news producer recovered full control of ANSeS last month with the resignation of Fernando Bearzi, Caputo's man who was in charge for just over a year. With his departure, he became the third official to leave that position in Javier Milei's administration, and Pettovello appointed Guillermo Arancibia. His deputy was number two in the organization, but in practice was the one who directed everything, from care units (UDAI) to management of retiree and beneficiary funds.
