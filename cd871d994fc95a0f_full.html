# Master Bank Letter of Credit Collapse Triggers Another Receivership in Power Trading

The IBS Energy group filed for judicial recovery, joining a recent wave of bankruptcies in the energy trading market amid sector price volatility. However, the IBS crisis, which has energy generation and trading subsidiaries, is also linked to the liquidation of Master Bank, sources told Brazil Journal.

While IBS was already suffering from turbulence in the electricity market, a group company invested millions in a green biomass plant in a project financed by Finep. The shock came when one of the guarantees for the Finep loan crumbled: it was a letter of credit from Master Bank.

IBS needed a new guarantee, but at the same time, banks became alarmed by the financial problems of various energy traders and withdrew credit. In court, IBS argued that "the crisis in the electricity sector, combined with increased risk perception of traders, resulted in severe credit restrictions and greater rigor from financial institutions."

This year alone, Elétron Energy filed for judicial recovery, Tradener filed a precautionary measure for creditor mediation, and Boven was disconnected by the Electricity Trading Chamber (CCEE), among other cases.

With a structure of cross-guarantees between its subsidiaries and problems in trading operations, the IBS Group feared that contract terminations and early debt maturities would create a destructive spiral. Recently, while preparing the recovery filing, the company laid off several employees, according to sources.

In its court documents, IBS stated that its case "is not isolated, but part of a broad sectoral crisis, characterized by price instability, credit restrictions, liquidity deterioration, and increased contractual risk." IBS lawyers also argue there is a "structural rupture of the sector, of a systemic nature."

In the recovery filing, IBS explained that Master Bank had issued a letter of credit in favor of a group company, the holding International Business, within the guarantee structure of the UTE Cidade do Livro generation project. The company argued in court that a new guarantee under similar conditions "is not feasible in the current market environment," citing that "various sector agents have been adopting restructuring measures, including through judicial recovery."
