# Livestock Prices Down 10% from Peak: Will Meat Prices Fall at Retail?

Livestock prices have already fallen 10 percent from their peak: will the value of meat at the butcher counter drop? After averaging 5,000 pesos per kilo in mid-March, in April values fell as they were not validated on the street.

In mid-March, the INMAG (Novillo Market Agricultural Livestock Index), the reference indicator for the livestock market in Argentina, was positioned at 4,492 pesos. In the last week, however, it was positioned at 4,166 pesos.

In recent weeks, the mentioned shortage of animal supply was not sufficiently acute to counteract the crisis in beef consumption—which fell to 47.3 kg per person per year, the lowest level in two decades—and fat cattle prices entered a readjustment phase that, to date, has translated into declines of around 10%.

The question now is whether that drop will be reflected on the boards of butcher shops.

Johnny Perkins, a market operator, states in conversation with Clarín Rural that starting in April "prices began to calm down." "It was the result of the strong increase that occurred in February and March. After that, the commercialization of meat on the street began to show strong resistance to increases and, as expected, that had an impact on the market. Prices began to fall. I would say we had a decline of between 8% and 10% from that moment until today, and the outlook doesn't seem like it will change for now," he describes.

Indeed, in mid-March the INMAG (Novillo Market Agricultural Livestock Index), the reference indicator for the livestock market in Argentina, was at 4,492 pesos. In the last week, however, it was positioned at 4,166 pesos.

A recent Rosgan report details that if you observe fat cattle values at the MAG from the second half of last year to the present, the change in trend is evident. "In constant terms, between October 2025 and February of this year—when historical highs were reached—steers rose 28%, while young steers and heifers rose 32% and 33%, respectively. From that peak, in March and during the first twenty days of April, prices began to correct: steers fell back 9% and young steers and heifers 6%," it indicates.

The entity explains that historically, March and April tend to concentrate the largest price increases for fat cattle, given the greater fluidity of demand that transfers from retail counters. "However, in this season the adjustment was anticipated to February and at a magnitude that consumption cannot completely absorb," it warns.

From the retail perspective, the diagnosis is similar. "For a long time meat was very expensive and that caused many people to turn to chicken or pork. Today, for example, eating grilled beef or flank steak has become almost a luxury. Some specific cuts sell more, but there is no widespread demand. There are customers who keep buying without looking too closely at the price, but also many who come, ask, and end up taking other meats because beef seems expensive to them," says Romina Uz, a butcher in the Buenos Aires neighborhoods of Belgrano and Saavedra.

For Ariel Morales Anton, president of the Chamber of Slaughterers and Suppliers (CAMyA), the recent drop in livestock prices could partially transfer to retail counters, although not automatically or in equal proportion. "This is because within the supply chain there are costs—such as logistics, energy, and salaries—that have not decreased and continue to pressure the final price. Furthermore, consumption remains weak, which generates caution in the different links when adjusting prices," he explains.

In this context, Morales Anton believes that any correction in consumer prices will be partial and gradual. "If the downward trend in livestock consolidates, it could begin to be reflected more clearly in consumer prices in the coming weeks," he adds.

Perkins agrees that a recovery in consumption will not be immediate. "Commercialization remains stuck: Mrs. Rosa doesn't have money to spend and, furthermore, there is strong competition from other meats like chicken and pork, with very competitive prices. The outlook is not encouraging for now; we'll have to wait for the economy in general to settle a bit more," he warns.

Uz, for her part, adds the daily business perspective: "For us as merchants, the situation is complicated. When prices go up, it's not just meat: chicken, pork, rent, and other costs also increase. So one has to adapt to all of that, not just to demand. What I see is more cautious consumption and very conditional on prices."
