# IPSA Falls Sharply to 10,700 Points Amid Middle East Tensions and Weak March Economic Data

Stock markets fell on this first Monday in May, as the Persian Gulf crisis has not advanced toward resolution but rather worsened with new signals that the region's critical infrastructure is in danger.

The local market performed especially poorly. The S&P IPSA dropped 1.8% to 10,716.13 points at the close of the Santiago Stock Exchange, largely influenced by "heavyweights" such as Cencosud (-3.3%), Latam (-3%), and Bci (-2.4%), with losses led by Ripley (-4.1%), IAM (-3.9%), and Itaú (-3.8%). Parque Arauco (-2%) performed relatively in line with the IPSA. The stock closed at $3,940, above the $3,600 price the company set in its last capital increase. The mall operator saw initial reactions to its first quarter results, which showed profits above general analyst estimates.

Early on, there was negative news regarding the national economy, as the Imacec registered an interannual decline in March, primarily due to poor performance in mining production and other goods. The IPSA had suffered a second weekly loss of more than 2% at Thursday's close, ahead of Friday's Workers' Day holiday.

The losses show how the recovery of Chilean assets has been hindered, a recovery that had extended for four weeks amid bets on de-escalation of the Middle East conflict.

## International Markets

On Wall Street, which closed Friday at new historic highs, the Dow Jones lost 1.1%, the S&P 500 slid 0.4%, and the Nasdaq dropped 0.2%. Across the Atlantic, the eurozone's Euro Stoxx 50 fell back 2%, while United Kingdom markets remained closed for the Early May holiday. Previously in Asia, Hong Kong's Hang Seng jumped 1.2%.

Brent oil rebounded 5.2% to $113.7 per barrel following news that the port city of Fujairah in the United Arab Emirates was attacked by Iranian drones. Fujairah is an oil and refined products hub that had been gaining greater relevance recently, as it is not located in the Strait of Hormuz.

Donald Trump has promised that the US will lead ships trapped in this important passage out of Hormuz, as part of the so-called "Operation Freedom" that the president unveiled this weekend through a post on his Truth Social network, which is already underway.

On Monday, Trump posted: "Iran has launched some attacks against nations outside of Operation Freedom, including a South Korean cargo ship. Maybe it's time for South Korea to join the mission! We have shot down seven small vessels or, as they like to call them, 'fast' boats. That's all they have left. Apart from the South Korean ship, there has been no damage so far crossing the strait."

Secretary of War Pete Hegseth and Joint Chiefs of Staff Chairman Dan Caine will hold a press conference early Tuesday morning, according to the post.

Over the weekend, Iranian media reported that a US frigate was attacked near the Gulf of Oman after ignoring alerts from the Persian Navy. However, Washington denied that one of its ships suffered this attack.

But it was not the only recent sign of violence in the region, as a tanker was attacked by projectiles near Fujairah, less than a day after patrol boats had besieged a cargo ship, according to the UK maritime authority.

Iran warned the US Navy not to enter the Strait of Hormuz. The Islamic Republic confirmed this Sunday that it received the US response to its latest peace proposal. The 14 Iranian points are "solely focused on ending the war," and there are "no nuclear talks at this stage," according to a spokesman for the Foreign Ministry told state television.

Analysts have warned that the energy market faces a decisive month, as crude inventories are just weeks away from being depleted, a situation that would result in price increases to even higher levels.
