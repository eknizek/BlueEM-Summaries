# Will Milei change Argentina's economy or will Argentina's economy change Milei?

The President now does not hide the vast empirical evidence that bringing inflation down to a single digit annually is not possible to achieve in a couple of years. There is also an incentive for a decrease in the interest rate. The differences between Milei's 2026 and Macri's 2018.

Just over ten years ago, a press conference took place at the Ministry of Economy in which the economic team of then-President Mauricio Macri announced what was called the Fiscal Program and Inflation Targets 2016-2019. The inflation rate of 2015 (26.3% according to market estimates because Indec did not publish figures) would reach single digits by the end of Macri's term. In that economic team, led by Alfonso Prat-Gay who managed the presentation, were Luis Caputo and Santiago Bausili. The International Monetary Fund, a few months after that press conference, published in a box of a staff report on the Argentine economy that more than half of a sample of 35 countries that lowered inflation from levels like Argentina's to single-digit rates had taken more than ten years.

At that January 13, 2016 conference, Macri proposed to lower inflation faster than Colombia, Chile, or Uruguay. In 2019, he acknowledged the error: "I underestimated the difficulty of eradicating inflation in our country." Instead of closing at 5% as stated in the PowerPoint in the Economy microcine, it ended at 53.8%.

Milei learned from that Macri failure: to stabilize, an orthodox leg of the plan is needed (which Macri did not have). But he believed that with this, plus the elimination of remunerated liabilities from the Central Bank, inflation would fall and quickly. A year ago he said that inflation by this point would already have a death certificate. But while much of the acceleration of inflation in recent months is due to shocks in dollar prices, meat, and fuel, the idea is consolidating that there is an inertial inflation in the 2%-2.5% zone difficult to break through, to the point that last week Milei praised on X a work by an economist from Martín Guzmán's team (Fernando Morra), in line with that IMF work from ten years back.

Will the persistence of Argentines' survival mechanisms to defend themselves from inflation—that is, indexing—change the President's Austrian vision?

There are differences between Milei and Macri in how they are positioned at this stage of their respective administrations to face inflation. In the case of Cambiemos, his government faced an external deficit of more than 5% of GDP that caused a jump in the dollar when short-term capital withdrew amid a climate of global financial volatility and rising Federal Reserve rates. The second difference is that Milei had the support of the IMF and the United States. In that sense, the Argentine economy has been flying over the international conflict without major tensions beyond those expected in prices due to the impact of fuel.

For example, the Capital Foundation estimates that inflation this year would be 32%, in line with 2025 (31.5%). But the currencies of countries that are net exporters of oil, like Argentina, have appreciated in recent weeks, something that decompresses currency pressure, the opposite of what happened with Macri. Economists ranging from Marina Dal Poggetto to Nicolás Dujovne and including Rodolfo Santángelo suggest that this year the current account deficit would be very slight or even positive. "Argentina is better positioned today than with Macri to face an external shock," says Santángelo, who predicts a trade balance of around US$ 25 billion. "There will not be a crisis like the one in 2018."

The currency appreciation of net oil-exporting countries could mean a pressure cooker in the future for Argentina, especially since the Government resorts today to a currency anchor to prevent inflation from rising further. The level of the multilateral exchange rate is equivalent to that of November 2023. Colombia, Chile, and Mexico reached appreciations of more than 40% in their currencies during their stabilizations, according to that IMF work published ten years ago. That is why these countries took longer to lower inflation: because at some point they had to devalue. That is why many economists and the organization itself suggest to Milei that he buy all the reserves he can, to mitigate those sharp movements in the exchange rate or to have more margin. "They have to buy more dollars because the purchase of dollars by Argentines for hoarding remains high," says Santángelo. The Central Bank said Monday that it will add US$ 8 billion this year. This Tuesday it bought US$ 235 million and in the month US$ 2 billion. A JP Morgan study from that same day projects that "net international reserves will increase by at least 10 billion dollars in 2026, thanks to efforts to mobilize foreign currency financing and maintain Central Bank foreign currency purchases of at least 8 billion dollars this year."

In the Government, off the record, they say that in four months they could have bought almost double. And they see conditions to inject more pesos onto the street in exchange for those dollars since while they buy foreign currency the exchange rate falls and the interest rate will do so even more. The main doubt in the market is not whether there will be sufficient exports or if activity will rebound. The mystery is whether Argentines will return to demanding US$ 30 billion in 2027. The Central Bank anticipated Monday that retail dollar purchases in March were somewhat lower than in February and the pre-electoral period.

Will Milei have learned the lesson that Argentines buy dollars while they can, and that means while it's cheap for them? Will the Argentine economy change Milei heading toward the 2027 elections?

As Pablo Gerchunoff and Lucas Llach say in their essay "See on the throne noble equality, the rise and fall of the Argentine economy from 1880 to the present," the balance between equity and growth is the manifestation of classic dilemmas when stabilization, the currency situation, and opening interact. Milei will have to choose.
