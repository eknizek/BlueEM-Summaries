# Will Milei Change the Argentine Economy or Will the Argentine Economy Change Milei?

The President now does not hide the vast empirical evidence that bringing inflation down to single digits annually cannot be achieved in a couple of years. There is also an incentive to lower interest rates. The differences between Milei's 2026 and Macri's 2018.

A little more than ten years ago, a press conference took place at the Ministry of Economy in which the economic team of then-President Mauricio Macri announced what was called the Fiscal Program and Inflation Targets 2016-2019. The inflation rate of 2015 (26.3% according to market estimates because Indec did not publish figures) would reach single digits by the end of Macri's term. In that economic team, led by Alfonso Prat-Gay who managed the presentation, were Luis Caputo and Santiago Bausili. The International Monetary Fund, a few months after that press conference, published in a box of a staff report on the Argentine economy that more than half of a sample of 35 countries that had lowered inflation from levels like Argentina's to single-digit rates had taken more than ten years.

At that January 13, 2016 conference, Macri proposed to lower inflation faster than Colombia, Chile, or Uruguay. In 2019 he acknowledged the error: "I underestimated the difficulty of eradicating inflation in our country." Instead of closing at 5% as the PowerPoint said in the Economy Ministry auditorium, it ended at 53.8%.

Milei learned from that failure of Macri: to stabilize, an orthodox leg of the plan is needed (which Macri did not have). But he believed that with that, plus the elimination of Central Bank remunerated liabilities, inflation would fall and quickly. A year ago he said that inflation by this time would already have a death certificate. But while a good part of the acceleration of inflation in recent months is due to shocks in dollar, meat, and fuel prices, the idea is consolidating that there is an inertial inflation in the 2%-2.5% zone difficult to break through, to the point that last week Milei praised on X a work by an economist from Martín Guzmán's team (Fernando Morra), in line with that IMF work from ten years ago.

Will the persistence of Argentines' survival mechanisms to defend themselves from inflation—that is, indexing—change the President's Austrian vision?

There are differences between Milei and Macri in how they are positioned at this point in their respective administrations to face inflation. In the case of Cambiemos, his government faced an external deficit of more than 5% of GDP that caused a jump in the dollar when capital flows withdrew amid global financial volatility and Federal Reserve rate hikes. The second difference is that Milei had the support of the IMF and the United States. In that sense, the Argentine economy has been flying over international conflict without major tensions beyond those expected in prices from the fuel impact. For example, the Capital Foundation estimates that inflation this year would be 32%, in line with 2025 (31.5%).

But the currencies of net oil-exporting countries like Argentina have appreciated in recent weeks, something that relieves exchange pressure—the opposite of what happened with Macri. Economists ranging from Marina Dal Poggetto to Nicolás Dujovne and including Rodolfo Santángelo suggest that this year the current account deficit would be very slight or even positive. "Argentina is today better positioned than with Macri to face an external shock," says Santángelo, who forecasts a trade balance of around US$ 25 billion. "There will not be a crisis like the one in 2018."

The currency appreciation of net oil-exporting countries could mean a pressure cooker in the future for Argentina, especially since the Government is now resorting to an exchange anchor to prevent inflation from rising further. The level of the multilateral exchange rate is equivalent to that of November 2023. Colombia, Chile, and Mexico appreciated their currencies by more than 40% during their stabilizations, according to that IMF work published ten years ago. That is why these countries delayed in lowering inflation: because at some point they had to devalue.

That is why many economists and the organization itself suggest to Milei that he buy as many reserves as possible to moderate those sharp movements in the exchange rate or to have more margin. "They have to buy more dollars because Argentine purchasing for hoarding continues to be high," says Santángelo. The Central Bank said Monday that it will add US$ 8 billion this year. On Tuesday it bought US$ 235 million and in the month there have been US$ 2 billion. A JP Morgan study from that same day projects that "international net reserves will increase by at least 10 billion US dollars in 2026, thanks to efforts to mobilize foreign currency financing and maintain Central Bank foreign exchange purchases valued at at least 8 billion US dollars this year."

In the Government, under their breath, they say that in four months they could have bought almost double. And they see conditions to pour more pesos into circulation in exchange for those dollars, since while buying foreign currency the exchange rate falls and interest rates will fall even more. The market's main doubt is not whether there will be sufficient exports or whether activity will pick up. The question is whether Argentines will again demand US$ 30 billion in 2027.

The Central Bank anticipated Monday that retail dollar purchases in March were somewhat lower than in February and the pre-electoral period. Will Milei have learned the lesson that Argentines buy dollars while they can, and that means while it's cheap for them? Will the Argentine economy change Milei ahead of the 2027 elections?

As Pablo Gerchunoff and Lucas Llach say in their essay "Behold on the Throne the Noble Equality, Rise and Fall of the Argentine Economy from 1880 to the Present," the balance between equity and growth is the manifestation of classical dilemmas when stabilization, exchange situation, and openness interact. Milei will have to choose.
