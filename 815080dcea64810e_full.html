Why Does GameStop Want to Take on Debt to Buy eBay?

04 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Why Does GameStop Want to Take on Debt to Buy eBay?

The video game retailer GameStop drew market attention on Monday (04) by announcing the purchase of the eBay marketplace for US$ 56 billion. The amount was surprising, as it is almost five times higher than GameStop's market value, which stands at around US$ 11 billion. The money to fund the deal would come from three sources, according to the statement released by the company to shareholders.

The first is the retailer's own cash, which ended January 2026 with approximately US$ 9.4 billion in cash and net investments — a result of years of restructuring and a US$ 4.2 billion capital raise in zero-coupon long-term debt, carried out during CEO Ryan Cohen's tenure. The second source is external financing. GameStop has already received the green light from TD Securities bank, guaranteeing the availability of up to US$ 20 billion in credit for the acquisition. With the operation, the retailer would take on debt of almost twice its market value. Finally, the company stated it would use stock issuance as a third financing source. As half of the offer would be paid in GameStop shares, the dilution of current shareholders also enters the equation.

The news did not resonate well with investors, who began to divest their positions in the company. Around 2 p.m., GameStop shares on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) were down almost 8%, to US$ 24.42.

## Why eBay?

The strategic logic, according to the CEO, lies in the complementarity between the two businesses. eBay is a marketplace with almost universal brand recognition, but which has been losing momentum: the company spent US$ 2.4 billion on sales and marketing in 2025 and managed to add only one million active buyers — growth of less than 0.75%. GameStop sees considerable fat to cut there.

The company promises US$ 2 billion in annual cost reductions in the first year after closing the deal — US$ 1.2 billion in marketing alone, US$ 300 million in product development, and US$ 500 million in administrative expenses. With these cuts, eBay's earnings per share would jump from US$ 4.26 to US$ 7.79, according to GameStop's projections.

Beyond the numbers, the retailer offers a physical trump card: its network of approximately 1,600 stores in the United States could serve as infrastructure for authentication, logistics, and live commerce on eBay — an advantage in categories like collectible items, where in-person verification adds value.

## The Man Behind the Bet

Canadian billionaire Ryan Cohen, founder of Chewy and current CEO of GameStop, would be the CEO of the company resulting from the merger with eBay. Since taking over the video game retailer in January 2021, he has transformed a US$ 381 million loss into a US$ 418 million profit in 2025, cut operating expenses by nearly half, and eliminated the company's legacy debt. He owns approximately 9% of GameStop, receives no fixed salary or cash bonuses, and would be compensated exclusively by the combined company's performance.

The proposal for eBay is, in many ways, Cohen's biggest bet so far — and also the riskiest. Taking on debt for a company that until recently was known for its financial fragility to buy a business four times larger requires flawless execution. The market, for now, remains on the fence.
