The Dream of Homeownership Becomes More Distant for the Middle Class and Rent Skyrockets in the Country - Estadão

# The Dream of Homeownership Moves Further Away for the Middle Class and Rent Skyrockets in Brazil

For three years, English tutor Rafael Bezerra has been renting a 40-square-meter apartment in Santa Cecília, a central region of São Paulo. A representative of the middle class with above-average income but no property assets, the 44-year-old from Rio de Janeiro dreams of buying a home, but faces barriers in the form of down payment costs, the burden of long-term financing, and the rising cost of living.

Bezerra pays R$ 1,890 to rent the apartment. "A find. I've already seen new properties in the area that cost double," he says. Monthly bills, therapy expenses, and leisure costs make it even more difficult to save money. Self-employed, without inherited properties and little savings, the teacher illustrates how renting is entrenched in the Brazilian economy.

Stories like Rafael's are becoming more common. Between 2016 and 2025, the number of rented properties jumped from 12.2 million to 18.9 million, according to data from the Continuous National Household Sample Survey (Pnad Contínua). This represents an increase of approximately 55%. Although the number of properties built in the country increased 18.9% in this period, from 66.7 million to 79.3 million, nearly a quarter are rented.

In contrast, the proportion of owner-occupied homes decreased from 66.8% in 2016 to 60.2% in 2025.

The numbers contrast with what the population wants: an Ipsos study released last year showed that 76% of people who pay rent are interested in acquiring a property, although 36% of them don't believe they will achieve this goal. In Rafael's case, the dream persists. With the expansion of Minha Casa, Minha Vida (My House, My Life), which now includes families with income up to R$ 13,000, he intends to finally buy his own home. "I had to study personal finance to learn how to manage money and change the dynamics of my family, which never managed to build assets," he explains.

## Down Payment, Financing, and Credit

The unemployment rate, currently at 6.1%, consolidates a historically heated labor market. Accumulated inflation in 12 months closed at 4.14% through March, and 2025 GDP ended with growth of 2.3%. Despite positive indicators, the perception of purchasing power has decreased and people are in debt. According to Serasa data, Brazil reached 81.7 million people in default in 2026, an increase of 38.1% compared to 2016. The value of debts grew 176% in the period.

According to economist André Sacconato, professor at FIPE/USP, the most affected group is the middle class. "The middle class had real salary increases. However, inflation affecting this group is greater than other social strata. It's an economy based on services, which are rising more than other products," Sacconato explains. "The price variation for the middle class is greater than the growth of nominal income, which is why they have the impression that money is running short."

In this environment of squeezed budgets, accumulating the money needed to cover 20% to 30% of a property's value just for the down payment seems like a distant dream. This is the first barrier when deciding to move out of renting. Add to this the high costs of mortgage financing, with annual rates above 10%, and rigorous credit analysis.

The solution for lower-income populations has materialized in the strengthening and expansion of Minha Casa, Minha Vida, which has become one of the Lula Government's flagship programs in recent decades. The housing program offers buyers lower interest rates and more predictable credit access.

This is how MCMV transformed the country's real estate market in recent years. Data from Secovi-SP shows that 54.3% (85,400 units) of residential units launched in São Paulo fit the program. In 2016, the segment represented only 19% of launches. Over the past decade, the program expanded and began attracting developers that were previously specialized in the middle class.

While the economic segment benefits from MCMV, the wealthy accumulate returns on money invested with a Selic rate close to 15%. The luxury and super-luxury market in São Paulo moved over R$ 28 billion in 2025 and the number of apartments launched jumped almost 60%. In the midst of this movement, the middle class lost ground in the real estate market.

Alberto Ajzental, coordinator of the Real Estate Business course at Fundação Getulio Vargas (FGV), highlights that savings resources (SBPE), traditionally used to finance homes for the middle class, especially through the Housing Finance System (SFH), are losing their market share. "This makes credit scarcer and more expensive for those who don't fit within MCMV subsidies," he explains.

Data from the Brazilian Association of Real Estate Credit and Savings Entities (Abecip) show that from 2016 to 2021, during the pandemic when interest rates reached 2%, the number of units financed via SBPE jumped from 199,000 to 866,000. However, since then the number has been in decline and closed 2025 with 457,000 units financed.

"Real estate credit now operates with a greater composition between savings funding and other sources of resources, such as Real Estate Credit Letters (LCIs), which have higher acquisition costs. This dynamic influences the final financing rate and the installment values," Abecip states in a note. However, the entity affirms that the real estate market remains resilient with expectations of 16% growth in real estate credit in 2026. "Furthermore, the creation of Band 4 represents significant progress, offering more favorable financing conditions for this public," it states.

## The Middle Class Rent

Squeezed between the rich and the poor, the middle class is more impacted by high interest rates in financing, doesn't have the subsidies of the economic segment, and struggles to accumulate down payment money.

"You know that meme 'how do I save money for emergencies if my life is an eternal emergency room?'" joked communication consultant Carol Herling. "Health insurance, healthcare costs, therapy, veterinary, and food are more expensive. Even my asthma medication had a price increase," she lists.

The 88-square-meter apartment she shares with her husband, two dogs, and a cat in Higienópolis, São Paulo, costs R$ 4,200, plus R$ 1,800 in condo fees.

Herling's perspective illustrates that even the rental alternative is increasingly painful. Data from the Fipezap Index show that rent became 8.63% more expensive in the last 12 months. In March 2020, the average price per square meter of a rental property in the country was R$ 30.37. In March this year, the average price jumped to R$ 52.34. The variation is well above the Broad National Consumer Price Index (IPCA).
