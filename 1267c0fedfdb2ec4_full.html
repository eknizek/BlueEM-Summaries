# Desenrola 2 provides relief but does not resolve: Brazilian debt is structural

On June 6, 2023, the Official Gazette of the Union published the Provisional Measure that institutionalized Desenrola Brasil – the Emergency Program for Renegotiation of Debts of Defaulting Individuals, Desenrola 1. Three years later, Desenrola 2 is in the works and is set to be launched on Monday, May 4. The original program was successful, celebrated the Ministry of Finance in May 2024, with a balance of R$ 53 billion in refinancing benefiting 15 million people. Desenrola 2 could help twice as many Brazilians, but will it be sufficient to resolve the situation if the program pushes debtors to take out more loans, even at 1.99% per month interest?

A point of concern: in Desenrola 1, each R$ 1 renegotiated resulted in R$ 1.15 in new defaults, according to the Central Bank. What will be the outcome of the second initiative? Difficult to predict, but it should generally be positive. This includes the use of R$ 4.5 billion from the FGTS, an amount announced on Wednesday, April 29, by Labor and Employment Minister Luiz Marinho, plus support from the Fundo Garantir de Operações (FGO) which could be strengthened with funds forgotten by account holders in banks. These "guarantor" instruments protect institutions against losses, but perhaps only temporarily, because conventional credit is not showing significant contraction. In March of this year, according to the Central Bank, the total balance of operations advanced 0.9% versus February and 9.7% over 12 months, to R$ 7.2 trillion. Variations similar to those observed a year earlier, respectively, expansion of 0.6% and 9.9%.

The Central Bank and research institutes reveal, however, that default is explosive. Although the stock of credit eligible for negotiation is estimated at R$ 100 billion, with discounts of 70%, 80%, or even 90%, between R$ 20 billion and R$ 30 billion could be renegotiated, estimated Finance Minister Dario Durigan. For experts, it could be even more because the FGTS guarantee, even modest, is a powerful tool for convincing institutions that deal with a significant contingent of indebted people who prefer to keep them off their balance sheets.

According to the Map of Default and Debt Renegotiation – derived from the "Serasa Limpa Nome" program – in March of this year, Brazil had 82.8 million defaulters with R$ 557 billion in debt, at an average value of R$ 1,647.64. The average value of agreements closed was R$ 756 with discounts totaling R$ 18.4 billion. Still in March, according to Serasa Experian, 50.51% of the Brazilian population was in default. By state, the three most indebted are Amapá, 65.1% of the population, Federal District, 62.77%, and Amazonas, 60.10%. The three least indebted: Santa Catarina, 40.46%, Piauí, 41.20%, and Espírito Santo, 42.93%. By segment, the institution adds, the most significant debt is credit card debt, 27.3% of the total; basic accounts such as water, electricity and gas, 21%; debts with finance companies, 20.2%; and debts in services in general, 11.5%, among others of lesser significance.

Will we have Desenrola 3 or more? "Desenrola 2 is positive and will have results, but temporary ones because indebtedness in Brazil is structural. We will still have Desenrola 3, 4, or more," evaluates Alex Agostini, chief economist at Austin Rating, a Brazilian credit rating agency. He reminds NeoFeed that the 2023 experience, Desenrola 1, was also relevant. "Credit channels were recovered at that time, post-pandemic, when indebtedness reflected job and income loss. Considering that 60% of GDP comes from family consumption, the government needed to address indebtedness to prevent the economy from losing further traction," he recalls.

But Brazil has poor income distribution and its concentration as a structural problem, even though the government resorts to social programs as compensation. "In some countries, like the Nordic ones, this welfare exists. But nations have fiscal balance and tax structures established for years that compensate for income differences that are minimal," says Agostini. He warns that in Brazil, disparities are very large and the population has needs beyond basic ones. And the remaining needs end up being met through credit. "In practice, the Brazilian seeks a better quality of life. And indebtedness is not a lack of financial education, but a lack of available resources," observes the economist. "And that is a point that justifies the new edition of the program. Another point is that the government realized that indebtedness is affecting its image and decided to take some action. It is also important to remember that in Desenrola 1, the Selic rate was lower. Today it is just below 15%. Still the highest in two decades, but without curbing demand for credit because the need to improve social conditions is latent. Structural."

Also structural, the economist cites, is banking concentration. "We have about 140 banks today, but 85% of credit is in the hands of the five largest institutions. Therefore, there is limited competition among a few players. Another structural issue is the fact that the National Treasury acts as an actor similar to the citizen. As a borrower of resources, the Treasury aims at decisions that promote social balance. But, compared to the citizen, the Treasury is a powerful competitor for offering no risk."

One more structural difficulty, adds Agostini, is public spending that keeps interest rates under pressure. "And interest rates will only fall with a change in fiscal policy that should be austere and long-term. An option that would help monetary policy to naturally reduce the interest rate, benefiting not only families, but also the financial lives of companies with relevant results. Companies could produce more at a lower cost, employing more people and with greater productivity, thereby raising available income."
