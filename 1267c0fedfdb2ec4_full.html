# Desenrola 2 Provides Relief but Does Not Resolve Brazil's Structural Debt Crisis

On June 6, 2023, the Official Gazette of the Union published the Provisional Measure that institutionalized Desenrola Brasil – Emergency Program for Renegotiation of Debts of Defaulted Individuals, known as Desenrola 1. Three years later, Desenrola 2 is in the works and is scheduled to be launched on Monday, May 4.

The original program worked well, celebrated the Ministry of Finance in May 2024, with a balance of R$ 53 billion in refinancing benefiting 15 million people. Desenrola 2 could help twice as many Brazilians, but will it be sufficient to resolve the situation if the program pushes the debtor into more borrowing even at a rate of 1.99% per month? A point of concern: in Desenrola 1, each R$ 1 renegotiated resulted in R$ 1.15 in new defaults, according to the Central Bank. What will be the balance of the second initiative? Difficult to predict, but it should generally be positive. This is partly due to the use of R$ 4.5 billion from the FGTS, an amount disclosed on Wednesday, April 29, by the Minister of Labor and Employment Luiz Marinho, plus support from the Fundo Garantir de Operações (FGO) which could be strengthened with funds forgotten by account holders in banks. These "guarantor" instruments shield institutions against losses, but perhaps only temporarily because conventional credit does not show significant contraction. In March this year, according to the Central Bank, the total balance of operations advanced 0.9% against February and 9.7% over 12 months, to R$ 7.2 trillion. Variations close to those observed one year before, respectively, expansion of 0.6% and 9.9%.

The Central Bank and research institutes reveal, however, that default rates are explosive. Although the stock of credit eligible for renegotiation is estimated at R$ 100 billion, with discounts of 70%, 80% or even 90%, between R$ 20 billion and R$ 30 billion could be renegotiated, estimated Finance Minister Dario Durigan. For experts, it could be even more because the FGTS guarantee, even modest, is a powerful tool for persuasion especially for institutions dealing with a significant contingent of indebted individuals who prefer to keep them off their balance sheets.

According to the Map of Default and Debt Renegotiation – derived from the "Serasa Limpa Nome" program – in March this year, Brazil had 82.8 million negative-registered individuals with R$ 557 billion in debts, at an average value of R$ 1,647.64. The average value of agreements reached was R$ 756 with discounts totaling R$ 18.4 billion. Also in March, according to Serasa Experian, 50.51% of the Brazilian population was in default. By state, the three most indebted are Amapá, 65.1% of the population, Federal District, 62.77% and Amazonas, 60.10%. The three least indebted: Santa Catarina, 40.46%, Piauí, 41.20% and Espírito Santo, 42.93%. By segment, the institution adds, the most significant debt is credit card debt, 27.3% of the total; basic accounts such as water, electricity and gas, 21%; debts with finance companies, 20.2%; and debts for services in general, 11.5%, among others of lesser importance.

Will we have Desenrola 3 or more? "Desenrola 2 is positive and will produce results, but temporary ones because debt in Brazil is structural. We will still have Desenrola 3, 4 or more," evaluates Alex Agostini, chief economist at Austin Rating, a Brazilian credit risk rating agency. In an interview with NeoFeed, the specialist recalls that the 2023 experience, Desenrola 1, was also significant. "Credit channels were recovered at that moment, post-pandemic, when debt reflected job and income loss. Considering that 60% of GDP comes from family consumption, the government needed to address the debt to prevent the economy from losing more traction," he recalls.

But Brazil has income inequality and concentration as a structural problem, even though the government resorts to social programs as compensation. "In some countries, like the Nordic ones, this welfarism exists. But nations have fiscal balance and tax structures established for years that compensate for income differences that are minimal," says Agostini. He warns that in Brazil, disparities are very large and the population has needs beyond the basic ones. And additional needs end up being met through credit. "In practice, the Brazilian seeks a better quality of life. And debt is not lack of financial education, but lack of available resources," observes the economist. "And that's a point that justifies the new edition of the program. Another point is that the government realized that debt is affecting its image and decided to take action. It's also important to remember that in Desenrola 1, the Selic rate was lower. Today it's just below 15%. Still the highest in two decades, but without deterring demand for credit because the need to improve social conditions is latent. Structural."

Also structural, the economist notes, is banking concentration. "We have about 140 banks today, but 85% of credit is in the hands of the five largest institutions. Therefore, there is limited competition among few players. Another structural issue is the fact that the National Treasury acts as an actor similar to the citizen. As a borrower of resources, the Treasury targets decisions that promote social balance. But, compared to the citizen, the Treasury is a powerful competitor because it offers no risk." Another structural difficulty, adds Agostini, is public spending that keeps interest rates under pressure. "And interest rates will only fall with a change in fiscal policy that should be austere and long-term. A choice that would help monetary policy to naturally reduce the interest rate benefiting not only families, but also the financial lives of companies with relevant results. Companies could produce more at lower cost, employing more people and with greater productivity raising, by extension, available income."
