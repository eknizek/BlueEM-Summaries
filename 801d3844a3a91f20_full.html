Carryover effect: this month's inflation will reflect fuel price increases and sets high floor for May index

# Carryover effect: this month's inflation will reflect fuel price increases and sets high floor for May index

Amid volatility in international oil prices due to the extension of the Middle East conflict, and the back-and-forth over the reopening and closure of the Strait of Hormuz, experts rule out an immediate drop in fuel prices when the freezing agreement promoted by YPF ends on May 15. For this to happen, there must be a change in the trend, they anticipate. That is, a truce that persists and is respected, as well as a stabilization of crude oil value, which continues with permanent ups and downs. Despite last week's decline, the barrel rose again on Monday. At the close of this article, Brent was trading at US$ 95.78, almost 6% above where it closed on Friday.

Analysts point out that prices could drop if oil value eventually decreases significantly, a situation they consider unlikely to occur in the short term, as long as fluctuations in negotiations continue and contradictions persist in public statements by leaders, and global production recovery is delayed with infrastructure damaged by attacks.

Combined with the Government's decision to postpone the planned increase in fuel taxes to early April, the pump freeze functioned as a temporary anchor—a buffer, in the words of Horacio Marín, YPF's CEO—to prevent a larger price jump that would affect sales and have an inflationary effect. Had it not been applied, fuel would have continued rising at least 30% since the war began, while with the tax update, a liter of super fuel would have climbed above $2,000 on average in the City to $2,250, according to estimates by analysts like Nadin Argañaraz of the Argentine Fiscal Analysis Institute (Iaraf) and Daniel Dreizzen, former Planning Secretary and now at Aleph Energy.

When this 45-day brake ends in mid-May, YPF will evaluate the situation to determine next steps. But in principle, prices will not drop, the company anticipates, because once the agreement with the sector ends, that month and a half of losses will need to be compensated. Furthermore, there is still a lag relative to the public regarding costs. Nevertheless, going forward, the oil company is confident that when the war ends or international prices find equilibrium, the trend will be downward then. The question they are asking is what the new floor for the barrel will be. They consider it positive that it has dropped from the US$ 120 it reached, but they believe it must continue falling, though they think it will take time to return to pre-war levels, below US$ 70.

## Impact of fuel price increases on inflation

In parallel, the effect will be felt again in inflation. In March, fuels registered increases of over 20% that were not fully evident in the index released last week. This lower impact is explained by the measurement dynamics, which capture increases according to when in the month they are applied. Thus, the increases were reflected partially in the last 3.4% figure published by Indec for last month. Therefore, the carryover will impact the April CPI again and leaves a high floor.

"The impact of the fuel price increase in the March CPI was 7%, according to Indec, and for April we estimate it will be 6% due to the moderation of increases from YPF's stabilization decision, the natural price setter, which competitors follow," said Ricardo Delgado of Analytica. Gonzalo Carrera of Equilibra agreed that "there's still some shock in fuel that will hit at least half in April."

According to EcoGo's latest weekly survey, "beyond the fuel freeze, the statistical carryover of 8% inherited from March still persists." Meanwhile, for Capital Foundation, "fuels put a high floor on inflation for the fourth month." "The March increase was partially reflected in the CPI, leaving significant carryover. Thus, April's record has a floor of 0.6 points," it noted.

However, consulting firms project a deceleration. "Fuels could rise about 13% against last month due to the effect of increases throughout March that, therefore, affect April. That 13% would contribute about half a point of inflation this month, but it has been holding since the second week," considered Camilo Tiscornia of C&T Economic Advisors. "The carryover effect has been diluting in April and we're returning to the deceleration trend. If there are no new fuel increases, that trend will continue," added Aldo Abram of Liberty and Progress.
