Fuel Price Increases Set High Floor for May Inflation as March Carryover Effects Persist

# Fuel Price Increases Set High Floor for May Inflation as March Carryover Effects Persist

Amid volatility in international oil prices due to the extension of the Middle East conflict and ongoing discussions about reopening and closing the Strait of Hormuz, experts rule out an immediate drop in fuel prices when YPF's price freeze agreement ends on May 15th. For prices to fall, there would need to be a change in the trend—that is, a lasting and respected truce, as well as stabilization of crude oil values, which continue to experience constant fluctuations. Despite last week's decline, the barrel rose again on Monday. At the close of this article, Brent crude was trading at US$95.78, nearly 6% higher than Friday's close.

Analysts note that prices could decline if oil values drop significantly, but they consider this unlikely in the short term as long as fluctuations continue in negotiations, contradictions persist in leaders' public statements, and global production recovery is delayed due to infrastructure damaged by attacks.

Combined with the Government's decision to postpone the planned fuel tax increase to early April, the pump price freeze functioned as a temporary anchor—a buffer, in the words of YPF CEO Horacio Marín—to prevent a larger jump in prices that would affect sales and have an inflationary effect. Without it, fuel would have continued rising at least 30% since the war began. With the tax update, premium gasoline would have exceeded $2,000 on average in the City, reaching up to $2,250, according to estimates from analysts like Nadin Argañaraz from the Argentine Fiscal Analysis Institute (Iaraf) and Daniel Dreizzen, former Planning Secretary now at Aleph Energy.

When this 45-day freeze ends in mid-May, YPF will assess the situation to determine next steps. However, in principle, prices will not fall, the company anticipates, because once the sector agreement ends, it will need to compensate for that month and a half of losses. Moreover, there is still a lag in public prices relative to costs. Nevertheless, looking ahead, YPF is confident that when the war ends or international prices find equilibrium, the trend will indeed be downward. The question being asked is what the new floor for the barrel will be. They consider it positive that it has fallen from the US$120 it reached, but believe it must continue falling, though they think it will take time to return to pre-war levels below US$70.

## Impact of Fuel Price Increases on Inflation

In parallel, the effect will be felt again in inflation. In March, fuels registered increases greater than 20% that were not fully reflected in the index released last week. This lesser impact is explained by the measurement methodology, which captures increases according to when they are applied during the month. Thus, the increases were partially reflected in the last 3.4% figure published by Indec for last month. Therefore, the carryover will impact April's CPI again and leaves a high floor.

"The impact of the fuel increase on March's CPI was 7%, according to Indec, and for April we estimate it will be 6% due to the moderation of increases from YPF's stabilization decision, the natural price-setter followed by competitors," said Ricardo Delgado of Analytica. Gonzalo Carrera of Equilibra agreed that "there's still some shock in fuel that will hit at least half in April."

According to EcoGo's latest weekly survey, "beyond the fuel freeze, the statistical carryover of 8% inherited from March still persists." Meanwhile, for Fundación Capital, "fuels put a high floor on fourth-month inflation. The March increase was partially reflected in the CPI, leaving significant carryover. Thus, April's reading has a floor of 0.6 percentage points."

However, consulting firms project deceleration. "Fuels could rise about 13% from last month due to the increases throughout March that consequently affect April. That 13% would contribute about half a point of inflation this month, but it has remained stable since the second week," considered Camilo Tiscornia of C&T Economic Advisors. "The carryover effect has been diluting in April and we're returning to the deceleration trend. If there are no new fuel increases, that trend will continue," added Aldo Abram of Liberty and Progress.
