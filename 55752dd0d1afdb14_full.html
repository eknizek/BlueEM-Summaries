# Are CDTs a Good Investment? Here's How the Highest Interest Rates Are Being Passed On

With the increase in interest rates from the Central Bank in 2026, it is increasingly common to hear that one of the most attractive investment options for people with savings are the famous Certificates of Deposit to Term, or better known as CDTs.

But really, have they increased at the same speed as the Central Bank's rates?

So far this year, most members of the Board of Directors of the Central Bank have decided to raise interest rates by 200 basis points in order to control higher inflation expectations that are above 6 percent for the close of this year when they were previously around 4 percent.

In part, these greater pressures are due to the 23 percent increase in the minimum wage decreed by the Government of Gustavo Petro and more recently to the higher prices that could come from the conflict in Iran, especially because basic goods such as gas and fertilizers would become more expensive.

"The war in Iran could compromise growth and stability of the global economy in the context of great uncertainty about its duration and intensity. Its effects on the Colombian economy would be mixed. On one hand, it would improve the terms of trade thanks to the increase in oil prices. On the other, it would make basic goods such as gas and fertilizers more expensive, which the country needs to import in significant quantities. This could accentuate inflationary pressures for the present year, derived from higher energy prices and increases in food prices," can be read in the meeting minutes.

In total, interest rates have increased 200 basis points so far this year. In January, the increase was 100 points, from the level of 9.25 to 10.25 percent, the first increase in 33 months; and in the last meeting in March, the majority of the co-directors decided to raise them another 100 points, to 11.25 percent.

Precisely, this last increase was what caused discord between the Government and the Central Bank. When seeing this 100-point increase, the Minister of Finance, Germán Ávila, who voted to reduce the rates, left the meeting and threatened not to return to any board, something paradoxical since he is the one who presides over it.

"It is absurd to propose taking monetary policy measures that will lead the economy to contraction right at this moment. The government establishes a clear and decisive distance. While the scenario is one of using the little-legitimate majority in the country, we believe we should not participate," Ávila said at the time.

The next session in which the Central Bank Board could raise interest rates again will be held this Thursday, April 30, and despite Ávila's initial threat, a few days ago he left open the possibility of returning and resuming institutional dialogue with the Central Bank.

"The Government is willing to continue with the debate and hold the necessary debate in the board of directors," he affirmed in a control debate held in the Third Commission of the Senate.

## Have Higher Rates Been Passed On to CDTs?

The Central Bank's rates and those of CDTs always move in the same direction, so if the Central Bank has been increasing them in recent months, banks have also raised theirs to be more competitive, something that generates higher returns for savers but concerns those who need to borrow right now and take out loans.
