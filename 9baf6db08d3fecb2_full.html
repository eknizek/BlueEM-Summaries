# Lovable's 'barefoot' strategy: no fixed roadmap, plans change every 15 hours

At Lovable's headquarters in Stockholm, a pile of shoes at the entrance is not just a Swedish custom—it's a physical reminder of a culture that prioritizes pragmatism over protocol. It was in this environment that Fabian Hedin, cofounder and CTO of the startup, shaped the thesis he currently defends before an audience in Las Vegas, United States. In the race for artificial intelligence, rigid adherence to a business plan is the fastest path to obsolescence.

In a market that moves on an hourly scale, Lovable has emerged as a case study in radical agility. The Swedish startup, founded in the final days of 2023, was valued at $6.6 billion in December 2025 after raising $330 million in a funding round led by CapitalG and Menlo Ventures, an Alphabet fund.

Lovable is one of the leading names driving "vibe coding," a technology that allows users, even without knowledge of code, to describe a project in plain language and have a complete program created by AI.

For Hedin, the traditional concept of an annual roadmap has been replaced by a mindset of constant pivoting, where complex technical decisions are constantly reassessed. "It's very difficult to make a 12-month roadmap, both from the technical side and the product side. There's a saying that 'planning is everything, the plan is useless,'" he said, using an expression attributed to Dwight D. Eisenhower, the 34th president of the United States. "So, it might be worth doing some planning, but you should expect not to have to follow it. And that's something we make a point of emphasizing internally."

For him, the AI market is so dynamic that technical and product decisions are revisited not monthly, but on 15-hour scales. "At Lovable, if something made sense two months ago, but things have changed today, we simply change it. We don't force the team to follow a mistake just because it was on paper," he said at Google Cloud Next 2026.

Despite the speed at which transformations happen, the entrepreneur says it's important to have clarity about the business's purpose. Lovable's mission—to allow anyone, the "99%" who don't know how to program, to turn ideas into reality—remains the only non-negotiable element of the original plan.

## Back to Basics

This agility wasn't always the rule. The Swedish entrepreneur recounted a painful lesson learned early in the journey, when the team was seduced by the hype of future AI capabilities. At the time of GPT-3.5, Lovable invested months of engineering in building an ultra-advanced agent orchestration system. "We were living too much in the future, betting on external events that hadn't happened yet," he revealed.

As a result, the startup created a product that didn't meet customers' immediate needs and consumed vital resources. The difficult decision came soon after: discard months of code and "go back to basics." "It was a strong emotional decision. We had to tell the engineers that we were wrong and that we would build what makes sense today," he said.

The story served as an example for the audience, made up of new entrepreneurs. On one hand, it translates the advice of creating value today for businesses, without waiting for the next "perfect" model, investing in connecting existing capabilities creatively to solve real market pain points.

On the other hand, there's the recommendation to stop designing architectures for three years from now. In an ecosystem that receives deep updates every quarter, building rigid infrastructures is an invitation to waste.
