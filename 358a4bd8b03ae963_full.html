# Fed and earnings from four of the Magnificent Seven mark a crucial Wednesday for Wall Street

The combination of the Federal Reserve's decision and the publication of results from four of the Magnificent Seven marks one of the most decisive sessions of the quarter for financial markets.

The U.S. monetary authority is on track to keep interest rates unchanged, while the market evaluates signals about the future of monetary policy in an environment of geopolitical uncertainty. At the same time, Amazon (AMZN), Microsoft (MSFT), Alphabet (GOOGL), and Meta (META) concentrate attention with their results, in a session that tests valuations in the technology sector.

"We think the numbers will be good in general terms, but we believe capital expenditure forecasts will be increased for the whole year," said Javier Cabrera, analyst at XTB. "On the other hand, we will have interest rate decisions from the most important central banks, mainly the Fed and ECB, although no changes are expected."

Technology stocks arrive at this appointment at an especially sensitive point following recent market behavior, after reaching historic highs on Monday and correcting sharply in the following session.

The immediate trigger comes from reports published by The Wall Street Journal indicating that OpenAI failed to meet its internal objectives for user growth and sales in recent months. Although the company dismissed the report as "pure clickbait" and assured that its business is "running at full speed," the episode was enough to revive investor skepticism about artificial intelligence's ability to generate short-term returns.

The projected investment volume has reinforced the importance of this earnings season. It is estimated that major technology companies will collectively allocate approximately $645 billion in 2026, 56% more than the previous year, in an environment where, according to Charu Chanana, chief investment strategist at Saxo Bank, "the market no longer rewards AI ambition alone," but rather "now demands proof that spending continues to generate sustained growth, higher profits, and clearer return on investment."

## The Federal Reserve: A Pause with a More Restrictive Bias

The Fed meeting occurs in an environment where inflationary risks coexist with signals of weakness in the labor market, within the framework of the institution's dual mandate. Inflation has been driven by an energy shock, with monthly growth in March of 0.9% and an annual rate of 3.3%, a movement that conditions monetary policy expectations.

Matthew Ryan, analyst at Ebury, argues that "the energy shock has already left its mark on U.S. inflation" and anticipates that "a period of persistently elevated energy prices seems likely," which could affect the central bank's decisions.

From ING, James Knightley highlights that "the vast majority of FOMC members saw employment risks tilted to the downside," but "progress on inflation would likely be slower due to economic headwinds stemming from Middle East developments."

In this context, President Jerome Powell could harden his tone without altering the immediate course of monetary policy. "We expect Powell to adopt a moderately more restrictive tone during his press conference given upward pressure on inflation from the war," said Ryan. However, he notes that "he will likely present the energy shock as a complicating factor rather than a trigger for action."

The market also evaluates the transition toward a possible change in leadership, following the nomination of Kevin Warsh as the next president of the Federal Reserve. Ryan notes that "his comments suggesting a move away from asset purchases were seen as positive for the dollar," while "his plans to abandon forward guidance and change the inflation framework introduce uncertainty."

Despite these factors, the base scenario points to interest rate stability in the short term. "We expect the Fed to keep rates unchanged during the next meetings while assessing the war's impact on inflation," said the Ebury analyst, who anticipates a rate cut by September.

## Amazon and Microsoft: AI Execution Under Scrutiny

On the corporate front, Amazon arrives at results with revenue expectations of $177.2 billion and EPS of $2.12, according to Bloomberg data, after beating estimates in seven of the last eight quarters. Deutsche Bank analysts anticipate "results in line with consensus and near the upper end of guidance, driven by resilient e-commerce trends and accelerated AWS growth."

The cloud business remains the main catalyst, with signals of accelerating demand. The German bank emphasizes that "AWS appears to be a source of strength, with data indicating acceleration in both new commitments and consumption growth," while market focus shifts toward margins in an artificial intelligence investment environment.

Meanwhile, Microsoft presents results with estimated revenue of $81.5 billion and EPS of $4.03, according to Bloomberg data, also with a track record of eight consecutive quarters beating forecasts. Bank of America (BAC) notes that "Azure growth depends on artificial intelligence capacity coming online, requiring a beat to drive stock momentum."

The market closely follows artificial intelligence monetization, particularly the deployment of Copilot. "We believe momentum in Copilot paid seats is necessary to validate the return on AI investment," say BofA analysts in a report. Chanana raises the question that "the key question is whether demand for AI products and services remains strong enough to justify the wave of investment," in a scenario where the market maintains tolerance for spending but with more defined limits.

## Alphabet and Meta: Advertising, Cloud, and Cost Discipline

Alphabet faces the publication with estimated revenue of $91.6 billion and EPS of $2.81, with a track record of results that continues to demonstrate resilience in the advertising market while facing the challenge of proving profitability in AI investments.

Meta also releases results in an environment where advertising demand remains solid, but investors scrutinize whether the company's efficiency measures and AI spending balance deliver the expected returns.
