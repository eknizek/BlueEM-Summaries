# The Fed and Earnings from Four of the Magnificent Seven Mark a Key Wednesday for Wall Street

The combination of the Federal Reserve's decision and the publication of results from four of the Magnificent Seven marks one of the most decisive sessions of the quarter for financial markets.

The U.S. monetary authority is headed toward keeping interest rates unchanged, while the market evaluates signals about the future of monetary policy in an environment of geopolitical uncertainty. At the same time, Amazon (AMZN), Microsoft (MSFT), Alphabet (GOOGL), and Meta (META) are concentrating attention with their results, in a session that tests valuations in the technology sector.

"We think the figures will be good in general terms, but we believe capital expenditure forecasts will be raised for the whole year," said Javier Cabrera, analyst at XTB. "On the other hand, we will have interest rate decisions from the most important central banks, mainly the Fed and ECB, although no changes are expected."

Technology stocks arrive at this meeting at a particularly sensitive point following recent market behavior, after reaching historic highs on Monday and correcting sharply in the following session.

The immediate trigger comes from reports published by The Wall Street Journal indicating that OpenAI had not met its internal objectives for user growth and sales in recent months. Although the company characterized the report as "pure clickbait" and assured that its businesses "are running at full steam," the episode was enough to revive investor skepticism about artificial intelligence's ability to generate returns in the short term.

The planned investment volume has reinforced the importance of this earnings season. It is estimated that large technology companies will allocate, collectively, some $645 billion in 2026, 56% more than the previous year, in an environment where, according to Charu Chanana, chief investment strategist at Saxo Bank, "the market no longer exclusively rewards AI ambition," but rather "now demands proof that spending continues to generate sustained growth, higher profits, and clearer investment returns."

## The Federal Reserve: Pause with More Restrictive Bias

The Fed meeting occurs in an environment where inflationary risks coexist with signals of weakness in the labor market, within the framework of the institution's dual mandate. Inflation has been driven by the energy shock, with a monthly advance in March of 0.9% and an annual rate of 3.3%, in a movement that conditions monetary policy expectations.

Matthew Ryan, analyst at Ebury, argues that "the energy shock has already left its mark on U.S. inflation" and anticipates that "a period of persistently high energy prices seems likely," which could affect the central bank's decisions. From ING, James Knightley highlights that "the vast majority of FOMC members saw employment risks tilted to the downside," but "progress on inflation would likely be slower due to economic headwinds stemming from Middle East developments."

Within this framework, Chairman Jerome Powell could toughen his tone without altering the immediate course of monetary policy. "We expect Powell to adopt a moderately more restrictive tone during his press conference given the upward pressure on inflation from the war," said Ryan. However, he notes that Powell "will likely present the energy shock as a complicating factor rather than a trigger for action."

The market is also evaluating the transition toward a possible leadership change, following the nomination of Kevin Warsh as the next Federal Reserve chairman. Ryan points out that "his comments suggesting a move away from asset purchases were seen as positive for the dollar," while "his plans to abandon forward guidance and change the inflation framework introduce uncertainty."

Despite these elements, the base scenario points to rate stability in the short term. "We expect the Fed to keep rates unchanged during the upcoming meetings while it assesses the impact of the war on inflation," said the Ebury analyst, who anticipates a rate cut by September.

## Amazon and Microsoft: AI Execution Under Scrutiny

On the corporate front, Amazon arrives at results with revenue expectations of $177.2 billion and EPS of $2.12, according to data compiled by Bloomberg, after beating estimates in seven of the last eight quarters. Deutsche Bank analysts anticipate "results in line with consensus and near the top end of guidance, driven by resilient e-commerce trends and accelerated AWS growth."

The cloud business remains the primary catalyst, with signals of acceleration in demand. The German bank emphasizes that "AWS appears to be a source of strength, with data indicating acceleration in both new commitments and consumption growth," while market focus shifts toward margins in an artificial intelligence investment environment.

Meanwhile, Microsoft presents results with estimated revenue of $81.5 billion and EPS of $4.03, according to Bloomberg data, also with a track record of eight consecutive quarters beating expectations. Bank of America (BAC) notes that "Azure growth depends on artificial intelligence capacity coming online, requiring an above-expectations result to drive the stock."

The market is closely monitoring artificial intelligence monetization, particularly Copilot deployment. "We believe momentum in Copilot paid seats is necessary to validate the return on artificial intelligence investment," say BofA analysts in a report. Chanana raises the question that "the key question is whether demand for AI products and services remains strong enough to justify the wave of investment," in a scenario where the market maintains tolerance toward spending, but with more defined limits.

## Alphabet and Meta: Advertising, Cloud, and Cost Discipline

Alphabet faces publication with estimated revenue of $91.6 billion and EPS of $2.81, with a track record of results demonstrating resilience in the advertising market and growth in cloud services, while Meta similarly navigates advertising dynamics and capital expenditure levels tied to artificial intelligence infrastructure investments.
