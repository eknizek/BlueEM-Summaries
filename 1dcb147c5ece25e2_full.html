Inflationary pressures will rise in second half, projects Banamex

20 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Inflationary Pressures Will Rise in Second Half, Banamex Projects

The path toward consolidating stable prices still presents challenges. Iván Arias, director of Economic Studies at Banamex, warned that it is premature to "declare victory" in the face of external shocks, predicting that inflationary effects will materialize with greater force during the second half of the year. The specialist emphasized that, while the country has shown notable strength compared to crises of past decades, caution must be the compass guiding current economic policy.

For Banamex's executive, the dynamics of international conflicts and global supply chains remain latent risk factors for Mexican consumers' wallets. This view suggests that the Bank of Mexico will maintain strict monitoring, as the transfer of international costs to final consumer prices typically has a lag that will coincide with the end of 2026.

In this regard, Sergio Kurczyn, director of Economic Studies, believed that the central bank did make an error in its latest monetary policy decision because it should not have cut the rate to 6.75 percent. "We do believe it made a mistake this last time. They want to reach 3.00%, as they've been saying for 20 years they will, but they always delay... Nobody believes the Bank of Mexico's forecasts beyond six months."

Regarding economic growth expectations, Banamex does not consider viable the 3.00% growth for 2026 estimated by the Treasury Department, and projects a lower rate of 1.6 percent.

## A Rise in Tortilla Prices Would Be Disastrous

A price increase in tortillas would not only represent a blow to the wallets of the most vulnerable sectors, but would have "disastrous" effects on national economic dynamics, warned Rodolfo Ostolaza, subdirector of Economic Studies at Banamex. In an environment where wage growth has failed to keep pace with the rising cost of living in recent years, the increase in this basic product, estimated at between two and four pesos per kilogram, threatens to become a drag on domestic consumption and, therefore, on national production.

For Banamex's analyst, tortillas are not just another product in the basic basket; they are the axis around which food security and household spending stability revolve. He explained that the increase in tortilla prices would generate a negative domino effect, as being an essential item, its consumption is inelastic; that is, families cannot simply stop buying it, so they sacrifice other sectors of the economy to cover that additional cost.

"Income won't increase. So you'll stop consuming to acquire something you need to live. If you brake consumption, production and demand brake," he explained.
