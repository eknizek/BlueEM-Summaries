# Inflationary Pressures Will Rise in Second Half of Year, Banamex Projects

The path toward consolidating stable prices still presents challenges. Iván Arias, director of Economic Studies at Banamex, warned that it is premature to "celebrate victory" in the face of external shocks, predicting that inflationary effects will materialize with greater force during the second half of the year. The specialist emphasized that, while the country has shown notable strength compared to crises of decades past, caution should be the compass of current economic policy.

For the Banamex executive, the dynamics of international conflicts and global supply chains remain latent risk factors for the wallets of Mexicans. This perspective suggests that the Bank of Mexico will maintain strict monitoring, since the pass-through of international costs to final consumer prices typically has a lag that will coincide with the end of 2026.

In this regard, Sergio Kurczyn, director of Economic Studies, believed that the central bank did make an error in its latest monetary policy decision because it should not have cut the rate to 6.75 percent. "We do believe it made a mistake this last time. They want to reach 3.00%, as they've been saying for the past 20 years they will reach, but they always delay it... Nobody believes the Bank of Mexico's forecasts beyond six months."

Regarding economic growth expectations, Banamex does not consider viable a growth rate of 3.00% for 2026 as estimated by the Secretary of Finance and Public Credit, and instead projects a lower rate of 1.6 percent.

## Tortilla Price Increase, Disastrous

An upward adjustment in tortilla prices would not only represent a blow to the wallets of the most vulnerable sectors, but would have "disastrous" effects on the dynamics of the national economy, warned Rodolfo Ostolaza, subdirector of Economic Studies at Banamex. In an environment where wage growth has failed to keep pace with the rising cost of living in recent years, the increase in this basic product, estimated at between two and four pesos per kilo, threatens to become a drag on domestic consumption and, therefore, on national production.

For the Banamex analyst, tortilla is not just another product in the basic basket; it is the axis around which food security and household spending stability revolve. He explained that the increase in tortilla prices would generate a negative domino effect, since being an essential item, its consumption is inelastic; that is, families cannot simply stop buying it, so they sacrifice other sectors of the economy to cover that additional cost.

"Income will not increase. So you will stop consuming in order to acquire something you need to live. If you slow consumption, you slow production and demand," he explained.
