Inflationary pressures to rise in second half, Banamex forecasts

19 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Inflationary pressures to rise in second half, Banamex forecasts

The path toward consolidating stable prices still presents challenges. Iván Arias, director of Economic Studies at Banamex, warned that it is premature to "celebrate victory" against external shocks, forecasting that inflationary effects will materialize with greater force during the second half of the year. The specialist emphasized that, while the country has shown notable strength compared to crises of past decades, caution should be the compass guiding current economic policy.

For Banamex's executive, the dynamics of international conflicts and global supply chains remain latent risk factors for Mexican consumers' wallets. This outlook suggests that Banco de México will maintain strict monitoring, since the transfer of international costs to final consumer prices typically has a lag that will coincide with the end of 2026.

Sergio Kurczyn, director of Economic Studies, believed that the central bank did make an error in its latest monetary policy decision by cutting the rate to 6.75 percent. "We do believe it made a mistake this last time, they want to reach 3.00%, as they've said for 20 years they would reach, but they always delay... Nobody believes Banco de México's forecasts beyond six months."

Regarding economic growth expectations, Banamex does not consider viable the 3.00% growth for 2026 estimated by the Finance Ministry, and forecasts a lower rate of 1.6 percent.

## A tortilla price increase would be disastrous

An increase in tortilla prices would not only represent a blow to the most vulnerable sectors, but would have "disastrous" effects on national economic dynamics, warned Rodolfo Ostolaza, subdirector of Economic Studies at Banamex. In an environment where wage growth has failed to keep pace with inflation in recent years, the increase in this basic product, estimated between two and four pesos per kilo, threatens to become a drag on domestic consumption and, consequently, national production.

For Banamex's analyst, tortilla is not just another product in the basic basket; it is the axis upon which food security and household spending stability revolve. He explained that a tortilla price increase would generate a negative domino effect, since as an essential item, its consumption is inelastic; that is, families cannot simply stop buying it, so they sacrifice other sectors of the economy to cover that additional cost.

"Income won't increase. So you'll stop consuming to acquire something you need to live. If you slow consumption, you slow production and demand," he explained.
