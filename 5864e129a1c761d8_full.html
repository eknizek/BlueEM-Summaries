# Brazil's Tax Burden Rises to 32.4% of GDP in 2025, Highest Level on Record

Brazil's gross tax burden reached 32.4% of GDP in 2025, totaling R$ 4.12 trillion, according to data released Friday (April 10) by the National Treasury. The result, presented in the Bulletin of Estimates of the Gross Tax Burden of the General Government, represents an increase of 0.18 percentage points (p.p.) of GDP compared to 2024 and marks the highest level in the historical series that began in 2010.

The indicator measures the proportion of taxes collected by the three levels of government (federal, state, and municipal) in relation to the size of the economy. In practice, it shows what portion of everything produced in the country is allocated to the payment of taxes such as income taxes, contributions, and fees.

The advance in 2025 was driven by the federal government, with an increase of 0.26 p.p. of GDP. The increase in federal tax collection was primarily driven by growth of 0.23 p.p. of GDP in the collection of IRRF (Withholding Income Tax). Municipalities contributed with an increase of 0.03 p.p., while state governments showed a decline of 0.10 p.p.

In addition to IRRF, the federal results were boosted by increased collection of IOF (Tax on Financial Operations), which rose 0.10 percentage points of GDP—reflecting foreign exchange and credit operations and increased tax rates—and by social security contributions to the RGPS (General Social Security Regime), which advanced 0.12 percentage points, accompanying the gradual re-taxation of payroll and the growth of formal employment.

States underperformed the economy, mainly due to a 0.09 percentage point decline in GDP in ICMS (Goods and Services Tax) collection, despite nominal revenue growth. In municipalities, there was an increase of 0.03 percentage points of GDP, driven mainly by ISS (Service Tax) of 0.02 points, in line with the expansion of the services sector. According to the Treasury, taxes on property, such as IPTU (Real Estate Tax), also contributed, albeit to a lesser extent.

The composition of the tax burden remained relatively stable in 2025. Taxes on goods and services remain the primary source of collection, although they declined slightly as a proportion of GDP (from 13.87% to 13.78%). Taxes on income, profits, and capital gains increased their share (from 9.04% to 9.16%). Among social contributions, those destined for the RGPS grew (from 5.28% to 5.40% of GDP), following the evolution of the labor market, while contributions to individual pension schemes remained virtually stable.

Despite the recent increase, Brazil's taxation level remains close to that of developed countries, slightly below the OECD (Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development) average, which hovers around 34% of GDP, and above the Latin American average (approximately 21%).
