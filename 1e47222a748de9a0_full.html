# The Game is Congress

"Have you already decided who you're going to vote for?" is the question we've heard most during the last week. We infer that our interlocutor is evidently interested in knowing our presidential vote. It's normal. The campaign for head of state consumes almost all the political oxygen and the population's attention span. In an electoral cycle that, moreover, judging by the polls, many people didn't pay too much attention to until the last days, it was asking too much to look beyond that.

But that error could be enormous. As we know, today we are choosing not only the two candidates who will advance to the runoff, but also all members of the chambers of deputies and senators. And if the political history of the last decade were a helpful guide, it would probably whisper to us that we should take the vote for the bicameral Congress much more seriously than the presidential vote.

There would indeed be some reasons to qualify its advice. One of them is that—since the reinstatement of the Senate—presidential vacancy due to "permanent moral incapacity" has a double filter. It must be approved by qualified majorities of at least two-thirds in both chambers. That means 87 deputies and now, additionally, 40 senators. That is, it's still possible to remove the president using pure political force in Congress—as has been done before—but it will no longer be so simple. Just 21 senators could block any attempt at presidential removal, capricious or not. The additional protection gives more weight to the president.

Still, the advice from recent political history should not be ignored. How much time has been invested listening to presidential debates? Interviews in the media with candidates for head of state? Analysis of the résumés of aspirants to the sash? Coffee shop discussions or taxi conversations? Almost everything has revolved around that, and around a single outcome that carries much uncertainty. In other words, only one person out of the 35 in contention will be president and no one knows who it will be right now.

At the same time, however, it already seems almost inevitable that some people—the first names on the parliamentary lists of parties with the highest voting intention—will take their seats in three and a half months. We could have gotten to know them better. They are already preparing their swearing-in for late July and assembling their legislative staff teams.

That is why attention should really be focused there. Each senator's vote, especially, could carry enormous weight on the country's key decisions. There are, we insist, only 60 people. In addition to the aforementioned vacancy for moral incapacity, this group has been granted, for example, the responsibility to change anything they see fit from the legislative proposals sent by the Chamber of Deputies. Bills modified by the Senate do not return to the deputies, as happens in other countries, but can be sent directly to the President of the Republic for promulgation.

The Senate also appoints, at the Executive's proposal, the Comptroller General, the President of the Central Reserve Bank, and the Superintendent of the SBS. It directly elects the Ombudsman and the members of the Constitutional Court. Some decisions require a simple majority; others, an absolute majority; and a minority requires two-thirds approval, but it's not an exaggeration to say that a good portion of the country can be managed with 31 people; the rest is managed with 41.

With regard to almost all major decisions, it is true, the new arrangement requires at least two-thirds of the votes from both chambers. This is the case, for example, with constitutional reform. The Constitution does not directly contemplate the possibility of a constitutional assembly, but that number of votes could be enough to attempt something similar, or a route as has been done before.
