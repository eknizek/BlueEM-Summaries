# Fictor Judicial Recovery Reveals R$1.8 Billion Circulation Through Shell Companies

Thousands of creditors, dozens of empty companies and R$4.3 billion in debt. The judicial recovery (RJ) of Fictor, granted about ten days ago by the 3rd Bankruptcy Court of the State of São Paulo, begins surrounded by suspicions of fraud, asset confusion, creation of fictitious credits and asset stripping, as well as accusations against its main partners and doubts about their integrity, according to creditors, court documents and reports from the judicial administrator Laspro.

NeoFeed obtained exclusive access to more than 50 documents involving cases that Fictor, which tried to buy Master last November, faces in court. The set of documents indicates that funds raised from investors were redistributed among group companies and partners through mutual loans and advances without clear accounting records, including operations that would directly benefit CEO Rafael Góis, whose assets grew sharply while his personal debts exploded.

The company's debt to related parties, according to the judicial administrator, jumped from near zero in 2024 to R$1.8 billion in 2025. This is the holding's liability to related parties, and other group companies had a similar structure, which may indicate that the movement is larger. The conglomerate's debt to group companies was R$1.13 billion in January of this year.

"This indicates the creation of fictitious 'intercompany' credits so that the Group itself can dominate the General Creditors' Assembly and approve a judicial recovery plan harmful to real creditors (investors)," says lawyer Felipe Gousen da Silveira, who represents more than 200 creditors of the company, in a petition against Fictor's partners, in which he points out "serious indications of fraud, asset confusion, asset stripping and corporate shielding".

At the CVM, an investor harmed by the company sent a representation through lawyer Carlos Ayalla, in which he points out "robust indications of fraud, reckless management, inducing investors into error and irregular collection of popular savings".

A significant portion of the accusations stems from information present in technical reports conducted by judicial administrator Laspro, appointed by the Bankruptcy Court. According to Laspro, the balance sheets of companies linked to Fictor present a series of inconsistencies, indications of asset confusion and even asset stripping by the company's partners.

According to the report, amounts raised by Fictor Invest via Sociedades de Conta em Participação (SCP) were directed to financing group activities. However, Fictor's numbers show a series of loans made between its own companies, advances and payments, and unexplained loans in the name of its partners.

## Operations with Partners

Fictor's CEO and founder, Rafael Góis, appears at the center of suspicions when moving extremely high amounts incompatible with the company's financial situation. According to Fictor's balance sheet presented by Laspro, Góis would have a debt of R$173.1 million. Fictor, in turn, would have a debt to Góis of R$373 thousand, stemming from a mutual loan.

The defense of creditors, which had access to Rafael Góis's income tax return, points to a substantial increase in the CEO's assets. According to Silveira's petition sent to the Bankruptcy Court, Góis's total assets would have increased from R$27.16 million in 2023 to R$161 million in 2024, with declared income of R$4.3 million and R$400 thousand exempt.

Góis's personal debts, on the other hand, would have increased from R$17.3 million to R$294.8 million, which, according to Silveira, "characterizes the creation of apparent personal insolvency (fictitious liability) to avoid attachments and executions".

According to the defense, Góis would have declared a debt of R$134.7 million with Fictor Holding in 2024. The petition further points out that Góis would have declared R$109 million in the Fictor Agro FIM fund and an acquisition of Luiz Phillippe Gomes Rubini's stake in Fictor Invest for R$161.7 million.

"This simulates a billion-dollar purchase in the individual person to justify the transit of fortunes or money laundering," the petition states.

In Fictor's January 2026 balance sheet, presented by Laspro, Fictor's debt from mutual loans would be R$478.6 million, with a significant portion to related parties. As an individual, Luiz Phillippe Gomes Rubini, former Fictor partner, has the largest balance to receive from mutual loans signed with the company: R$34.4 million. Another partner, Rafael Paixão Ferreira Giuseppe, would have R$373 thousand to receive from mutual loans (yes, an amount equal to that of CEO Rafael Góis).

Laspro, however, points out, based on bank statements, that Fictor would have received R$20 million in October 2025 from a Giuseppe account, but that it would not have been recorded.

"The cross-referencing of bank statements with the 2025 verification balance sheet demonstrated that the loan and mutual item in the name of Rafael Paixão presented movement inconsistent with amounts received by Fictor Holding and requires clarification."

According to Laspro, the cross-referencing of bank transfers made in October 2025 by Fictor Holding in favor of other group companies totaled R$131.6 million. However, when comparing the numbers with the 2025 balance sheet, the administrator stated that "corresponding accounting records for these amounts were not identified".

"The accounting inconsistency may compromise the reliability of the balance of the respective account, as well as the adequate representation of the group's patrimonial and financial position and requires clarification from the applicant, pointing out the accounting record of this movement," stated Laspro.
