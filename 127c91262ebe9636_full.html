# Exclusive: How Over R$1.8 Billion Circulated Among Shell Companies and Fictor Partners

Thousands of creditors, dozens of empty companies and R$4.3 billion in debt. The judicial reorganization (RJ) of Fictor, granted about ten days ago by the 3rd Bankruptcy Court of the State of São Paulo, begins surrounded by suspicions of fraud, confusion of assets, creation of fictitious credits and asset stripping, as well as accusations against its main partners and concerns about their integrity, according to creditors, court documents and reports from the judicial administrator Laspro.

NeoFeed had exclusive access to more than 50 documents involving legal proceedings that Fictor, which attempted to buy Master in November of last year, is facing in court. The collection of documents indicates that funds raised from investors were redistributed among group companies and partners through mutual loans and advances without clear accounting records, including operations that would directly benefit CEO Rafael Góis, whose assets grew abruptly while his personal debts exploded.

The company's debt to related parties, according to the judicial administrator, jumped from close to zero in 2024 to R$1.8 billion in 2025. This is the holding company's liability to related parties, and other group companies had a similar structure, which may indicate that the movement is even larger. The conglomerate's debt to group companies was R$1.13 billion in January of this year.

"This indicates the creation of 'fictitious' credits (intercompany) so that the Group itself can dominate the General Assembly of Creditors and approve a judicial reorganization plan harmful to real creditors (investors)," says lawyer Felipe Gousen da Silveira, who represents more than 200 creditors of the company, in a petition against Fictor's partners, in which he points to "serious evidence of fraud, confusion of assets, asset stripping and corporate shielding."

At the CVM, an investor harmed by the company submitted a representation through lawyer Carlos Ayalla, in which he points to "robust evidence of fraud, reckless management, inducing investors into error and irregular collection of popular savings."

A significant part of the accusations stems from information contained in technical reports prepared by the judicial administrator Laspro, appointed by the Bankruptcy Court. According to Laspro, the financial statements of companies linked to Fictor present a series of inconsistencies, evidence of confusion of assets and even company stripping by the company's partners.

According to the report, the amounts raised by Fictor Invest through Sociedades de Conta em Participação (SCP) were directed to finance group activities. However, Fictor's numbers show a series of loans made between its own companies, advances and payments, and unexplained loans in the names of its partners.

## Operations with Partners

CEO and founder of Fictor, Rafael Góis, appears at the center of suspicions by moving extremely high amounts incompatible with the company's financial situation. According to Fictor's balance sheet presented by Laspro, Góis would have a debt of R$173.1 million. Fictor, in turn, would have a debt to Góis of R$373 thousand, from a mutual loan.

The creditors' defense, which had access to Rafael Góis's income tax return, points to a substantial increase in the CEO's assets. According to Silveira's petition sent to the Bankruptcy Court, Góis's total assets would have increased from R$27.16 million in 2023 to R$161 million in 2024, with declared income of R$4.3 million and R$400 thousand exempt.

Góis's personal debts, on the other hand, would have increased from R$17.3 million to R$294.8 million, which, according to Silveira, "characterizes creation of apparent personal insolvency (fictitious liability) to avoid arrests and enforcement." According to the defense, Góis would have declared a debt of R$134.7 million to Fictor Holding in 2024.

The petition further points out that Góis would have declared R$109 million in the Fictor Agro FIM fund and an acquisition of Luiz Phillippe Gomes Rubini's stake in Fictor Invest for R$161.7 million. "This simulates a billion-dollar purchase in the individual to justify the transfer of fortunes or money laundering," states the petition.

In Fictor's balance sheet from January 2026, presented by Laspro, Fictor's debt from mutual loans would be R$478.6 million, with a significant portion to related parties. In the individual capacity, Luiz Phillippe Gomes Rubini, former Fictor partner, is the one with the largest balance to receive in mutual loans signed with the company: R$34.4 million. Another partner, Rafael Paixão Ferreira Giuseppe, would have R$373 thousand to receive from mutual loans (yes, an amount equal to CEO Rafael Góis's).

Laspro, however, points out, based on bank statements, that Fictor would have received R$20 million in October 2025 from a Giuseppe account, but this would not have been recorded. "The crossing of the bank statement with the trial balance for 2025 demonstrated that the loan and mutual item in the name of Rafael Paixão presented movements inconsistent with the amounts received by Fictor Holding and requires clarification."

According to Laspro, the crossing of bank transfers made in October 2025 by Fictor Holding in favor of other group companies totaled R$131.6 million. However, when comparing the numbers with the 2025 balance sheet, the administrator stated that "the corresponding accounting records of these amounts were not identified."

"The accounting inconsistency may compromise the reliability of the balance of the respective account, as well as the adequate representation of the patrimonial and financial position of the group and requires clarifications from the petitioner, pointing out the accounting record of this movement," stated Laspro.
