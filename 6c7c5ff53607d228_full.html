# Investment Fund Administration Industry Grew 23.5% Last Year, Recording Record AUM of $43.85 Billion

The investment fund industry returned to a growth path in 2025, following a previous year that slowed its exponential advance since the beginning of the millennium, according to records from the Chilean Association of Investment Fund Administrators (Acafi).

According to the latest industry report, public investment funds recorded growth of 23.5% as of last December compared to the end of the previous year, and posted a record of US$43.853 billion in assets under administration (AuM). The assets were distributed among 889 active funds, representing an increase of 31 vehicles compared to 2024.

Despite the strong year, Acafi pointed to spaces for capital recovery in a segment of contributors that in recent years has been surpassed by private investors: institutional players, such as pension funds and insurance companies.

"They are fundamental actors in the development of the capital market, due to their ability to concentrate savings with long-term horizons, and can invest in assets that require scale, horizon and stability, such as infrastructure, housing, private debt or energy," said María José Montero, the new president of the industry association, to DF.

The industry administers US$29.953 billion in alternative assets. For the association's leader, the challenge to increase institutional participation in the industry "is a matter of system design."

"There are currently asymmetries in the investment regime that, without necessarily intending to, end up favoring foreign vehicles over comparable local solutions, or making it difficult to gain exposure to certain long-term assets, even when these may be appropriate from a risk and return perspective," Montero warned.

This comes in a context where "there are several well-structured, regulated and developed instruments in Chile that manage to achieve that objective and that, moreover, bring the benefit of developing infrastructure, market and jobs for the country."

## Review and Update

To address this scenario, Montero assured that, as Acafi, "we have been working with regulators to review and update these limits, both for pension funds and insurance companies, with the goal of advancing toward a system that allows more efficient capital allocation, consistent with long-term horizons and, ultimately, with better results for affiliates and insured parties."

In this line, the industry association detailed that they have raised to authorities the importance of reviewing frameworks in search of "equalizing conditions between local and foreign investment when economic risk is similar; moving from rigid limits by asset type toward assessments based on actual risk; allowing more efficient investment structures, and properly recognizing the long-term nature of assets such as infrastructure or private debt."

"All of this must be accompanied by greater transparency and comparable metrics on costs and performance," the association added.

## Alternative Funds Lead Industry

As for asset classes, the investment fund industry continues to be dominated by alternative vehicles. Funds dedicated to private markets and real investments grew 11% in 2025, to US$29.953 billion, representing 68% of the segment's total assets.

In detail, real estate funds led the way with 20%; followed closely by private capital with 18%; and private debt with 16%.

Meanwhile, according to Acafi, the securities funds segment—dedicated to stocks and bonds—experienced a year of consolidation, "driven mainly by high returns (from the Santiago Stock Exchange and foreign markets) rather than by new inflows."

In this way, public assets vehicles came to represent 32% of the industry, with US$13.901 billion. Within this category, debt funds account for the largest percentage at 21% of the total, and equity funds at 10%.
