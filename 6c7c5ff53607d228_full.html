# Investment Fund Administrator Industry Grew 23.5% Last Year, Setting Record Assets Under Management of $43.853 Billion

The investment fund industry returned to the path of growth in 2025, following a previous year that slowed its exponential advance since the beginning of the millennium, according to records from the Chilean Association of Investment Fund Administrators (Acafi).

According to the latest report from the industry group, public investment funds registered growth of 23.5% as of last December, compared to the end of the previous year, and recorded a record of $43.853 billion in assets under management (AuM). The assets were distributed among 889 active funds, representing an increase of 31 vehicles compared to 2024.

Despite the good year, Acafi pointed to opportunities for capital recovery in a segment of contributors that in recent years has been surpassed by private investors: institutional investors, such as pension funds and insurance companies.

"They are fundamental actors in the development of the capital market, due to their ability to concentrate savings with long-term horizons, and they can invest in assets that require scale, horizon and stability, such as infrastructure, housing, private debt or energy," said María José Montero, the new president of the industry group, to DF.

The industry administers $29.953 billion in alternative assets. For the association's leader, the challenge to increase their participation in the industry "is one of system design."

"There are today asymmetries in the investment regime that, without necessarily intending to, end up favoring foreign vehicles over comparable local solutions, or making it difficult to gain exposure to certain long-term assets, even when these may be appropriate from a risk and return perspective," Montero warned.

This comes in a context where "there are several well-structured, regulated and developed instruments in Chile that manage to achieve that objective and which, moreover, brings the benefit of developing infrastructure, market and jobs for the country."

To face this scenario, Montero assured that, as Acafi, "we have been working with regulators to review and update these limits, both for pension funds and insurance companies, with the aim of advancing toward a system that allows more efficient capital allocation, consistent with long-term horizons and, ultimately, with better results for affiliates and policyholders."

In this line, the industry group detailed that they have raised with authorities the importance of reviewing frameworks in search of "equalizing conditions between local and foreign investment when economic risk is similar; advancing from rigid limits by asset type toward evaluations based on effective risk; allowing more efficient investment structures, and appropriately recognizing the long-term nature of assets such as infrastructure or private debt."

"All of this should be accompanied by greater transparency and comparable metrics for costs and performance," the association added.

Alternative assets lead the industry. Regarding asset classes, the investment fund industry continues to be dominated by the world of alternative assets. Vehicles dedicated to the private market and real investments grew 11% in 2025, to $29.953 billion, accounting for 68% of the total assets in the segment.

In detail, real estate funds led the way, with 20%; followed closely by private capital, with 18%; and private debt, with 16%.

Meanwhile, according to Acafi, the equity funds segment—dedicated to stocks and bonds—experienced a year of consolidation, "driven mainly by high returns (from the Santiago Stock Exchange and foreign markets) rather than by new flows."

In this way, public asset vehicles reached 32% of the industry, with $13.901 billion. Within this category, debt funds take the largest percentage, with 21% of the total, and equity funds, with 10%.
