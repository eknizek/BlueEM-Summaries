# French company sues Argentina in U.S. court to collect $40 million arbitration award

Twelve years after receiving a favorable ruling in its case against Argentina at ICSID — the international tribunal of the World Bank to which companies resort in their disputes with countries — French water company Saur International is seeking to collect the $40 million plus interest from the sentence. In 2014, Argentina was condemned to pay approximately $40 million plus costs (the original claim had been for $144 million) as compensation for the nationalization of the company Obras Sanitarias Mendoza (OSM) in 2010. Although the contract was terminated by the Mendoza provincial government, the sentence fell upon the National State.

Saur International was a shareholder in the Mendoza water company until the province expropriated it in 2010. The lawsuit comes 12 years after obtaining a favorable ruling at ICSID. Saur resorted to a Washington court to file the claim.

Due to the 2001 crisis, the company first had its rates frozen and then, over the years, was not granted the adjustments agreed upon with authorities. There were several years of negotiations until Mendoza finally terminated the contract.

The ICSID condemnation was in the form of compensation for damages generated through the "violation of two standards of the Agreement for the Promotion and Reciprocal Protection of Investments between Argentina and France (APPRI)." Additionally, according to what was stated at the time, the expropriation was marked by "unfair and inequitable treatment towards Saur's investments."

However, since ICSID has no means of executing a sentence, to be able to collect, Saur must go to U.S. courts. For this reason, the company filed a lawsuit in the Washington Court this Thursday. The compensation is not for the entire expropriation, since Saur had only a minority and indirect participation in OSM of 32.08%.

**What is ICSID, a headache for Argentina**

ICSID, the International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes, is an international tribunal that depends, administratively, on the World Bank. It is an arbitration mechanism between States and companies when there are cases of contract breaches. It is conceived as an institutional framework of agreement between parties.

Argentina ranks second, behind Venezuela, in the ranking of countries with the most claims in that tribunal. Of the 65 cases the country faced — among those already closed and pending ones — 19 are related to energy supply contracts; 11 with financial investments and another 10 with water utilities (such as Saur's). Other sectors such as construction and mining follow further behind.

92% of claims against Argentina came from companies from the United States, Canada, and Europe. One third (22) of all claims against Argentina comes from U.S. investors, followed by Spain (10), France (8), and Italy (6). According to ICSID information processed by the international think tank Transnational Institute, in 86% of cases involving the Argentine State, the resolution was favorable to the plaintiff company.
