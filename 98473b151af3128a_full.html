Paraguay Had Strongest Latin Currency Against Dollar in April

02 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Paraguay Had Strongest Latin Currency Against Dollar in April

The foreign exchange landscape of Latin America underwent a reordering in April. In a month marked by dollar weakness and commodity momentum, it was not the large economies that led, but rather Paraguay, whose currency positioned itself as the strongest in the region. The guarani (USDPGY) surpassed currencies such as the Brazilian real (USDBRL), the Mexican peso (USDMXN), and the Colombian peso (USDCOP), in a movement that reflected more internal flows than global factors. This dynamic was driven by exports, lower demand for dollars, and pronounced seasonality in the foreign exchange market.

The appreciation of the guarani resulted from a significant increase in the supply of foreign currency, driven by agricultural exports and lower corporate demand for dollars. Revenue from soybeans and their derivatives grew strongly in the first quarter, while the maquiladora sector increased its exports. Ingrid Herrera, manager of the Economics Department at MF Economía Inversores, highlights that "the 39.8% increase in revenue from soybean exports and by-products, in a context of high international prices and greater local supply of these products" was one of the determining factors.

This set of factors increased the supply of foreign currency in the local market, pushing the exchange rate downward. The weight of the soybean complex is determinant: soybean derivatives represented 45.1% of export revenues at the end of the first quarter, which explains the magnitude of this movement.

## Exports and Rates

The appreciation of the guarani occurred in a context where other regional currencies also appreciated, although for different reasons. The Chilean peso was one of the currencies that stood out most, with an appreciation of around 3.3% in April, driven by external and local factors that reinforced the inflow of foreign currency and improved risk perception. This advance resulted from the combination of a weaker dollar in the global market, high copper prices, and a political change that raised investment expectations, with projects worth around $20 billion entering the environmental system in the first month of government.

Felipe Cáceres, analyst at Capitaria, summarizes part of this movement by observing that "copper, maintaining historically high levels near $6 per pound, improves the country's terms of trade." The copper price increased external revenues and strengthened the currency, while the political factor added an additional element of confidence.

However, this balance depends on external variables. Cáceres warns that "a correction in copper prices would have a direct impact on the peso, since each $0.10 drop in copper prices translates into an increase of CLP$15 to CLP$20 in the exchange rate."

In Brazil, the real also proved strong, although supported by a different combination of factors. Roberto Pereira, co-founder of ATEX Investimentos, highlights that "oil above $100 per barrel strengthened the trade balance and expanded the flow of foreign currency," while "the Selic at high levels maintained the attractiveness for carry trade." This rate differential sustained the inflow of capital, even in a context of global volatility.

The Colombian case was also linked to oil. The currency was sustained by the increase in crude oil prices, together with internal factors, such as the tax payment season for major taxpayers, which increases demand for pesos and the inflow of foreign currency. BBVA indicates that "the Colombian peso has been sustained by elevated oil prices. High oil prices in the short term may continue to fuel demand for the peso, while a reversal in the medium term, accompanied by a certain 'normalization' of global supply, could reduce support for the currency."

## Pressure in Peru and Stability in Argentina

Not all currencies followed the regional trend. The Peruvian sol (USDPEN) deviated from regional behavior due to internal factors. Fernando Ruiz, CEO of Kambista, states that "electoral uncertainty seems to have begun playing a fundamental role in the sol's performance, as markets assess different scenarios for the second round, which will occur in June." The analyst states that investor fears "reside in the probability that a candidate considered anti-market, such as Roberto Sánchez, could win the second round and implement substantial changes in economic policy that affect private investment and significantly alter the current scenario." This political factor exerted pressure on the exchange rate, even at times when the dollar weakened globally, marking a disconnect from the rest of Latin America.

In Argentina, the dynamics were different. The exchange rate remained relatively stable within a band system, with a moderate increase in April. Milo Farro, analyst at Rava Bursátil, highlights that "there are still $3.2 billion pending settlement in the free foreign exchange market, which means there will be quite a significant supply flow in May," in a context where the trade surplus and settlement of the agricultural sector increase the supply of foreign currency. This flow, combined with the seasonality of the main harvest, introduces an additional factor that could ease pressure on the dollar in the coming months.
