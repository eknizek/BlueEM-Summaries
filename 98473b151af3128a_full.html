Not Brazil, Not Mexico: Paraguay Had Latin America's Strongest Currency Against the Dollar in April

# Not Brazil, Not Mexico: Paraguay Had Latin America's Strongest Currency Against the Dollar in April

The foreign exchange landscape in Latin America underwent a reordering in April. In a month marked by dollar weakness and commodity momentum, it was not the major economies that led, but rather Paraguay, whose currency positioned itself as the region's strongest. The guarani (USDPGY) surpassed currencies such as the real (USDBRL), the Mexican peso (USDMXN), and the Colombian peso (USDCOP), in a movement that reflected more internal flows than global factors. This dynamic was driven by exports, lower dollar demand, and pronounced seasonality in the foreign exchange market.

The appreciation of the guarani resulted from a significant increase in foreign currency supply, driven by agricultural exports and lower corporate demand for dollars. Revenue from soybeans and their derivatives grew strongly in the first quarter, while the maquiladora sector increased its exports. Ingrid Herrera, manager of the Economics Department at MF Economía Inversores, highlights that "the 39.8% increase in revenue from soybean exports and their byproducts, in a context of rising international prices and greater local supply of these products" was one of the determining factors.

This combination of factors increased foreign currency supply in the local market, pushing the exchange rate downward. The weight of the soy complex is determinative: soy derivatives represented 45.1% of export revenues at the end of the first quarter, which explains the magnitude of this movement.

## Exports and Rates

The appreciation of the guarani occurred in a context where other regional currencies also appreciated, although for different reasons. The Chilean peso was one of the currencies that stood out most, with an appreciation of about 3.3% in April, driven by external and local factors that reinforced foreign currency inflows and improved risk perception. This advance resulted from the combination of a weaker dollar in the global market, high copper prices, and a political change that elevated investment expectations, with projects worth around $20 billion entering the environmental system in the first month of government.

Felipe Cáceres, analyst at Capitaria, summarizes part of this movement by observing that "copper, maintaining historically elevated levels close to $6 per pound, improves the country's terms of trade." The price of copper raised external revenues and strengthened the currency, while the political factor added an additional element of confidence.

However, this balance depends on external variables. Cáceres warns that "a correction in the copper price would have a direct impact on the peso, since each $0.10 drop in copper price translates into a CLP$15 to CLP$20 increase in the exchange rate."

In Brazil, the real also proved strong, although supported by a different combination of factors. Roberto Pereira, co-founder of ATEX Investimentos, highlights that "oil above $100 a barrel strengthened the trade balance and expanded foreign currency flows," while "the Selic at elevated levels maintained the attraction for carry trade." This interest rate differential sustained capital inflows, even in a context of global volatility.

The Colombian case was also linked to oil. The currency was sustained by increased crude oil prices, together with internal factors, such as the tax payment season for major taxpayers, which increases demand for pesos and foreign currency inflows. BBVA indicates that "the Colombian peso has been sustained by elevated oil prices. High oil prices in the short term may continue fueling demand for the peso, while a reversal in the medium term, accompanied by a certain 'normalization' of global supply, could reduce support for the currency."

## Pressure in Peru and Stability in Argentina

Not all currencies followed the trend. The Peruvian sol (USDPEN) diverged from regional behavior due to internal factors. Fernando Ruiz, CEO of Kambista, states that "electoral uncertainty appears to have begun playing a fundamental role in the sol's performance, as markets evaluate different scenarios for the second round, which will occur in June." The analyst states that investor concerns "reside in the probability that a candidate considered anti-market, such as Roberto Sánchez, could win the second round and implement substantial changes in economic policy that affect private investment and significantly alter the current scenario."

This political factor exercised pressure on the exchange rate, even at moments when the dollar was devaluing globally, marking a disconnect from the rest of Latin America.

In Argentina, the dynamics were different. The exchange rate remained relatively stable within a band system, with a moderate increase in April. Milo Farro, analyst at Rava Bursátil, highlights that "there are still $3.2 billion pending settlement in the free foreign exchange market, which means there will be a quite significant supply flow in May," in a context where the trade surplus and liquidation of the agricultural sector increase foreign currency supply. This flow, combined with the seasonality of the main harvest, introduces an additional factor that could ease pressure on the dollar in the coming months.
