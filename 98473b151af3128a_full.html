Neither Brazil nor Mexico: Paraguay had the strongest Latin American currency against the dollar in April

02 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Neither Brazil nor Mexico: Paraguay had the strongest Latin American currency against the dollar in April

The foreign exchange landscape in Latin America underwent a reorganization in April. In a month marked by dollar weakness and commodity gains, it was not the large economies that led, but Paraguay, whose currency positioned itself as the strongest in the region. The guarani (USDPGY) outperformed currencies such as the real (USDBRL), the Mexican peso (USDMXN), and the Colombian peso (USDCOP), in a movement that reflected more internal flows than global factors. This dynamic was driven by exports, lower dollar demand, and pronounced seasonality in the foreign exchange market.

The appreciation of the guarani resulted from a significant increase in foreign currency supply, driven by agricultural exports and lower corporate demand for dollars. Revenues from soybeans and their derivatives grew strongly in the first quarter, while the maquila sector increased its exports. Ingrid Herrera, manager of the Economics Department at MF Economía Inversores, highlights that "the 39.8% increase in revenue from soybean exports and by-products, in a context of high international prices and greater local supply of these products" was one of the determining factors.

This set of factors increased the supply of foreign currency in the local market, pushing the exchange rate down. The weight of the soybean complex is decisive: soybean derivatives represented 45.1% of export revenues at the end of the first quarter, which explains the magnitude of this movement.

**Exports and Rates**

The appreciation of the guarani occurred in a context where other regional currencies also appreciated, although for different reasons. The Chilean peso was one of the currencies that stood out the most, with an appreciation of about 3.3% in April, driven by external and local factors that reinforced foreign currency inflows and improved risk perception.

This advance resulted from a combination of a weaker dollar in the global market, high copper prices, and a political change that raised investment expectations, with projects worth approximately US$ 20 billion entering the environmental system in the first month of government. Felipe Cáceres, analyst at Capitaria, summarizes part of this movement by observing that "copper, maintaining historically high levels, close to US$ 6 per pound, improves the country's terms of trade."

The price of copper elevated external revenues and strengthened the currency, while the political factor added an additional element of confidence. However, this balance depends on external variables. Cáceres warns that "a correction in copper prices would have a direct impact on the peso, since each US$ 0.10 drop in copper price translates into an increase of CLP$ 15 to CLP$ 20 in the exchange rate."

In Brazil, the real also proved strong, although supported by a different combination of factors. Roberto Pereira, co-founder of ATEX Investimentos, highlights that "oil above US$ 100 per barrel strengthened the trade balance and expanded foreign currency flows," while "the Selic at high levels maintained the attractiveness for carry trade." This interest rate differential sustained capital inflows, even in a context of global volatility.

The Colombian case was also linked to oil. The currency was supported by the increase in crude oil prices, together with internal factors, such as the tax payment season for major taxpayers, which increases demand for pesos and foreign currency inflows. BBVA indicates that "the Colombian peso has been supported by high oil prices. High oil prices in the short term may continue to fuel demand for the peso, while a reversal in the medium term, accompanied by a certain 'normalization' of global supply, could reduce support for the currency."

**Pressure in Peru and Stability in Argentina**

Not all currencies followed the trend. The Peruvian sol (USDPEN) deviated from regional behavior due to internal factors. Fernando Ruiz, CEO of Kambista, states that "electoral uncertainty appears to have begun to play a fundamental role in the sol's performance, as markets evaluate different scenarios for the runoff, which will occur in June." The analyst states that investor concern "lies in the probability that a candidate considered anti-market, such as Roberto Sánchez, could win the runoff and implement substantial changes in economic policy that affect private investment and significantly alter the current scenario."

This political factor exerted pressure on the exchange rate, even at times when the dollar was depreciating globally, marking a disconnect from the rest of Latin America.

In Argentina, the dynamic was different. The exchange rate remained relatively stable within a band system, with a moderate increase in April. Milo Farro, analyst at Rava Bursátil, highlights that "there are still US$ 3.2 billion pending settlement in the free exchange market, which means there will be a fairly significant supply flow in May," in a context where the trade surplus and settlement by the agricultural sector increase the supply of foreign currency. This flow, combined with the seasonality of the main harvest, introduces an additional factor that may ease pressure on the dollar in the coming months.
