Kapitalo Sees Fundamentals in Flow to Brazilian Stock Exchange

14 April 2026 · 10:53 PM

# "It's Not Crazy Foreigners Buying". Kapitalo Sees Fundamentals in the Flow

The flow to the Brazilian stock exchange has fundamentals: the expectation that we may be at the beginning of a commodity price cycle, according to the variable income managers at Kapitalo. For Hegler Horta and Bruno Mauad, the geopolitical changes that began during the pandemic and have intensified recently are increasing commodity demand – which attracts investments to the country despite all local uncertainty.

"It's not the crazy foreigner who pressed a button and bought a bunch of index out of nowhere," Mauad told Brazil Journal.

With this thesis, the fund's main positions since 2022 are commodity exporters (such as Vale and Suzano), companies linked to the oil chain (such as Brava and Vibra), and Embraer. In 2024, much of the return came from BRF, when the stock more than doubled as a result of the turnaround before the merger with Marfrig – aligning positive micro factors with a favorable external macro environment.

Horta and Mauad are responsible for the variable income portfolio of Kapitalo's multimarket funds and also the Tarkus stock fund – which is among the most profitable long-only funds in the country over 12, 24, 36, and 60 months, according to a survey by Outliers Advisory. The fund felt the blow in March, when it lost 6% – double the long-only average – amid doubts about global growth. The managers' reaction was to reduce gross exposure but maintain the portfolio, betting on greater commodity demand. The adjustment was also a response to increased electoral uncertainty. The fund also has short positions: since Lula's last election, shorts are concentrated in retail stocks and other companies dependent on the Brazilian economy.

"We never bought the idea that there would be a consistent drop in interest rates. The market tried to anticipate this move several times, but always ended up bumping into fiscal reality," said Horta.

Below are the main excerpts from the conversation.

**You reduced exposure in March with the abrupt scenario change amid the war. How are you investing now?**

**Hegler Horta:** The scenario we had been working with since the end of last year was still relatively robust global growth. There were some signs of deceleration, but at the same time central banks indicated room for interest rate cuts — an environment therefore more pro-risk. If activity remained strong, great for emerging markets; if it decelerated, there would be room for stimulus via rates. Regarding Brazil, we were more cautious. We didn't see much room for interest rate cuts, mainly because of still loose fiscal policy and the electoral context. Given this, we assembled a portfolio more focused on global demand, with mining companies, paper and pulp producers, and Embraer, among other companies. At the same time, we reduced exposure to domestic names, especially the most leveraged ones, and set up some shorts. This strategy has been gradually built since 2022.

Since the beginning of the new government, we never bought the idea that there would be a consistent drop in interest rates. The market tried to anticipate this move several times, but always ended up bumping into fiscal reality. The portfolio still maintains these characteristics, but the oil shock in March called into question some of the external scenario assumptions — since it can affect consumer income and inflation at the same time, making it difficult for central banks to act. That's why we reduced both positions linked to external demand and shorts in domestic names. We decreased gross exposure, but net exposure remains around 100%. In the midst of a significant shock, correlations between assets change and volatility increases. Even while maintaining conviction in our thesis, we reduced exposure until the scenario becomes clearer.

**Despite this external shock, Brazil continues to receive foreign investments.**

**Bruno Mauad:** Our view is that foreign flows to Brazil are fundamentally justified. It's not the crazy foreigner who pressed a button and bought a bunch of index out of nowhere. The world has been going through a deglobalization process since COVID, when countries and companies realized they needed local production of some strategic assets and inventories. The war in Iran intensified this geopolitical discussion. Someone closes a strait and you're left without fertilizers. Commodity demand has already increased, and Brazil benefits from having iron ore, oil, and copper.

**Horta:** The world will need to work with larger inventories, and this will potentially benefit Brazil. Regarding oil, before the war we were slightly underweight, like most managers, because there was supply pressure. We've increased our position a bit now and are more neutral. In the short and medium term, the market should remain tight, with countries replenishing strategic reserves – and those without reserves saw they need to build them up. I don't think oil prices will go back to where they were before the war.

**Have some stocks become expensive on the exchange – because they rose too much, or because the scenario changed?**

**Mauad:** It's difficult to give a single answer because the Brazilian market is very heterogeneous. Companies linked to the domestic economy are binary: they can seem cheap or expensive depending on the macro outcome. If we project the continuation of economic policy after the election, with real interest rates in the 8% to 11% range, many are expensive — and some have already fallen by the wayside this year. But if there's a policy change with a significant drop in the cost of capital, these same assets could seem cheap.

In the case of commodities, there's also much discussion anchored in assumptions. Taking Vale as an example: some projections assume iron ore returning to $60–70. If the price holds closer to $100, the stock is cheap. Our view is that exporters still offer value, partly because we have a different reading of this cycle.

**How are you positioning in case the electoral "outcome" indicates sustained interest rate cuts that would benefit the stock exchange?**

**Horta:** It's still too early to make big bets. Let me stress the argument a bit: Lula versus Bolsonaro again – nobody convinces anybody of anything anymore. Depending on how the race unfolds, the election will be decided by a small portion of voters who will choose the candidate a month before. What happens in that month will be decisive, and we have no idea what that will be. There's already been a stabbing, already been Trump. Marketing people will always test different themes to try to identify which might generate public support. We can have any theory, but if a new fact emerges a few days before voting, that could determine the outcome. That's why we prefer to be more cautious. This risk reduction we did in the portfolio in March was also related to this. At the beginning of the year, we saw 52%-48% with some bias toward Lula, as the incumbent. The surprise in recent months is that Lula is seeming like a weaker candidate. Today we see something closer to 50-50, perhaps even slightly.
