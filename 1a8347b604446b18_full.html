The 'Mercado Livre factor' in logistics warehouse scarcity in Brazil

15 April 2026 · 09:44 PM

# The "Mercado Livre Factor" in Logistics Warehouse Scarcity in Brazil

In an environment of high interest rates, moderate economic growth, and global uncertainties, Brazil's logistics warehouse market follows its own logic. While sectors more sensitive to credit slowdown, industrial and logistics condominiums maintain high absorption, falling vacancy rates, and consistent rent appreciation. Behind this disconnect lies an increasingly dominant driver: the advance of e-commerce, primarily led by Mercado Livre.

A survey by Newmark, obtained exclusively by NeoFeed, shows that the company already occupies approximately 1.8 million square meters (m²) in São Paulo—clearly leading the ranking of the sector's largest tenants. Following are Amazon, Shopee, and DHL, all expanding. From an estimated gross leasable area (GLA) occupied of just under 18 million m² in São Paulo, Mercado Livre occupies 9% of spaces. The company led by Fernando Yunes maintains its appetite for new areas—in mid-March it announced a R$ 57 billion investment in its Brazilian operations in 2026.

One recent movement discussed by the real estate market is the occupation of Avon's headquarters in southern São Paulo. The cosmetics company is transferring its operations to the interior, and the property, acquired by the Goodman manager, is being converted into a logistics center. The location, near Interlagos, is a strategic area for supplying the entire southern region of the capital. The Australian company specializing in acquiring, developing, and managing industrial properties and high-quality logistics spaces has already signed a contract with Mercado Livre.

Meanwhile, the Brazilian logistics map is beginning to be redesigned beyond São Paulo's borders—the state still concentrates around 50% of the national warehouse market. E-commerce companies and logistics operators are expanding their presence in other regions. The Northeast is advancing, with expansion around major capitals. The Midwest, especially Goiás and Brasília, is also recording accelerated growth. Vitória stands out as a strategic hub, driven by port infrastructure and the consolidation of a relevant consumer center. And southern Minas Gerais, on the border with São Paulo, simultaneously serves both Minas and São Paulo markets.

Of a total of 42 million m² of occupied logistics warehouses in Brazil, Mercado Livre accounts for approximately 3 million m² (or 7% of the total). In Bahia and Minas Gerais, e-commerce occupies more than 200,000 m², corresponding to 15% and 7%, respectively, of occupied stock. The states of Rio Grande do Sul, Paraná, Espírito Santo, and Ceará are those with the lowest percentage occupation (2%), ahead only of Amazonas (1%), according to the Newmark study.

"The main driver continues to be e-commerce, and especially Mercado Livre, which is growing very aggressively," says Mariana Hanania, head of research and market intelligence at Newmark. "To deliver faster, they need to be closer to consumer markets. Not just in the Southeast region. The expansion is happening throughout Brazil," she adds.

This movement of geographic expansion is likely to gain momentum in the coming years, as e-commerce expands its share of retail—currently around 10% in Brazil.

## Accelerated Occupation

The presence of players like Mercado Livre helps explain why the sector started 2026 at an accelerated pace. In Newmark data, net absorption in São Paulo reached 500,000 m² in the first quarter—a volume six times higher than recorded in the same period of 2025 and alone already representing more than one-third of all last year's absorption. A RealtyCorp Analytics survey—another study obtained exclusively by NeoFeed—points to absorption of 366,000 m² in the same period, reinforcing the same trend of heated demand. The difference between the data is that Newmark considers exclusively high-standard logistics condominiums (AAA, AA, and A classes) in the state of São Paulo, while RealtyCorp adopts a broader approach, including assets of different standards.

However, the prominence of a single brand does not tell the entire story of logistics warehouse expansion. Operating indicators show a sector pressured by demand from all sides. The vacancy rate in São Paulo fell to 6.4%—the lowest level in the historical series, according to Newmark—compared to 8.1% in the previous quarter. According to RealtyCorp Analytics data, which adopts different methodology and universe, vacancy fell from 7.35% to the same 6.4% in the period. In eight of the main regions analyzed by Newmark, availability is already below the state average, a clear sign of relative scarcity, especially in higher-quality assets.

Prices continue on an appreciation trajectory. The average asking rental value reached R$ 33.1 per m² per month, with a 3% increase in the quarter and 15% in 12 months, according to Newmark. In regions of greater pressure in the capital (especially within a 30 km radius of the center), values already exceed R$ 40 per m², even surpassing those practiced in tertiary-standard corporate offices.

"The vacancy rate is at the lowest level in the historical series, and demand continues to exceed new warehouse deliveries. This sustains price increases and demands strategic planning by companies," says Marcos Alves, CEO of RealtyCorp.

This combination of low vacancy, rising prices, and high absorption would normally indicate a market near exhaustion. But that is not the case. There is an explanation for this.

Brazil remains a market underdeveloped in logistics. Today, the country has approximately 0.7 m² of warehouses per inhabitant. In the United States, this ratio exceeds 4 m². This difference reveals significant expansion potential and helps explain why the sector grows even amid adverse macroeconomic conditions.

A Newmark survey indicates that there are approximately 3.6 million m² under construction in Brazil, with approximately 1.5 million just in São Paulo, with deliveries expected throughout 2026. Construction activity represents 7.88% of total stock in the state—a level lower than observed five years ago, according to RealtyCorp data.

Five years ago, construction activity exceeded annual net absorption, meaning the market delivered more space than it absorbed. Today, that equation has reversed. Absorption now represents more than 9% of stock over the past four quarters, a ratio that points to structural tightness in the supply of logistics real estate.
