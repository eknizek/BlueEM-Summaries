Colombia's Supertransporte warns transport centers of nine critical failures or preventive closures

10 April 2026 · 09:41 PM

# Colombia's Supertransporte Warns Transport Centers of Nine Critical Failures or Preventive Closures

Yesterday marked the deadline set by the Superintendent of Transport for Automotive Diagnostic Centers (CDA) to correct nine irregularities detected in technical-mechanical inspections and pollutant emissions reviews. Those who have not implemented the guidelines will be sanctioned.

The Superintendent of Transport, Alfredo Enrique Piñeres Olave, clarified regarding Circular 024 from February 9, 2026, that: "The instructions contained in the circular do not create new obligations, but rather clarify and develop existing duties within the regulatory framework applicable to Automotive Diagnostic Centers and those certified under Sicov."

He further added that "the specific instruction was to correct non-compliance with regulations and strengthen operational, technological, and procedural controls of Sicov applicable to technical-mechanical inspection and pollutant emissions review."

The ruling was issued after the entity analyzed improvement plans recommended to 166 CDAs between 2023, 2024, and the first half of 2025, and found that a significant number of these organizations continued repeating the same failures in their inspection processes.

## Failures Compromising Road Safety

Among the irregularities are the lack of complete and continuous video evidence throughout the entire inspection process, the absence of effective license plate detection upon vehicle entry and exit, and conducting sensory inspection in undefined or unreported locations. These three failures alone break the traceability of the process and prevent verification of whether an inspection was conducted correctly.

Operational problems with direct implications for inspection validity include: inspectors using cell phones or performing unrelated tasks while reviewing a vehicle, insufficient lighting on inspection lines—which must be a minimum of 600 lux—and failure to complete the mandatory PIN burn before starting each inspection. Without this step, the system should not allow the process to continue.

## What the System Will Now Block Automatically

One of the most concrete changes brought by Circular 024 involves the technological controls that Sicov-certified operators must implement. The system will prevent approval of inspections that present Type A defects, have incomplete Uniform Results Format (FUR) or inconsistent information, or have been signed by technical personnel not previously registered with Sicov.

Additionally, VPN use is prohibited, as are remote inspection signatures or any unauthorized system access to validate reviews. Certified operators must implement mechanisms that prevent these activities from being performed outside the CDA. Any data that does not comply with established guidelines will generate automatic return of the inspection result to the center.

## What Vehicle Owners Can Demand

For citizens bringing their cars for inspection, the CDA is obligated to record the entire process on video continuously, operate under adequate lighting conditions, employ inspectors properly registered with Sicov, and document in the FUR any vehicle novelties, including whether it lacks a spare tire or is armored.

Any changes to infrastructure or technical personnel must be reported with at least five days' advance notice. The Superintendent of Transport will monitor compliance with these instructions through Sicov, with monthly review of video evidence submitted by certified operators.

CDAs that have not remedied identified failures are exposed to administrative sanctioning processes that can result in temporary closure of operations for up to six months.
