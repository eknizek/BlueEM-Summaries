# Renova Mitigates Renewable Energy Curtailment with R$1 Billion Data Center Bet for Crypto Mining

Forced cuts in centralized solar and wind energy generation by Brazil's National Electric System Operator (ONS) to prevent grid overload — a practice known as curtailment — are forcing energy companies to seek alternatives to mitigate losses that reached R$6.5 billion last year for the 1,500 centralized renewable plants affected by the cuts.

Many companies have abandoned permits for new solar and wind farms, while others have reduced their portfolios through asset sales or implemented severe cost reductions, including staff layoffs.

Renova Energia, a publicly traded company that was pioneering in installing large-scale wind farms in the country, took a creative approach: building a set of data centers consuming 85 megawatts (MW) of energy around its Alto Sertão III Wind Complex, with 26 facilities across the municipalities of Igaporã and Caetité in Bahia. Dedicated to cryptocurrency mining, the data center leverages the electricity generated by the turbines, connected directly to the wind complex's substation. When the ONS prevents generated energy from being injected into the grid, the load is directed to the data center.

"We needed to do something different to get different results, otherwise we wouldn't survive," says Sandro Yamamoto, director of new business, regulation and institutional relations at Renova, to NeoFeed.

With 1.2 gigawatts (GW) in projects developed, implemented and operational across four states in the Northeast, Renova also operates solar facilities and is considered one of the major renewable energy companies in the country. However, beyond facing the nightmare of curtailment — last year, the average energy cuts at the Alto Sertão III Wind Complex were 30% — the company faced financial challenges between 2018 and 2020 and exited judicial recovery in February 2025.

"We concluded that it was pointless to keep fighting for compensation for curtailment losses; we accepted that they would be frequent and studied various ways to increase revenue until we arrived at the data center solution," Yamamoto adds.

The first step was to send the company's technical team abroad to learn about projects best suited to the infrastructure of the Bahia complex. The chosen model was Tier 1 data center — which requires less redundancy in electricity supply, cooling and connectivity and has flexible consumption. This means the data center does not consume energy 100% of the time.

"This flexibility allows us to reduce load during certain periods, adapting to electrical system conditions and cuts, making the solution compatible with curtailment challenges," the executive explains.

In practice, with this model, the wind complex can divide the generated energy between the grid and the data center. "For these 26 facilities, there are substations that collect our generation and then transport our energy to large substations on the basic grid," Yamamoto says. "We installed this data center next to that collecting substation, which is ours."

## Business Model

The next step was implementing the business model. On one front, a company team mapped all difficulties to make the project viable, from physical infrastructure in the complex area to regulatory issues with clients — Renova also has an energy trading division. Comprehensive planning was done involving energy production.

"To calculate how much energy we would use in the data center, we had to list all our sales contracts — the ones expiring, we decided not to renew," Yamamoto reveals. For remaining contracts, Renova purchased energy on the market to meet those commitments, freeing up part of its generation for the data centers.

Another company front sought potential clients for the new business. The project, valued at R$1 billion, was divided between Renova and data center companies — Yamamoto doesn't reveal how many or which ones due to contractual requirements, but NeoFeed learned from sources in the electrical sector that at least three data center companies, two of them major cryptocurrency mining operations and one smaller, have already signed contracts. Renova provides energy generation plus all infrastructure for the associated company to arrive and install, including land, environmental, electrical, civil, mechanical, connectivity and property security aspects. The partner covers the heavier part — the equipment that runs the data.

What stands out is the speed with which the project went from concept to producing results. "The first contract was signed in March last year, work began in May, and by October we were already starting the first energization," the Renova executive says.

The 85 MW energy consumption structure is already complete, including electrical, civil and mechanical parts, including containers and transformers. Currently, computers totaling 42 MW of data generation are already assembled, tested and functioning. The remaining computers will arrive soon and should become operational within three months, at most.

Yamamoto reveals that in practice, the data center is already alleviating the curtailment problem at the Alto Sertão III Wind Complex. "In January, when the data center infrastructure in operation was small, our cuts were about 65% of produced energy," he reveals. In March, with most equipment running, cuts dropped to 17%. "We went from leading the curtailment rankings to off the list in just two months," he celebrates.

Victor Hugo Iocca, director of Electrical Energy at Abrace Energia — an association representing large electricity and natural gas consumers — views Renova's initiative to harness the wind farm's energy potential positively.

"The solution to reduce curtailment for centralized renewable facilities, especially in the Northeast, involves increasing energy consumption close to generation," Iocca says. "With the transmission reinforcements planned by ONS, curtailment tends to decrease, but I consider betting on battery storage systems at existing facilities to have greater potential."
