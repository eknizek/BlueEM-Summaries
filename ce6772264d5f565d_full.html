# Belas Artes University Sees Uncontrolled Expansion as Major Problem in Brazil's Higher Education

Patricia Cardim is the fourth generation of her family leading Belas Artes University Center, an institution completing 101 years in 2026. For her, what makes family management work is "root governance," which the university's CEO describes as the transmission of the institution's culture in its daily operations. "The family is here almost like a goalkeeper protecting the culture, so that the DNA is not lost," says Cardim. "There is a shared understanding that anyone here is passing through. Belas Artes is only in its first 100 years."

For the executive director, the expansion of higher education offerings in Brazil has come at the expense of curriculum quality. The loss of tradition, in her view, has been a setback for the market. "Belas Artes shows that excellence can be assured by the passion of the family and rigor in EBITDA," she states, noting that most competitors have not followed the same path.

"We don't sell our thesis of excellence as a commodity. There was expansion, but without quality. That is the great pain of Brazilian higher education today," she declares, arguing that the absence of educators leading the management of the largest sector groups is a problem. The university offers a range of distance learning courses (EAD), which underwent changes from the Education Ministry last year. The heir defends the Executive's regulation as a way to ensure the quality of such courses, but believes the changes could also restrict educational access in some regions. "Our digital product costs the equivalent of in-person courses at other colleges," she analyzes.

Today, the university center has 620 employees and the average monthly tuition for offered courses is R$ 3,800.

**How is governance defined between family and professional management?**

I am 43 years old and have worked here since I was 15. My father entered the institution at 13. I call what was done "root governance," different from the structured governance seen today in family businesses, with methodology and selection of the best professional on the market. It was something very organic: a transmission of culture in the daily operations of the institution. I was exposed to all departments, this coexistence, daily contact with the academic environment. There is a shared understanding: anyone who passes through here is temporary. Belas Artes is only in its first 100 years. We are here to protect the mission drawn by the founder.

**The landscape of private education in Brazil has changed compared to what Belas Artes has experienced. Today there are publicly traded companies, a variety of courses and large scale. How do you compete in this environment?**

The transition of the higher education market—which was practically entirely in the hands of educator families and came to be concentrated in these large publicly traded groups—generated an abrupt change in the quality of teaching in Brazil. There was expansion, but without quality. That is the great pain of Brazilian higher education today. Belas Artes remained firm as a family business, valuing the thesis of excellence. We have an EBITDA benchmarked to that of large publicly traded groups, but without losing quality. What happened in Brazil is unfortunate. My main criticism of these companies is the absence of educators leading management. Belas Artes is well professionalized and has good managers from that market, but if there is no protection of the responsibility of knowing that what we produce needs to go beyond the institution's walls, education is lost.

The Brazilian higher education market, in general, has competed with increasingly lower tuition fees. Belas Artes is outside this cycle, moving in the opposite direction. The in-person market shrinks 9% annually; the college grows 15% annually in that same segment.

**Belas Artes began offering distance learning courses during the pandemic, a modality that has suffered regulatory pressure. How do you deal with changes and the possibility of other alterations to the framework?**

We had one course affected, Pedagogy, which was 100% online and had to incorporate in-person meetings. Within Belas Artes, we didn't suffer: we adapted the course. In terms of Brazil, there are cases like Univesp, our state digital university, which serves teachers in the Pedagogy course in riverside regions of the Amazon. It prevents many people in that distant Brazil from taking a quality Pedagogy course. I understand the measure because it wasn't made for universities like ours—it was made to contain that large mass of low-quality higher education. What was being sold was sometimes just a PowerPoint. The more the Education Ministry moves toward containing expansion without quality but with a focus on quality, the better for us.

**There are still those who don't see arts courses as employable careers. How does the university sustain a portfolio centered on the arts given this mentality?**

It was once more difficult to explain to families and students that these courses aim to train professionals for the creative economy. We never wanted to train someone just for a hobby. The objective of graduation and bachelor's degrees is to train professionals for the creative economy market. And they are working.
