# With 'Root Governance', Belas Artes Sees Unqualified Expansion as Major Problem in Brazilian Higher Education

Patricia Cardim is the fourth generation of her family to lead Centro Universitário Belas Artes, an institution completing 101 years in 2026. For her, what makes family management work is "root governance," which the CEO of the college describes as the transmission of the institution's culture in its daily operations. "The family is here almost like a goalkeeper of culture, so that the DNA is not lost," says Cardim. "There is a shared understanding that anyone here is just passing through. Belas Artes is only in its first 100 years."

For the executive director, the expansion of higher education offerings in Brazil happened at the expense of curriculum quality. The loss of tradition, in her view, was a loss for the market. "Belas Artes shows that it is possible to ensure excellence through the family's passion and rigor in EBITDA," she states, noting that most competitors did not follow the same path.

"We do not sell our excellence thesis as a commodity. There was expansion, but without quality. That is the great pain of Brazilian higher education today," she declares, arguing that the absence of educators leading the management of the largest groups in the sector is a problem.

The university offers a range of distance learning (EAD) courses, which underwent changes by the Ministry of Education last year. The heir defends Executive regulation as a way to ensure the quality of such courses, but believes the changes could also restrict educational access in some regions. "Our digital product costs the equivalent of the in-person offering of other colleges," she analyzes.

Today, the university center has 620 employees and the average monthly tuition for the courses offered is R$ 3,800.

**How is governance defined between family and professional management?**

I am 43 years old and have worked here since I was 15. My father joined the institution at 13. I call what was done "root governance," different from the structured governance seen today in family businesses, with methods and selection of the best professionals on the market. It was something very organic: a transmission of culture in the daily operations of the institution. I was exposed to all departments, this interaction, daily contact with the academic environment. There is a shared understanding: anyone who passes through here is just passing through. Belas Artes is only in its first 100 years. We are here to protect the mission drawn by the founder.

**The private education landscape in Brazil has changed compared to what Belas Artes has witnessed. Today there are publicly traded companies, a variety of courses and large scale. How do you compete in this environment?**

The transition of the higher education market—which was practically entirely in the hands of educator families and became concentrated in these large publicly traded groups—generated a sharp change in the quality of education in Brazil. There was expansion, but without quality. That is the great pain of Brazilian higher education today. Belas Artes remained firm as a family company, valuing the excellence thesis. We have an EBITDA aligned with that of large publicly traded groups, but without losing quality. What happened in Brazil is tragic. My main criticism of these companies is the absence of educators leading management. Belas Artes is well professionalized and has good managers from this market, but if there is no protection of the responsibility of knowing that what we produce needs to go beyond the walls of the institution, education is lost.

The Brazilian higher education market, in general, has competed through increasingly lower tuition fees. Belas Artes is outside this cycle, moving in the opposite direction. The in-person market shrinks 9% per year; the college grows 15% per year in this same segment.

**Belas Artes began offering distance learning courses during the pandemic, a modality that has suffered regulatory pressure. How do you deal with changes and the possibility of further alterations to the system?**

We had one course affected, Pedagogy, which was 100% online and had to incorporate in-person meetings. Within Belas Artes, we did not suffer: we adapted the course. In terms of Brazil, there are cases like Univesp, our state digital university, which serves Pedagogy teachers in river regions of the Amazon. It prevents many people in that distant Brazil from taking a quality Pedagogy course. I understand the measure because it was not made for universities like ours—it was made to contain that large mass of low-quality higher education. What was being sold was sometimes just a PowerPoint. The more the Ministry of Education moves toward containing expansion without quality, but with a focus on quality, the better for us.

**There are still those who do not see arts courses as an employable career. How does the university sustain a portfolio centered on the arts given this mentality?**

It was already more difficult to explain to families and students that these courses aim to train professionals for the creative economy. We never wanted to train someone just as a hobby. The objective of undergraduate and bachelor's degrees is to train professionals for the creative economy market. And they are working.
