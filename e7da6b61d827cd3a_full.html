# Capeco: Construction Sector Growth of 13.2% in Q1 2026, but Sharp Deceleration Expected for Remainder of Year

The sector recorded its best quarterly performance since 2012, driven by advances in public works, cement consumption, and private investment. However, the industry warned that electoral uncertainty, rising costs, and deteriorating business expectations would reduce the pace of growth in the coming months.

By Melissa Rodríguez Enciso

Construction would have grown 13.2% in the first quarter of 2026, its best result for a similar period since 2012 (not counting the rebound registered after the pandemic), according to estimates from the Peruvian Construction Chamber (Capeco). The industry stated that the sector's performance was driven by advances in public works, growth in cement consumption, and recovery in private investment linked to mining, housing, and works-for-taxes programs.

Guido Valdivia, executive vice president of Capeco, explained that the sector accumulated eleven consecutive months of growth and maintained double-digit expansion rates at the start of the year. "Construction ends the first quarter on the rise, growing all three months," he said during the presentation of the 100th edition of the Economic Report on Construction (IEC).

According to the report, following the 8.9% growth registered in February, March showed an expansion close to 15%, supported mainly by higher cement consumption and recovery in public works. With this, the first quarter would have closed with an advance of 13.2%.

Valdivia stated that this performance confirmed the sector's resilience in a context marked by political and electoral uncertainty. "Construction continued to be the economic sector that grew the most, and that reveals consistency, strength of the construction sector, resilience, because despite the problems we have, which have worsened this month of April (referring to electoral uncertainty), construction continues to perform importantly," he affirmed.

Capeco detailed that cement consumption maintained a positive trend for almost a year and reached in March one of its highest recent growth levels, only below the December 2025 peak. This was added to by the recovery of public investment, which grew 8.1% in the first quarter compared to the same period of the previous year. The execution advance was led by regional governments and municipalities, whose investment levels increased 22.3% and 17.8%, respectively. In contrast, the national government registered a decline of 3.6%.

## Expectations Begin to Deteriorate

Despite the favorable quarterly result, Capeco warned that the sector would lose momentum for the rest of the year. The industry explained that the deepening of the political crisis and uncertainty regarding the economic proposals of presidential candidates have already begun to affect business expectations and investment decisions.

"The depth of the electoral crisis is generating uncertainty that could have consequences for the construction sector's performance for the rest of the year," Valdivia indicated.

A survey conducted by Capeco among construction companies showed a reduction in sector growth perspectives. The industry noted that two months ago companies projected a 7.1% increase in their operations for this year, but that forecast has now been reduced to 6.3%. For the second two-month period, expectations fell to 4.8%, with the infrastructure segment showing the greatest deterioration.

Valdivia stated that the sector's current expansion rate would not be maintained for the rest of the year. "If we are growing at a rate of 13% in a quarter and we are going to end at 5% or 6%, we are talking about the pace not being sustained and falling sharply."
