# Agronomist Helped Southeast Brazil Produce Premium Wines; Now Sees Sector Mature

On any trip to visit the dozens of wineries that have been established in Southeast Brazil, one name appears repeatedly: Murillo de Albuquerque Regina. The agronomist's name is mentioned by nearly all producers when explaining how they decided to plant grapes between São Paulo and Minas Gerais.

This is because he developed a method that "tricks" grapevines, making the grape harvest occur in winter instead of summer—as happens worldwide—when climatic conditions in the region are ideal for grapes that can become fine wines. In just over two decades, an area that previously focused almost entirely on coffee began producing robust fine wine production.

Recognized in December 2025 as Agronomist of the Year by the Association of Agricultural Engineers of the State of São Paulo, Regina acknowledges the importance of his research but avoids the label of sole protagonist. "The movement is the result of collective effort," he said in an interview. For him, an agronomic research idea only makes sense when it leaves the paper. "The result of the study either becomes a scientific article that might gather dust on a library shelf, or it becomes wine in a bottle, employment in the field, employment in the city, employment in a restaurant, employment in a hotel."

The agronomist, born in southern Minas Gerais, built his reasoning from a simple observation. As a teenager, while following coffee cultivation, he noticed the decisive role winter climate played in grain quality. "Since adolescence I knew the importance of the winter climate of southern Minas Gerais for coffee quality," he said. Sunny days, cold nights, and dry soil form the backdrop for coffee harvests and, later, he would realize, for great wines worldwide.

During his studies in viticulture and enology, Regina found the connection. "When I learned that the climate for making quality wine is exactly that, I thought it was very logical: if we want to make wine here, we have to reverse the plant's cycle," he said. The key would be shifting grape maturation to autumn and winter, the drier period with greater temperature variation in the Southeast. "And what is the instrument to reverse the plant's cycle? It's doing differentiated pruning," he explained.

From this interpretation, he began testing, in the early 2000s, the management that became known as double pruning, or winter harvest. At this point, Regina assumes technical leadership. "In the case of winter wine, in the purely technological and scientific aspects, I consider myself the father," he stated. "I was the first in Southeast Brazil to plant a grapevine cutting from a vine experiment to say: this plant will have to respond to differentiated pruning so I can harvest and have the grape mature starting in May to harvest in July."

Science, however, only converted to economic results when it became wine. Regina recalls that upon harvesting the first experimental crops and confirming the grape's potential, he proceeded to make wine. "I rushed to make wine," he said. At Epamig, where he worked as a researcher, he modernized the winemaking structure to serve as an incubator base for pioneers. "Here at Epamig I had already modernized a winery to serve as a business incubator base."

The economic context helped, according to him. "At that time in the 2000s, coffee was going through a gigantic crisis, low prices, and coffee growers were seeking alternatives," he said. Meanwhile, a profile of investors from outside agriculture emerged—doctors, businesspeople, and professionals attracted by wine. "There's a coffee crisis, there's wine quality, and there's the curiosity of many people from other sectors because of wine's glamour."

Geography completed the scenario. The Southeast concentrates income, population, and tourist routes. "If you analyze well and add up the population of São Paulo, Minas Gerais, and Rio de Janeiro states, we are 80 million inhabitants," he stated. The new wine regions are just a few hours' drive from these major centers, which facilitates both direct sales and wine tourism.

Two decades later, Regina argues that the model has proven financially sustainable. "If there were no commercial sustainability, we would have been losing money for 21 years, and no one can sustain a business like that," he said. He rebuts the criticism that winter wine is expensive compared to imported wines of similar quality. "I believe it's a mistake to say that winter wine is expensive."

According to him, Brazilian labels between R$ 100 and R$ 150 compete with foreign wines that enter the country at much higher prices. "We have to compare comparable things." The higher cost, he acknowledges, comes from low productivity and tax burden. "The taxes levied on wine in Brazil are at minimum double what our South American competitors face."

Today, he describes the sector as in a consolidation phase. "Winter wine today has already moved past that first phase of curiosity and is in consolidation," he stated. Wineries that planted in the early years now invest in restaurants, lodges, and visitor centers, but Regina says this is not sufficient for larger operations. "Many are producing around 50,000 bottles per year, but that cannot be sustained by wine tourism alone."

The strategy, according to him, involves balancing wine tourism, sales to restaurants and specialty retailers, and online channels. In parallel, collective organization has gained weight. Leading the National Association of Winter Wine Producers (Anprovin), which has more than 50 affiliated wineries, Regina helped structure a certification and traceability system. "To ensure this wine is not subject to fraud," he said. In February 2025, the sector obtained geographical indication for winter wines from southern Minas, an initial step toward a designation of origin.

For the agronomist, the future of the Southeast as a wine region depends less on euphoria and more on time. "Will there be a wine crisis? Yes, certainly," he said. Still, he said the path is irreversible. "In my opinion, this wine will never cease to exist, because it has the same unique absolute truth as coffee. It is good."

According to Regina, the challenge now is to respect the time of the grapevine, of wine, and of the sector's own maturity so that Southeast Brazil consolidates, with method and patience, a definitive place on the wine map.
