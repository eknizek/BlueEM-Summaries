# This agronomist helped Southeast Brazil produce premium wines. Now he sees the sector mature

In any trip to discover the dozens of wineries that have been established in Southeast Brazil, one name appears with recurring frequency: Murillo de Albuquerque Regina. The agricultural engineer's name is repeated by nearly all producers who explain how they decided to plant grapes between São Paulo and Minas Gerais.

This is because he developed a method that "tricks" the grapevines and causes grape harvesting to stop occurring in the summer, as happens worldwide, and instead takes place in winter, when climatic conditions in the region are ideal for grapes that can become good wines.

In just over two decades, the area that previously focused almost entirely on coffee has become a thriving producer of fine wines.

Recognized in December 2025 as Agronomist of the Year by the Association of Agricultural Engineers of the State of São Paulo, Regina acknowledges the importance of his research but avoids the label of sole protagonist. "The movement is the result of collective effort," he said in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

For him, an agronomic research idea only makes sense when it moves beyond paper. "The result of the study either becomes a scientific article and has a chance of ending up on a library shelf, or it becomes wine in a bottle, employment in the countryside, employment in the city, employment in restaurants, employment in hotels."

The agronomist was born in Southern Minas Gerais and built his reasoning from simple observation. As a teenager, while following coffee cultivation, he noticed the decisive role of winter climate in grain quality. "Since adolescence I knew the importance of Southern Minas Gerais' winter climate for coffee quality," he stated.

Sunny days, cold nights, and dry soil form the backdrop for coffee harvesting and, later, he would realize, also for the world's great wines.

During his training in viticulture and enology, Regina found the connection. "When I learned that the climate for making quality wine is exactly that, I thought it was very logical—if we want to make wine here, we have to reverse the plant's cycle," he said.

The key would be to shift grape maturation to autumn and winter, the drier season with greater temperature variation in the Southeast. "And what is the instrument to reverse the plant's cycle? It's doing differentiated pruning," he explained.

Starting from this interpretation, he began testing in the early 2000s the management that would become known as double pruning, or winter harvest. At this point, Regina takes on technical protagonist role. "In the case of winter wine, in that purely technological and scientific question, I consider myself the father," he stated.

"I was the first in Southeast Brazil to plant a grapevine seedling, in a grapevine experiment, to say: this plant will have to respond to differentiated pruning so that I can harvest it and have it mature grapes from May to harvest in July," he explained.

Science, however, only converted into economic results when it became wine. Regina recounts that upon harvesting the first experimental crops and confirming the grape's potential, he set about winemaking. "I rushed to make wine," he said.

At Epamig, where he worked as a researcher, he modernized the winemaking structure to serve as a support base for pioneers. "Here at Epamig I had already modernized a winery to serve as a business incubator base."

When businessmen and producers asked if the wine really worked, he did not resort only to scientific articles. "Instead of just talking, I opened a bottle."

Economic context helped, according to him. "At that moment in the 2000s, coffee was going through a gigantic crisis with low prices, and coffee growers were seeking alternatives," he said.

At the same time, a profile of investors from outside agriculture emerged—doctors, businessmen, and professionals attracted by wine. "There's a coffee crisis, there's wine quality, and there's the curiosity of many people from other sectors because of wine's glamour."

Geography completed the scenario. The Southeast concentrates wealth, population, and tourism routes. "If you analyze it well and add the population of São Paulo, Minas Gerais, and Rio de Janeiro states, we are 80 million inhabitants," he stated.

The new wine regions are just hours' drive from these major centers, which facilitates both direct sales and wine tourism.

Two decades later, Regina argues that the model has proven financially sustainable. "If there were no commercial sustainability, we would be 21 years into losing money, and no one can sustain a business like that," he said.

He rebuts criticism that winter wine is expensive when compared with imported wines of similar quality. "I believe it's a mistake to say that winter wine is expensive wine."

According to him, Brazilian labels between R$ 100 and R$ 150 compete with foreign wines that enter the country at much higher prices. "We have to compare comparable things."

The higher cost, he acknowledges, comes from low productivity and tax burden. "The taxes levied on wine in Brazil are at minimum double what our competitors in South America pay."

Today, he describes the sector as in a consolidation phase. "Winter wine today has already moved past that first phase of curiosity and is consolidating," he stated.

Wineries that planted in the early years now invest in restaurants, inns, and visitor centers, but Regina says this is not sufficient for larger operations. "Many are producing around 50,000 bottles per year, but this cannot be sustained solely with tourism."

The strategy, according to him, involves balancing wine tourism, sales to restaurants and specialty retail, and online channels.

In parallel, collective organization has gained weight. As head of the National Association of Winter Wine Producers (Anprovin), which has more than 50 member wineries, Regina helped structure a certification and traceability system. "To ensure this wine is not subject to fraud," he said.

In February 2025, the sector obtained indication of origin for winter wines from Southern Minas, an initial step toward a designation of origin.

For the agronomist, the future of the Southeast as a wine region depends less on euphoria and more on time. "Will there be crisis in wine? Yes, definitely," he said.

Still, he said the path is irreversible. "In my opinion, this wine will never cease to exist because it has the same and only absolute truth of coffee. It is good."

According to Regina, the challenge now is to respect the time of the grapevine, the wine, and the sector's own maturity so that Southeast Brazil consolidates, with method and patience, a definitive place on the wine map.
