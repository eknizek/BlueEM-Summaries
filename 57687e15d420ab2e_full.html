# OPINION: Confidence, the Asset Brazil Mistreats

"Commerce and manufactures can seldom flourish long in any state in which there is not a certain degree of confidence in the justice of government." — Adam Smith

Brazil is not a country poor in resources. It is a country poor in confidence.

Since Pero Vaz de Caminha, we have repeated the legend of abundance: "plant it, and everything grows." Minerals grow. Land grows. Sun grows. Water grows. What doesn't grow is predictability. A rich country is not one that has the most resources. It is one that has rules in which people believe. Confidence is knowing that today's rule will be valid tomorrow. That contracts will be honored. That the law doesn't change according to the mood of those in power. It is knowing that judges judge—they don't govern.

Rule of law is not academic theory. It is the following: no one is above the rule, including the government itself and magistrates. When this works, capital enters. When it fails, it demands more—or leaves. The investor doesn't debate morality. He calculates. If he perceives politicized or casuistic decisions, he adds a risk premium. If he perceives that the rule can be bent, he adds another. If conflicts of interest are tolerated, he adds yet another. At some point, the math doesn't add up.

Brazil suffers not only from a fiscal deficit. It suffers from an institutional deficit. When public positions come to be seen as instruments of personal power and increased income, confidence decreases. When judicial decisions seem unpredictable or selective, confidence decreases. When conflicts of interest are treated as a bureaucratic detail, confidence decreases.

Confidence doesn't disappear all at once. It is gradually eroded. First, one tolerates the small deviation. Then normalizes the conflict. Then rationalizes the abuse. Degradation never arrives screaming. It arrives being accepted.

One need only observe recent episodes involving relations between political power, the financial system, and public institutions. Even when the facts are still being clarified, the mere appearance of institutional promiscuity is already sufficient to produce the most corrosive effect possible: doubt.

A country can survive inflation. It can survive external crises. Even bad governments. What it cannot survive is the permanent sensation that the rule doesn't apply.

Without confidence, the cost of capital rises. With expensive capital, investment falls. Without investment, there is no growth. Without growth, there is no prosperity. The rest is discourse.

Confidence doesn't appear on a country's balance sheet. But when it disappears, all the numbers get worse.

Adam Smith knew this nearly 250 years ago.

*Mateus Bandeira is a board member, former CEO of Falconi, and former president of Oi and Banrisul.*
