# Opinion: Confidence, the asset Brazil mistreats

"Commerce and manufactures can seldom flourish long in any state in which there is not a certain degree of confidence in the justice of government." — Adam Smith

Brazil is not a country poor in resources. It is a country poor in confidence. Since Pero Vaz de Caminha, we have repeated the legend of abundance: "if you plant it, everything grows." Ore grows. Land grows. Sun grows. Water grows. What doesn't grow is predictability. A rich country is not one that has the most resources. It is one that has rules in which people believe. Confidence is knowing that today's rule will be valid tomorrow. That contracts will be honored. That the law doesn't change according to the mood of those in power. It is knowing that judges judge — they do not govern. Rule of law is not academic theory. It is the following: no one is above the rule, including the government itself and the magistrates. When this works, capital flows in. When it fails, it demands more — or leaves. The investor does not debate morality. He calculates. If he perceives politicized or casuistic decisions, he adds a risk premium. If he perceives that the rule can be bent, he adds another. If conflicts of interest are tolerated, he adds yet another. At some point, the math doesn't add up.

Brazil suffers not only from a fiscal deficit. It suffers from an institutional deficit. When public positions are seen as instruments of personal power and increased income, confidence diminishes. When judicial decisions seem unpredictable or selective, confidence diminishes. When conflicts of interest are treated as bureaucratic details, confidence diminishes. Confidence doesn't disappear all at once. It is gradually eroded. First, small deviations are tolerated. Then conflicts are normalized. Then abuses are rationalized. Degradation never comes screaming. It comes being accepted. One need only observe recent episodes involving relations between political power, the financial system, and public institutions. Even when the facts are still being clarified, the mere appearance of institutional promiscuity is sufficient to produce the most corrosive effect possible: doubt. A country can survive inflation. It can survive external crises. Even bad governments. What it cannot survive is the permanent sensation that the rule doesn't apply. Without confidence, the cost of capital rises. With expensive capital, investment falls. Without investment, there is no growth. Without growth, there is no prosperity. The rest is just talk. Confidence doesn't appear on a country's balance sheet. But when it disappears, all the numbers get worse. Adam Smith already knew this almost 250 years ago.

Mateus Bandeira is a board member, former CEO of Falconi, and former president of Oi and Banrisul.
