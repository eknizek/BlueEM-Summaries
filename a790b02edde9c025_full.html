# Petro-Perú Adds Four Presidents in Less Than 3 Months Amid Liquidity Problems

Roger Arévalo assumed the presidency on March 26, 2026 and left office in early May, after remaining in the position for just over a month. This context coincides with liquidity problems and profits reported in the first quarter, while specialists warn that the financing scheme could transfer risk to the State.

By Melissa Rodríguez Enciso

05/04/2026, 04:53 p.m.

Petro-Perú seeks to access financing of up to US$2 billion amid serious liquidity problems. The scheme in which this new bailout is framed could imply the participation of the State as guarantor, warn specialists.

At the end of April, Petro-Perú, then under the presidency of Roger Arévalo Ramírez, warned Congress of difficulties in sustaining its operations due to lack of capital for crude oil purchases, which prompted the request for financial resources. In that presentation, even risks to fuel supply were alerted if financing was not secured. This new request occurs amid constant changes in the company's Board of Directors. In less than three months, Petro-Perú has had four board presidents.

Elba Rojas Álvarez de Mares held the position from late December 2025 to March 2026. Following her departure, Edgar Zamalloa Gallegos served as president from March 7 to March 26, 2026. Then, Roger Arévalo assumed office on March 26, 2026 and left the position on May 2, 2026, after just over a month in office. The change to Edmundo Lizarzaburu Bolaños was finalized in the early morning of May 3, 2026, after which he was officially named the new board president.

## Latest Results

Last week, the state company reported a net profit of US$133.2 million in the first quarter of 2026, its first positive result in four years. This performance was mainly due to the rise in international crude oil prices, which increased the value of inventories and improved margins between oil and its derivatives, as well as lower operating expenses, the company reported to the Securities Market Superintendency (SMV).

However, the company faces liquidity restrictions. It registered a reduction in fuel sales associated with lack of capital to acquire crude oil and scheduled shutdowns at the Talara Refinery, which affects its operations.

David Tuesta, president of the Private Council for Competitiveness and former director of the state company, questioned whether the profits reflect a real improvement. "That profit is absolutely useless in terms of liquidity," he stated.

In the same vein, Carlos Paredes, former president of Petro-Perú, explained that these results are mainly due to cyclical factors, such as the increase in crude oil prices that raises the value of inventories, and this effect could reverse.

Tuesta warned that the underlying problem remains payment capacity. "The company maintains a high level of indebtedness, close to US$7 billion, and does not have the capacity to generate sufficient cash to meet its obligations," he added.

He also questioned the advisability of granting new state support under these conditions, considering that structural problems have not been resolved. In the same line, Paredes argued that, excluding these extraordinary factors, the company would have continued to record losses and maintain a cash deficit situation.

The scheme proposed to obtain financing, which would contemplate the participation of a private party with state backing, is one of the points of greatest concern. "If the company does not pay, which is highly likely, the State will have the debt," affirmed Tuesta, warning about the implications for the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF).
