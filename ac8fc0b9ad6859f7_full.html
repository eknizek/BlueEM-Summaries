# Controversy over proposal to eliminate mandatory motorcycle insurance (SOAT) for vehicles up to 250 cc in Colombia

The proposal to eliminate Mandatory Traffic Accident Insurance (SOAT) for motorcycles up to 250 cubic centimeters has sparked debate in Colombia over its possible effects on the health system, public finances, and road safety. The initiative was announced by Senator Paloma Valencia as part of her presidential campaign and contemplates a modification to the National Traffic Code.

The project seeks to exempt this segment of motorcycles from SOAT, provided they are registered in the RUNT and comply with the engine displacement limit. Additionally, it establishes a six-month transition period for the Government to regulate the new scheme and define how the care of victims in accidents involving these vehicles will be financed.

## Scope of the measure and user profile

The proposal comes amid discussions about SOAT costs and their impact on motorcyclists. According to the statement of reasons, more than 12 million motorcycles circulate in the country, equivalent to more than 62% of the automotive fleet, and 93.5% correspond to motorcycles up to 250 cc. The document notes that approximately 92% of owners belong to strata 1, 2, and 3, while 60% of motorcyclists have incomes below two minimum wages.

In this context, the initiative proposes that the motorcycle plays a central role as a mobility tool and income generator for activities such as deliveries, messaging, and self-employment. The regulation establishes that traffic authorities will verify the exemption through consultation of the RUNT and traffic license. It also indicates that if a motorcycle is modified and exceeds 250 cc, it will lose the benefit and must reacquire mandatory insurance.

The project clarifies that SOAT elimination does not exempt drivers and owners from their civil, criminal, or administrative responsibilities in case of accidents. Likewise, it states that the measure cannot be interpreted as a barrier to initial medical care for victims.

## Impact on health and system sustainability

One of the central points of debate is related to financing care for traffic accident victims. The statement of reasons itself acknowledges that the measure could generate pressure on the Administrator of Resources of the General Health and Social Security System (ADRES), which currently covers part of these costs when SOAT does not exist or the vehicle is not identified.

The document warns that eliminating the insurance requirement for low-displacement motorcycles would result in a reduction in available resources for accident care. However, it does not define a specific mechanism to compensate for that drop nor detail how the new scheme would be financed. According to figures included in the project, in 2025 ADRES reported transfers of $702 billion associated with traffic accident claims, plus $326 billion allocated to health institutions for victim care in cases without SOAT or with unidentified vehicles.

## Risks, figures, and questions

The debate also incorporates road safety data. According to figures cited in the project, motorcycles account for the highest number of deaths on the country's roads. In 2024, there were 5,291 deaths of motorcycle users, equivalent to 62% of total fatalities in traffic accidents.

Currently, lower-displacement motorcycles have differential SOAT rates. For 2026, motorcycles under 100 cc pay up to $167,000, while those between 100 and 200 cc have rates of up to $224,300.

One element that could generate discussion during the legislative process is that, although the regulation sets the benefit for motorcycles up to 250 cc, some parts of the statement of reasons also mention motorcycles up to 200 cc as reference.
