# Controversy over proposal to eliminate SOAT for motorcycles up to 250cc in Colombia

The proposal to eliminate Mandatory Traffic Accident Insurance (SOAT) for motorcycles up to 250 cubic centimeters has sparked debate in Colombia over its potential effects on the health system, public finances, and road safety. The initiative was announced by Senator Paloma Valencia as part of her presidential campaign and contemplates a modification to the National Traffic Code.

## Scope of the measure and user profile

The proposal comes amid discussions about the cost of SOAT and its impact on motorcyclists. According to the statement of reasons, more than 12 million motorcycles circulate in the country, equivalent to more than 62% of the vehicle fleet, and 93.5% corresponds to motorcycles up to 250cc. The document notes that approximately 92% of owners belong to socioeconomic strata 1, 2, and 3, while 60% of motorcyclists have incomes below two minimum wages.

In this context, the initiative proposes that the motorcycle plays a central role as a mobility tool and income generation for activities such as deliveries, courier services, and independent work.

The project seeks to exempt this segment of motorcycles from SOAT, as long as they are registered in the RUNT (National Vehicle Registry) and comply with the displacement limit. It also establishes a six-month transition period for the Government to regulate the new scheme and define how to finance care for victims in accidents involving these vehicles.

The regulations establish that traffic authorities will verify the exception through consultation with the RUNT and the traffic license. It also indicates that if a motorcycle is modified and exceeds 250cc, it will lose the benefit and must reacquire mandatory insurance.

The project specifies that eliminating SOAT does not exempt drivers and owners from their civil, criminal, or administrative responsibilities in case of accidents. It also states that the measure cannot be interpreted as a barrier to initial medical care for victims.

## Impact on health and system sustainability

One of the central points of debate is related to financing care for traffic accident victims. The statement of reasons itself acknowledges that the measure could generate pressure on ADRES (Administrator of Resources of the General System of Social Security in Health), which currently covers part of these costs when there is no SOAT or the vehicle is not identified.

The document warns that eliminating the insurance requirement for low-displacement motorcycles would result in a reduction in available resources for accident care. However, it does not define a specific mechanism to compensate for that decline or detail how the new scheme would be financed.

According to figures included in the project, in 2025 ADRES reported transfers of $702 billion pesos associated with traffic accident claims, in addition to $326 billion pesos destined to health institutions for care of victims in cases without SOAT or with unidentified vehicles.

## Risks, figures, and questions

The debate also incorporates road safety data. According to figures cited in the project, motorcycles account for the highest number of deaths on the country's roads. In 2024, there were 5,291 deaths of motorcycle users, equivalent to 62% of total fatalities in traffic accidents.

Currently, lower-displacement motorcycles have differential SOAT rates. For 2026, motorcycles under 100cc pay up to $167,000 pesos, while those between 100 and 200cc have rates of up to $224,300 pesos.

One element that could generate discussion during the legislative process is that, although the regulation sets the benefit for motorcycles up to 250cc, some sections of the statement of reasons also mention motorcycles up to 200cc as a reference.
