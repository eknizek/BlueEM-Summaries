# KPTL Brings New Partners to Accelerate Growth Momentum

With over 20 years of experience in Brazilian venture capital and a portfolio that has practically doubled in size since the merger that created the management company in 2019, KPTL is entering a new growth phase. The firm has reached 70 invested companies and ten funds under management – and to keep pace with the accelerated rhythm, it has just brought three new partners on board to strengthen senior management.

"The first question I receive from investors when I launch a new vehicle is: who will manage the fund? Investors are very concerned about the allocation of people at the management company. This reaches a level of formalism that goes into the fund's regulations. But I only have 24 hours a day, and my partners do too. If I don't bring in new people, I can't grow. I would need to wait for a 10-year fund to finish before starting another. There's a time allocation engineering involved," explains Renato Ramalho, CEO of KPTL, in an interview with Startups. He refers to partners Gustavo Junqueira, co-founder and current COO of KPTL, and Christiane Bechara, CFO.

For Renato, there was a need to expand the senior team to allow the management company's founders to dedicate themselves to more strategic areas, in addition to the question of divestments, one of the firm's focuses for this year. The three new partners have in common years at the company and what Renato calls a "partner DNA." Two of them are heavyweight names in the market and have been at KPTL for about six years: Adriano Pitoli, who was a partner at Tendências Consultoria, and Danilo Zelinski, who spent the last decade at Blackstone and today leads KPTL's climate vertical. Tomás Moraes, 25 years old, is the youngest of the three. He joined as an intern and gained prominence through the quality of his deliverables. Today he leads the prospecting area, working alongside Gustavo Junqueira.

## Niche Thesis

But the arrival of the new partners has a second dimension beyond time engineering. It also marks a shift in KPTL's own investment strategy, which has increasingly specialized in sectoral theses. When the Brazilian venture capital market was still in its infancy in the early 2000s, all management companies operated in an agnostic manner. "Everyone invested in what they could, because you didn't have ecosystems of entrepreneurship niched by sector," Renato recalls. In 2006, when KPTL launched Criatec 1, creating an exclusive agro fund would have been unfeasible: "I wouldn't be able to raise funds because I couldn't show my investors that there was enough of an entrepreneurship ecosystem in agro for me to invest R$ 200 million."

It was only in 2020, with the fifth fund, that KPTL made its first genuinely niched bet in agro. The specialization proved its value. "You create team specialization and a very deep level of sectoral and technological knowledge," says Renato. It is in this context that Danilo Zelinski and Adriano Pitoli fit in: both have built, over years of work at the management company and in their careers, genuine specialization in their respective verticals: climate and mass public services (or govtechs). For Renato, the arrival of the professionals to the partner position "not only frees the other partners for the growth of the management company, but brings depth and specialization in sectors where Brazil has comparative advantages."

## Health Fund

The newest fund managed by the firm is sectoral, focused on the health segment. The vehicle has as its thesis to invest in technology-based companies focused on health promotion and loss reduction, with special focus on self-management plans – structures created by large corporations to manage the health of their own employees, regulated by ANS. Structured by Unimed-BH, through its Horizontes Hub, in partnership with Abertta Saúde, a self-management operator of the ArcelorMittal group in Brazil, the fund currently has R$ 60 million to invest. But according to KPTL's CEO, the idea is for the vehicle to reach R$ 200 million. "That's a size where we can have budget and a dedicated team with perfect depth," notes Renato.

## "I'd Like a Calmer World"

Beyond the agenda of new funds and partners, Renato doesn't hide that the macroeconomic environment weighs heavily. "We are currently very restricted on capital in Brazil, due to interest rates, basically. All the money from private savings is in fixed income," he says. The effect is twofold: it limits the volume available for investment and reduces market liquidity. "Private equity hasn't been able to take companies to the stock exchange for four years. The liquidity problem hits everyone," he observes, adding: "I'd like a slightly calmer world. I'd like these interest rates to come down to a calmer place and the election to be as peaceful as possible, without conflict. Then we could continue doing our work better."

Even so, the pace of divestments remains steady. "Whoever has liquidity knows they have to take advantage of this moment of more apathy, while at the same time trying to negotiate better because they have money and the market doesn't." In practice, this means more pressure on valuations and tighter negotiations. However, KPTL's CEO says you "can't complain": "We've lived through much worse periods for selling companies. Today it's not difficult to open a conversation when you have a good asset in hand."
