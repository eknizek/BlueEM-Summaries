# KPTL Brings New Partners to Accelerate Growth Pace

With over 20 years of activity in Brazilian venture capital and a portfolio that has practically doubled in size since the merger that created the management company in 2019, KPTL is entering a new growth phase. The firm has reached 70 invested companies and ten funds under management – and to keep pace with this accelerated rhythm, it has just brought in three new partners to strengthen the seniority of its operations.

"The first question I receive from investors when I launch a new vehicle is: who will manage the fund? Investors are very concerned with how management personnel are allocated. This reaches a level of formalism that goes into the fund's regulations. But I have 24 hours a day, as do my partners. If I don't bring in new people, I can't grow. I would have to wait for a 10-year fund to end before starting another one. There's a time allocation engineering involved," explains Renato Ramalho, KPTL's CEO, in an interview with Startups. He refers to partners Gustavo Junqueira, cofounder and current COO of KPTL, and Christiane Bechara, CFO.

For Renato, there was a need to increase the senior team to allow the founders of the management company to dedicate themselves to more strategic areas, in addition to the matter of divestitures, one of the firm's focuses for this year. The three new partners have in common years at the company and what Renato calls a "partner DNA." Two of them are heavyweight names in the market and have been at KPTL for about six years: Adriano Pitoli, who was a partner at Tendências Consultoria, and Danilo Zelinski, who spent the last decade at Blackstone and now leads KPTL's climate vertical. Tomás Moraes, 25 years old, is the youngest of the three. He started as an intern and gained ground through the quality of his work. Today he leads the prospecting area, working alongside Gustavo Junqueira.

## Niche Thesis

But the arrival of new partners has a second dimension beyond time engineering. It also marks a shift in KPTL's own investment strategy, which has increasingly specialized in sectoral theses. When the Brazilian venture capital market was still in its infancy in the early 2000s, all management companies operated in an agnostic manner. "Everyone invested in what they could because you didn't have entrepreneurship ecosystems segmented by sector," Renato recalls.

In 2006, when KPTL launched Criatec 1, creating an exclusive agriculture fund would have been unfeasible: "I wouldn't be able to raise capital because I couldn't show my investor that there was a sufficient entrepreneurship ecosystem in agriculture for me to invest R$ 200 million."

Only in 2020, with the fifth fund, did KPTL make its first genuinely niche bet in agriculture. The specialization proved its value. "You create team specialization, very deep sectoral and technological knowledge," says Renato. It is in this context that Danilo Zelinski and Adriano Pitoli fit: both have built, over years of work at the management company and in their careers, genuine specialization in their respective verticals: climate and mass public services (or govtechs). For Renato, the arrival of these professionals to partner positions "not only frees other partners for the growth of the management company, but brings depth and specialization in sectors where Brazil has comparative advantages."

## Health Fund

The newest fund managed by the firm is indeed sectoral, focused on the healthcare segment. The vehicle's thesis is to invest in technology-based companies aimed at promoting health and reducing claims, with special focus on self-managed plans – structures created by large corporations to manage the health of their own employees, regulated by ANS (Brazil's health insurance regulator). Structured by Unimed-BH, through its Horizontes Hub, in partnership with Abertta Saúde, an ArcelorMittal group self-managed health operator in Brazil, the fund currently has R$ 60 million to invest. But according to KPTL's CEO, the vehicle is meant to reach R$ 200 million. "That's a size that allows us to have budget and a dedicated team with perfect depth," points out Renato.

## "I'd Like a Calmer World"

Beyond the agenda of new funds and partners, Renato doesn't hide that the macroeconomic environment weighs on him. "We are very capital-restricted in Brazil because of interest rates, basically. All private savings money is in fixed income," he says. The effect is twofold: it limits the volume available for investment and reduces market liquidity. "Private equity hasn't been able to take companies public for four years. The liquidity problem affects everyone," he observes, adding: "I'd like a slightly calmer world. I'd like these interest rates to come down to a calmer level and for the election to be as peaceful as possible, without conflict. Then we could continue doing our work better."

Nevertheless, the pace of divestitures remains steady. "Those who have liquidity know they have to take advantage of this moment of apathy, while at the same time trying to negotiate better because they have money and the market doesn't." In practice, this means more pressure on valuations and tighter negotiations. However, KPTL's CEO says "there's no use complaining": "We've lived through much worse periods for selling companies. Today it's not difficult to open a conversation when you have a good asset in hand."
