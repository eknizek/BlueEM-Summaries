# Clínica General del Norte Suspends Scheduled Surgeries Over EPS Payment Arrears Exceeding $441 Billion

Clínica General del Norte confirmed the suspension of scheduled surgeries following a financial crisis stemming from unpaid EPS (health insurance) accounts totaling over $441 billion, affecting its operations and raising alarms in Barranquilla. The institution acknowledged delays in payments to specialists, though it assured that emergency care remains operational.

"While there are delays in payment to some specialists at the Institution, the situation does not put patients at risk," the official statement reads. The clinic insisted that critical services continue functioning with normal operations.

Since April 22, the institution indefinitely suspended elective procedures, a measure reflecting accumulated financial deterioration over recent months. This decision marks a turning point in the care center's operational capacity in Barranquilla.

The problem stems from delayed payments by EPS, affecting the clinic's cash flow. The institution reports total outstanding debt of $441 billion with an average age of 261 days. This level of arrears has limited the clinic's ability to meet obligations, particularly to healthcare professionals.

Delays in specialist payments have reached up to four months, creating mounting tension in medical operations. The most visible impact is reflected in reduced surgical procedures. Under normal conditions, the clinic performed between 60 and 80 surgeries daily. Currently, that figure has dropped to between 6 and 10 procedures, concentrated on urgent cases.

The decrease in surgical activity also reflects reduced availability of specialized personnel. The number of active anesthesiologists has decreased from over 20 professionals to just five or six in operation.

This situation prompted an organized response from the medical community. The Colombian Health Services Labor Union formally filed a partial protest limited exclusively to elective surgeries, maintaining guaranteed critical care. Anesthesiologists stated the decision was made after exhausting dialogue options with administration. The union emphasized that the protest seeks structural solutions without compromising patient safety.

In communication sent to the clinic, specialists reiterated that critical care will continue operating without interruption, with no suspension of "urgent cases, emergencies, or vital emergency surgeries."

Despite these efforts, the suspension of elective procedures is already generating effects on service timelines. Hundreds of patients face rescheduling that could extend wait times within the local healthcare network.

The impact extends to employment, affecting stability for medical and administrative staff. Uncertainty over payments has weakened the institution's relationship with specialized personnel.

From an administrative perspective, the clinic maintains it is pursuing efforts to recover resources and working on sustainable payment agreements to gradually normalize financial obligations. It indicated that payments are being prioritized within current cash flow limitations, aiming to protect staff welfare while stabilizing financial operations.

The crisis has not gone unnoticed by local authorities. District ombudsman Miguel Ángel Alzate previously warned about the situation. Institutional alerts reflect concern over the impact on Barranquilla's healthcare system.

Medical associations have requested intervention from the National Health Superintendence to strengthen oversight of both the clinic and responsible EPS. Anesthesiologists demanded establishment of a dialogue table within five business days and a written payment normalization schedule with clear dates. Until a formal agreement explaining the payment delays is reached, the protest will remain in effect.
