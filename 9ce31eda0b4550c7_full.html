# Clínica General del Norte Suspends Scheduled Surgeries Due to EPS Debt Exceeding $441 Billion

Clínica General del Norte confirmed the suspension of scheduled surgeries following a financial crisis stemming from EPS receivables exceeding $441 billion, affecting its operations and raising alerts in Barranquilla. The institution acknowledged delays in payments to specialists, though it assured that priority care remains active. "While there are delays in payment to some specialists at the Institution, the situation does not put patients at risk," the official statement says. The clinic insisted that critical services continue operating with normal functionality.

Since April 22, the institution has indefinitely suspended elective procedures, a measure reflecting accumulated financial deterioration over recent months. The decision marks a turning point in the operational capacity of the healthcare center in Barranquilla.

The problem stems from delays in payments by EPS, which has affected the clinic's cash flow. The institution reports total debt of $441 billion with an average age of 261 days. This level of receivables has limited the clinic's ability to meet its obligations, especially to healthcare personnel. Payment delays to specialists reach up to four months, creating growing tension in medical operations.

The most visible impact is reflected in the reduction of surgical procedures. Under normal conditions, the clinic performed between 60 and 80 surgeries daily. Currently, that figure has dropped to between 6 and 10 procedures, concentrated on urgent cases.

The decrease in surgical activity also responds to reduced availability of specialized personnel. The number of active anesthesiologists dropped from over 20 professionals to just five or six in operation. This situation prompted an organized response from the medical body. The Union Organization Health Services of Colombia formalized a partial protest limited exclusively to elective surgeries, maintaining guaranteed critical care.

Anesthesiologists noted that the decision was made after exhausting dialogue channels with administration. The group emphasized that the protest seeks a structural solution without compromising patient safety. In their communication to the clinic, specialists reiterated that priority care will continue operating without interruption, stating that "urgent cases, emergencies, and vital emergency surgeries have not been suspended, nor will they be suspended."

Despite this effort, the suspension of elective procedures is already generating effects on service timeliness. Hundreds of patients face rescheduling that could extend waiting times within the local health network.

The impact also extends to labor matters, affecting the stability of medical and administrative personnel. Uncertainty about payments has weakened the relationship between the institution and its specialized talent.

From an administrative perspective, the clinic maintains it is advancing efforts to recover resources. The institution affirms it is working on sustainable payment agreements to gradually normalize its financial obligations and is prioritizing payments within current cash flow limitations, aiming to protect personnel welfare while stabilizing financial operations.

The crisis has not gone unnoticed by local authorities. Ombudsman Miguel Ángel Alzate had already warned about the situation. Institutional alerts reflect concern about the impact on Barranquilla's health system. Medical groups have requested intervention by the National Health Superintendency to strengthen oversight of both the clinic and the EPS responsible for payments.

In parallel, anesthesiologists demanded the establishment of a dialogue table within five business days and a written schedule establishing clear dates for normalizing payments. Until a formal agreement exists explaining the causes of delays, the protest will remain in effect. The continuation of the partial suspension maintains pressure on administration to accelerate concrete solutions.
