# Wheat, Corn and Soy Begin Gaining Momentum After Months of Low Prices

After several months of low prices, wheat, corn and soy on the Chicago Board of Trade are beginning to show signs of greater dynamism, reported Scotiabank.

The rise is somewhat firmer in wheat and corn, supported by risks of the fragile truce between the United States and Iran maintaining a global context with high uncertainty and by supply-related concerns. Soy, for its part, is also being favored by war-related concerns, but is beginning the week stable due to slow-moving demand.

According to the report, wheat has been the cereal with the best price performance so far in 2026, accumulating an increase of 18%, and is beginning the week with additional gains. Part of the momentum comes from an increase in the geopolitical risk premium, in a context where the truce in the Middle East is perceived as fragile, while attention remains on the United States, where wheat crop conditions in producing areas such as Kansas, Oklahoma and Texas continue to be affected by droughts and weather-related problems.

While forecasts are beginning to show possibilities of rainfall toward the end of the month, the market continues to react cautiously to any change in forecasts, which keeps prices under pressure in the short term.

Despite short-term upside risks, the global outlook is somewhat less tight. According to the latest World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates (WASDE) report from the U.S. Department of Agriculture (USDA), in April expectations for global wheat production were revised upward, driven mainly by better prospects in the European Union and Russia, while expected global demand was slightly reduced and inventories showed an increase.

Regarding corn, it has had much more moderate performance so far this year, with an increase close to 3%, and begins the week with more limited movements compared to wheat. The market's perception is of relatively ample global supply due to high inventory levels, which has limited more sustained price increases.

This continues to find support thanks to firmer demand compared to previous seasons, added to the U.S. campaign that is starting up. The USDA's crop progress report showed that in mid-April, about 5% of corn was already planted, with the south leading, while the corn belt, which concentrates around two-thirds of U.S. production, has a slower start.

According to the April WASDE report, global production was revised upward, with increases in India due to greater planted area, and in South Africa due to increases in both area and yield, plus an upward adjustment in Russia, which reinforces the perception of a well-supplied market.

Soy also comes with double-digit advances so far this year (+13%), but begins the week stable. In this market, attention is on China, where flows are gradually improving from the U.S., although they still remain below last year's levels. Chinese soybean imports from the United States rebounded in March compared to the first months of the year, following the trade truce concluded in late October, although volumes still remain below 2025 levels.

Similarly, shipments from Brazil to China grew, but fell short of expectations due to stricter inspections following problems found in some shipments, including grains with pesticide and fungicide coatings, as well as cases of heat damage and insect presence, providing more room for U.S. cargoes to enter—if available.
