# Vorcaro Used Financial Carousel Scheme When Buying Bank That Became Master, Central Bank Shows

Daniel Vorcaro attempted to buy Banco Máxima, the institution that gave rise to Master, using an embryonic version of the financial carousel scheme that is now under investigation, according to a Central Bank document that proposed vetoing the operation in February 2019. The scheme involves the circulation of resources among companies controlled by the same person, without any actual entry of external funds.

At that time, Vorcaro and other prospective buyers failed to prove the origin of the resources that would be used to capitalize Máxima, nor could they convince the Central Bank that they had the economic capacity to take control of the bank.

When contacted starting on Tuesday (14th) to comment on the problems with the Máxima purchase, Vorcaro's legal team stated through his press office on Friday (17th) that he would not comment.

The structure used by the former banker is detailed by Sidnei Corrêa Marques, who at the time was the Central Bank's director of Financial System Organization and Resolution and reporter on the case. Folha had access to two documents that became public via LAI (Freedom of Information Law).

The Central Bank pointed out that, according to the documentation presented, both the resources used to purchase shares from Saul Sabbá (then controller of Máxima) for R$ 40 million, and the additional capital contributions made by Vorcaro to the bank, of R$ 48.1 million and R$ 22.5 million, originated from the distribution of earnings from Viking Participações, a company known for owning aircraft that Vorcaro used to travel in. This, in turn, resulted from the revaluation of assets—land and investment funds.

Central Bank technicians suspected this was "circularization" of resources—a practice in which money merely circulates among related companies, without any actual entry of new resources. According to investigations, this maneuver was used by Vorcaro in the Master case.

The director stated that Central Bank supervision determined that "a significant portion of resources to carry out the acquisition of shares of the current controller originated from Banco Máxima itself, through sequential transfers of resources between the institution, companies owned by Daniel Vorcaro, and investment funds."

When questioned during the process, according to the document, Vorcaro did not present elements that clarified the origin of the resources in a "clear and unequivocal" manner.

Much of the detailed breakdown of the financial carousel is redacted in the Central Bank document. In the portions not under confidentiality, the authority describes that in 2016, Viking's financial statements recorded investments worth R$ 112.5 million. The documents showed the company's participation in two other companies, WWS and Superávit, which between 2015 and 2016 recorded extraordinary valuations in real estate (4,800% and 600%, respectively).

The Central Bank identified weaknesses in the appraisals that supported these accounting results from Viking, due to the manner in which the assets of these two companies were calculated. After revaluing the assets, the company subscribed, on September 1, 2017, approximately 2.6 million shares of the Brazil Realty fund through the transfer of more than 500,000 shares of WWS (54.67% of its capital). In that same month, Viking made a new subscription for fund shares (more than 2.1 million), through the transfer of the remaining shares of WWS.

Vorcaro is being investigated by the CVM (Securities and Exchange Commission) because of operations involving Brazil Realty, a closed real estate investment fund, for practices such as asset overvaluation.

"Banco Máxima, chaired by Daniel Vorcaro, did not present any document or information demonstrating, in a clear and unequivocal manner, the origin of the resources used in the acquisition of control and in the capital increases of the institution, therefore not satisfying the normative commands that require demonstration of the regular origin of resources," said the Central Bank director in his vote.

The scope of the operation to purchase Máxima was modified over the course of the process. On September 15, 2017, Vorcaro would purchase from Sabbá shares representing 56.87% of the bank's capital for R$ 40 million. Later, the form of payment was changed to R$ 36 million in Brazil Realty fund shares and R$ 4 million in cash. On January 16, 2019, it was agreed that other participants would enter the deal: Augusto Lima (10% of shares), Bruno Guedes (3%), Yan Tironi (3%), Alexandre Seguim (3%), Antonio Neto (3%), Felipe Simonsen (1.75%), and Armando Gallo (1.75%).

Banco Máxima was facing financial difficulties and was on the brink of being liquidated by the Central Bank. A former Central Bank member estimates that the damage to the financial system would have been much smaller if Máxima's liquidation had occurred soon after Vorcaro's purchase was rejected in February 2019, or even before, since the size of the problem for the FGC (Credit Guarantee Fund) only grew. From the beginning of negotiations in 2017 until the approval of Máxima's purchase by Vorcaro in October 2019, the bill to be paid by the FGC nearly tripled.
