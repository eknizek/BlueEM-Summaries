# Vorcaro already used financial carousel scheme when buying bank that became Master, shows Central Bank

Daniel Vorcaro attempted to buy Banco Máxima, the institution that gave rise to Master, using an embryonic version of the financial carousel scheme that is now under investigation, according to a Central Bank document that proposed vetoing the operation in February 2019. The scheme involves the circulation of resources between companies owned by the same controller, without any actual inflow of external funds.

At that time, Vorcaro and other bidders were unable to prove the origin of the resources that would be used to capitalize Máxima, nor could they convince the Central Bank that they had the economic capacity to take control of the bank.

When contacted starting Tuesday (14) to comment on problems with the Máxima purchase, Vorcaro's defense stated through his press office on Friday (17) that he would not make any statements.

The structure used by the former banker is detailed by Sidnei Corrêa Marques, then director of Financial System Organization and Resolution at the Central Bank and rapporteur of the process. Folha had access to two documents that became public via LAI (Freedom of Information Law).

The Central Bank noted that, according to documentation presented, both the resources used to purchase shares from Saul Sabbá (then controller of Máxima) for R$ 40 million, and the additional capital contributions made by Vorcaro to the bank, of R$ 48.1 million and R$ 22.5 million, originated from the distribution of results from Viking Participações, a company known for owning aircraft that he customarily used. This, in turn, resulted from the revaluation of assets—land and investment funds.

Central Bank technicians suspected this was "circularization" of resources—a practice in which money merely circulates between related companies, without any real inflow of new resources. According to investigations, this maneuver was used by Vorcaro in the Master case.

The director stated that Central Bank oversight determined that "an expressive portion of resources to carry out the acquisition of shares by the current controller originated from Banco Máxima itself, through sequential transfers of resources between the institution, companies owned by Daniel Vorcaro, and investment funds."

When questioned during the process, according to the document, Vorcaro did not present elements that clarified the origin of the resources in a "clear and unequivocal" manner.

Much of the detail regarding the financial carousel is redacted in the Central Bank document. In the sections not under confidentiality, the agency describes that in 2016, Viking's financial statements recorded investments valued at R$ 112.5 million. The documents showed the company's participation in two other entities, WWS and Superávit, which between 2015 and 2016 registered extraordinary appreciations in real estate (4,800% and 600%, respectively).

The Central Bank identified weaknesses in the appraisals that supported these accounting results from Viking, based on how the assets of these two companies were calculated. After revaluation of assets, the firm subscribed, on September 1, 2017, approximately 2.6 million units of the Brazil Realty fund through the transfer of more than 500,000 shares of WWS (54.67% of its capital). In that same month, Viking made a new subscription of fund units (over 2.1 million) through the transfer of the remaining WWS shares.

Vorcaro is being investigated by the CVM (Securities and Exchange Commission) due to operations involving Brazil Realty, a closed real estate investment fund, for practices such as asset overvaluation.

"Banco Máxima, presided over by Daniel Vorcaro, did not present any document or information demonstrating, in a clear and unequivocal manner, the origin of the resources used in the acquisition of control and in the capital increases of the institution, and consequently did not satisfy the normative requirements that demand demonstration of the regular origin of resources," said the Central Bank director in his vote.

The scope of the operation to purchase Máxima was modified throughout the process. On September 15, 2017, Vorcaro would purchase from Sabbá shares representing 56.87% of the bank's capital for R$ 40 million. Later, the form of payment was changed to R$ 36 million in Brazil Realty fund units and R$ 4 million in cash.

On January 16, 2019, it was agreed that other participants would enter the transaction: Augusto Lima (10% of shares), Bruno Guedes (3%), Yan Tironi (3%), Alexandre Seguim (3%), Antonio Neto (3%), Felipe Simonsen (1.75%), and Armando Gallo (1.75%).

Banco Máxima was facing financial difficulties and was on the brink of being liquidated by the Central Bank. A former Central Bank member believes that damage to the financial system would have been much less if Máxima's liquidation had occurred soon after the rejection of Vorcaro's purchase in February 2019 or even before, since the magnitude of the problem for the FGC (Credit Guarantee Fund) only continued to grow. From the beginning of negotiations in 2017 until the approval of Máxima's purchase by Vorcaro in October 2019, the bill to be paid by the FGC nearly tripled.
