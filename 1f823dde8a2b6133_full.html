# Solar Energy "Theft": The New Trend Causing Shock in Brazil's Power Sector

Walking through practically any Brazilian city, it's not difficult to encounter solar energy panels on the roofs of houses and businesses, or even on land beside roads and in remote locations. These photovoltaic generation systems have become popular in the country over the last decade supported by generous incentives – including discounts on electricity bills – and have reached the mark of 4.3 million installations. In terms of installed power generation capacity, there are almost 48 gigawatts spread across these small-scale installations – equivalent to more than three Itaipu dams. Or at least that's what was thought.

Now, a study conducted by technicians at the National Electric System Operator (ONS) revealed that, in fact, so-called distributed generation is even more representative in the electrical grid. However, not all systems are properly registered and regularized. In a conservative projection, the ONS estimated at least 14 additional gigawatts in photovoltaic panels operating irregularly in 2025. The growth was spectacular: in the previous year, this informal park was "only" 5.7 GW.

"It's solar energy 'theft,'" a sector authority in Brasília told Brazil Journal, admitting that the discovery caused shock. "Of course we had some notion that there was some inadequacy. But we were very surprised by the volumes. We didn't have this dimension."

According to sector rules, all solar panel installations by individuals or companies must be registered with the local energy distributor and the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel). It's not mere bureaucracy: those using these systems receive credits on their electricity bill for the energy produced – an incentive funded by charges collected in electricity tariffs. The subsidies have been gradually reduced since a 2022 law, but installations completed by January 2023 continued to receive full incentives.

By not informing Aneel about expansions, the individual or company keeps the additional capacity hidden and continues to receive the full benefit on all generated energy – not on the declared capacity. The problem is that this creates additional costs for other consumers, similar to what occurs with traditional electricity "theft."

The problem is associated with the enormous fragmentation of distributed solar generation: thousands of companies, many of them small, and sometimes even individuals installing panels on their own in their homes or building mini power plants.

"Because of this, there's a certain culture of transgression in the sector; a more open interpretation of norms," said a business entrepreneur. "The guy is authorized to put a small solar panel, a hair-thin one. Then he puts two hair-thin ones. Another puts three, four. Aneel doesn't inspect, partly because they don't even have the staff for it. And when you looked, it became a huge head of hair," said an expert who follows the issue.

The problem only appeared because the ONS realized that distributed generation has actually been much larger than what was declared by those who installed the panels. From there, the agency conducted the technical study to estimate the volumes. Some distributors also detected the distortion, and one concessionaire even sent drones to conduct inspections, flagging some cases of mini solar plants twice the registered size, according to sources.

Beyond improperly benefiting from the incentives granted to the sector, irregular installations generate risks of blackouts or grid problems. This is because the ONS, which coordinates real-time operation of the electrical system, doesn't "see" this unregistered generation and doesn't account for it in its projections and operational decisions, nor do the distributors.

An area with many solar panels requires more transformers in the grid, for example, but the distributor cannot make that investment if it officially has no knowledge of the installations.

"We're reaching a point where you could have a blackout caused by this 'invisible' distributed generation," said an executive from an energy concessionaire.

Solving the problem will require firm action from Aneel, but a source assesses that an adjustment in rules is necessary, since the current punishment foresees the summary disconnection of irregular solar panels and also of the consumer unit associated with them. In practice, many people would end up without power, without the right to defend themselves in the process, which would certainly also trigger legal disputes.

"It ends up being difficult to implement because it's not reasonable, seems a bit disproportionate. It's like if a guy crosses outside the crosswalk and I go there and shoot him in the head."

Aneel is beginning discussions on how to resolve the situation, which should involve refactoring those flagged in irregular situations, with an opportunity for regularization, as occurs with the discovery of traditional electricity "theft." Differentiated treatment should also be evaluated between cases involving residences and those involving mini power plants that sell production to third parties, usually in subscription models.

"The most serious cases are the mini power plants. Because that consumer who put solar on their roof, unfortunately, often doesn't even know they're doing something wrong. It was someone who ended up selling it to them. But the situation needs to be regularized, come what may," said a sector authority.

Aneel is conducting a public consultation on how to address the issue and has determined that distributors conduct an audit to find irregular cases. In a recent meeting to discuss the problem, Aneel's director-general, Sandoval Feitosa, said that some situations are "police matters," involving intentional submission of false information to the regulator to obtain gains.

"There are fraud situations that in due time will be harshly penalized."

In these cases, according to him, the agency will pursue "civil and criminal accountability" of those involved.

On electricity bills, the tariff discounts granted to those who invest in distributed generation systems cost R$16 billion in 2025, funded by a charge collected from other consumers, the so-called Energy Development Account (CDE).
