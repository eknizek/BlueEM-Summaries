04 May 2026 · 09:41 PM

# Ecuador lowers tariff on Colombian products from 100% to 75% amid crisis

The Government of the Republic of Ecuador, under the administration of President Daniel Noboa Azin, issued an official statement on May 4, 2026, announcing the reduction of the so-called "security rate" applied to imports from Colombia, dropping from 100% to 75%.

The measure, which takes effect immediately, is presented as a step to "advance toward bilateral cooperation mechanisms in security matters" and seek the strengthening of development in the border zone between both nations.

This tariff adjustment occurs just three days after the maximum levy of 100% entered into force, which had been formalized by Ecuador's National Customs Service (Senae) through a resolution issued on April 9.

According to official records, this trade conflict has escalated steadily since the beginning of the year, when a 30% rate was initially imposed on the grounds that Colombia was not implementing sufficient actions against drug trafficking and illegal mining.

## Paloma Valencia's Claims

Democratic Center candidate Paloma Valencia asserted through her social networks that this decision by Ecuador's Government is due to her efforts. "Today I spoke by telephone with President Daniel Noboa of the Republic of Ecuador. I expressed my commitment to cooperate in the recovery of security on both sides of the border, in case I am elected president of Colombia. President Noboa made the decision to lower tariffs from 100% to 75% as a show of his goodwill to work with the next government," the candidate stated.

## Timeline of tariff escalation

The dispute between Quito and Bogotá has gone through various stages of hardening. After implementing 30% in January and February, the Ecuadorian Government raised it to 50% and finally to 100% on May 1.

President Noboa justified these actions by pointing to a "lack of implementation of concrete measures" by the Colombian Government to secure the common border.

During the period when the rate remained at 100%, the only Colombian goods that received exemptions were petroleum products, energy generation, and related items.

The Colombian Government qualified the impact of this policy as a "flagrant violation" of the Liberalization Program of the Cartagena Agreement and the principles of the Andean Community.

## Colombia's response and reciprocal measures

In response to Ecuador's decisions, President Gustavo Petro's government adopted measures of economic pressure. Among the most notable actions are the suspension of electrical interconnection and the closure of land border crossings for the entry of key Ecuadorian agricultural products, specifically rice and bananas.

Likewise, Colombia's Ministry of Commerce had prepared a draft decree to raise its own tariffs up to 75% in response to Ecuador's policy. The objective of this measure was to "counteract distortions" in bilateral trade and "protect national production" in the face of what they considered unviable competitive conditions.

Technical projections warned that if the conflict persisted, Colombian exports to Ecuador could fall 79%, representing a loss of approximately 1.452 billion dollars.

## Social and economic impact on the border

The situation has generated deep alarm in the populations of Tulcán and Ipiales, where the economy depends closely on binational flow. Transporters in the area reported intense truck movement in the hours before the 100% tariff took effect in an attempt to avoid excessive costs.

Juan Carlos Benavides, a transporter in the region, warned about the consequences of the conflict: "until today is the movement... all this would go bankrupt."

Julio Erazo, representative of currency exchange workers on the border, stated that the crisis has transformed labor festivities into "the day of unemployment, the day of hunger, and the day without work."

## Underlying political tensions

Beyond tariffs, the diplomatic relationship has been affected by security incidents and cross-declarations. In early March, an Ecuadorian bombing occurred against criminal gang camps on the border; a device fell in Colombian territory, although "it was determined that it had bounced without detonating," allowing that specific episode to be closed.

However, the political climate remained tense due to statements by President Petro, who described former Ecuadorian Vice President Jorge Glas as a "political prisoner," which led to accusations from Noboa about alleged links by the Colombian leader with drug trafficking sectors.

Recently, following Noboa's accusations about a supposed guerrilla incursion from Colombia, President Petro asked his counterpart to "stop believing lies" and proposed a personal meeting on the border to "build peace."

The current adjustment to 75% in the security rate occurs in this context of dialogue attempts, while business associations from both countries maintain their call for governments to sit down to negotiate to prevent "economic vulnerability" from permanently paralyzing regional exchange.
