# Time Bombs Left by the Outgoing Congress

By Luis Miguel Castilla

The end of the Legislative branch's term is a particularly critical moment for the economy. In a context where most political parties lost their registration by failing to surpass the electoral threshold, congressmen do not face consequences for the measures they approve, but they do have incentives to benefit their bases ahead of the October local and regional elections, in which they will still participate with valid registration.

Until the end of the legislative session, the outgoing Congress will continue meeting and approving laws, while the country's attention is focused on the ongoing electoral process and the transfer to the new Government. As of April 18, according to the Congress website, there were 6,038 bills in commission, 1,980 with rulings, and 332 on the plenary agenda. 57% of pending rulings were being promoted by five political groupings without future registration: Perú Libre (23.1%), Acción Popular (9.2%), Bloque Magisterial (8.7%), Podemos Perú (8.5%), and Alianza para el Progreso (7.3%).

On the economic front, there are three areas where the risk is particularly critical.

**Debt Extinction**

According to unfavorable reports issued by the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) and the Congress website, there are 153 bills with negative fiscal impact at various stages of processing. Their potential fiscal cost exceeds S/ 280 billion, equivalent to 22.4% of GDP. Among the most expensive initiatives are two presented by Acción Popular: one that would extinguish fines and interest from Reactiva Perú debtors for S/ 87 billion, and another incentive for early payment that would grant discounts of up to 100% of late-payment interest for an additional S/ 82 billion.

There are also reductions in corporate income tax rates for technology companies, exemptions for artisanal miners, and tax benefits for small and medium-sized businesses of all kinds. The pattern is consistent: benefits concentrated in sectors with high electoral density and diffuse costs transferred to all taxpayers.

Repeated tax amnesties destroy a culture of compliance. If taxpayers learn that Congress will periodically forgive their debts, the incentive to pay ceases to exist. Tax non-compliance already reaches 9.3% of GDP according to the MEF, and preferential treatments perpetuate a mercantilist behavior that seems to have no limits. The Fiscal Council has warned about this risk repeatedly, without the Executive or Legislative branches having taken effective containment or correction measures.

**Mining Concessions**

The ruling led by Juntos por el Perú (JPP) and approved in March by the Energy and Mining Commission proposes reducing mining project concession periods from 30 to 15 years, when the average real timeframe in Peru exceeds 40 years and copper projects can require up to 62 years. It also proposes increasing concession maintenance costs by up to 300%, in a country where it already pays three to four times more than in Chile, Colombia, or Canada. And it proposes eliminating the irrevocable nature of the concession, which is precisely the guarantee that makes long-term investment viable.

What makes the situation even more serious is that this ruling ignored negative reports issued by the relevant authorities and warnings from consulted specialists. If the plenary ratifies this ruling, the damage would operate on two simultaneous fronts: disinvestment in formal mining and expansion of illegal mining. Reducing concession periods creates windows of vulnerability that illegal operators immediately exploit. Last year alone, illegal gold exports reached a historic high of USD 11.536 billion, nearly equaling legal exports (USD 11.708 billion). Formal mining cannot compete in such an environment if the State does not provide the minimum legal certainty that makes it viable.

**Labor Rigidity**

Three initiatives in an advanced state threaten to make the labor market more rigid and informal. The first would require prior judicial authorization to dismiss a union leader, which in practice means that a worker involved in a serious fault could remain in their position for the two or three years the process takes (JPP ruling). The second, authored by the Bloque Magisterial, introduces the perverse incentive of linking the financing capacity for worker salaries at the Labor Inspection Superintendency to the level of revenue from sanctions it issues. The third, promoted by Perú Libre, expands leave and benefits charged to EsSalud, whose actuarial deficit is already documented, which would increase formality costs in a labor market with 70% informality.

Faced with this situation, the Executive has tools at its disposal: veto laws, appeal to the Constitutional Court, and publicly disclose the harm of each initiative before its voting in the plenary. So far, it has not used them with the firmness that the situation requires, due to the endemic weakness that characterizes it.

The next Government will inherit breached fiscal rules, a stressed credit rating, and an unprecedented legislative liability. That legacy is already sealed. What remains to be decided is how much more is added in the next 60 days, while no actor assumes responsibility for preventing it.
