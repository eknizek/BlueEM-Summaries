Outgoing Congress leaves fiscal time bombs for next administration

25 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# The Outgoing Congress's Time Bombs

By Luis Miguel Castilla

The end of the Legislative branch's term is a particularly critical moment for the economy. In a context where most political parties lost their registration by failing to exceed the electoral threshold, congressmen face no consequences for the measures they approve, but they do have incentives to benefit their bases ahead of the October local and regional elections, in which they will still participate with valid registration.

Until the end of the legislature, the outgoing Congress will continue to meet and approve laws, while the country's attention is focused on the ongoing electoral process and the transfer to the new Government. Until April 18, according to the Congress portal, there were 6,038 bills in commission, 1,980 with opinions, and 332 on the plenary agenda. 57% of pending opinions were being promoted by five political groups without future registration: Perú Libre (23.1%), Acción Popular (9.2%), Bloque Magisterial (8.7%), Podemos Perú (8.5%), and Alianza para el Progreso (7.3%).

On the economic front, there are three areas where the risk is particularly critical.

## Debt Extinction

According to unfavorable reports issued by the Ministry of Economy and Finance and the Congress portal, there are 153 bills with negative fiscal impact at different stages of processing. Their potential fiscal cost exceeds S/ 280 billion, equivalent to 22.4% of GDP. Among the most expensive initiatives are two presented by Acción Popular: one that would eliminate fines and interest for Reactiva Perú debtors by S/ 87 billion, and another incentive for early payment that would grant discounts of up to 100% of late payment interest for an additional S/ 82 billion. There are also income tax rate reductions for technology companies, exemptions for artisanal miners, and tax benefits for small and medium-sized businesses of all kinds. The pattern is consistent: concentrated benefits for sectors with high electoral density and diffuse costs transferred to all taxpayers.

Repeated tax amnesties destroy a culture of compliance. If taxpayers learn that Congress will periodically forgive their debts, the incentive to pay ceases to exist. Tax non-compliance already reaches 9.3% of GDP according to the MEF, and preferential treatments perpetuate a mercantilist behavior that seems to have no limits. The Fiscal Council has warned about this risk repeatedly, without the Executive or Legislative branches taking effective containment or corrective measures.

## Mining Concessions

The opinion led by Juntos por el Perú and approved in March by the Energy and Mining Commission proposes reducing mining project concession terms from 30 to 15 years, when the average real timeframe in Peru exceeds 40 years and copper projects can require up to 62. It also proposes increasing concession maintenance costs by up to 300%, in a country where three to four times more is already paid than in Chile, Colombia, or Canada. And it proposes eliminating the irrevocable nature of the concession, which is precisely the guarantee that makes long-term investment viable.

What makes the situation even more serious is that this opinion ignored negative reports issued by the relevant authorities and warnings from consulted specialists. If the plenary ratifies this opinion, damage will operate on two simultaneous fronts: disinvestment in formal mining and expansion of illegal mining. Reducing concession periods creates windows of unprotection that illegal operators immediately exploit. Last year alone, illegal gold exports reached a historical high of USD 11.536 billion, nearly matching legal exports (USD 11.708 billion). Formal mining cannot compete in that environment if the State does not provide the minimum legal certainty that makes it viable.

## Labor Rigidity

Three initiatives in advanced stages threaten to make the labor market more rigid and informal. The first would require prior judicial authorization to dismiss a union leader, which in practice means that a worker involved in serious misconduct could remain in their position for the two or three years the process takes (JPP opinion). The second, authored by Bloque Magisterial, introduces the perverse incentive of linking the financing capacity of remuneration for workers of the Labor Inspection Superintendency with the level of revenue collection from the sanctions it issues. The third, promoted by Perú Libre, expands leaves and benefits charged to EsSalud, whose actuarial deficit is already documented, which would increase the costs of formality in a labor market with 70% informality.

Facing this situation, the Executive has tools at its disposal: to observe the laws, resort to the Constitutional Court, and make public the harm of each initiative before its plenary vote. So far it has not used them with the firmness the situation requires, due to the endemic weakness that characterizes it. The next Government will inherit violated fiscal rules, a stressed credit rating, and unprecedented legislative liabilities. That legacy is already sealed. What remains to be decided is how much more is added in the next 60 days, while no actor assumes responsibility for preventing it.
