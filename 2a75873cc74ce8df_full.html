# Sabesp Sees Copasa Privatization Timeline as 'Tight' with Added Risk

Sabesp is studying the privatization of Copasa, but any eventual participation in the process will still depend on a detailed analysis of the risk and return involved, said the company's CEO, Carlos Augusto Piani. In parallel, the company is also awaiting bids for the Universaliza São Paulo program, scheduled for the second half of the year and viewed as more obvious opportunities.

"São Paulo is very close to us. Minas Gerais is a little further away. But we will also look if it comes to market," Piani said while participating in a Bradesco BBI event on Tuesday.

"I think the timing is very tight," he added, speaking about the preparations for the auction.

Piani also noted that the sanitation regulation model in Minas Gerais "is not as robust as the model in São Paulo," with some definitions still pending, which means the business has "a little more risk."

When discussing appetite for Copasa, he also pointed out that Equatorial Energia, which became Sabesp's reference shareholder, "went six to eight years without investing in anything" because it could not find assets with returns adjusted to risk. "In any case, we are looking. In M&A, you have to always be present."

Piani, who was chairman of Equatorial before taking over Sabesp, also said that any eventual entry into the Copasa dispute will depend on Sabesp being up to date with its obligations in São Paulo. In this regard, however, he noted that the company is confident in meeting universalization targets.

He also revealed that Sabesp is discussing an upward adjustment to the investments planned for the universalization of sanitation services in its areas. The current capex projected through 2029 is R$ 70 billion; the new number has not yet been finalized.

"Today we have over a thousand ongoing works, almost 40,000 people working on our universalization projects," said Piani.

According to him, the capex adjustment will not be "anything absurd" and stems from higher-than-expected inflation, amid a heated infrastructure project market in São Paulo and the advancement of some investments due to this year's water scarcity.

"We will invest a little more. We are discussing exactly that amount, but there was nothing extraordinary."
