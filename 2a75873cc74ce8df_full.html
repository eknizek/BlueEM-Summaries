Sabesp sees Copasa privatization timeline as 'tight' with 'somewhat higher risk'

07 April 2026 · 03:56 PM

Sabesp is studying the privatization of Copasa, but any eventual participation in the process will still depend on a detailed analysis of the risks and returns involved, said the company's CEO, Carlos Augusto Piani. In parallel, the company is also awaiting bids for the Universaliza São Paulo program, scheduled for the second half of the year and seen as more obvious opportunities. "São Paulo is very close to us. Minas Gerais is a little further away. But we will also look if it comes to market," Piani said while participating in a Bradesco BBI event on Tuesday. "I think the timing is very tight," he added, speaking about the preparations for the auction. Piani also noted that the sanitation regulatory model in Minas "is not as robust as São Paulo's model," with some definitions still pending, which makes the business have "somewhat higher risk." When speaking about the appetite for Copasa, he also pointed out that Equatorial Energia, which became Sabesp's reference shareholder, "went six to eight years without investing in anything" because it did not find assets with returns adjusted to risk. "In any case, we are looking. In M&A, you have to always be present." Piani, who was chairman of Equatorial before taking over Sabesp, also said that any eventual entry into the competition for Copasa will depend on Sabesp being up to date with its obligations in São Paulo. On that front, however, he pointed out that the company is confident in reaching universalization goals. He also revealed that Sabesp is discussing an upward adjustment to planned investments for universalizing sanitation services in its areas. The current capex projected through 2029 is R$ 70 billion; the new figure has not yet been finalized. "Today we have over a thousand works in progress, almost 40,000 people working on our universalization projects," said Piani. According to him, the capex adjustment will not be "anything absurd" and stems from higher-than-expected inflation, amid a heated infrastructure project market in São Paulo and the anticipation of some investments due to this year's water scarcity. "We will invest a little more. We are discussing exactly that amount, but there was nothing major."
