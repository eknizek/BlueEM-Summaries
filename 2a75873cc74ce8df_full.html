# Sabesp Views Copasa Privatization Timeline as "Tight" and Higher Risk

Sabesp is studying the privatization of Copasa, but any eventual participation in the process will still depend on a detailed analysis of the risks and returns involved, said the company's CEO, Carlos Augusto Piani. In parallel, the company is also awaiting bids from the Universaliza São Paulo program, scheduled for the second half of the year and seen as more obvious opportunities.

"São Paulo is very close to us. Minas Gerais is a little bit further away. But we will also look if it comes to market," Piani said while participating in a Bradesco BBI event this Tuesday.

"I think the timing is very tight," he added, speaking about the preparations for the auction.

Piani also noted that the sanitation regulation model in Minas Gerais "is not as robust as the São Paulo model," with some definitions still pending, which makes the business have "a little more risk."

When speaking about appetite for Copasa, he also pointed out that Equatorial Energia, which became Sabesp's reference shareholder, "went six to eight years without investing in anything" because it couldn't find assets with returns adjusted to risk. "In any case, we are looking. In M&A, you always have to be present."

Piani, who was chairman of Equatorial before assuming the role at Sabesp, also said that any eventual entry into the Copasa bid will depend on Sabesp being up to date with its obligations in São Paulo. On this point, however, he noted that the company is confident in meeting universalization targets.

He also revealed that Sabesp is discussing an upward adjustment in investments planned for the universalization of sanitation services in its areas. The current capex projected through 2029 is R$ 70 billion; the new number is not yet finalized.

"Today we have more than a thousand ongoing works, almost 40,000 people working on our universalization projects," said Piani.

According to him, the capex adjustment will be "nothing absurd" and stems from higher-than-expected inflation, amid a heated infrastructure project market in São Paulo and the anticipation of some investments due to this year's water scarcity.

"We will invest a little more. We are discussing exactly that amount, but there was nothing more to it."
