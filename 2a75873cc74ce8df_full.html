# Sabesp sees Copasa timeline as "tight" and "somewhat riskier"

Sabesp is studying the privatization of Copasa, but any eventual participation in the process will still depend on a detailed analysis of the risks and returns involved, said the company's CEO, Carlos Augusto Piani. In parallel, the company is also awaiting bidding processes for the Universaliza São Paulo program, scheduled for the second half of the year and seen as more obvious opportunities.

"São Paulo is very close to us. Minas Gerais is a little further away. But we will also look if it comes to market," Piani said while participating in a Bradesco BBI event on Tuesday.

"I think the timing is very tight," he added, speaking about the preparations for the auction.

Piani also noted that the sanitation regulation model in Minas "is not as robust as São Paulo's model," with some definitions still pending, which means the business has "somewhat more risk."

When discussing appetite for Copasa, he also pointed out that Equatorial Energia, which became Sabesp's reference shareholder, "went six to eight years without investing in anything" because it could not find assets with returns adjusted to risk.

"In any case, we are looking at it. In M&A, you have to always be present."

Piani, who was chairman of Equatorial before taking over Sabesp, also said that any eventual bid for Copasa will depend on Sabesp being up to date with its obligations in São Paulo. On this front, however, he noted that the company is confident in meeting universalization targets.

He also revealed that Sabesp is discussing an upward adjustment to planned investments for the universalization of sanitation services in its areas. The current capex projected through 2029 is R$ 70 billion; the new number has not yet been finalized.

"Today we have more than a thousand ongoing projects, almost 40,000 people working on our universalization projects," said Piani.

According to him, the capex adjustment will not be "anything absurd" and stems from higher-than-expected inflation, amid a heated infrastructure project market in São Paulo and the advancement of some investments due to this year's water scarcity.

"We will invest a bit more. We are discussing exactly this amount, but there was nothing out of the ordinary."
