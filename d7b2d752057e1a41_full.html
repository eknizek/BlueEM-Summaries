# Oil shock puts Brazil's Central Bank in a dilemma

The Economist published an article whose title — in free translation — "global energy markets are on the brink of disaster" already anticipates its content. It states that the disruption of supply, combined with short-term demand rigidity, is pushing global energy markets, especially in Asia, to a critical point. The adjustment is not occurring smoothly: governments are already resorting to administrative measures (rationing, mobility restrictions, and school closures). Even in an optimistic scenario of a quick agreement between countries in dispute in the Middle East, the energy market will remain under pressure for several months.

Given that refining and logistics capacity is limited, the spot prices of derivatives in the international physical market, typically at Asian hubs like Singapore, have risen more than crude oil compared to pre-war levels: gasoline 50%; diesel 88%; and aviation kerosene 128%. This is, therefore, a supply shock of significant magnitude that, in addition to raising prices, tends to reduce global growth.

There are positive and negative impacts of this crisis for Brazil, but if the scenario described by The Economist confirms itself, the balance tends to be unfavorable.

Being a net oil exporter, the country will see a relevant improvement in its terms of trade (price of exports/price of imports). This, combined with the high interest rate differential, tends to strengthen the real. Additionally, the shock should increase revenue from the oil sector, with a net positive effect on public accounts.

The most obvious negative side is inflationary pressure. Even in an optimistic scenario of geopolitical crisis normalization within one or two months, expectations for IPCA-2026 have risen between 0.8% and 1%. To a lesser extent, the Central Bank's Focus survey also points to rising expectations for 2027 and 2028.

Supply shocks of this magnitude, which combine inflation and growth deceleration, place central banks before a classic dilemma in conducting monetary policy. In the Brazilian case, this dilemma is more acute. If the Central Bank hesitates to react to the anchoring of expectations, it risks its credibility and the entrenchment of inflation. On the other hand, maintaining the real Selic rate close to 10% imposes an explosive trajectory on public debt/GDP and asphyxiates credit, potentially worsening the solvency crisis already observed in families and businesses.

This Wednesday, the Central Bank will likely announce a new homeopathic reduction of 0.25 percentage points in the Selic and reinforce dependence on data. In other words, uncertainty will remain elevated.

In a supply shock scenario, monetary policy operates with limited effectiveness: it can contain expectations, but at the cost of deepening deceleration.
