# Companies Exist to Cross Rivers

"He who doesn't risk it doesn't cross the river," says the proverb. In the business world, this phrase makes a lot of sense since no company grows, innovates, invests, or transforms without taking risks. To think otherwise would be to ignore the nature of business activity. Without risk, there is no value creation. Companies are not meant to stay on the riverbank. Growing, innovating, investing, or transforming requires advancing in scenarios of uncertainty, making decisions with incomplete information, and accepting that not every outcome can be controlled beforehand. That is why risk is not an anomaly in business; it is part of its essence.

What is truly dangerous is not taking risks, but doing so without criteria, without checks and balances, and without corporate governance capable of distinguishing between audacity and recklessness. It is one thing to assume risks consciously, deliberately, and aligned with strategy, and quite another to advance without fully understanding the current or the depth of the river, relying more on enthusiasm than on sound judgment.

This point is especially important today, in an environment that has become more complex and volatile. To financial and operational risks are added others that can affect with equal or greater intensity business continuity, reputation, license to operate, or future viability. Cybersecurity, integrity, compliance, organizational culture, sustainability, technological disruption, organized crime, and regulatory and geopolitical tensions are no longer peripheral matters but central to strategic conversation.

In this context, the board's role is not to manage the company or replace management in risk management, but to ensure that the most significant risks—the "material" ones—are properly identified, understood, prioritized, and governed. Not as a formal exercise, nor as a routine review of matrices that reassure more than they explain, but as part of the quality of decisions.

A good board is not there to eliminate risk, because that would paralyze the company. Nor is it there to celebrate it without question, as if every bet were synonymous with vision. Its contribution lies in distinguishing between necessary risk and recklessness disguised as conviction; in asking incisive questions before facts impose them; in challenging assumptions, demanding sufficient information, and promoting a culture where timely warnings are not seen as brakes but as a contribution to sustainable success.

Corporate governance should be understood as a capacity to improve judgment, not merely to reinforce controls. Crossing the river remains part of the business task. What distinguishes a well-governed organization is not that it avoids all currents, but that it knows how to read them in time, measure their strength, and not confuse courage with suicide.
