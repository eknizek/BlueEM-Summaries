Mosaic halts fertilizer plants in Brazil following sulfur price surge

19 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

Mosaic will halt the production of approximately one million tons of phosphate in Brazil at a time when geopolitical risk in the Middle East has elevated prices of the key input. Mosaic will begin shutting down its facilities in Araxá and Patrocínio "as part of its efforts to reduce costs and reallocate capital," the company said in a statement on Tuesday (7). The company also plans to proceed with the sale of its assets in Araxá.

The decision comes amid a surge in sulfur prices, which is used in the production of phosphate fertilizer. Mosaic said in December that it would halt production at its Fospar and Araxá plants due to increased input costs. Sulfur prices were already elevated due to increased demand from metal mining. But the Middle East conflict has driven them even higher, with spot prices in Brazil rising 40% since the start of the war last month, according to Bloomberg Green Markets data through April 3.

Nearly half of global sulfur trade is vulnerable to disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz. Although tensions eased after the U.S. and Iran agreed Tuesday night to a two-week ceasefire, with Iran committing to reopening the Strait of Hormuz, market turbulence is likely to continue. Normalization of maritime transport could take months, and shipping companies still await proof that transit will remain safe, meaning sulfur shipment disruptions are "structural, not temporary," according to Manuja Pandey, who covers fertilizers at ICIS, a commodity pricing company.

Mosaic's closures in Brazil represent a setback for the country's efforts to reduce its dependence on foreign agricultural nutrients. The agricultural powerhouse imports the majority of its fertilizers, including more than three-quarters of its phosphate consumption.

Another phosphate producer, Itafos, halted new orders in Brazil last month for a week while assessing its sulfur costs. CEO David Delaney said Wednesday that the company remains "very cautious" and still expects sulfur prices to remain higher year-over-year.

Mosaic said that its shutdowns should have limited impact on profits, amid elevated sulfur costs. On Tuesday, Mosaic shares fell as much as 4.5% following news of the ceasefire, before reducing most of the losses.
