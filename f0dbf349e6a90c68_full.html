# Finance Minister Quiroz Defends Tax Adjustments to Offset Potential Fiscal Loss from Corporate Tax Cut

On Wednesday, Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz arrived at Congress with the responsibility of presenting the miscellaneous bill before a packed session of the Chamber's Finance Commission, where the initiative began its legislative process. But before that mission, the official held a press conference to clarify doubts and answer questions from accredited journalists.

Although he arrived at Congress confident that the Kast government's flagship project would not suffer the same fate as its predecessor—whose tax reform was blocked by the then-opposition, which rejected the idea of legislating—the minister appeared tense. In fact, the press meeting was postponed from 2:00 p.m., the time the ministry had called, to 3:30 p.m., because Quiroz was running late to Valparaíso.

During the conference, he began by expressing his hope that "tomorrow will be remembered as a before and after, in terms of our country's economic growth. And above all, job creation, in terms of overcoming our decline," and expressed his enthusiasm regarding the bill's legislative process, which he believes is well-designed.

He detailed that while the Finance Commission will be the primary one, the initiative will also be reviewed by the Environment and Labor commissions, in matters within their jurisdiction.

## The Minister's Aspirations

He did not hesitate to be transparent about his aspirations for the initiative's progress, explaining that he wants it to pass the Chamber by May 20 and be ready for promulgation in June, because "it is fundamental to begin the second half of the year with this project approved and the country moving forward."

Given that the project has utmost urgency, the Finance Minister noted that there has been "good scheduling" and emphasized that there has been good coordination and dialogue with other political sectors, especially mentioning the People's Party (PDG), with whom an agreement was sealed to provide votes for the legislative proposal, but also the libertarians who are not part—he recalled—of the ruling coalition. Regarding them, he pointed out that they have concerns about different topics that "we will be addressing along the way, some are in the project, others come later."

In fact, he stated that in the coming days, the government will work on topics related to the agreement with the PDG's technical teams and others, but clarified that the VAT return on medicines and diapers "is a bonus to people that is associated with the 19% VAT, but it is not a VAT reduction."

This is because "the project is now well-defined, addresses important issues, tackles our tax competitiveness problem, which is quite serious; and also the problem of our excess bureaucratic barriers to investment," he detailed.

An aspect that Quiroz highlighted of the initiative, which he would present to deputies in minutes, is that it involves "compensation with immediate reduction and, above all, future spending containment measures"; but also measures that allow better allocation of resources, he noted.

In this sense, he gave the example of the data crossing that the Internal Revenue Service (SII) will be able to conduct under the supervision of the Finance Ministry. On the other hand, he pointed to a spending adjustment—which he described as "superfluous and inefficient"—that is underway and that "also contributes to this balance, because this myth that every tax cut must be compensated with a raise is a myth that assumes all public spending is perfect. And because we think there is much public spending that is not perfect, there is even much that lends itself to abuse and fraud; on that side, adjustment also goes to the final balance," although he clarified that this "is not all the final balance, there are also spending containment measures in the initiative."

## "The Adjustment Significantly Exceeds the Deficit"

When asked how the net fiscal loss through 2031—since growth measures fail to compensate for tax reduction—converses with ministry cuts (or adjustment) and the objective of fiscal balance to reach a zero effect, Quiroz simply responded: "It converses perfectly, because the adjustment significantly exceeds the deficit."

He reinforced that "the small deficits that occur are more than compensated by the fiscal efforts we are making" in reference to the 3% adjustment being carried out across ministries, although he did not provide an approximate figure being considered for this item. He only indicated that complete fiscal projections will be known when the fiscal decree is released.

He only mentioned there will be an "additional adjustment" estimated at around US$1 billion and that "it is different for each ministry because they face different realities."

The minister's state of mind became evident when asked about the temporary VAT exemption for housing and whether its effect would translate to lower prices for those intending to buy a home or if it would only benefit real estate companies and construction firms.

For now, he emphasized that the benefit will only extend for 12 months, so it is well limited, and "afterwards, there must necessarily be a decrease in the cost and price of homes, with the reform being made to the General Urban Planning and Construction Ordinance; therefore, by pure market logic the price will fall," he explained.

But after a few seconds, he added: "We don't have a police force or a Dirinco watching what happens with prices. We are a different government," alluding to the Industry and Commerce Bureau created in 1960 and dependent on the Economy Ministry, responsible for overseeing, setting prices, receiving and investigating consumer complaints, and imposing sanctions.
