# Colombia-Venezuela Trade Aims for $1.6 Billion After New Caribbean Office Opening

The Colombian-Venezuelan Chamber announced the opening of its Caribbean Regional Office in Santa Marta on April 28, with the objective of strengthening binational trade. The initiative becomes key given the goal of reaching $1.6 billion in exchange this year. The announcement comes in a context of greater economic certainty in Venezuela and renewed business interest in resuming binational operations. Experts estimate that the neighboring country's GDP will grow more than 10% in 2026.

The new headquarters will operate from the Las Américas Free Trade Zone, a strategic enclave for international commerce. From there, business coordination, investment, and the development of new value chains between both countries will be facilitated. The opening will be accompanied by a high-level seminar with the participation of business people, authorities, and experts. The objective will be to analyze opportunities and challenges in binational integration amid a new economic phase. Topics to be discussed include banking correspondence and payment mechanisms in international operations. OFAC regulations and regulatory conditions will continue to be determining factors for the viability of binational businesses.

Venezuela's economic context shows reactivation signals driven by the growth of its exports and strategic sectors. Exports are expected to double in 2026, boosting key productive activities. This momentum will favor sectors such as gas, oil, mining, infrastructure, energy, and health. Sectoral diversification opens a range of opportunities for Colombian companies interested in investing or expanding in that market.

"The presence of the Colombian-Venezuelan Chamber in the Caribbean not only strengthens our institutional presence, but sends a clear message about the role that Santa Marta can play as a business hub in the Caribbean," said Germán Umaña Mendoza, president of the Chamber's board of directors.

The Colombian Caribbean positions itself as a strategic platform for economic integration thanks to its geographical proximity and port infrastructure. Cities such as Santa Marta, Barranquilla, and Riohacha lead this business dynamism. In 2025, this region represented 23% of exports to Venezuela, according to DANE. This weight in bilateral trade supports the decision to locate the new office in Santa Marta as a key point.

During the first two months of 2026, exports to Venezuela reached $132 million. Imports amounted to $11 million, reflecting a favorable trade balance for Colombia in this recent period. The main products exported include food, beverages, and tobacco at 28.7%. Chemical products also stand out at 22.7% and plastic materials at 11.9%, consolidating a diversified offering toward that market.

The Chamber seeks to have this office function as a comprehensive business services center focused on facilitating internationalization. Market information, regulatory advice, and support in foreign trade processes will be offered. It will also provide access to business networks in both countries and institutional support. The availability of updated information about the regulatory environment will be key to reducing risks and leveraging business opportunities.

Carlos Jaramillo Ríos, executive president of the Santa Marta Chamber of Commerce, said that "the opening of the Colombian-Venezuelan Chamber is a strategic step for the Colombian Caribbean, because it strengthens regional integration and opens new opportunities for our entrepreneurs in matters of commerce and investment."

The institutional scenario is also strengthened with the entry into force of the Agreement on Promotion and Reciprocal Protection of Investments in April. This instrument seeks to guarantee legal stability for investors in both countries. "This instrument has the fundamental purpose of promoting and facilitating direct investment between the two countries, guaranteeing a stable, transparent, and predictable legal environment," said Luis Felipe Quintero, executive president of the Colombian-Venezuelan Chamber.

The agreement contemplates the protection of new and existing investments, expanding business opportunities. This legal framework reinforces confidence to advance investment projects and consolidate sustainable economic relations between Colombia and Venezuela.

Venezuela's new economic phase is leading entrepreneurs to reconsider that market as a strategic destination for investment and as a supplier of goods. This change in perception is driving the reactivation of commercial contacts. The Colombian-Venezuelan Chamber seeks to consolidate itself as a business bridge in this economic reconfiguration. The opening in the Caribbean reflects a commitment to boost trade and investment at a time of binational transformation.
