# Café OMA and Presto advance in their reorganization: Supersociedades calls key hearing to define their future

The Café OMA and Presto chains are entering a decisive stage in their business reorganization processes. The Superintendency of Corporations has called for April 20, 2026, a confirmation hearing for the agreements presented by Restcafé S.A.S. and Franquicias y Concesiones S.A.S., in a move that could redefine their operations and position in Colombia's food market.

The convocation was formalized through Administrative Orders 2026-01-151067 and 2026-01-151056, with which the supervisory body advances in reviewing the agreements filed by the companies' promoter between March 31 and April 1. These documents have majority support from creditors: 82% in the case of Franquicias y Concesiones S.A.S. (Presto) and 76% for Restcafé S.A.S. (OMA).

The process develops in a context of high competition within the food sector, where brands seek to maintain operations, protect market share, and adjust their financial structures to continue operating under sustainable conditions.

## What is sought in this reorganization agreement?

The agreements presented by both companies aim to reorganize significant liabilities. In the case of Restcafé S.A.S., the figure approaches 114 billion pesos, while Franquicias y Concesiones S.A.S. projects the restructuring of approximately 104 billion. This volume of obligations reflects the financial challenges faced by these enterprises in an environment of constrained consumption and growing competition among food chains, both national and international.

In this scenario, reorganization becomes a tool to preserve operations and adjust payment conditions with creditors. The majority support of creditors for the agreements suggests interest in maintaining the continuity of both companies in the market. However, the process still depends on judicial validation, which will determine if the terms comply with established legal requirements.

## Key aspects of the evaluation

During the hearing scheduled for April 20, the bankruptcy judge will evaluate the legality of the agreements and verify key aspects before their eventual confirmation. Among them, the existence of obligations related to social security and mandatory withholdings, elements that must be current to advance in the process. This step is determinative, as approval of the agreements would allow the companies to continue their activities under new financial conditions. Otherwise, the process could take another direction within the insolvency regime.

The Superintendent of Corporations, Billy Escobar, highlighted the scope of such mechanisms within the business system. "This agreement is an example of how the business insolvency regime allows for the preservation of relevant productive units and the protection of employment, through tools that facilitate company recovery," he stated.

From the Superintendency of Corporations, the institutional focus regarding these processes was reiterated. "From the Superintendency of Corporations we reiterate our commitment to safeguarding the business fabric and promoting solutions that contribute to the country's economic stability," Escobar added.
