Small Fuel Station Operators Hit Hardest by Fuel Price Caps

27 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Small Fuel Station Operators Hit Hardest by Fuel Price Caps

The price cap on fuel is a policy that is putting small fuel station operators at risk, some of whom could close or stop selling diesel, generating shortages in some areas of the country. In an interview with Excélsior, sector representatives said that they have not been called to meetings with the Federal Executive, so they believe that only the position of large corporations or fuel station groups is being taken into account, leaving aside those who have a limited number of stations.

Salvador Jiménez, fuel station operator from Puebla and owner of three service stations, said that large business consortiums can compensate for losses from the price cap at some of their stations with those that do manage to be profitable. However, for the small owner, many of them with just a single station, it is unsustainable to maintain low prices. He explained that in Mexico, approximately 70% of service stations belong to a single business entity, which can be interpreted as single-station companies, meaning that the operators' assets are compromised due to the lack of profitability of diesel, which is a product that represents 30 to 35% of sales.

On the matter, Sergio Ramírez, fuel station operator from the state of Nayarit, said that the imposition of price caps on regular gasoline and diesel reduce profit margins, which could make it difficult to cover the basic operating expenses of service stations.

The current cost structure indicates that the acquisition price at Petróleos Mexicanos (Pemex) terminals is 26.19 pesos per liter, so the margin would be 1.81 pesos. However, this is not a gross profit, as fixed expenses must be deducted, which include fuel transportation, averaging between 50 and 60 cents per liter, and road losses or fuel theft, which ranges between 20 and 40 cents.

To these amounts are added items for payroll, insurance, facility maintenance, connectivity services, bank commissions, cleaning expenses, and regulatory costs that can represent up to 40 cents per liter of fuel sold.

The operators agreed that stopping buying and selling diesel is becoming a possibility for many stations, which would cause fuel shortages in some areas of the country.

Under Pressure

Adding to this situation is what operators consider as "pressure" to join the price agreement. If they do not, due to the economic and financial circumstances of each company, they are exposed through banners placed by the Federal Consumer Protection Agency (Profeco) that label them as abusive stations with high prices, which has a direct impact on their image and sales.

Additionally, some are also subject to exhaustive inspections by the Tax Administration Service (SAT) and the National Guard that can result in closures of up to six months.
