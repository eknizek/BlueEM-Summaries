# Small Gas Station Operators Hit Hardest by Fuel Price Controls

The fuel price cap is a policy that is putting small gas station operators at risk, with some potentially closing or ceasing diesel sales, which could generate shortages in some areas of the country. In an interview with Excélsior, sector representatives stated that so far they have not been invited to meetings with the Federal Executive, so they believe only the position of large corporations or gas station groups is being considered, leaving aside those with a limited number of stations.

Salvador Jiménez, a gas station entrepreneur from Puebla and owner of three service stations, said that large business consortiums can offset losses from the price cap at some of their stations with those that are still profitable. However, for the small owner, many of whom have just a single station, it is unsustainable to maintain low prices. He explained that in Mexico, approximately 70% of service stations belong to a single business entity, which can be interpreted as single-station companies, meaning that entrepreneurs' assets are compromised due to lack of diesel profitability, a product that represents 30 to 35% of sales.

On the subject, Sergio Ramírez, a gas station entrepreneur from the state of Nayarit, said that the imposition of price caps on regular gasoline and diesel reduce profit margins, which could make it difficult to cover basic operating expenses of service stations.

The current cost structure indicates that the acquisition price at Petróleos Mexicanos (Pemex) terminals is 26.19 pesos per liter, so the margin would be 1.81 pesos. However, this is not gross profit, as fixed expenses must be deducted including fuel transport, which averages between 50 and 60 cents per liter, and road losses or fuel theft, which range between 20 and 40 cents.

Added to these amounts are payroll, insurance, facility maintenance, connectivity services, banking commissions, cleaning expenses, and regulatory costs that can represent up to 40 cents per liter of fuel sold.

The entrepreneurs agreed that ceasing to buy and sell diesel is becoming a possibility for many stations, which would cause fuel shortages in some areas of the country.

Under Pressure

This situation is compounded by what entrepreneurs consider "pressure" to join the price agreement, as failure to do so, due to the economic and financial circumstances of each company, results in them being publicly criticized through banners placed by the Federal Consumer Protection Agency (Profeco), labeling them as abusive stations with high prices, which has a direct impact on their image and sales.

Furthermore, some are also subject to exhaustive inspections by the Tax Administration Service (SAT) and the National Guard that can result in closures of up to six months.
