Diesel Price Falls R$0.20 Per Liter in Two Weeks

27 April 2026 · 10:05 PM

# Diesel Price Falls R$0.20 Per Liter in Two Weeks

The price of S-10 diesel at Brazilian gas stations fell for the second consecutive week after reaching its peak for the year in early April, according to the weekly survey by ANP (National Agency of Petroleum, Natural Gas and Biofuels). Market sources say the drop reflects the pass-through by importers of cheaper diesel purchased abroad, and that the fuel subsidy created by President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) to address the impacts of the war in Iran has not yet had a major impact.

According to ANP, Brazilian gas stations sold S-10 diesel on average at R$7.38 per liter last week. This represents a drop of R$0.11 per liter compared to the previous week. Over two weeks accumulated, the decline is R$0.20 per liter.

The price is still well above the R$6.10 per liter that was in effect in the week before the first attacks by Israel and the United States on Iran, which led to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, the route through which one-fifth of global oil production passes.

The surge in prices following the start of the war raised concerns in the government, which announced a series of measures to try to contain the increase and its possible impacts on voter sentiment ahead of the presidential election. In March, Lula announced an exemption of federal taxes worth R$0.32 per liter and a subsidy of R$0.32 per liter for producers and importers. In April, with state support, he increased the subsidy to R$1.52 per liter.

The program, however, was slow to take off. In the first period, which ended in March, it attracted the two largest producers — Petrobras and Mataripe Refinery — but was not considered attractive by major importers and distributors such as Vibra, Ipiranga, and Raízen. Vibra Energia, the country's largest distributor, only decided to join the subsidy on April 10, after the government expanded the benefit from R$0.32 to R$1.52 per liter. In 2025, the company was responsible for 20% of Brazilian private imports. Raízen and Ipiranga, however, remain out.

According to ANP, nine other companies also qualified to receive the subsidy in the second period, which began in early April, including Vibra and mid-sized importers such as Temape (Maritime Terminals of Pernambuco).

The drop in diesel prices at the pump occurs in parallel with a reduction in the selling price by producers and importers: according to ANP, these suppliers reduced their prices by R$0.18 per liter over the last two weeks. Since there was no drop at Petrobras refineries, this movement reflects decisions by private companies.

"We are seeing very high volatility in prices," says Sergio Araújo, president of Abicom (Brazilian Association of Fuel Importers). He states that he does not yet see effects of the subsidy on pump prices. "Few agents joined the first period. And there is still no date for payment of these amounts to these companies," he argues.

The initial expectation was that the first period of the subsidy would be paid to producers and importers by April 15, but a provisional measure from the government extended the deadline to the end of the month. ANP has not reported how much it will have to pay.

Last Friday (24), the agency approved a change in the price ceiling for refineries that produce diesel from domestic oil, a category that currently is limited to Petrobras. The change raises this value by approximately R$0.05 per liter.

Petrobras promoted an adjustment in the diesel price at its refineries shortly after the announcement of the first subsidy in March. At that time, the government also announced an exemption from federal taxes on the fuel, which totaled R$0.32 per liter. The increase at the state-owned company's refineries was R$0.38 per liter. The company's president, Magda Chambriard, said at the time that without the subsidy, she would have to raise the product by R$0.70 per liter.
