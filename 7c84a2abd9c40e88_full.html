# U.S. Commerce Office Report Keeps Brazil on 'Watchlist'

The U.S. Trade Representative's Office (USTR) released its 2026 Special 301 Report on Thursday, April 30, analyzing the global situation regarding the protection and enforcement of intellectual property rights among U.S. trading partners.

The USTR announcement states that more than one hundred trading partners were evaluated and that Vietnam was identified as a "Priority Foreign Country." This designation means the office will decide within 30 days whether to initiate an investigation based on Section 301 regarding Vietnam's intellectual property practices. Section 301 of the 1974 Trade Act authorizes the U.S. government to investigate and adopt measures such as tariffs or trade sanctions against countries accused of unfair practices.

Among the classification changes in the 2026 edition, six countries were placed on the Priority Watch List (Chile, China, India, Indonesia, Russia, and Venezuela), while 19 trading partners are placed on the Watch List, including Brazil. Argentina and Mexico moved from the "Priority Watch List" to the "Watch List," which according to the report is due to relevant improvements in their intellectual property policies.

Regarding Brazil, the report states that the U.S. maintains a long-standing concern about "importation, distribution, sale, and widespread use of counterfeit goods," such as gaming consoles and illicit streaming services. It mentions signal diversion in streaming services and the circulation of counterfeit products on Rua 25 de Março, a traditional popular commerce center in São Paulo.

"Despite police raids and large-scale seizures, the Rua 25 de Março region continues to be one of the largest markets for counterfeit products, partly because operations are not followed by deterrent penalties," the report states.

"The Port of Santos, which is the busiest container port in Latin America, and the Triple Border region between Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina also continue to be significant entry points for counterfeit products."

In the document, the U.S. encourages Brazil to join, as quickly as possible, the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO), the Treaty on Interpretations and Performances and Phonograms, and the Copyright Treaty. Strong protection of copyright represents a crucial incentive for investment in innovation in the country, the report notes.

"The United States urges Brazil to ensure transparency and due process in the protection of geographical indications (GIs), and to ensure that the granting of GI protection does not deprive interested parties of the ability to use common names, particularly as Brazil advances with the EU-Mercosur Trade Agreement," the document states.
