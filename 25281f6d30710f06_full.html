# Backed by Galapagos, Alvorada expands bet on Venezuelan oil

A Brazilian oil drilling company has decided to expand its operations in Venezuela, betting that sanctions relief will stimulate the country's battered industry, highlighting regional appetite to participate in a U.S.-led recovery.

Alvorada Heavy Industries, backed by Galapagos Capital — an asset manager founded by a former BTG Pactual partner — will expand production across three blocks on the northeastern edge of the vast Orinoco oil belt and is negotiating to assume additional areas, said board chairman Paulo Buzanelli in an interview with Bloomberg News.

The company is also evaluating opportunities in oil services, logistics, and processing. "We are entering a new phase," Buzanelli said by phone from Caracas, Venezuela's capital, where he was on a business trip. "We are in a structured expansion, at a larger scale."

Founded in 2023, Alvorada is part of a group of Brazilian oil companies positioned to grow in Venezuela following the capture of leader Nicolás Maduro by the United States in January and the subsequent easing of sanctions.

Venezuela's vast oil and gas reserves offer potential opportunities for independent Brazilian companies that have struggled to expand in the domestic market after state-owned Petrobras reduced asset sales under President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's administration.

Alvorada produces roughly 4,000 barrels per day across its three Venezuelan blocks and plans to reach 20,000 barrels daily within two years. Unlike most of Orinoco's extra-heavy oil, the oil produced is medium quality, which allows it to dilute heavier types from other projects to facilitate pipeline transportation, Buzanelli said.

Another Venezuelan project under evaluation by Alvorada could raise total production to 30,000 barrels per day within months.

The company is negotiating with other international investors besides Galapagos, which manages approximately $4.2 billion and was founded in 2019 by Carlos Fonseca, former head of private equity at BTG.

Although years of neglect and mismanagement limit Venezuela's production potential in the short term, the sector's recovery has gained new relevance as war with Iran drives up international oil prices and pressures global supply. On April 12, Donald Trump suggested that China could send oil tankers to Venezuela after the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Buzanelli was already betting on a Venezuelan recovery when Alvorada acquired the production contracts three years ago. "We understood there could be an opening similar to what happened after the fall of the Soviet Union," he said. "What we are seeing today is the materialization of that thesis."
