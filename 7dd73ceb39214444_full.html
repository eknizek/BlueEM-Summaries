# Buenos Aires municipality audit reveals only half of road maintenance budget spent

The Buenos Aires municipality of Baradero recently disclosed the budgeted income and funds invested for maintaining rural roads in the district, revealing, according to the local Rural Society's lawyer, that it did not allocate even half of the money received for this purpose.

The figures were disclosed following a ruling by the Administrative Court of Appeals of San Nicolás, based on a lawsuit filed by the rural organization. According to Pablo Torres Barthe, lawyer for the Rural Society of Baradero, the figures presented by the municipal government—led by Union for the Homeland (UxP) mayor Esteban Sanzio—show that during 2025, the municipality budgeted approximately $1.1 billion in revenue from road maintenance taxes and provincial allocation for maintaining rural roads, of which it spent nearly $320 million through October.

The municipality allocated less than 40% of collected funds to rural road maintenance. The budget details are divided into two categories: funds received from the province through tax revenue sharing and funds collected directly from the road maintenance tax. According to the budget, through the Rural Real Estate Tax collected by the province, Baradero would receive $453.8 million and $106 million in two concepts, while income from the "Conservation and improvement of the municipal road network" (road tax) would total nearly $555 million.

However, examining the "Summary of Annual Expenses" reported by the municipality, it shows that through October 31, spending on road maintenance was just over $318 million, which according to Torres Barthe's projections could reach $450 million including the final quarter.

"If you plan to collect $500 million and receive $500 million more from the province for rural roads and you inform me that you spent $320 million through October 31, 2025, unless you made a sublime investment in the last quarter, the numbers don't add up," said the lawyer who handles similar cases in Henderson, Azul, General Alvarado, and Necochea, among other districts.

According to his analysis, "what's happening here is that they only use funds for rural roads from what they receive from the province, but the road tax revenue goes to general expenses, surely."

Although these are preliminary figures, Torres Barthe indicated they will now focus on "the fine details: budget and expense sheets we need to review. But based on what the municipality officially reports and the municipal ordinance budget, the numbers don't add up. We'll also request that they report the complete 2025 figures so we can compare actual income versus allocated spending."

"Now that we have the official numbers the municipality reports, we'll audit them and if there's any misappropriation, we'll request the tax refund and, if applicable, we'll file a criminal complaint, because the tax is a specific allocation required by the municipality itself, which tells you: 'I'll charge you this tax to do this,' but then charges the tax and doesn't do it," concluded Torres Barthe.

For Pablo Roller, president of the Rural Society of Baradero, "even if we accept the numbers they're giving, which aren't real, of the $1.1 billion that entered the municipality and should have been allocated to the road network, at best it was $450 million."

"Expenses don't even reach 40% of what was collected from the tax and received by provincial law, allocated to rural roads. However, they're throwing in urban roads and other things, but we need to look at it in more detail, open that information and get to work," Roller concluded.
