# Property Tax Blockades: How Up-to-Date is Colombia's Cadastral Registry

The protests over property tax increases occurring in different departments across the country stem from the cadastral update process carried out by the Gustavo Petro Government.

One of the most ambitious goals set by the Executive upon taking office was to increase property registry updates from a mere 9 percent to 70 percent in just four years of government. This task involved thoroughly mapping properties in remote regions such as Amazonas, Guainía, and Guaviare for the first time, as well as reaching municipalities across all departments that had gone more than 30 years without updates.

Leading this process has been the country's highest cadastral authority, the Instituto Geográfico Agustín Codazzi (Igac), which has been responsible for assessing the cadastral value of different properties, along with more than 40 different managers, such as the Special Administrative Unit for District Cadastre in Bogotá.

## More Than 75 Million Hectares Updated

In total, according to the latest official information, more than 75 million hectares have been updated, meaning 859 municipalities have reduced decades of backlog in their land values. On one hand, the country has 326 municipalities with updated multi-purpose cadastral information (44 million hectares), and on the other, 568 others result from the automatic massive adjustment of cadastral values provided for in the National Development Plan.

The latter, established by Article 49 of said Plan, is an exceptional measure that allows reducing the backlog in land values in territories with up to 32 years without updated information. In 2025, 48 municipalities were updated through this mechanism, and this year another 520 from departments such as Santander, North Santander, Boyacá, Casanare, Caldas, Tolima, Cauca, Huila, Valle del Cauca, Nariño, Bolívar, Atlántico, Magdalena, Cesar, and La Guajira, among others.

It should be noted that most of the recent protests are due to this increase.

## Why the Protests?

Citizens whose cadastral information has been updated now must pay higher taxes since the appraisal is the key input that municipalities use to establish property taxes, applying the rates defined by different municipal councils.

However, the Government asserts that Law 1955 of 2019 stipulates that property tax cannot increase more than 50 percent compared to the previous year for rural properties smaller than 100 hectares. This means that if a person paid 50,000 pesos in 2025, they cannot pay more than 75,000 the following year.

"98.6 percent of properties with an extension of less than 100 hectares are within the maximum limit of the 50 percent increase compared to what was paid in the previous year," they indicate.

Even so, in dialogue processes carried out between citizens, the Interior Ministry, and Igac, it has been stated that cases where exaggerated increases are indeed contemplated will be reviewed.
