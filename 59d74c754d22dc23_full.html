Property tax blockades: How up-to-date is Colombia's cadastral information?

16 April 2026 · 11:11 PM

# Property Tax Blockades: How Up-to-Date is Colombia's Cadastral Information?

The protests over increases in property tax that are occurring in different departments across the country stem from the cadastral update process being carried out by Gustavo Petro's Government.

One of the most ambitious goals the Executive set when taking office was to increase the country's property updates from a paltry 9 percent to 70 percent in just four years of government. This task involved thoroughly knowing, for the first time, properties in remote regions such as Amazonas, Guainía, or Guaviare, as well as reaching municipalities in all departments that had gone more than 30 years without updates.

Behind this process has been the country's top cadastral authority, the Agustín Codazzi Geographic Institute (Igac), which has been responsible for conducting cadastral appraisals of different properties, along with more than 40 different managers, such as the Special Administrative Unit for Cadastral District of Bogotá.

## Over 75 Million Hectares Updated

According to the latest official information, more than 75 million hectares have already been updated, meaning 859 municipalities have reduced decades of backlog in their land values. On one hand, the country has 326 municipalities with updated multipurpose cadastral information (44 million hectares), and on the other, another 568 are the result of automatic massive cadastral value adjustments provided for in the National Development Plan.

The latter, established by article 49 of the National Development Plan, is an exceptional measure that allows reducing the backlog in land values in territories with up to 32 years without updated information. In 2025, 48 municipalities were updated through this mechanism, and this year another 520 municipalities from departments such as Santander, Norte de Santander, Boyacá, Casanare, Caldas, Tolima, Cauca, Huila, Valle del Cauca, Nariño, Bolívar, Atlántico, Magdalena, Cesar, and La Guajira, among others.

It should be noted that most of the recent protests are due to this increase.

## Why the Protests?

Citizens whose cadastral information has been updated must now pay higher taxes since the appraisal is the key input for municipalities to establish property taxes, applying the rates defined by different municipal councils. However, the Government assures that Law 1955 of 2019 indicates that property tax cannot increase more than 50 percent compared to the previous year for rural properties smaller than 100 hectares, meaning that if a person paid 50,000 pesos in 2025, next year they cannot pay more than 75,000.

"98.6 percent of properties, having an extension smaller than 100 hectares, are within the maximum limit of a 50 percent increase compared to what was paid in the previous year," they state.

Nevertheless, in dialogue processes that have taken place between citizens, the Ministry of the Interior, and Igac, it has been indicated that cases will be reviewed where exaggerated increases are indeed being considered.
