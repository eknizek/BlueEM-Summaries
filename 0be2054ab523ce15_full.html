# Government Seeks Alternatives for Training Continuation After Sence Franchise Elimination

On Wednesday, the José Antonio Kast government officially announced the decision to eliminate the tax deduction for the National Training and Employment Service (Sence) — a tax incentive granted by the State that allows companies to deduct training-related expenses from their tax payments, up to 1% of the Annual Payroll of Taxable Remuneration — which, according to data from the Ministry of Finance, has an annual fiscal cost of $300 billion.

This measure seeks to reorder public spending, with the Executive arguing that the decision is based on the absence of conclusive evidence regarding its impact on improving training.

## What Comes After Its Elimination?

This is a question that several actors related to the training world have raised, as the franchise is practically the main source of financing for labor training. Therefore, the government, particularly through the Ministry of Labor, is evaluating mechanisms to ensure continuity of social training programs currently financed by the franchise.

Sources close to the ministry acknowledged that the decision to eliminate the franchise was complex. "The President has been clear that difficult decisions would be made, and this is one of them, given all it entails," commented an insider.

The ministry emphasized that it is clear about the need for more and better training, focused on employability, especially in a context of technological changes and a challenging labor market. Therefore, regardless of the legislative process outcome, the ministry is working to strengthen and expand labor intermediation and insertion programs, making better use of available resources — such as those from the Solidarity Unemployment Fund — which are currently underutilized according to experts.

This instrument includes labor qualification programs, such as labor competency assessment, competency certification, labor training, and even labor intermediation. In parallel, there is a willingness to promote a new public-private training scheme focused on measurable results in employability and wages.

## Modifications to the System

Although the decision to eliminate the franchise may be radical, at least six public and non-public commissions and institutions have evaluated the program and unanimously concluded that it does not meet its objectives and presents major inefficiencies and transparency shortcomings.

One of the most important was the Commission Reviewing the Training and Labor Intermediation System, which stated in its 2011 report that there are no "positive effects of training via tax deduction on worker employability, and only very marginal effects on wages."

Later, in 2018, the National Productivity Commission detected that 90% of training executed via tax deduction lasts less than 40 hours, and noted that the only courses reporting positive effects on wages are those lasting more than 280 hours, but these represent less than 1% of the offering. More recently, in 2025, the Budget Directorate (Dipres) found that the tool is used almost exclusively by large companies, from which 88% of trained workers come. Ten percent corresponds to medium-sized firms and barely 2% to small ones, with a minimal percentage of trained workers from microenterprises. Additionally, it did not observe a significant effect on wages, employment, or employment intensity in any of the years analyzed.
