# Government Seeks Alternatives for Training Continuity After Elimination of Sence Franchise

On Wednesday, the government of José Antonio Kast officially formalized the decision to eliminate the tax deduction for the National Training and Employment Service (Sence) — a tax incentive provided by the State that allows companies to deduct training-related expenses from their tax payments, up to 1% of the Annual Payroll of Taxable Remuneration — which, according to data from the Ministry of Finance, has an annual fiscal cost of $300 billion.

A measure with which the Executive seeks to reorganize public spending, arguing that the decision is based on the absence of conclusive evidence about its impact on improving training.

After its elimination, what comes next? A question that several actors involved in the training sector have raised, since the franchise is practically the main source of financing for labor training. This is why the government, particularly through the Ministry of Labor, is evaluating mechanisms to ensure continuity of social training programs that are currently financed through the franchise.

Sources close to the ministry acknowledged that the decision to eliminate the franchise was complex. "The President has been clear that difficult decisions would be made, and this is one of them, given all that it entails," commented a knowledgeable source.

The ministry emphasized that it clearly understands the need for more and better training, focused on employability, especially in a context of technological changes and a challenging labor market. Therefore, regardless of the outcome of the legislative process, the ministry is working to strengthen and expand labor mediation and placement programs, making better use of available resources — such as those from the Solidarity Unemployment Fund — which are currently underutilized according to experts.

This instrument includes labor qualification programs, such as labor competency assessment, competency certification, labor training, and even labor intermediation. In parallel, there is a willingness to promote a new public-private training scheme, focused on measurable results in employability and wages.

## Modifications to the System

Although the decision to eliminate the franchise may be radical, at least six public and private commissions and institutions have evaluated the program and unanimously concluded that it fails to meet its objectives, presents major inefficiencies, and lacks transparency.

One of the main commissions was the Review Commission of the Training and Labor Intermediation System, which stated in its 2011 report that there are "no positive effects of training via tax deduction on worker employability, and only very marginal effects on wages."

Later, in 2018, the National Productivity Commission detected that 90% of training programs executed via tax deduction last less than 40 hours, and noted that the only courses that report positive effects on wages are those lasting more than 280 hours, but they account for less than 1% of the supply.

More recently, in 2025, the Budget Directorate (Dipres) found that the tool is used almost exclusively by large companies, which account for 88% of those trained. 10% corresponds to medium-sized firms and barely 2% to small businesses, with a minimal percentage of those trained from microenterprises. Additionally, it observed no significant effect on wages, employment, or employment intensity in any of the years analyzed.
