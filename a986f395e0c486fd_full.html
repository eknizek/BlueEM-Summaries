# Funds Race to Attract Retail Investors to SpaceX Ahead of Potential IPO

Retail investors eager to gain exposure to companies like SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic PBC are rapidly buying stakes in a series of funds that invest in the most attractive names in private markets.

One of the closed-end funds skyrocketed last month to reach nearly 3,000% of the value of its underlying assets, amid enthusiasm for these tech firms, creating a dynamic similar to the meme stock era. For sellers, the window of opportunity is limited. With these companies rapidly approaching initial public offerings, the exclusivity factor could soon disappear.

This phenomenon reflects a speculative boom in private markets, where increasingly more retail investors are entering complex and illiquid assets without fully understanding the risks. The situation raises concerns amid broader volatility in private markets, where doubts about valuations have affected investors and some funds have had to restrict withdrawals.

Funds like Fundrise Innovation Fund LLC and Robinhood Ventures Fund I offer access to companies traditionally reserved for venture capital and institutional investors. They claim to be democratizing markets by opening previously restricted opportunities. Powerlaw Corp., another fund, also plans to go public and is awaiting SEC approval. These closed-end funds hold stakes in major tech companies and allow retail investors to participate by buying shares when they hit the market.

"It makes sense for funds to go public before the companies themselves," said Owen Lamont, portfolio manager at Acadian Asset Management. "The right time is when the underlying asset is scarce, not when it's abundant."

Some funds present themselves as venture capital for the public market. However, instead of betting on early-stage companies, they bundle shares of companies in advanced stages before their public debut. The average cost, including fees, for four funds offering access to major tech companies is 3.6%.

The sharp increase in these fund prices has raised concerns about excessive valuations and whether investors are overpaying for indirect exposure to high-profile companies. Retail investors also face counterparty and execution risks. Some stakes are acquired through special purpose vehicles or futures contracts. OpenAI has criticized these structures and warned it could invalidate them or cancel the underlying shares.

In closed-end funds, a key metric is net asset value. Since the number of shares is fixed, these funds can trade at premiums or discounts to that value. At one point, Fundrise's Innovation Fund traded at nearly 30 times its net value. If that multiple applied to its investments, it would imply extreme valuations, such as around $40 trillion for SpaceX, based on $1.25 trillion estimates.

"It's mathematically possible that SpaceX's stock price could rise enough to justify that premium, but it's highly unlikely," said Lamont.

Fundrise, which previously focused on real estate, claims its mission is to democratize private markets. Its CEO, Ben Miller, has defended this vision, though some investors expressed doubts before the fund's public launch. Fundrise shares began trading at $31.25 on March 19 and reached a high of $575 days later, implying a premium of nearly 3,000%. They later fell following reports questioning these valuations. They currently trade around $85.

Analysts warn that these types of investments may depend on new buyers willing to pay even higher prices.

Robinhood Ventures also went public in March, though with more moderate premiums around 47%. Destiny Tech100, launched in 2024, also registered elevated premiums in its early weeks.

In some cases, access to companies like OpenAI has limitations. While some institutional investors own direct shares, other funds use intermediary structures such as special vehicles or futures contracts. At least 42% of Fundrise's investments are channeled through these vehicles, while for Powerlaw the proportion reaches 86%. Robinhood Ventures claims to have acquired shares directly or through secondary transactions.

OpenAI indicated it does not endorse these structures and warned they could invalidate rights over underlying shares. "These are funds that claim to have legal contracts over underlying shares, while issuers dispute this," Lamont concluded.
