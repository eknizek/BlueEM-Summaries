# "Extreme Gravity": Sugar Industry Warns of Government Natural Gas Supply Regulation During Harvest Season

The Argentine Sugar Center has sounded an alarm with the National Energy Secretariat over recent changes in natural gas supply regulations: the new scheme puts the normal development of the harvest and the continuity of activity in the NOA (Northwestern Argentina) at risk. In a letter signed by its president, Jorge Feijóo, addressed to Secretary María Carmen Tettamanti, the entity expressed the "profound concern of the sugar industry" over the impact of modifications to both available volumes and gas prices, and requested that access to the fluid be guaranteed "under supply and price conditions similar to those of last year."

According to the statement, this is a seasonal activity tied to the sugar cane production cycle, whose harvest extends between April and November, coinciding with the months of highest gas demand, and cannot be rescheduled. Added to this is its energy-intensive nature, where natural gas constitutes a critical input for the operation of sugar mills.

In that context, the CAA warned that potential supply interruptions or the inability to face significantly higher prices than the previous year could directly result in the paralysis of activity. The situation is aggravated because the current campaign already presents delays of between 15 and 30 days due to intense rainfall recorded in Tucumán, which made it difficult for machinery to enter the fields and delayed the effective start of harvesting and grinding.

In line with this assessment, Sergio Fara, a representative of the Union of Independent Sugarcane Growers of Tucumán, explained that "with excess water, machinery cannot enter the field, therefore the harvest is delayed." Although the traditional mass marking the start of the harvest was held in the town of La Florida, which symbolically marks the beginning of grinding, the pace of activity has not normalized due to climate conditions.

The energy scenario, however, is the factor that most concerns the sector. The CAA warned that if supply restrictions or abrupt cost increases are implemented, the consequences would be of "extreme gravity": from unharvested sugarcane to a decline in production destined for the domestic market estimated at 1.3 million tons, along with an impact on exports of 600,000 tons and on bioethanol production (around 625,000 cubic meters), key for blending with gasoline. This situation would also have direct effects on employment, the value chain, and regional economies of the NOA, highly dependent on sugar industry activity.

In that sense, the entity recalled the "historic inequality" in energy infrastructure between regions of the country, which places northern Argentina in a situation of structural disadvantage.

The sugar sector's complaints are part of a broader context of changes in the gas transport and supply system, formalized through Resolution 66/2026. The regulation recognizes a structural shift in the energy matrix, with the growing prominence of Vaca Muerta and the Neuquén Basin as main supply sources, to the detriment of northern basins. This change implies a reduction in gas availability in the NOA, resulting from the decline of the Northwestern Basin, the fall in shipments from the Plurinational State of Bolivia, and to a lesser extent, from the Austral Basin. This is compounded by growing dependence on Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) imports to cover winter demand, at significantly higher costs.

In parallel, the Industrial Union of Tucumán had already warned through a letter to Tucumán Governor Osvaldo Jaldo that the reconfiguration of the transport system implies a 35% reduction in firm capacity available to industries in the province, prioritizing residential supply and leaving the productive sector exposed to restrictions during winter. Additionally, the alternative of supplying regasified LNG presents prices that far exceed conventional gas rates (more than six times the cost of unsubsidized residential supply), making it unviable for many industrial activities.

In the medium term, distributor Naturgy NOA (the provider for that region) indicated that Transportadora de Gas del Sur S.A. plans to expand transport capacity beginning in 2027, including works on the Perito Francisco Pascasio Moreno Pipeline. However, the sector warns that 2026 will be a critical transition year.
