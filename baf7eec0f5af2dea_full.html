# The Legal Arsenal That Private Pension Funds Are Preparing to Defend $25 Billion in Retirement Savings

Behind closed doors, private pension funds began to outline a strategy to halt the decree ordering the transfer of billions to Colpensiones.

**By Carlos Arturo García Mahecha, Economic Journalist**

April 26, 2026

The meeting was not on the agenda of this year's 19th Annual Congress of Asofondos. It took place behind closed doors, in one of the private rooms of the gathering that concludes this Friday in Cartagena, while outside technical presentations and public panels continued. Inside, however, the tone was different: the presidents of private pension funds (AFP) and the top of the industry discussed how to respond to a decision they already considered inevitable. It was not a surprise. For more than three months, the market had been following the signals: drafts, warnings, speeches. The Government was preparing a decree to force the transfer of savings from those who moved to Colpensiones during the window of opportunity opened by pension reform, even under scrutiny by the Constitutional Court.

But when Decree 0415 finally arrived, it came without nuance. Without substantive adjustments. Without changes to what had already been announced. And, above all, with an order difficult to execute: the transfer of nearly 25 trillion pesos—resources currently invested—in a matter of days.

According to one of the meeting attendees, the discussion in that room was not whether the decree was convenient. It was what to do about it. The conclusion was unanimous: there will be a battle.

"It is up to us to do everything necessary to protect retirement savings… we will spare no effort to defend these resources," said Andrés Velasco, president of Asofondos.

This is not merely an industry position. It is the definition of a strategy loaded with legal actions, intervention before higher courts, and an institutional front to stop what they consider a decision that breaks the system's rules. Because, fundamentally, the debate is not only legal or financial. It is structural, the industry leaders insisted.

**Money Invested**

To understand the scale of the problem, one must start with the basics: these resources are not sitting in an account waiting to be transferred. The pension system based on savings works precisely because the money is invested. It moves. It multiplies over time. Today, the funds manage more than 540 trillion pesos, of which nearly 70 percent are accumulated returns.

But the decree interrupts this cycle, since it requires transferring resources that are in complex portfolios, such as public debt securities, infrastructure investments, and international assets. "Dismantling them in weeks implies assuming losses or, at best, transferring risks," Asofondos stated.

Miguel Largacha, president of Porvenir, raised it with a warning that is repeated throughout the sector as a diagnosis: "If we had to liquidate 25 trillion pesos… prices will fall dramatically, damaging public debt instruments and the market in Colombia."

However, the Government foresaw a partial solution by allowing part of it to be delivered in securities. But not everything can move that way. The rest would have to be sold—30 percent of that portfolio.

**Breaking Point**

Until now, the rule was clear: resources are transferred when a person retires. However, the decree advances that moment. For the funds, this is not merely an operational detail. They explain that it is a fundamental change, insofar as it converts long-term savings into short-term liquidity, a change that has implications far beyond the specific transfer.
