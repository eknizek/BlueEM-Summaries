# Private Pension Funds (AFP) Prepare Legal Strategy to Defend $25 Billion in Pension Savings

Behind closed doors, private pension funds began to outline a strategy to halt the decree ordering the transfer of billions to Colpensiones. Andrés Mauricio Velasco, president of Asofondos and Santiago García, president of the Board of Directors of the industry group and AFP Skandia.

The meeting was not on the agenda of this year's 19th Annual Congress of Asofondos. It took place behind closed doors, in one of the private rooms of the gathering that concludes this Friday in Cartagena, while outside technical presentations and public panels continued. Inside, however, the tone was different: the presidents of the private pension funds (AFP) and the leadership of the industry group discussed how to respond to a decision they already considered inevitable. It was not a surprise. For more than three months, the market had been following the signals: drafts, warnings, speeches. The Government was preparing a decree to force the transfer of savings from those who moved to Colpensiones during the opportunity window opened by the pension reform, still under Constitutional Court scrutiny.

But when Decree 0415 finally arrived, it came without nuances. Without substantive adjustments. Without changes to what had already been announced. And, above all, with an order difficult to execute: the transfer of approximately 25 trillion pesos—resources that are currently invested—in a matter of days. According to one of the meeting attendees, the discussion in that room was not whether the decree was convenient. It was what to do about it. The conclusion was unanimous: there will be a battle.

"It is up to us to do everything that corresponds to protect pension savings… we will not spare efforts to defend those resources," said Andrés Velasco, president of Asofondos.

It is not just an industry position. It is the definition of a strategy loaded with legal actions, intervention before high courts, and an institutional front to stop what they consider a decision that breaks the system's rules. Because, fundamentally, the debate is not only legal or financial. It is structural, industry executives insisted.

## Invested Money

To understand the dimension of the problem, you have to start with the basics: those resources are not in an account waiting to be transferred. The pension system based on savings works precisely because the money is invested. It moves. It multiplies over time. Today, the funds manage more than 540 trillion pesos, of which about 70 percent are accumulated returns.

But the decree interrupts that cycle, insofar as it forces the transfer of resources that are in complex portfolios, such as public debt securities, infrastructure investments, and international assets. "Dismantling them in weeks implies assuming losses or, at best, transferring risks," noted Asofondos.

Miguel Largacha, president of Porvenir, raised it with a warning that is repeated throughout the sector as a diagnosis: "If we had to liquidate 25 trillion pesos… prices will fall hugely, damaging public debt instruments and the market in Colombia."

However, the Government foresaw a partial solution: allowing part to be delivered in securities. But not everything can move that way. The rest would have to be sold, about 30 percent of that portfolio.

And that is where the technical problem becomes a systemic problem.

## Breaking Point

Until now, the rule was clear: resources are transferred when the person retires. However, the decree advances that moment. For the funds, this is not an operational detail. They explain that it is a fundamental change, insofar as it converts long-term savings into short-term liquidity, a change that has implications that go beyond the specific transfer.
