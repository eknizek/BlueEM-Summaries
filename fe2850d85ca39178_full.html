# Indecopi: Chasing Mice or Ghosts?

By Alejandro Falla

Amid so much electoral news, an investigation launched by Indecopi went unnoticed—one concerning an alleged agreement between competitors to manipulate product prices in an interior region. This process is just beginning. There is still much ground to cover, and we must presume the innocence of those involved. Only at the end will we know if this complaint has any merit. We will have to wait.

The resumption of investigative activities of this kind by Indecopi is positive. Since the implementation of the business concentration control regime (2021), there has been a clear reduction in the number and importance of investigations into collusion. With limited resources, Indecopi's activities have focused on reviewing concentration operations that, in the vast majority of cases, pose no risk to competition. Since the regime began, 93% of notified operations were approved outright as they posed no risks. They generated no problems whatsoever.

One of the operations that was conditioned due to posing a competition risk never materialized (KKR/Telefónica/Entel operation). In this latter case, the investment of public resources spent reviewing an operation that never left the drawing board was thrown in the trash. Pointless spending chasing mice or mere ghosts.

This neglect of cartel investigations over recent years has generated a decline in the perception of Indecopi's effectiveness in detecting agreements between competitors, at least in the eyes of those who closely follow its activities. According to results from a survey conducted by the Competition Center (CECO) of Adolfo Ibáñez University (Chile) on the perception of free competition institutions in Peru, there has been a constant decline over the last four years in the perception of its effectiveness in detecting such conduct. This is serious.

These types of agreements not only harm consumers but also call into question the free market's capacity to generate well-being for people. The social upheaval in Chile (2019), which ended in questioning its economic model and initiated a constitutional reform process, was fueled by prior revelations of collusive agreements in markets very close to ordinary people (toilet paper, pharmacies, and supermarkets).

An ineffective Indecopi against collusive conduct delegitimizes the economic model of the Constitution and plays into the hands of those who want to modify it—who, judging by election results, are not few. The resumption of these activities is positive. But a headless Indecopi without leadership will not go far with these initiatives.

Over the last six years, it has changed hands several times (5 in total). Its current president allegedly resigned; speculation circulates about his replacement. Do the candidates still in the race want an Indecopi chasing mice or ghosts? Will they strengthen its independence, or do they want it weak so they can manipulate it to their liking? The bets are on!
