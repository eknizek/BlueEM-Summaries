# Indecopi: Chasing Mice and Ghosts?

By Alejandro Falla

Amid so much electoral news, an investigation launched by Indecopi went unnoticed—an inquiry into an alleged agreement between competitors to manipulate product prices in a region of the interior. This process is just beginning. There is much work ahead, and we must presume the innocence of those involved. Only at the end will we know if this complaint has any merit. We will have to wait.

The resumption of investigation activities of this kind of conduct by Indecopi is positive. Since the implementation of the business concentration control regime (2021), there has been an evident reduction in the number and importance of investigations into collusion. With scarce resources, Indecopi's activities have focused on reviewing concentration operations that largely represent no risk whatsoever to competition. Since the regime began, 93% of notified operations were approved outright for posing no risks. They generated no problems.

One of the operations that was conditioned for posing a risk to competition never materialized (the KKR/Telefónica/Entel operation). In this case, the investment of public resources spent reviewing an operation that never got off paper was thrown in the trash. Useless spending chasing mice or mere ghosts.

This neglect of cartel investigations in recent years has generated a decline in the perception of Indecopi's effectiveness in detecting agreements between competitors, at least in the eyes of those who closely follow its activities. According to the results of a survey conducted by the Competition Center (CECO) of the Adolfo Ibañez University (Chile) on the perception of free competition institutions in Peru, there has been a constant decline over the past four years in the perception of its effectiveness in detecting such conduct. This is serious.

These types of agreements not only harm consumers but also cast doubt on the ability of the free market to generate well-being for people. The social upheaval in Chile (2019) that ended in questioning its economic model and the start of a constitutional reform process was fueled by previous revelations of collusive agreements in markets very close to people's daily lives (toilet paper, pharmacies, and supermarkets). An ineffective Indecopi in the face of collusive conduct delegitimizes the economic model of the Constitution and aids those who want to modify it, who, judging by the election results, are not few in number.

The resumption of these activities is positive. But a headless and leaderless Indecopi will not go far in these initiatives. Over the past six years it has changed hands several times (5 in total). Its current president has reportedly resigned; speculation circulates about his replacement. Do the candidates who remain in the race want an Indecopi chasing mice or ghosts? Will they strengthen its independence or do they want it weak so they can control it at will? The bets are on!
