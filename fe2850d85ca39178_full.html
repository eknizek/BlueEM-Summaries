# Indecopi: Chasing Mice and Ghosts?

By Alejandro Falla

Amid so much electoral news, an investigation launched by Indecopi went unnoticed—one concerning a alleged agreement between competitors to manipulate product prices in a region of the interior. This process is just beginning. There is much work ahead and we must presume the innocence of those involved. Only at the end will we know if this complaint has any merit. We will have to wait.

The resumption of investigation activities of this type of conduct by Indecopi is positive. Since the implementation of the business concentration control regime (2021), there has been an evident reduction in the number and importance of investigations into collusion. With limited resources, Indecopi's activities have focused on reviewing concentration operations that by and large represent no risk whatsoever to competition. Since the regime began, 93% of notified operations were approved outright as they posed no risks. They generated no problems at all. One of the operations that was conditioned due to representing a competition risk never came to fruition (the KKR/Telefónica/Entel operation). In this latter case, the investment of public resources spent reviewing an operation that never got off the ground was thrown in the trash. Useless spending chasing mice or simple ghosts.

This neglect of cartel investigations in recent years has generated a decrease in the perception of Indecopi's effectiveness in detecting agreements between competitors, at least in the eyes of those who follow its activities more closely. According to results from a survey conducted by the Competition Center (CECO) at the Adolfo Ibáñez University in Chile on the perception of free competition institutions in Peru, there has been a constant decline over the last four years in the perception of its effectiveness in detecting this type of conduct. This is serious.

These types of agreements not only harm consumers, but they call into question the free market's ability to generate wellbeing for people. The social upheaval in Chile (2019) that ended in a questioning of its economic model and the beginning of a constitutional reform process was fueled by prior revelations of collusive agreements in markets very close to people (toilet paper, pharmacies, and supermarkets). An ineffective Indecopi in the face of collusive conduct delegitimizes the economic model of the Constitution and plays into the hands of those who want to modify it—who, judging by the election results, are not few.

The resumption of these activities is positive. But a headless Indecopi without leadership will not go very far in these initiatives. In the last six years it has changed hands several times (5 in total). Its current president reportedly resigned; speculation circulates about his replacement. Do the candidates remaining in the race want an Indecopi chasing mice or ghosts? Will they strengthen its independence or do they want it weak so they can manipulate it as they please? Bets are running!
