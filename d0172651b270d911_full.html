# Peru's Q1 2026 GDP growth estimated at 3% by Scotiabank

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) grew around 3% during the first quarter of 2026, a rate slightly lower than the 3.2% recorded in the fourth quarter of 2025, according to Scotiabank estimates in its Weekly Report.

The report explained that, although economic activity accumulated an expansion of 3.2% between January and February, in March GDP experienced a notable deceleration of around 2%, as a consequence of the temporary suspension of Camisea gas supply during the first half of the month, which affected not only hydrocarbon production but indirectly sectors such as Industry and Transport.

In February, GDP grew 3.7%, exceeding the 3.4% expected by the analyst consensus, according to Bloomberg. The month's growth was exacerbated by the increase in Taxes (+8.9%), which contributed 0.7 percentage points to February's result. Apart from this, GDP continued to be driven by non-primary sectors linked to domestic demand, given the dynamism of consumption and private investment, particularly highlighting Construction (+8.9%), Commerce (+6.1%), and Services (+3.4%).

On the other hand, primary sectors showed negative performance, notably the Fishing sector (-5.2%), lower catch of species for direct human consumption, especially for the canned fish industry, Mining and Hydrocarbons (1.1%), decline in oil, molybdenum, and lead production, and Agriculture (-0.1%), early harvesting of crops such as grapes in previous months due to the possibility of heavy rains associated with the El Niño phenomenon.

For March, Scotiabank estimated GDP grew around 2%, its lowest expansion level since November 2025. As is known, during the first half of the month, the supply of natural gas and natural gas liquids from Camisea was interrupted due to an incident in the gas pipeline. For this reason, hydrocarbon production registered a 35% drop in March, which would subtract 0.6 percentage points from GDP growth in the month. Additionally, restrictions on natural gas and vehicular natural gas (GNV) supply would have impacted the activity pace of the Manufacturing and Transport sectors, respectively.

However, other forward-looking indicators such as local cement dispatches (+16.8%), increased imports (+19.7%), and sales of new light vehicles (+38.3%), would reflect that domestic demand maintained in March the dynamism shown during the first two months of the year.
