# Change in Food Labeling Would Generate Costs and Impact SMEs, According to ANDI

According to the industry group, more than 50,000 companies would be impacted by the measure.

The Food Industry Chamber of ANDI has expressed "profound concern" regarding the new resolution project from the Ministry of Health that aims to repeal the current front-of-package nutritional labeling regulations in Colombia. This industry initiative warns of "technical and economic burdens" that could destabilize a sector composed of 51,200 companies, directly affecting the supply and livelihoods of thousands of Colombian workers.

The industry group emphasizes that the impact falls on micro and medium-sized enterprises, which represent "98.5%" of the entire food business sector in the country. According to the Chamber, the proposal is "completely inconvenient" because no exhaustive analysis has been conducted on the variables that ensure the functioning and financial stability of these organizations.

One of the most critical points raised by industrialists is the lack of openness from the Ministry to convene "working sessions" that would demonstrate the actual negative impact of the regulation. The industry maintains with concern that there is a "lack of evidence and scientific rigor" in the adoption of these new requirements. This would require redesigning labels on packaged foods.

## Technical and Economic Impact on the Food Industry

The adoption of these new requirements is clearly shaping up as a potential "technical barrier to international trade," according to technical evaluations conducted by the Chamber's legal experts. The industry group asserts that the requirements are not "properly substantiated" under the mandatory standards of the World Trade Organization or the Andean Community. This scenario of uncertainty would complicate trade exchange and the competitiveness of local products.

Beyond elevated design costs, the food industry faces the challenge of managing inventories in minimal timeframes to avoid waste of already-printed packaging materials.

ANDI emphasizes that the government proposal introduces various "anti-technical concepts" regarding specific products without the support of "robust scientific evidence" that justifies such changes. Legal instability concerns merchants that depend on stable rules.

The resolution project addresses sensitive issues by attempting to create a technical definition of ultra-processed foods that, according to ANDI, currently "does not exist in any regulation in the world." This regulatory innovation would require including an "ULTRA-PROCESSED WARNING" for those products that use at least one industrial chemical or biotechnological additive in their formulation. Such measures are rated as disproportionate by the industrial sector responsible for production.

## New Warning Labels and Ultra-Processed Definitions

The ministerial project also contemplates the creation of new micro-labels intended for small-sized packaging and significantly hardens the visual rules for nutritional tables. One of the most controversial provisions is the prohibition of "QR code use," despite this technological tool being officially referenced in international Codex Alimentarius standards. This restriction limits complementary digital information.

Regulatory restrictions also extend beyond health claims, which would be limited for any food that must carry the warning labels currently proposed by the Ministry.

Camilo Montes, Executive Director of the Chamber, emphasizes that this "new technical regulation is not properly justified" and is being promoted without the scientific evidence necessary for a change of this depth. The executive warns of "unnecessary additional costs."

The implementation of these measures would affect consumer perception of food products that have historically complied with food safety and nutrition standards endorsed by authorities. By supporting limitations on making nutritional claims, the Ministry could be restricting relevant information for daily eating. The industry insists that dialogue is the only path to achieving effective regulation.

## Regulatory Challenges and Transition Periods for Companies

The regulation establishes a transition period of merely "six months" once the Ministry of Health's final resolution is officially published in the Official Gazette. For ANDI, this timeframe is completely insufficient considering that companies must "completely redesign their labels" and perform technical design calculations. Additionally, the process requires formally requesting the depletion of existing stock and managing the printing of new materials.

It is paradoxical for industry representatives that the Government intends to issue a new resolution when Colombia already has regulated front-of-package labeling. Resolutions 810 of 2021, 2492 of 2022, and 254 of 2023 have been the frameworks in effect during the last three years. The change is proposed "without having conducted the ex post evaluation" required by Colombian regulations to measure actual effectiveness. This administrative action would go against the "good regulatory practices" to which the country formally committed before the OECD and the National Department of Planning.

The industry group makes an urgent call to the business sector, academics, and scientists to analyze the draft and send their comments directly to the Ministry of Health.
