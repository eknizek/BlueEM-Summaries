# Changes to Food Labeling Would Create Costs and Hurt MSMEs, Says ANDI

The Food Industry Chamber of ANDI has expressed "profound concern" regarding the new draft resolution from the Ministry of Health that seeks to repeal the current nutritional front-of-package labeling regulations in Colombia. This industry initiative warns of "technical and economic burdens" that could destabilize a sector comprising 51,200 companies, directly affecting the supply chain and livelihoods of thousands of Colombian workers.

The industry highlights that the impact falls on micro and medium-sized enterprises, which represent "98.5%" of the entire food business ecosystem in the country. According to the Chamber, the proposal is "completely inadvisable" because no exhaustive analysis has been conducted on variables that ensure the functioning and financial stability of these organizations.

One of the most critical points raised by industry representatives is the Ministry's lack of openness to convene "working sessions" that would demonstrate the real negative impact of the regulation. The industry maintains with concern that there is a "lack of evidence and scientific rigor" in adopting these new requirements. This would force the redesigning of packaged food labels.

According to technical evaluations by the Chamber's legal experts, adopting these new requirements clearly appears as a potential "technical barrier to international trade." The industry assures that the requirements are not "properly substantiated" under mandatory standards of the World Trade Organization or the Andean Community. This scenario of uncertainty would complicate trade exchange and competitiveness of local products. Beyond elevated design costs, the food industry faces the challenge of managing inventories in minimal timeframes to avoid waste of already-printed packaging materials.

ANDI emphasizes that the government proposal introduces various "anti-technical concepts" regarding specific products without support from "robust scientific evidence" justifying such changes. Legal instability concerns retailers who depend on stable rules.

The draft resolution addresses sensitive issues by attempting to create a technical definition of ultra-processed foods that, according to ANDI, currently "does not exist in any regulation in the world." This regulatory innovation would require including "ULTRA-PROCESSED WARNING" for products employing at least one chemical or industrial biotechnological additive in their formulation. Such measures are deemed disproportionate by the industrial production sector.

The ministry project also contemplates creating new micro-seals for small-sized packaging and significantly tightens visual rules for nutritional tables. One of the most controversial provisions is the prohibition of "QR code usage," despite this technological tool being officially referenced in international Codex Alimentarius standards. This restriction limits complementary digital information.

Regulatory restrictions extend beyond health claims, which would be limited for any food required to carry the warning seals currently proposed by the Ministry.

Camilo Montes, Executive Director of the Chamber, emphasizes that this "new technical regulation is not properly justified" and is promoted without necessary scientific evidence for a change of this magnitude. The director warns of "unnecessary additional costs."

The implementation of these measures would affect consumer perception of food products that have historically complied with safety and nutrition standards endorsed by authorities. By supporting limitations on making nutritional declarations, the Ministry could be restricting relevant information for daily nutrition.

The industry insists that dialogue is the only path to achieving effective regulation.

The regulation establishes a transition period of merely "six months" once the Ministry of Health's final resolution is officially published in the Official Gazette. For ANDI, this timeframe is completely insufficient considering that companies must "completely redo their labels" and perform technical design calculations. Additionally, the process requires formally requesting inventory depletion and managing the printing of new materials.

It is paradoxical for industry representatives that the Government intends to issue a new resolution when Colombia already has regulated front-of-package labeling. Resolutions 810 of 2021, 2492 of 2022, and 254 of 2023 have been the frameworks in effect over the past three years. The change is proposed "without conducting the ex-post evaluation" required by Colombian regulations to measure actual effectiveness. This administrative action would go against "good regulatory practices" to which the country formally committed before the OECD and the National Planning Department.

The industry makes an urgent call to the business community, academics, and scientists to analyze the draft and send their comments directly.
