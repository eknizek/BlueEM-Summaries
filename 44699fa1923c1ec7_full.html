New Food Labeling Regulations Would Increase Costs and Harm Colombian SMEs, Says ANDI

24 April 2026 · 07:20 PM

# Change in Food Labeling Would Generate Costs and Affect SMEs, According to ANDI

The Food Industry Chamber of ANDI has expressed "deep concern" about the new draft resolution from the Ministry of Health that seeks to repeal current front-of-pack nutritional labeling regulations in Colombia. This industry initiative warns about "technical and economic burdens" that could destabilize a sector composed of 51,200 companies, directly affecting the supply and livelihoods of thousands of Colombian workers.

The industry emphasizes that the impact falls on micro and medium-sized enterprises, which represent "98.5%" of the entire food business fabric in the country. According to the Chamber, the proposal is "completely inconvenient" because no exhaustive analysis has been conducted on the variables that ensure the functioning and financial stability of these organizations.

One of the most critical points raised by industrialists is the Ministry's lack of openness to convene "working groups" that would demonstrate the actual negative impact of the regulation. The industry asserts with concern that there is "lack of evidence and scientific rigor" in adopting these new requirements. This would require redesigning labels on packaged foods.

## Technical and Economic Impact on the Food Industry

The adoption of these new requirements clearly appears to be a potential "technical barrier to international trade," according to technical assessments conducted by the Chamber's legal experts. The industry assures that the requirements are not "properly substantiated" under mandatory standards from the World Trade Organization or the Andean Community. This scenario of uncertainty would complicate trade exchange and the competitiveness of local products.

Beyond elevated design costs, the food industry faces the challenge of managing inventories in minimal timeframes to avoid waste of already-printed packaging materials.

ANDI emphasizes that the government proposal introduces various "anti-technical concepts" about specific products without the support of "robust scientific evidence" justifying such changes. Legal instability concerns retailers who depend on stable rules.

The draft resolution addresses sensitive issues by attempting to create a technical definition of ultra-processed foods that, according to ANDI, currently "does not exist in any regulation in the world." This regulatory innovation would require including "ULTRA-PROCESSED WARNING" for products that use at least one chemical or industrial biotechnological additive in their formulation. Such measures are deemed disproportionate by the industrial sector responsible for production.

## New Warning Labels and Definitions of Ultra-Processed Foods

The ministerial project also contemplates creating new micro-seals for small-sized packages and significantly tightens visual rules for nutritional tables. One of the most controversial provisions is the prohibition of "using QR codes," despite this technological tool being officially referenced in international Codex Alimentarius standards. This restriction limits complementary digital information.

Regulatory restrictions extend beyond health claims, which would be limited for any food that must carry the warning seals currently proposed by the Ministry.

Camilo Montes, Executive Director of the Chamber, emphasizes that this "new technical regulation is not properly justified" and is being promoted without the scientific evidence necessary for such a profound change. The executive warns of "unnecessary additional costs."

The implementation of these measures would affect consumer perception of food products that have historically complied with safety and nutrition standards endorsed by authorities. By supporting limitations on making nutritional declarations, the Ministry could be restricting relevant information for daily nutrition. The industry insists that dialogue is the only path to achieve effective regulation.

## Regulatory Challenges and Transition Periods for Companies

The regulations establish a transition period of just "six months" once the Ministry of Health's final resolution is officially published in the Official Journal. For ANDI, this timeline is completely insufficient considering that companies must "redo all their labels" and conduct technical design calculations. Additionally, the process requires formally requesting inventory depletion and managing the printing of new materials.

It is paradoxical for industry representatives that the Government intends to issue a new resolution when Colombia already has regulated front-of-pack labeling. Resolutions 810 of 2021, 2492 of 2022, and 254 of 2023 have been the frameworks in effect for the past three years. The change is proposed "without having conducted the ex-post evaluation" required by Colombian regulations to measure actual effectiveness. This administrative action would contradict "good regulatory practices" to which the country formally committed before the OECD and the National Planning Department.

The industry makes an urgent call to the business fabric, academics, and scientists to analyze the proposal.
