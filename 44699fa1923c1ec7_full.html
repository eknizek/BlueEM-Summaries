# Food Labeling Changes Would Create Costs and Impact Colombian SMEs, Says Business Group

The Food Industry Chamber of Andi has expressed "deep concern" regarding the new draft resolution from the Ministry of Health that seeks to repeal the current frontal nutritional labeling regulations in Colombia. This industry initiative warns of "technical and economic burdens" that could destabilize a sector comprised of 51,200 companies, directly affecting the supply and livelihoods of thousands of Colombian workers.

The business group emphasizes that the impact falls on micro and medium-sized enterprises, which represent "98.5%" of the entire food business fabric in the country. According to the Chamber, the proposal is "completely inadvisable" because there has been no exhaustive analysis of the variables that guarantee the functioning and financial stability of these organizations.

One of the most critical points highlighted by industry leaders is the lack of openness from the Ministry to convene "working tables" that would demonstrate the real negative impact of the regulation. The industry insists with concern that there is "lack of evidence and scientific rigor" in the adoption of these new requirements, which would force a redesign of packaged food labels.

The adoption of these new requirements appears clearly to be a potential "technical barrier to trade" internationally, according to technical evaluations conducted by the Chamber's legal experts. The business group asserts that the requirements are not "properly substantiated" under the mandatory standards of the World Trade Organization or the Andean Community. This scenario of uncertainty would complicate trade exchange and competitiveness of local products. Beyond the high design costs, the food industry faces the challenge of managing inventories in minimal timeframes to avoid waste of already-printed packaging materials.

Andi emphasizes that the government proposal introduces various "anti-technical concepts" about specific products without the backing of "robust scientific evidence" that justifies such changes. The legal instability concerns retailers that depend on stable rules. The resolution project addresses sensitive issues by attempting to create a technical definition of ultra-processed foods that, according to Andi, currently "does not exist in any regulation in the world." This regulatory innovation would require including "ULTRA-PROCESSED WARNING" for those products that use at least one chemical or industrial biotechnological additive in their formulation. Such measures are characterized as disproportionate by the industrial sector responsible for production.

The ministerial project also contemplates the creation of new micro-seals intended for small-sized packages and significantly tightens the visual rules for nutritional tables. One of the most controversial provisions is the prohibition of "QR code usage," despite this technological tool being officially referenced in international Codex Alimentarius standards. This restriction limits complementary digital information. The regulatory restrictions extend beyond health claims, which would be limited for any food product that must bear the warning seals currently proposed by the Ministry.

Camilo Montes, Executive Director of the Chamber, emphasizes that this "new technical regulation is not properly justified" and is being promoted without the scientific evidence necessary for a change of this magnitude. The executive warns of "unnecessary additional costs." The implementation of these measures would affect consumer perception of food products that have historically complied with safety and nutrition standards approved by authorities. By supporting limitations on making nutritional claims, the Ministry could be restricting relevant information for daily nutrition. The industry insists that dialogue is the only path to achieving effective regulation.

The regulation establishes a transition period of merely "six months" once the Ministry of Health's final resolution is officially published in the Official Gazette. For Andi, this timeline is completely insufficient considering that companies must "redesign all of their labels" and perform technical design calculations. Additionally, the process requires formally requesting inventory depletion and managing the printing of new materials.

It is paradoxical for industry representatives that the Government intends to issue a new resolution when Colombia already has regulated front labeling. Resolutions 810 of 2021, 2492 of 2022, and 254 of 2023 have been the frameworks in force over the last three years. The change is proposed "without having conducted the ex-post evaluation" that Colombian regulations require to measure actual effectiveness. This administrative action would go against the "good regulatory practices" to which the country formally committed before the OECD and the National Planning Department.

The business group makes an urgent call to the business community, academics, and scientists to analyze the draft and send their comments directly to the Ministry of Health.
