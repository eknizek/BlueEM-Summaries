# We have a unique opportunity – Intercorp Financial Services CFO

By Michela Casassa

For years, Peru was a case study in macroeconomic stability. However, recently, political uncertainty and global volatility have partially removed it from investors' radar. Today, in a quiet but consistent manner, that is beginning to change.

This is not about speaking of a "new Peruvian miracle," but rather recognizing concrete signals that are putting the country back on the map. The first is external: the global context is reconfiguring priorities. The need to diversify supply chains, reduce geopolitical dependencies, and ensure access to critical minerals—especially copper—has repositioned Peru as a strategic player. In a world advancing toward artificial intelligence and energy transition, few countries have a comparable combination of resources, scale, and proximity to relevant markets.

The second signal is internal. Despite political noise, macroeconomic fundamentals have held up better than expected. Fiscal discipline, the credibility of the Central Bank, and the solidity of the financial system continue to be assets that the market recognizes. They are not headlines, but they do function as anchors of confidence in moments of uncertainty.

There is, furthermore, a third factor: Peru continues to be perceived as an economy with room for growth. Facing more saturated markets or with lower prospects, here there are still sectors with clear opportunities—from infrastructure and mining, to digital financial services and consumption—that can capture value in the coming years.

In my meetings with international investors, I always notice that they position Peru as one of the top economies in Latin America and the financial system as a solid system with good digitalization advances. We are also seeing many new investors interested in the Peruvian market and in Intercorp Financial Services' stock. These are concrete signals.

However, this renewed interest is not guaranteed. It depends on the decisions we make today. The main one is transforming stability into predictability. For the global investor, it is not enough for a country to weather crises; it is essential that it offers clarity on the rules of the game, capacity for execution, and reasonable timelines in regulatory processes.

It is also time to tell the story better. Peru is not competing only for capital; it is competing for attention. In an environment where flows move quickly and decisions are made with incomplete information, the narrative—based on data, but well articulated—becomes a strategic tool.

The challenge, then, is not only to take advantage of a better context, but to raise our ambition. To move from being an attractive destination for its resources to one for its ecosystem: solid institutions, greater innovation, and active integration with global trends.

Peru has not stopped being interesting. Today, with the world seeking new equilibriums, we have a concrete opportunity to return to the center of the global conversation and build a story of more sustained, inclusive, and relevant growth in the long term.
