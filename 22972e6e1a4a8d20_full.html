# Candidates Avoid Discussing Employment Costs: What Do They Propose to Combat Informality?

On International Labor Day, the Peruvian labor market still faces structural challenges associated with high informality, elevated non-wage costs, low productivity, and limited youth labor insertion. Fuerza Popular (FP), Juntos por el Perú (JPP), and Renovación Popular (RP) recognize the need to promote formalization and improve labor insertion. However, their proposals present different approaches and, in several cases, lack concrete implementation mechanisms.

## Recent Improvements, Persistent Challenges

The labor market showed significant advances in 2025, led by formal employment, in a scenario where private investment grew 10%, its highest rate since 2012. National employment increased 1.5%, its fastest pace since the post-pandemic period, and real incomes grew 5.3%, the highest rate in 16 years. This dynamism was driven by formal employment, which expanded 4.7%, its greatest advance in over a decade. According to IPE estimates, these factors contributed to reducing poverty to approximately 25.6% in 2025.

In the first quarter of 2026, although employment continued advancing in the capital, underemployment grew 1.1%, its first increase in five quarters, and remains 12% above its pre-pandemic level. This suggests that, although employment has been recovering, improvements in job quality have stalled. Thus, labor proposals should start from an essential principle: sustained creation of quality employment requires greater private investment. Measures that increase labor rigidity, generate regulatory uncertainty, or increase formal hiring costs only discourage it.

Meanwhile, the most recent figures show that youth employment had a slight recovery. However, it is far from reversing the deterioration accumulated since the pandemic: there are still 185,000 fewer young people working than in 2019.

## Formalization

In Peru, labor informality still exceeds 70%—above countries like Chile and Colombia—and among young people, it exceeds 76%, which restricts their productivity and access to higher incomes. Part of this is explained by barriers that discourage business growth, linked to administrative procedures, the design of tax regimes, and elevated hiring and dismissal costs. These factors constitute strong disincentives for formalization of smaller businesses, which also register lower productivity levels. In 2024, their productivity was below 2019 levels and was 23 times lower than that of large enterprises.

In this regard, FP and RP agree that a strategy for labor formalization is administrative simplification and reduction of bureaucratic barriers, particularly for MSMEs. FP proposes eliminating unnecessary costs and procedures associated with starting business activities through the creation of a national interoperable electronic single window, which would reduce the dispersion of procedures they currently face to formalize. In the case of RP, the plan proposes massive digitalization of procedures and the creation of an institute for promotion and development of SMEs. However, it does not detail its institutional design or its coordination with existing entities. Meanwhile, JPP does not present concrete proposals on this matter. Rather, it indicates that it will eliminate, through a new Constitution, tax regimes and contracts it considers favorable to large enterprises, without specifying which would be modified or proposing an alternative scheme; this will only affect legal certainty.

In this regard, it should be noted that at the company level, there is a general regime and three other special ones for smaller enterprises. The problems in these latter are fully diagnosed: they do not serve as a transition to the general regime, they promote atomization, they do not incentivize buying or selling formally, and they facilitate tax evasion. Therefore, any reform should specify how to correct these aspects without increasing regulatory uncertainty or creating new formalization costs. In that line, RP proposes eliminating current tax regimes and creating a single simpler one, although it does not develop the design. FP does not present specific proposals on this matter.

A structural challenge that receives no attention in the government plans is the persistence of high non-wage labor costs. According to the IDB, in Peru they amount to 72% of the average formal salary, above the 51% regional average, mainly due to costs associated with dismissal indemnities and bonuses. Despite this, no party proposes a specific agenda to reduce these costs.

There are also other labor regulations that distort business growth decisions. Thus, there is evidence that the threshold of 20 workers for mandatory profit sharing causes a concentration of firms just below that limit. In this regard, according to Horizonte Laboral projections (2026), a staggered profit-sharing scheme—which reduces the marginal cost of exceeding said threshold—would have the support of FP and RP in the Senate, while JPP would be opposed.

Another labor rigidity to consider is the minimum wage. Its increases affect hiring costs not only through higher monthly wages but also because they raise associated benefits and contributions—such as bonuses, CTS, vacation, and Essalud—. In the general regime, formally hiring a worker at the minimum wage can imply an additional cost to the employer of up to approximately 59% of gross salary. Thus, an increase in the minimum wage raises effective annual labor costs and discourages formal hiring, especially in low-productivity units. Although 13 of its 15 increases since 2000 responded to political factors, JPP proposes adjusting it according to the "needs of the population," without defining technical criteria. FP and RP do not propose changes.
