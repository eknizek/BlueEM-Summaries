# Bananinha Paraibuna Grows 3x, Evaluates New Factory and Rejects M&A

Bananinha Paraibuna, a family business in its third generation, has grown threefold and now bills almost R$ 50 million annually. Without a formal CEO and still 100% family-owned, the company has dominated the national market for 50 years with just one factory in Paraibuna, a town of 18,000 inhabitants in the interior of São Paulo state.

Now, the company is aiming for a new growth phase anchored in a lean portfolio, channel expansion, and reinforcement of its positioning in preservative-free foods, known as clean label products.

Founded by the grandfather of CMO Letícia Paiola in 1975, the company was started by tailor Célio Geraldo da Silva, who began making traditional Minas Gerais sweets in large copper pots at home to sell on Rodovia dos Tamoios after a bankruptcy. It was uncle Valter who transformed the artisanal operation into a large-scale business.

"My grandparents started with guava paste, which my uncles say was really good, it's a shame they didn't continue with it, but what really paid off was the banana snack," says Letícia, an advertising professional with a degree from ESPM in São Paulo who has been dedicating herself to the factory professionally since 2016.

Currently, her father Marcos and uncles Valter, Jane, and Rute respectively oversee administration, operations, sales, and finance. Beyond Letícia, other grandchildren are also becoming involved in the business. "My grandfather passed away when I was 15. I'm very proud of what he built and deeply admire my uncles' work," says the 33-year-old executive.

"A transformation is happening. Every Thursday we have meetings, with the board in the morning and marketing in the afternoon. It's just a matter of time before we have more market professionals inside the company," she evaluates. She also reports having already received inquiries for both minority stake sales and full control sales, including an offer from a large Brazilian group, but did not advance with any. "We want to maintain the family bond and there are things that money can't buy."

Annual revenue, which was R$ 18 million in 2020, reached R$ 46 million in 2025, and the company expects to hit R$ 60 million in 2026 and surpass R$ 90 million within three years. Responsible for 95% of this revenue, more than 8,000 bars are produced daily, Monday through Friday, with recently extended shifts. The sugar-free version (23g) represents 60% of the business compared to the traditional version (36g).

The current structure would allow expansion with the creation of another production line, but a new plant will likely be necessary, possibly in another city for logistical reasons. "God willing, we'll need a new factory. I'd like it to be in Paraibuna, but we've already started evaluating other places," she says. One possibility, due to proximity to the Dutra highway, is São José dos Campos, where she currently lives, 30 minutes from Paraibuna.

"If we grow as much as we expect in five or six years, we'll need at least one new area for banana storage," which currently occupies half of the 4,000 m² factory. For larger moves, the company relies on bank credit but is working to rebuild cash reserves for investment at lower costs, as was the case before the pandemic. "It's a never-ending project to improve cash and margins."

Paraibuna processes 15 tons of fresh bananas daily – not pulp – which guarantees its clean label status. The peels are donated to local farmers or livestock producers. "My father always said that the banana snack wasn't for us, but to generate jobs for other people," Letícia explains.

This purity has secured certifications such as gluten-free, lactose-free, kosher, and vegan status, providing easier access to these markets. However, the company still faces challenges in others that seemed simpler. "One of the most difficult markets is Rio de Janeiro, which enchants me with the challenge, with other more established competitors and cheap combat banana snacks, with informal (non-industrial) production, the bar sweet."

The secret to conquering the national market was precisely capillarity, which began with candy wholesalers in São Paulo. "Our distribution was always impeccable. I once had a boss at Colgate who said the banana snack was better distributed than her toothpaste," she laughs. More recently, the brand has reached the checkout counters of major supermarket and pharmacy chains, as well as e-commerce, which today represents 9% of revenue. "We've always prioritized the distributor, but today we work with more channels of greater contribution," she states.

Only after reviewing the portfolio of 24 SKUs – "the peanut brittle didn't take off," she laments – will the family think about new products. This could be an opportunity to embrace the protein trend. "We've already studied this, but we're very concerned about quality. I once had a chocolate supplier who changed the formula and stopped being vegan. I lost money on packaging, but I didn't want to sell with wrong communication," Letícia recalls.

"We have innovation projects for probiotics, vitamins, and functional nutrition with the effect of weight-loss pens, but our core is the banana snack. And we want to continue being the best-selling one in the country."
