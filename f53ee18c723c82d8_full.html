# Oil Market Gripped by Desperate Rush for Barrels

While investors focused on the fragile Iranian ceasefire this week, a desperate scramble for crude oil cargoes was unfolding in the oil market, with traders and refineries searching the world for immediately available supplies.

In the North Sea, the world's most important physical crude oil market, traders presented 40 purchase offers for cargoes this week, of which only four were met by sales offers. Cargoes for delivery in the coming weeks were traded at unprecedented prices, above $140 (R$ 703) per barrel. Elsewhere, refineries have sought increasingly distant supplies, leading to a series of unusual transactions and skyrocketing premiums for any crude oil ready for immediate shipment.

Traders said panic moves in the world's major physical oil markets demonstrated the scale of the crude oil deficit that will be felt in coming weeks as the loss of Middle Eastern supplies leaves a growing gap.

Soaring prices signal that some European refineries will likely need to follow the lead of Asian counterparts and reduce production, they said—a measure that could help balance the crude oil market but would deepen shortages of essential products like diesel and aviation fuel.

"There is simply a shortage of crude oil," said Neil Crosby, head of research at Sparta Commodities AS. "Physical Brent is in chaos and has risen too much. At this pace, even European refineries will have to reduce utilization, perhaps as early as next month."

The frenzy in physical oil trading contrasts with the futures market, where June-delivery crude fell 13% this week, closing at around $95 (R$ 477) per barrel, amid optimism about the ceasefire. There were some initial signs of increased activity in the Strait of Hormuz over the weekend, with two Chinese and one Greek supertanker transiting the waterway, but traffic still remains well below pre-war levels.

Even if weekend negotiations lead to a resumption of normal flows through the strait, relief is unlikely to arrive in time to prevent a crisis. It takes weeks for crude oil from the Gulf to reach refineries in Asia and Europe.

"The last cargoes that transited the Strait of Hormuz before the conflict are now arriving at their destinations. This is where paper-traded markets meet physical reality, and the 40-day gap in global energy flows becomes truly exposed," said Sultan al Jaber, CEO of Abu Dhabi National Oil Co., in a LinkedIn post Thursday.

This gap can be seen in the premium refineries are willing to pay to secure crude oil cargoes available in the short term. Traders from some Asian refineries, speaking on condition of anonymity, said they were no longer focused on price and simply sought to secure oil barrels wherever they could to ensure energy security.

Dated Brent—the most important benchmark in the physical oil market, used to price millions of barrels a day—hit a record $144 (R$ 723) per barrel before this week's ceasefire, surpassing its 2008 highs, even as futures remained well below their record levels. By Friday, it had fallen to $126 (R$ 632) per barrel, still more than $30 (R$ 150) above Brent futures for June delivery, while traders including Trafigura Group and Gunvor Group were offering more than $22 (R$ 110 per barrel above Dated Brent for North Sea crude cargoes for delivery in late April and early May.

Nigerian supplies for loading next month were offered at up to $25 (R$ 125) per barrel above the benchmark, compared to less than $3 (R$ 15) before the start of the war with Iran.

Asian countries, most dependent on the Strait of Hormuz for crude supplies, went beyond their traditional sources to scour the world for barrels. Japanese refineries led a push to buy oil from the U.S., which is exporting at record levels. A wave of purchases by Chinese refineries pushed oil shipments from Vancouver, Canada to a historic record this month. And Indian refineries have ramped up purchases of Venezuelan oil. In the first week of April, tankers loaded nearly 6 million barrels to the South Asian country, double the volumes seen in the same period in March.

The focus is on barrels available as quickly as possible—and refineries are willing to pay more for speed. Japanese refineries chartered smaller-than-usual vessels for their American oil purchases so they can transit the Panama Canal and reach Japan faster.

On Saturday, President Donald Trump posted on social media about "massive numbers" of oil tankers heading to the U.S. to load its oil.
