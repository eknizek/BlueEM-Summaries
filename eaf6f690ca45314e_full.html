Court restores Anatel rule on irregular cell phones sold on Amazon

24 April 2026 · 09:59 PM

# Court Restores Anatel Rule on Irregular Cell Phones Sold on Amazon

The Regional Federal Court of the 3rd Region (TRF-3) reversed a decision that suspended the application of rules by the National Telecommunications Agency (Anatel) regarding the sale of irregular cell phones on the Amazon platform and forwarded the case discussion to the Superior Court of Justice (STJ). As a result, the previous decision favorable to the company is suspended, and Amazon again must comply with Anatel's rules for offering these products, at least until a new ruling.

The decision was signed on Thursday (23rd) by federal judge André Nekatschalow, vice-president of the TRF-3. In 2025, the agency established that marketplaces – online sales intermediation platforms – and other digital sales channels would become jointly and severally liable for offering non-homologated telecommunications products or those in disagreement with technical requirements. The rule establishes that these platforms are jointly responsible with sellers for violations related to offering irregular products that lack Anatel homologation.

The objective is to curb the sale of non-homologated cell phones, requiring the insertion of homologation codes, validation of this information, and adjustments to platform interfaces. However, the measure was questioned in court by companies that initially obtained favorable decisions to suspend Anatel's measures, arguing that marketplaces have no duty for prior inspection, applying the Internet Civil Code framework.

Faced with this, the regulatory agency filed appeals to the STJ and the Supreme Federal Court (STF). In the appeal presented, the agency states that the electronic commerce of non-homologated cell phones presents real risk not only to the health and safety of consumers using these products, but to cybersecurity, economic order, and radiofrequency spectrum management. The company argues that the agency's appeal is inadmissible according to STJ jurisprudence, since the ruling is consistent with the tribunal's precedent, which holds that marketplaces are internet service providers under the Internet Civil Code framework, not obligated to conduct prior inspection of third-party products.

In his decision, the judge explains that regarding platform liability, STJ jurisprudence has positioned itself against imposing prior control over third-party merchandise on marketplaces. "However, it is observed that such cases deal especially with merchandise/products violating copyright, trademark and patent rights and regulations issued by Inmetro, whereas the case in question concerns illicit products resulting from violation of provisions of the General Telecommunications Law, with potential influence on the management of the country's radiofrequency spectrum, a circumstance that recommends opening the special instance for the STJ's consideration on the matter," he states.

To Valor, Anatel councilor Edson Holanda assessed that the decision reaffirms the understanding being built by the regulatory agency with public authorities. "The argument that there is no responsibility for what is sold is very flawed. The Judiciary is beginning to recognize that from the moment there is economic participation, there must also be responsibility for marketed products. Jurisprudence is beginning to lean in this direction," he stated.

When contacted, Amazon did not comment by the time this report was closed.
