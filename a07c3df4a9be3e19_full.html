# Ex-XP Executive Launches 'Brazilian Maldives' Development with R$5B Investment in Northeast Brazil

A former XP partner, Julio Capua, is transforming an underdeveloped stretch of Ceará's coastline into a luxury destination, featuring a five-star hotel, crystal-blue lagoons, architect-designed homes with high-end services, and an clientele that increasingly includes prominent names from the financial market.

Capua, a kitesurfing enthusiast, left XP after the firm's stake sale to Itau in 2018, seeking to invest in something different at that time. Currently, Grupo Carnaúba encompasses 12 square kilometers in Preá, 15 minutes from Jericoacoara airport, with a projected R$2 billion investment planned for execution over two to three years.

Preá is considered one of the world's best destinations for kitesurfing—the sport combining board and kite—which led the investor to visit the region for the first time in 2014, become interested in the location, and discover opportunities to make the project viable. The first land purchase occurred in late 2019, a 500,000 square-meter beachfront area where Vila Carnaúba was born. "Then the pandemic came. Many people put assets up for sale and I saw the opportunity to do something bigger. We bought 12 million square meters, part on the beach and another part further back," explains the entrepreneur.

The first group of partners came from XP itself: friends Guilherme Benchimol, Gabriel Leal, Carlos Ferreira, and Eduardo Juaçaba. Later came institutional investors, real estate vehicles, and an XP fund with R$200 million in 2023. "So far, we've used about 700,000 square meters of our landbank. We still have over 11 million square meters to develop. In 10 years, this could exceed R$5 billion, possibly more than R$10 billion in gross sales value," he calculates.

The first phase of the main condominium, Vila Carnaúba, launched in 2022, sold all 115 lots, with 10 homes already completed and 45 under construction.

One of the challenges involved the 60,000 square meters of planned lagoons inspired by Lençóis Maranhenses. "At first, we didn't know how to do it. We tested: digging, understanding water color, how to make it blue with a white sand bottom. Today we know how to build lagoons at a cost of R$1,000 to R$1,200 per square meter," Capua says proudly after completing six lagoons in the first phase. The idea stemmed from local experience. "Once the wind picks up, the beach becomes a paradise for sailors but a nightmare for swimmers, reaching 30 knots (55 km/h). We needed to create a water attraction beyond the beach," he explains.

In the current stage, two new lagoons with 32 surrounding lots will be added, along with 24 branded residences managed by the Anantara luxury chain. "The homes will have the style of their Maldives resorts, with decks on the waterfront," says the CEO. The development's first hotel will also be from the Asian brand of Minor International—the brand's debut in Latin America, with 80 rooms and construction beginning in July. "The operator keeps the flag, and we keep the asset," he details.

Hotel ownership belongs to the Wings fund, a R$280 million partnership between Carnaúba's management company and TG Core. "We're in talks to bring other chains, such as Accor and Fasano. The idea is to establish Preá as a complete destination," says Capua, who also counts on subsidized financing from Banco do Nordeste (BNB) for up to 60% of the hotel project.

Operating since last season—which runs from June through Carnival—is Carnaúba Wind House, a hospitality club with 60 rooms, five-star service, and complete leisure infrastructure. Among investors and visitors to the complex are Álvaro Garnero, Alexandre Birman, Pablo Marçal, Tallis Gomes, Flávio Canto, Claude Troisgros, and Felipe Bronze.

Next year, another residential product should launch: 200 apartments at approximately R$2 million each.

To ensure luxury status with the necessary workforce while preventing irregular housing, Capua wants to launch a popular housing neighborhood to serve employees and potential service providers. Vila Cajueiro should begin with a thousand residences in tiers 1 and 3 of Minha Casa, Minha Vida, featuring wide streets, bike paths, and planned landscaping, 700 meters from Villa Carnaúba. "The idea is to ensure that job opportunities are absorbed by people who have somewhere to live, preventing favela formation. This project can grow significantly over time, potentially reaching 40,000 popular homes over many years, since the land is extensive," he says.

For Capua, Preá is still in its beginning stages—and that's what makes the thesis interesting, he argues. "The region is underdeveloped and has significant upside. Whoever comes falls in love with it."
