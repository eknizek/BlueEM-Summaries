Former XP executive creates 'Brazilian Maldives' with R$5 billion Northeast bet including Anantara hotel, lagoons, and residential development

# Former XP executive creates 'Brazilian Maldives' with R$5 billion Northeast bet

A partner at XP from the days of the autonomous agent office in Porto Alegre, Julio Capua is transforming a still largely unexploited stretch of Ceará's coastline into a luxury destination, with a five-star hotel, crystal-blue lagoons, branded homes with high-end services, and an audience that already includes well-known names from the financial market.

A kitesurfing enthusiast, Capua left XP after selling his stake to Itaú in 2018, wanting to invest in something smaller at that time. Currently, Grupo Carnaúba encompasses 12 km² in Preá, 15 minutes from Jericoacoara airport, with a projected R$2 billion to be spent over two to three years.

Preá is considered one of the world's best destinations for the sport combining board and kite – which took the investor to the region for the first time in 2014, sparked his interest in the location, and helped him discover opportunities to make the project viable. The first land was purchased at the end of 2019, a 500,000 m² area facing the beach, where Vila Carnaúba was born. "Then the pandemic came. Many people put assets up for sale and I saw the opportunity to do something bigger. We bought 12 million m², part of it on the beach and another part further back," the businessman explains.

The first group of partners came from XP itself: friends Guilherme Benchimol, Gabriel Leal, Carlos Ferreira, and Eduardo Juaçaba. Later came institutional investors, real estate vehicles, and an XP fund with R$200 million in 2023. "So far, we've used about 700,000 m² of the landbank. We still have over 11 million m² to develop. In 10 years, this could exceed R$5 billion, perhaps more than R$10 billion in gross merchandise value," he calculates.

The first phase of the main condominium, Vila Carnaúba, launched in 2022, had all 115 lots sold, with 10 homes already completed and 45 under construction.

One of the challenges was the 60,000 m² of planned lagoons inspired by the Lençóis Maranhenses. "At first, we didn't know how to do it. We tested: digging, understanding the water color, how to make it blue with a white sand bottom. Today we know how to build lagoons at a cost of R$1,000 to R$1,200 per m²," Capua says proudly after completing the construction of six of them in the first phase.

The idea came from local experience. "After the wind picks up, the beach becomes a paradise for sailors but a nightmare for swimmers, reaching 30 knots (55 km/h). We needed to create a water attraction beyond the beach," he explains.

In the current phase, there will be two new lagoons with 32 surrounding lots, plus 24 branded residences managed by the luxury Anantara network. "The homes will have the style of their resorts in the Maldives, with decks on the waterfront," says the CEO.

The first hotel in the development will also be from the Asian Minor International brand – marking the brand's debut in Latin America, with 80 rooms and construction set to begin in July. "The operation stays with the flag, and we keep the asset," he details.

The hotel property will be owned by the Wings fund, a R$280 million partnership between the Carnaúba manager and TG Core. "We're in talks to bring other networks like Accor and Fasano. The idea is to establish Preá as a complete destination," Capua says, noting he also has subsidized financing from Banco do Nordeste for up to 60% of the hotel project.

For those wanting to enjoy the amenities without managing a home, the Carnaúba Wind House opened last season – running from June through Carnival – a lodging club with 60 rooms, five-star service, and complete leisure infrastructure. Among investors and complex visitors are Álvaro Garnero, Alexandre Birman, Pablo Marçal, Tallis Gomes, Flávio Canto, Claude Troisgros, and Felipe Bronze.

Next year, another residential product should be launched, with 200 apartments around R$2 million each.

To ensure luxury status with the workforce it demands while preventing irregular housing, Capua wants to launch a popular housing neighborhood to serve condominium employees and potential service providers. Vila Cajueiro should begin with a thousand residences in tiers 1 and 3 of the Minha Casa, Minha Vida program, with wide streets, cycling paths, and planned landscaping, 700 meters from Villa Carnaúba. "The idea is to ensure that job opportunities are absorbed by people who have somewhere to live, preventing the formation of slums. This project could grow significantly over time, reaching 40,000 popular homes over many years, since the land is large," he says.

For Capua, Preá is still in its early stages – and that's what makes the thesis interesting, he argues. "The region is underdeveloped and has much upside. Whoever comes falls in love with it."
