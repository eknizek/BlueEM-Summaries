# Mercosur-EU deal brings opportunity and risk to machinery sector

The Mercosur-European Union free trade agreement comes into effect on May 1st, even if not in its entirety. In an initial assessment, the Brazilian agricultural machinery and equipment industry anticipates more imports and an increase in competition in the sector. However, some companies see a mix of risks and opportunities with the agreement.

"If we don't lower the 'Brazil cost,' in the long term the national industry will lose market share," says Pedro Estêvão Bastos, president of the Sectoral Chamber of Agricultural Machinery and Implements of the Brazilian Association of Machinery Industries (ABIMAQ).

The Europeans will put in place a phased elimination of the import tariffs currently levied on 95% of products exported by Mercosur. The South American bloc, in turn, will eliminate tariffs on 91% of the goods it imports from the EU.

According to Bastos, the cost of capital is the main component that makes Brazilian products around 20% more expensive than the average products in countries belonging to the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD).

"Much is said about the low productivity of the [Brazilian] industry, but it is not our workers who have low productivity, but rather the machinery around them, which is very old," says Bastos.

Renewing industrial machinery, however, will require the country to confront a structural problem generated by average interest rates above 20% per year. The Interim Trade Agreement (ITA), which provides for the tariff reduction, establishes deadlines of up to 15 years for the implementation of the entire bilateral trade agenda.

"We have this time to lower the cost of doing business in Brazil," he says.

The arrival of imported machinery, in any case, will not be automatic. There are "impediments," in the expression chosen by Bastos, that could delay the increase in foreign competition in a sector already dominated by multinationals. First, he points out, Brazil's current federal harvest funding program requires local content in agricultural machinery purchased with rural credit.

"There will be no financing for machines that will come ready-made from Europe," he observes.

Additionally, Bastos points out that higher value-added products require after-sales networks capable of serving all regions of the country. "It's not possible to import a machine, sell it here, and not have spare parts or anyone to repair it. The European manufacturer will have to set up shop here and establish its after-sales network, which is possible, but takes time," he adds.

On the other hand, he argues that by opening part of the European market to Brazilian agricultural products, the agreement tends to favor exports of grains and meat. "For our client, it's good, as it allows greater access to the European market, stimulating the increase in planted area here and, therefore, the growth of machinery sales," he says.

"I always see agreements as positive. The bigger the economic pie you participate in, the more alternatives you will have," says Carlos Daniel Haushahn, CEO of Jacto. He believes there are risks involved, especially for domestic companies, but says that the arrival of European companies tends to raise the technological level offered, stimulating the local industry to grow.

A similar line of reasoning is adopted by the vice president of agricultural industry sales for Latin America at CNH, Paulo Arabian. For him, trade liberalization tends to expand opportunities for both blocs, favoring access to new technologies, increased competitiveness and greater productive integration.

The reduction of trade barriers should be accompanied by a greater influx of technologies, components, and precision agriculture systems, says Cesar Roberto Guimarães de Oliveira, sales and marketing manager at Agritech. "The trend is for increased pressure on the national industry to gain efficiency and expand competitiveness," he says.

In his view, the full implementation of the agreement will have to combine market opening, more trade competition, and the need to adapt to EU technical and regulatory requirements.

Lucas Zanetti, product marketing manager at Massey Ferguson, believes that the agreement will require advances in production efficiency, sustainability, and traceability. "It's a positive move that stimulates innovation and strengthens the competitiveness of Brazilian agriculture on the international stage," he says.
