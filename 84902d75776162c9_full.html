# Brazil Adopts 'Dangerous Confrontational Narrative' with the US

Donald Trump lost a battle in February when the Supreme Court ruled his tariff announcement from a year ago illegal – but the US President hasn't given up on the trade war. The United States Trade Representative (USTR) – an agency subordinate to the White House – opened a series of investigations last year into possible irregular practices by trade partners, and Brazil is the target of some of them. The decision should come by July, and in the opinion of diplomat Marcos Troyjo – former Secretary of Foreign Trade and International Affairs – the Lula Government has been negligent in defending the interests of national exporters. For the economist and political scientist, the government's major motivation is confrontation with the US to gain popularity, in an attempt to revive "the political-electoral effect" from last year. "What makes the situation more sensitive is that Brazil is embracing a dangerous confrontational narrative," Troyjo told Brazil Journal. "I can't remember a time when the two largest economies on the continent, which are also the two largest democracies in the West, were so far apart," said Troyjo, who has just been appointed to the United Nations Foundation board.

Following are the main excerpts from the conversation.

There were signs of rapprochement between the Lula Government and the Trump Government last year, but Brazil continues under the threat of retaliation motivated by USTR investigations. Did the conversations not progress?

I can't remember a time when the two largest economies on the continent, which are also the two largest democracies in the West, were so far apart. It seems to me that now the Brazilian Government's major motivation is to use this approaching situation, with the result of the US trade representative's investigation, to revive the political-electoral effect from last year. The President of the Republic was clearly on a declining trajectory from January until July, and from August onwards, he managed to reverse the downward trend in a way, but by assuming a posture that helps very little with the real interests of the Brazilian economy – but which, from a political-electoral point of view, can yield results.

How could this attitude be reflected in the decision that will soon be issued by USTR investigations?

The Supreme Court decided at the beginning of the year that the tariffs imposed by Trump in April last year, invoked under a law called IEEPA (International Emergency Economic Powers Act), can only have limited duration. Maintaining them requires Congressional approval, a path Trump is unlikely to pursue – for a number of reasons, including the midterm elections in November. What Trump has at hand right now are investigations based on Section 301 of the Trade Act. So what should we be doing right now? We should be working behind the scenes, instructing our diplomats in Washington, we should be sending missions. Finance Minister Dario Durigan recently said he hopes the USTR investigation won't be mere "theater" to justify imposing tariffs. Will the decision ultimately be political?

You only need to spend 24 hours in Washington to perceive there's a lot of political electricity in the air. It's impossible to separate politics and geopolitics from commercial measures. If war is the continuation of politics by other means, as Von Clausewitz stated, geopolitics is also politics by other means. So obviously there are political elements – and these elements are guided by economic objectives. But the fact is that, in various sectors, we are much more protectionist than the Americans. There are also specific disputes – for example, regarding the technology of bank transfers. Another case is the Brazilian barrier against imported ethanol, which could have been removed years ago, but which, in American eyes, remains because of lobbying by producers in the Northeast. What makes the situation more sensitive is that Brazil is embracing a dangerous confrontational narrative. This is because the Government remembers that it managed to reverse a trajectory of declining popularity when it assumed this rhetoric last year. But Brazilian exporters who have the US market as their main destination for their highest value-added goods could pay an enormous price. If we look at trade numbers between the countries, never in the last hundred years have the US had such a small share of our exports. It fell to a single digit. The US has a GDP of $31 trillion. Even with all recent protectionism, they still import 12% of GDP – about $3.7 trillion. How much is Brazil occupying of that slice? Less than 1%. [In 2025, Brazilian exports to the US totaled $37.7 billion, compared to $40.4 billion in 2024.]

Which sectors could be spared from a tariff increase? Those more relevant to the American economy?

In some cases, American buyers have no alternatives for certain products supplied by Brazilian producers. I'm not just talking about coffee, orange juice, and aircraft. I'm also talking about beef, furniture, wood derivatives, certain items for engine components. It's not simple to replace products that are part of links in a long production chain. In this case, one of the actions the Brazilian Government could take to facilitate this negotiation – but it's the companies that are having to fend for themselves, forming sectoral coalitions. If there's no greater diplomatic effort, what are the possible consequences for trade between the countries?

One effect is the geographic diversification of Brazilian producers, who can move part of their operations to other countries that have more advantageous access to the US – like Paraguay, which today has excellent relations with the Trump Government. This is happening. There are protein companies, for example, with operations in Argentina and other jurisdictions. Plants in Brazil are destined for China and the domestic market, while the meatpacking plant can sell to the US from Argentina or Uruguay. Another negative effect is that the US, in terms of direct investment, is still the dominant actor, despite China being Brazil's largest trade partner. What incentive does this situation provide for an American investor? To keep the handbrake pulled, look at other opportunities, reduce Brazil's specific weight in the person's allocation portfolio. Who should Brazil be negotiating with?

You can't center everything on one interlocutor. It's important to distribute – and also use political representation channels in Washington, which are very common in the American capital's ecosystem. Going back to the geopolitical question, what bargaining power is there in negotiations with the US? Can rare earth reserves, for example, be used as a 'bargaining chip'?

Rare earths can indeed be used. Look at what China did. When the US threatened tariffs of 145%, the Chinese communicated at the negotiation table that they would impose restrictions...
