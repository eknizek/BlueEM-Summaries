# Foreign Investors Drive Petrobras Higher, but Managers Warn of Oil Price Risk

The massive inflow of foreign capital into the Brazilian stock exchange has directly benefited Petrobras (PETR4), driven by rising oil prices amid Middle East conflicts. However, national fund managers warn of the passive nature of this flow. Since foreign money enters via indices, it automatically purchases the highest-weighted stocks, ignoring specific fundamentals that may deteriorate over the long term.

Cesar Paiva (Real Investor) and Pedro Gonzaga (Mantaro Capital) discussed this scenario on the Stock Pickers podcast, presented by Lucas Collazo. They observe that although crude oil operates between $110 and $120 per barrel, historical trends suggest a return to the $50 to $60 range. According to the experts, changing portfolio strategy merely to ride this temporary wave could be a costly mistake for investors.

Maintaining investment philosophy discipline, regardless of market sentiment, was the main lesson reinforced at the Berkshire Hathaway meeting. Paiva argues that focus should remain on well-managed companies with growing profits at fair prices. For him, this "golden rule" is what protects wealth against sharp fluctuations caused by geopolitical events or capital migration flows.

On the domestic front, upcoming elections emerge as a possible value catalyst. However, Pedro Gonzaga sees a deeper movement: institutional deterioration in mature markets like the United States is forcing global investors to diversify their portfolios. In this context, Brazil emerges as a viable alternative for capital seeking refuge and real assets. The resilience of Brazilian companies, which have survived successive crises in recent years, combined with institutions that have responded with maturity to challenges, creates a rare window of opportunity.

Another highlighted point was the risk of compromising decades of history with a single wrong decision late in the journey. Pedro Gonzaga emphasized how Buffett maintained rigor in his latest bets, such as Apple and Japanese companies, ensuring that the compound interest effect was not interrupted by late-stage judgment errors. This, he adds, is a warning for investors seeking shortcuts at the end of the cycle.

Regarding the future of the Omaha event, managers signal they should return in 2027, though they note the meeting is changing profile. With smaller attendance and less "glamour" after Buffett stepped off the stage, the tendency is for the event to become more technical and focused on investors dedicated to value philosophy. This change, they say, could favor deeper interactions and richer exchanges of experience.
