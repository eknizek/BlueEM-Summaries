# As attacks on Banco de la República intensify, experts believe interest rates should rise even further

Analysts point out that increases would be more moderate, in a context of tensions with the Government and persistence of inflation.

Awaiting the next meeting of the Banco de la República board, scheduled for Thursday, April 30, the debate centers on whether the cycle of increases will continue and to what extent. The decision will depend on the recent behavior of inflation and the signals sent by the economic environment. Although it is uncertain whether the board will meet due to the boycott announced by the Minister of Finance, Germán Ávila, in the event that a meeting does take place, the experts consulted refer to the decisions that could be taken.

The boycott is one of the actions that analysts consider attacks on the independence of the Banco de la República, and which had an additional episode in the cabinet council this week in which President Gustavo Petro said that a co-director of the central bank, who was appointed by him, "was a guerrilla fighter for the EPL and is now a fascist."

In response to these attacks during a debate in Congress, the General Manager of the Banco de la República, Leonardo Villar, lamented the deterioration of the debate and arguments and asked the National Government to "lower the tone and language with which the relationship with the Banco de la República is being handled."

## Expectations for the meeting

Among analysts there is agreement that there is margin for new increases, although with less intensity compared to recent adjustments. For Juan David Ballén, director of Economics and Markets at Aval Asset Management, the bulk of the cycle would have already been executed. "We believe that the Banco de la República has already done most of the rate adjustment in this cycle, so the space remaining going forward is limited. In that context, a pause or increases of less than 100 basis points would not be a surprise," he explained.

The analysis is also based on an environment in which inflation expectations remain above 6.26%, more than double the target of 3%, which limits the possibility of cuts in the short term and keeps the discussion open on new adjustments.

In the same vein, Luis Fernando Mejía, CEO of Lumen Economic Intelligence, believes that the most likely scenario in the short term would be a pause. "I think that in April the most likely scenario is a pause. The Banco de la República already made a significant rate adjustment in the January and March meetings, which gives it room to evaluate with more information the recent evolution of inflation and expectations," he said.

According to his explanation, the Board could choose to wait for new information before making additional decisions. "The Board could wait for data from April and May before deciding whether a new increase is necessary in June," he noted, referring to the need to assess the trajectory of inflation and its persistence.

## Inflation maintains upward bias

Other analysts believe that, despite that margin, current conditions continue to justify restrictive monetary policy. José Ignacio López, president of ANIF, argued that the economy faces pressures that make faster inflation reduction difficult. "I think that if there is a next meeting, there would be a need to raise rates a bit more. Probably a 50 basis point increase would be appropriate," he said.

According to his explanation, there is an "inflationary inertia" that concentrates especially in the services sector, where prices have shown greater persistence.

A firmer position is that of Jorge Restrepo, who believes that the Bank should maintain the trajectory of increases. "In the April meeting, the Banco de la República must continue with a trajectory of rate increases," he stated.

Restrepo explained that inflation expectations remain elevated compared to what was projected months ago. "Around 3 percentage points for expected inflation in December of this 2026, a 7% expected inflation rate, when [the text cuts off]"
