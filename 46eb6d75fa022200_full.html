# Court of Accounts in Minas Gerais Halts Copasa Privatization Pending Final Ruling

The TCE-MG (Court of Accounts of the State of Minas Gerais) vetoed on Thursday (16th) that the Minas Gerais government conclude the privatization process of Copasa, the state sanitation company, before a final ruling from the court. The decision, made by the plenary based on the vote of councilor Agostinho Patrus, gives the green light for the state government to proceed with preparatory acts, which include conducting studies, audits, and sending documents to the CVM (Securities and Exchange Commission) and B3. However, it prohibits any act that results in the alienation of Copasa's controlling stake or the opening of a stock offering period before a conclusive ruling.

"It is important to highlight that this decision does not authorize privatization as a whole, but only specific stages that can be reversed. All definitions that will still emerge along the way, especially those that will be included in the sales notices, will be subject to rigorous oversight," says Patrus's vote, followed by the other councilors.

The court's approval was considered the final legal stage of the process. The expectation was that the Minas Gerais government would announce the details of the offering in the coming days so that the stock sale would happen by mid-May. With these prohibitions, the timeline may be revised. When contacted, the Minas Gerais Government had not responded by the time of publication.

Beyond the prohibition, the TCE-MG determined that any measure exceeding the limits of a preparatory act must be communicated to the court with 48 hours' notice. In an interview with journalists after the session, councilor Durval Ângelo stated that the publication of the privatization notice, for example, does not constitute a preparatory act and is therefore prohibited at this time.

"The pace of the process now depends on Copasa," he said. "Since the matter has already been considered by the plenary, any measure he [the government] takes from here on must return to the plenary. Whether to authorize or deny it. The matter today is hostage to the collegial body."

The opposition celebrated the decision. "Today the Court of Accounts put the brakes on the anything-goes approach the government was taking with Copasa," said state deputy Bella Gonçalves (PT). "The auction, which was scheduled to happen on the 23rd [of May], is no longer viable and every act of privatization will have to be communicated with 48 hours' notice," she added.

## Understanding Copasa's Privatization

Today, the Minas Gerais Government is Copasa's controlling shareholder, with 50.3% of the company. Under the privatization model—which will still be detailed—the state will maintain a residual stake of 5%. Of the 45% of Copasa to be offered, 30% is destined for a reference shareholder, which is a type of strategic partner—as Equatorial does with Sabesp in São Paulo. The other 15% will be offered to the market via bookbuilding, a process at the Stock Exchange where investors indicate the quantity of shares they want to buy. The reference investor must acquire the 30% stake alone.

People with knowledge of the process say the dispute is concentrated between two groups: Aegea and Sabesp, which should participate in a consortium with Equatorial. The companies have already publicly declared interest in Copasa, considered the last major sanitation asset to be offered to the private sector. Nevertheless, participation is not guaranteed.

In recent days, Sabesp CEO Carlos Piani expressed dissatisfaction with the privatization model of the state company. According to him, the timing would be too tight, and the risks involved in the project would be greater. Aegea, according to what Folha found, is focused on the project and aligning a proposal in partnership with its shareholders: Equipav, GIC (Singapore's sovereign fund), and Itaúsa.

An offer in Copasa's privatization, however, would need to be made amid market doubts about the company's financial situation. In recent days, Aegea released its 2025 balance sheet with a worsening in leverage (ratio between debt and Ebitda) and sparked an alert among rating companies and analysts by resubmitting its 2024 financial information with a write-down of R$5 billion.

For Aegea, the assessment is that buying Copasa could even help ease the debt indicator, since the asset would enter the portfolio generating substantial revenue immediately. Since the check to be disbursed by the reference shareholder will be large (approximately R$7 billion), Aegea should use resources from one of its shareholders. The strategy is similar to the one adopted in the Cedae privatization in Rio de Janeiro, where the company won two lots in consortiums with its shareholders.

Despite the Minas Gerais government's interest in seeking a reference shareholder, Copasa could end up becoming a corporation (where there is no principal controller, as happened with Eletrobras). This is because the model should provide for a price comparison between the two phases of the process. If the stock price in the bookbuilding process exceeds the price offered by the strategic partner, all shares offered by the Minas Gerais government will be sold to the general market, resulting in capital dispersion on the Stock Exchange—without a reference investor. The mechanism would be a kind of protection to prevent the government from "leaving money on the table."
