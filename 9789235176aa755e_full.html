# At Alliança Saúde, CEO Resigns and Underscores Turbulent Moment

In yet another move that reinforces growing concerns about its operations, Alliança Saúde announced on the morning of Friday, April 24, that Ricardo Sartim, CEO of the diagnostic medicine company, is leaving the corporation, which owns brands such as CDB and Multilab. In addition to being CEO, the executive was serving as interim CFO since November 2025 and was a member of the company's board of directors. According to the company, he cited personal reasons for his decision.

In a material fact disclosure, Alliança Saúde informed that it has already initiated a succession process for the CEO and CFO positions. It also announced the approval of the election of João de Saint Brisson Paes de Carvalho as a new board member until the next general assembly to be held by the group.

Sartim's departure is another chapter in the turbulent period experienced by the company. He was appointed to lead the operation exactly one year ago, replacing Isabella Tanure, daughter of Nelson Tanure. The businessman, in turn, assumed control of Alliança Saúde in 2022, when the group was still called Alliar, with an aggressive inorganic growth agenda. At the time, he pledged his stake as collateral to obtain a loan for the purchase of Ligga Telecom, acquired in 2020.

In February of this year, however, creditors seized Alliança's shares due to deteriorating financial performance of the operation. The shares were transferred to the Tessai fund, a fund linked to Geribá, a manager specializing in special situations. This marked the departure of Tanure from control of the operation and another stage in a sort of "fire sale" by the businessman, who, as with Alliança, saw his stakes in other companies such as Light and Prio transferred to meet creditor obligations.

While still under Tanure's control, Alliança Saúde hired BTG Pactual in December 2025 to advise it in seeking alternatives for restructuring the business, which included a potential sale of the operation, which did not materialize.

At the end of March, after the businessman's departure and the filing of a precautionary action seeking protection against creditors, the company obtained an injunction granting a 60-day period to suspend executions and withdrawals of assets essential to operations.

With a net debt of R$ 499.7 million at the end of the third quarter of 2025, Alliança Saúde postponed the disclosure of the fourth quarter and full-year consolidated balance sheet, citing, among other issues, the need to reflect the effects of the beginning of conversations with creditors. In April, in yet another turn of events, Alliança informed that Siemens Healthineers had unilaterally transferred approximately R$ 11.8 million that were deposited in an account linked to a financing contract between the two parties in October 2025.

"The measures adopted by Siemens have had significant impacts on the company's short-term liquidity, affecting its capacity to timely fulfill certain financial obligations, including commitments to suppliers and medical staff," the group stated at the time.

In the material fact disclosure in question, Alliança emphasized that even after the injunction was granted seeking protection for its liquidity, Siemens continued with "new attempts" and adopting judicial and extrajudicial measures for the "isolated and exclusive satisfaction of its credits."

Also in April, parallel to this turmoil, the company announced the termination of negotiations to acquire Grupo Meddi, a move that was considered extremely strategic for expanding operations, particularly in the Northeast.

Amid this saga, Alliança Saúde's shares have accumulated a 53.2% depreciation over 12 months and more than 40% in 2026. The shares closed yesterday's trading with a decline of 1.96%, quoted at R$ 3, giving the company a market value of R$ 456.9 million.
