# From Hot Coffee to Cold Brew

The strength of Colombia in the market for this beverage and its growth make it necessary to break stereotypes

Francisco Gómez is one of the Colombians who knows the most about coffee

Andrés Orlando Zambrano Díaz

30.04.2026 22:05

Updated: 30.04.2026 22:05

The visit occurs on a Friday and it's clear it's the last working day of the week because the Colcafé offices, in southern Medellín, don't have the accelerated pace of Mondays and especially don't feel the corporate formality of a company that ranks in the top 20 coffee buyers in the world—it's jeans day, or at least it seems that way, and ties and jackets wait in the closet for their turn until next week.

This is how we find Francisco Gómez, president of the coffee business of the Nutresa group, relaxed, although surely with few gaps in his schedule and focused on leading a company that has sales in more than 40 countries.

Gómez has just become the first Colombian to join the National Coffee Association (NCA) of the United States, an organization that includes the most representative coffee roasting and processing companies in the world. This means he is one of five members of the organization's executive committee and sits as an equal with presidents of major chains, such as the emblematic Starbucks, for example.

This is not a minor responsibility, much less honorary, as Gómez himself emphasizes, because it involves tangibly influencing the policies that determine coffee trade in the United States, the most important market in the world.

"This is important because it allows us to have a connection with the administration and the legislature of that country, both at the federal and state levels, to allow the business to continue developing end to end. This means it benefits everyone involved in the production chain. It's a way to make the business sustainable, to have freedom of movement of products and free trade between countries in a sector that is very protected in Latin America, but more open in the United States."

Several milestones have already been achieved in this work of advocating for coffee. Gómez highlights how last year the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) considered coffee as a healthy product. This becomes a powerful marketing argument because, for example, it allows putting a seal on the product that highlights this characteristic. Moreover, in many countries like Colombia, FDA concepts are practically a guarantee of quality and especially safety for the commercialization and visibility of a product.

Another scenario that shows the importance of being in an organization like the NCA was the so-called Liberation Day of the Donald Trump administration, on April 24, 2025, when that government announced the tariff package for all countries that traded with the United States. That day, Colombia entered the group of nations that had a 10 percent surcharge on their products.

"We worked very hard with the United States administration, with the Government and the Legislature to convey the message that coffee was not produced in that country, with the exception of Hawaii, where they do it but in very small volumes. The objective was to achieve lower tariffs or bring them to zero. It was very intense work and we managed to get coffee produced by Colombian farmers and from Brazil, for example, to enter with zero tariff."

A Life with Coffee

To reach this point, Gómez has prepared himself for three decades, always at Colcafé and with a sensitivity for the beverage business, since his grandfather worked for a long time in the chocolate world.

"He was the one who recommended I do my internship here, at Colcafé, after graduating as a production engineer from Eafit. He used to tell me that the coffee world had a very beautiful feeling because of the coffee roots we have."

He started in the factory, which has the austere appearance of all large factories, where cement, brick, metal, steel pipes, enormous tanks—which are in some cases like gigantic coffee makers—and the essential hoppers through which an unimaginable quantity of beans flows form part of the daily landscape. However, there is a fundamental difference that makes the tour pleasant, at least for a coffee enthusiast—the aroma.
