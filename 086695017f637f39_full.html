# Oil fell due to Middle East ceasefire: effects for Argentina on fuel, agriculture, dollar and airfares

International crude oil prices fell around 10% on Friday to the 90 dollar per barrel range on Brent, the reference for Argentina, due to the ceasefire in the Middle East war and the announcement of the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz. While one flower does not make spring, the hope of achieving lasting peace could have an economic impact on Argentina through fuel prices, agricultural and industrial costs, and dollar inflows. Additionally, airfares, which rose up to 30% over the last month and a half, could decrease.

## Sector by sector, the impact of oil

On the most visible front, fuel, the short-term impact is null. Oil companies maintain a valid price stability agreement for 45 days that extends until mid-May, so there will be no increases in gasoline and diesel during this period. But neither are decreases expected. The sector had already anticipated that it would not pass on any eventual drop in crude to pumps, since it also did not pass on in March and April all the increases that occurred in the world. In this scheme, the drop in oil operates more as a factor that moderates future expectations than as an immediate adjustment. If the barrel sustains at lower levels, it could slow down the pace of price updates once the current agreement expires. Gasoline and diesel prices reflect a barrel of between $92 and $94, despite reaching $110 during some weeks of the war.

The impact is more indirect but relevant for the agricultural sector. Diesel represents one of the main operational costs of farming, both in the production stage and in transport logistics. Current stability prevents new increases during the middle of the campaign. In addition, there is the effect on fertilizers, a key input for agricultural production. Nitrogenous fertilizers, such as urea, depend on natural gas for their production, so they tend to follow the dynamics of energy prices; furthermore, almost 35% of urea was exposed to the conflict in Iran.

Another relevant channel is liquefied natural gas (LNG) imports, which Argentina uses to cover peak demand during winter. The price of the reference in Europe (TTF) fell 7% to $13.50 per million BTU. Since the Government has not yet awarded the purchase of shipments, it is unclear who will take charge of this business. About 23 ships with liquefied gas are expected to arrive at the Escobar terminal, for which between 700 and 800 million dollars in total will be paid, a cost that the industry would assume.

All told, the energy balance could have a jump of approximately 2,000 million extra this year, taking into account the rise in oil prices, which were at $60 at the beginning of 2026 and are now expected at $75 in the medium term. Similarly, airline tickets, which have fuel as one of their main costs, could become cheaper again after jumping up to 30% due to the Middle East war. Fuel represents approximately 40% of total operating costs on longer-range flights, so it affects international flights more than domestic ones. Unlike other inputs, it is not elastic; companies cannot decide to consume less to save if they want to maintain their frequencies.

In summary, the fall in oil introduces potential relief on several fronts, but with gradual and heterogeneous effects. In fuel, there are no immediate changes; in agriculture, a perspective of more contained costs opens; in energy, LNG imports become cheaper and oil exports shrink; and on the external front, lower dollar inflows are expected.
