# A new geography of wine: northeastern Brazilian terroirs begin to gain ground

Much is said about the wine boom in the Southeast, but little is heard about wines from the Northeast. Although the first experiences happened in the 1970s in the São Francisco Valley, the region has not remained stuck in the past. A new wave of expansion is advancing through northeastern states beyond the river's margins.

"They treat northeastern wines as if they were all the same thing, but they're not," says agronomist Walter Leal, from Vitti Consultoria em Viticultura, to NeoFeed. "There is enormous diversity." After all, the Northeast is a large mosaic of different climates, soils, and altitudes. From the humidity of the forest zone and the cool mountain ranges of the agreste to the heat of the sertão, each microregion requires an agronomic solution. There are countless projects in different stages of development — some already well established; others still under study.

Brazil is the only country in the world to practice three types of viticulture. The South follows the traditional model typical of temperate areas, with harvests in summer. In the Southeast, Center-West, and in Bahia's Chapada Diamantina, double pruning shifted the vintage to winter. And the São Francisco Valley represents tropical viticulture, where irrigation control allows two or more harvests per year.

One of the new points on the map of Brazilian wines is the Borborema Plateau. Extending through the states of Alagoas, Pernambuco, Paraíba, and Rio Grande do Norte, with altitudes between 500 and 1,200 meters, the place houses "islands" of mild climate in the middle of the semiarid agreste — the high-altitude swamps. There, at 900 meters above sea level, the Pernambucano city of Garanhuns is establishing itself as a pole for wine production and wine tourism. The harvest occurs in summer, exactly as in conventional viticulture. With little rain and good thermal amplitude, the days are so hot that it allows grapes to mature without losing acidity — one of the most difficult characteristics to achieve in northeastern wines.

It all started in 2012, with Embrapa studies to discover new terroirs in the Northeast. "It was the foundation stone of this entire movement," explains to NeoFeed Patrícia Coelho de Sousa Leão, researcher at Embrapa Semiárido, responsible for the research. Although the Borborema is in the band of the globe between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, the vineyards produce one harvest per year — and the result is usually healthy grapes with perfect maturation. Could this be a fourth viticulture system? Agronomists and winemakers prefer to call it high-altitude tropical viticulture, just a variation.

## Award-winning Labels

The first winery in Garanhuns was Vale das Colinas. In 2013, during a wine course, the couple of doctors Michel Moreira Leite and Micheline Cavalcante Silva had the idea of planting vineyards. When they approached Embrapa, they learned about the research in the city. "We left enchanted," Michel tells NeoFeed. Thus was born, in 2017, Vale das Colinas, the first winery in the Pernambucano agreste and of all Borborema. Today, Michel and Micheline have 15 hectares of grapes — which is a lot compared to the two-hectare average size of wineries in Brazil. With 38 employees, Vale das Colinas produced 29,000 bottles in 2025 and received 26,000 tourists. Several of the wines produced by the couple have already been awarded, such as the red Dona Elisa Gran Reserva 2024, a 100% malbec, and the white Dona Cecília 2024, a blend of muscat petit grain and moscato giallo, both gold medals at the Grande Prova de Vinhos do Brasil 2026.

About 300 kilometers north, still on the Borborema, is Bananeiras, in Paraíba, another microregion that is beginning to get attention. The municipality was not part of Embrapa's research and entered the wine map of the Northeast through the initiative of two or three businesspeople. The pioneer was Johan Gonçalves, founder of Vinícola Gonçalves in 2023. After passing his business in João Pessoa to his children, he bought land in the region in 2022. "I wanted an activity," he tells NeoFeed. "And I thought about making wine." For now, he has only done vinifications for tests. Commercial harvests only when the construction of the industrial unit is finished.

Partner at the Alteza Condo Resort in Bananeiras, lawyer Telson Ferreira decided to create Vinícola Casa Ferreira in 2023, after traveling with his wife and a couple of friends to Serra Gaúcha. "We are making history by planting wine grapes in the interior of Paraíba," he tells NeoFeed. In 2024, he planted the first vines. And at the end of 2025 and beginning of 2026, he harvested his first crop. He took the grapes to be vinified at Vinícola Mello, in Garanhuns, by Chilean winemaker Cristián Sepulveda, from the winery and consultancy Terra Nossa de Espírito Santo do Pinhal, in the São Paulo portion of the Mantiqueira Mountains — responsible for the production of much of the wines from the new producing regions. "When he saw the grapes, he told me I had found a good terroir," the lawyer says proudly. Those who produce say that malbec and sauvignon blanc do very well in Bananeiras, but there is no scientific study yet on varieties or management in that microterroir. It is early, therefore, to determine a style for the wines.

## Dodging the Humidity

Of the northeastern projects, perhaps the most surprising is that of vineyards near the coast. Very humid, these areas have always been considered unsuitable for grape cultivation — especially vinifera grapes, which are more delicate and are exposed to all sorts of diseases. But there are those willing to take the risk. In Rio Grande do Norte, just 30 kilometers from Natal and 47 from Pipa beach, in São José do Mipibu, the Casa das 7 Evas winery is already in operation, although it is not yet completely finished. But why make wine there? Beyond the advancement of agricultural technologies that are spreading vineyards throughout Brazil, one must take into account the pioneering spirit of some entrepreneurs.
