# Brazil Joins Calls for Venezuela to Rejoin Mercosur as Debate Intensifies

Paraguay and Uruguay are also in agreement. Only Argentina disagrees.

With five days remaining before the provisional trade agreement between Mercosur and the European Union takes effect on May 1st, other debates are intensifying within the Southern Cone bloc, including the possibility of Venezuela's reentry into the alliance.

The Caribbean nation had entered as a full member "by default" in 2012 and was suspended in 2017, also in that manner, when the Ushuaia Protocol was applied—an internal democratic clause triggered by rising repression against the opposition by Nicolás Maduro.

Now bolstered by Donald Trump's protection of interim president Delcy Rodríguez, who has been in power since January following Maduro's capture, it was the center-right Vice President of Lula da Silva, Geraldo Alckmin, who spoke of a "different moment" for Venezuela. This could lead to an end to its regional isolation, something that three of the four founding members of the bloc consent to—minus Javier Milei's Foreign Ministry.

"Venezuela joined Mercosur, was suspended, and now entering a different moment, this will be rediscussed," Alckmin told several media outlets in Brasilia during ongoing dialogue between delegations.

While diplomatic sources indicate that Brazil's Foreign Ministry favors moving slowly with Venezuela's reentry into Mercosur, this is the first time the Lula administration has expressed such a position. Paraguay's government under Santiago Peña had already done so—a regional ideological ally of Milei who is nonetheless moving toward accepting Caracas, now legitimized by Donald Trump.

Last March, Paraguay's Minister of Industry and Commerce, Marco Riquelme, surprised with a statement favorable to advancing on this matter, a position also shared by Brazil and Uruguay.

"Regarding Venezuela's reincorporation into Mercosur, we fully agree and it is one of the initiatives we will raise during Paraguay's pro tempore presidency." That country heads Mercosur until mid-year.

In Uruguay, the position is also favorable, and conversations are being prepared accordingly.

Argentina's stance is the most curious because, while Economy Minister Luis Caputo had given the country's approval for the International Monetary Fund to resume formal contact with Venezuela—a pariah of political, financial, and economic organizations under Maduro—regarding Mercosur reentry, the Argentine government's position is contradictory.

There were two meetings of Argentine ruling party legislators in Parlasur with Rubén Javier Ruffi, institutional director of Mercosur at the Foreign Ministry. He conveyed that the Argentine government does not want Venezuela's entry. Two things occurred in the meetings: on one hand, the libertarian Parlasur members were angry with the Foreign Ministry for being called to a meeting for the first time after two years of government, and only "to be used." On the other hand, they reminded them that the government had stopped paying parliamentary travel allowances, preventing them from holding sessions.

The reason given for the Foreign Ministry's refusal—it is unknown whether this is a position agreed upon with the Pink House and Minister Caputo—is that Venezuela's reentry into Mercosur would represent competition in "energy" matters, because its energy potential and the immense U.S. interest in that country could—according to their conversation with diplomat Ruffi—clash with Argentina's interests in Vaca Muerta, although the hydrocarbon structures and potential differ greatly between the two countries.

It was precisely through Parlasur that the debate over Venezuela's reentry into Mercosur emerged. Unión por la Patria parliamentarian Gabriel Fuks, former deputy and former president of Cascos Blancos, was the first to present a motion for creating a committee for rapprochement with Venezuela, which, after being approved, was headed by Paraguayan legislator Rodrigo Gamarra and enabled the first meetings with Venezuelan parliamentary authorities in Panama, to continue throughout this year.

Nevertheless, while the Mercosur-European Union trade agreement begins taking effect this week by decision of the European Commission presidency, but will be analyzed by EU courts, Venezuela cannot be part of it. Nor can Bolivia, which is already a full member of the Southern bloc. This occurs because they were not fully involved in the trade agreement negotiations, which should be conducted separately in order to potentially incorporate them later if that agreement is in effect.
