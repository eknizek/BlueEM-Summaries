SulAmérica advances in interior Brazil to reach R$35 billion revenue target

# SulAmérica Advances in Interior Brazil to Reach R$35 Billion Revenue Target

Responsible for almost 60% of Rede D'Or's revenue in 2025, SulAmérica has set its growth strategy to reach R$35 billion in billing in 2026. And the path passes through regionalized plans, outside the traditional axis, such as São Paulo and Rio de Janeiro, with lower costs, but also with lower margins for reimbursements of consultations and exams.

The model consists of guaranteeing access to regional care networks - except for urgent and emergency needs, which have national coverage. An elective surgery, for example, can only be performed by local providers in the plan.

"We are looking at other regions, beyond the obvious ones, like Parauapebas [in Pará] and Feira de Santana [Bahia], which were not on our map," says Raquel Reis, CEO of SulAmérica, in an interview with NeoFeed. "This ensures care for many people and also helps us reduce reimbursements, since the person will be covered and will rarely need to seek a doctor outside our network," she adds.

Last year, the insurance and health plan operator achieved net revenue of R$33.2 billion, a 10.5% increase over 2024. EBITDA of R$2.3 billion grew 75.7%. Operating profit reached R$2.14 billion, 85.6% higher than the previous year.

Currently, SulAmérica has 5.9 million beneficiaries in Brazil, with 3.2 million in the health area and 2.7 million customers in the dental plan. Of the medical plan volume, two million are linked to large registered companies, and 1.2 million are customers of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs). And precisely to surpass the 6 million customer mark, the focus is now on small businesses, mainly in the regionalized model.

Over the past two years, the SME segment has recorded double-digit growth. "The corporate segment is very solid and defined. And the growth lever is exactly the beneficiaries who come from small and medium-sized enterprises, with plans that offer lower prices, adjusted regionally," says the executive.

With this, the company also wants to increase the rate of beneficiaries in copayment plans, where the monthly fee is 20% lower, and the patient pays part of the cost for consultations and exams. In the corporate segment, with over 200 lives, this modality already represents 80%. In SMEs, it's still 40%.

"This is not intended to discourage use. On the contrary. What we want is for the medical plan to be used correctly, with conscious utilization," she says.

According to Reis, SulAmérica's own employees — about 4,000 — and more than 80,000 from Rede D'Or, plus their dependents, are enrolled in the copayment modality. "Everyone has it, at all levels. From call center analysts to the Board President," she assures.

This ends up generating a change in the patient's own behavior and eliminating another problem, which is waste in exam requests. The estimate is that 20% to 25% of exams are never picked up at laboratories. When the patient pays a portion, the chance that they "forget" to go pick it up is lower.

Along with this more personalized medical plan model, SulAmérica, which was acquired in 2022 by Rede D'Or, also has as a growth lever the direct credentialing of Atlântica D'Or hospitals, a joint venture formed by the company founded by Jorge Moll and Bradesco. Currently, the new network has six hospitals (three in operation, which were incorporated, and three under construction). This year, units should be inaugurated in Ribeirão Preto and Taubaté, in São Paulo. Rede D'Or alone has 79 hospitals in 13 states today.

"Increasingly, we stop identifying as an insurer and start identifying as a healthcare company. And these Rede D'Or hospitals are an expansion base that helps us have greater negotiating power," says SulAmérica's CEO.

Data from the National Supplementary Health Agency (ANS) show that today 53 million people in Brazil are covered by some private health plan. With dental plans, there are 35 million more. Although this is a significant number, larger than Spain's population, for example, the coverage rate of medical plans is 25% relative to the 231 million residents in Brazilian cities. This shows the scope of growth that SulAmérica intends to pursue.

## Crusade Against Fraud

If the planned growth involves a reduction in the volume of reimbursements and more conscious use of the medical plan, SulAmérica still expends considerable energy fighting the problem of fraud. For this, the company developed a plan to combat irregularities, which includes increased use of artificial intelligence (AI) tools, improved audits, and implementation of anti-fraud governance.

In three years, the company has managed to prevent R$450 million from being spent on improper payments. Based on these actions, approximately six thousand beneficiaries were de-registered from SulAmérica between 2023 and 2025.

The results of initiatives against illegal medical reimbursement requests helped plummet the sinistral rate in health and dental operations. Last year, the indicator reached 79.5%, an advance of 3.2 percentage points over 2024, and 7.8 points less compared to 2023.

"We conducted a witch hunt against illegalities in reimbursements and medical procedure requests, and filed 800 criminal complaints against beneficiaries and service providers from 2023 until now," says Reis.

Fighting illegalities in reimbursement requests, however, is not a problem only for SulAmérica. The private health sector, which today has 670 operators with beneficiaries, faces this crisis. An estimate from the Brazilian Association of Health Plans (Abramge) shows that between 2019 and 2025, fraud related to reimbursements alone caused losses exceeding R$12.9 billion to companies.

"Abramge expresses concern with the impacts of reimbursement fraud on the supplementary health system, which generate significant losses and compromise the economic-financial balance of the sector," says the entity in a note to NeoFeed.

Beyond fraud, SulAmérica has also turned against the high cost of medicines and court decisions that force operators to fully cover the purchase for beneficiaries. According to the executive, it is not reasonable that a pharmaceutical industry should not share responsibility for the high costs of medications provided to patients.
