Citi sees opportunities for Latin America amid Middle East shock

15 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Citi sees opportunities for Latin America amid Middle East shock

The escalation of the conflict in the Middle East has generated turbulence in global markets, but Latin America is in a relatively comfortable position to weather the storm and has less to lose than other geographies. This is the assessment of Ernesto Revilla, chief economist for Latin America at Citigroup, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

"Even if the conflict with Iran is bad for everyone in the global economy, Latin America is relatively well positioned compared to other regions, particularly in relation to Asia and Europe," said Revilla.

The geographic distance from the conflict and the oil-producing structure of several countries in the region are among the protective factors cited by the economist. Furthermore, improvements in macroeconomic fundamentals — such as floating exchange rates and more robust international reserves — form a protective cushion for the region that did not exist in previous crises.

Citi's base scenario predicts that oil prices should decline from mid-June onwards, then normalize significantly. "We expect the impact of the conflict on oil prices to last a few more weeks, but for the ceasefire to hold, albeit in an unstable manner," said the economist.

In this context, regional growth should not be much different from the previous year, and inflation should not rise significantly. This is partly because governments in most countries in the region have some level of control over fuel price pass-throughs to consumers, absorbing the shock through fiscal pressure rather than direct inflation.

However, there is a more turbulent scenario in which the war extends beyond expectations, pressuring oil prices and inflationary impact. One of the major risks in this situation would be the possibility of higher interest rates in the United States, the world's largest economy.

"The worst scenario would be if the shock were large enough for the market to reprice expectations about U.S. interest rates," said Revilla. "If there were a shift toward an expectation of higher rates in the U.S., it could be very disruptive for the region."

## Brazil: expectation of rate cuts continues

In Brazil's case, Revilla praised the Central Bank's stance, which cut the basic interest rate by 0.25 percentage points in March to 14.75% per year. "The Central Bank of Brazil has demonstrated extraordinary prudence. It is a highly respected institution that has earned its prestige as an independent conductor of monetary policy," he said.

Still, Citi maintains its projection of a terminal rate for Brazil at 12% per year, with upside revision risk in the next round of analyses. "Given that Brazil was the country that would cut rates the most in 2026 before the Iran conflict, there is still room for maneuver once the scenario becomes a bit clearer," said Revilla.

Beyond the external context, Brazil faces the challenge of presidential elections, as does Colombia. Both countries, according to the economist, share a central problem: the fiscal trajectory is on an unsustainable path and the market demands clear signaling of change — from whoever wins the election.

"The market doesn't care so much about the color of the party that wins, but rather about the policies [implemented]. And in both cases, the market is waiting for fiscal announcements as a first priority," he said.

For Revilla, there is potential for increased investor confidence and greater flows of foreign capital to emerging markets depending on the measures announced shortly after the elections.

## Weak dollar and the structural window

One factor that has been surprisingly positive is the behavior of the dollar. Contrary to expectations that Trump administration tariffs would strengthen the American currency, the dollar has weakened over recent months, and this movement has been a favorable wind for Latin American currencies and assets.

"Latin American currencies have performed extraordinarily well in the face of many shocks, including tariffs, geopolitical reconfiguration, and the Iran conflict, precisely because of the weak dollar," said Revilla.

In the medium term, the economist believes the dollar will gradually resume its position of strength as a global reserve currency since "there is no alternative" to replace the American currency. The expectation is that this will occur over the next 12 months.

For Revilla, the moment also opens a more structural window of opportunity for the region. With the United States decoupling from parts of Asia and Europe, and without the capacity to produce everything internally, Latin America emerges as a natural supplier — of traditional commodities, but also of lithium, rare earths, and manufactured products.

"The United States cannot produce everything internally, neither manufactured goods nor commodities. And when you think about it, Latin America is perfectly positioned to supply a dynamic American economy with what it cannot produce," he said.

The economist affirms that the moment is one of opportunity for the region to be a structural destination for investments. "It's good to finally see Latin America not being the center of concerns or vulnerability."
