# Brazil Fails to Recognize That Value Addition Is No Longer Just Industrialization

One of the obsessions of the Brazilian left is that Brazil's development project must focus on adding value to national primary products: it is necessary to transform minerals, oil and agricultural products rather than export them in raw form. The principle has its correct side, but times have changed and with them, geopolitics and the concept of value to add.

Until recently, the idea of value addition almost always boiled down to advancing industrialization. There was even a former Finance Minister who believed that strength in commodity exports was one of the causes of deindustrialization, because they brought too many dollars and, with them, excessive appreciation of the national currency (Dutch disease), which in turn undermined the competitiveness of industrialized products.

Only now do Brazilian authorities seem to have understood the need to exploit rare earths. Until now, the sector deserved no attention or planning.

This is why so many developmentalist economists, not just from the PT, were denouncing what they understood as the transformation of Brazil "into a farm" or a backward country, an extractor of minerals. Things have changed greatly. Industrialization was and continues to be important, but no longer as an essential agent of value addition. Even more important has become the services sector. The major suppliers of computers and cell phones no longer insist on producing them internally. They concentrate on project development in their own Silicon Valleys and then relegate assembly to other places, which also applies to shoes, especially sneakers or clothing items.

New food security requirements have given great importance not only to production capacity, but also to the export of food commodities, such as soybeans, corn, wheat, coffee, sugar and meat. The United States, still the world's largest economic power, is the largest producer of agricultural commodities, and yet does not feel like "a farm."

It has been recalled several times in this space that few activities add more value than agriculture and mining. How much value does planting a sack of soybean seeds add? Ore underground is worth nothing, but once extracted, it is worth what it is worth.

Even today, the PT government insists on concentrating precious investments in oil refineries, which in a few more years will lose importance, rather than on accelerating oil production before it's too late. Brazil would benefit more if resources that could be allocated to new refineries were used to build fuel reserves, as the United States and European countries do.

Only now have Brazilian authorities seemed to understand the need to exploit rare earths. Until now, the sector deserved no attention and no planning.

In the end, blindest is he who does not wish to see.
