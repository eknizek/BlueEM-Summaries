# Crypto Sector Opposes Central Bank Recommendation to Restrict Stablecoins and Impose IOF Tax

Companies and associations in the crypto sector expressed concern on Thursday (30) with information that the Central Bank recommended, in a technical note sent to National Congress, the prohibition of stablecoins issued by unregulated companies in the country. The backdrop is the collection of IOF (Financial Operations Tax) on transactions. The subject was debated throughout Thursday afternoon in WhatsApp groups with sector entrepreneurs. The column saw screenshots of part of the conversation. When questioned about the matter also on Thursday afternoon, the Central Bank had not responded at the time.

The recommendation to Congress would be the institutional position of the monetary authority on the subject. The technical note concludes that stablecoins cannot be treated as generic virtual assets (as bitcoin is, for example), since they have an identifiable issuer, promise of stability, and backing in real assets.

Stablecoins are digital assets programmed to maintain stable value, generally pegged to a fiat currency, such as dollar or real, for example. Unlike bitcoin, its function is to serve as a stable medium of exchange and store of value in the digital environment, being issued by private companies.

According to sector companies, the document recommends that stablecoins operate within consolidated regimes of electronic money or bank deposit; that hybrid structures be avoided that compromise the uniqueness of the currency and transparency; that, if there is an intermediate category, it be created by law and supervised by the Central Bank; that it recognize tokenization as something that does not alter rights, risks or obligations, following the economic function, not the technological one.

The restriction would represent, for companies in this market, a defeat for the system that sustains a significant part of operations with crypto assets in the country. The complaint is also that the restriction would benefit foreign issuers at the expense of national ones.

"Stablecoins are global by definition. Restricting access in Brazil without a recognition mechanism would only lead users to channels that Brazil's Central Bank cannot monitor. Ripple believes there is a better path: to affirm the Central Bank's clear authority over stablecoins and apply the same asset segregation protections already established in the electronic money regime. This approach preserves supervision, protects consumers, and keeps Brazil connected to the global market, preserving the nature of stablecoin as a connector of the global economy," says Isabel Sica Longhi, director of public policy and regulation for Latin America at Ripple.

The sector's concern intensified with Fabio Araújo's arrival at Denor (Financial System Regulation Department of the Central Bank). Araújo is an economist and was one of the designers of Real Digital, the Central Bank's digital currency project. His view on stablecoins, according to associations and companies, is that they are not crypto assets, but rather privately issued currencies with an intermediate nature between electronic money and bank deposit.

In the view of entrepreneurs, the Central Bank's trend is to see stablecoins that are not under its jurisdiction as a threat to monetary sovereignty. The technical note prepared by the Central Bank would serve as support for deputy Aureo Ribeiro (Solidariedade-RJ), author of a bill pending in Congress that seeks to regulate the stablecoins market in Brazil.
