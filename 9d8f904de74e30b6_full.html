# Fuel Sector Launches Strategy to Prevent Diesel Shortage

The global price of diesel has suffered a severe impact since the beginning of the war in Iran in late February – primarily due to the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, through which approximately 20% of the world's oil production passes. It became inevitable that the cost of the product for Brazilian consumers would increase, but an intense mobilization by fuel distributors is preventing a problem that would certainly be much more serious: fuel shortage.

Considering that the price increase is also caused by reduced supply in the international market, the sector has designed a complex strategy to ward off the threat of a systemic blackout in vital sectors of the Brazilian economy, such as transportation, agribusiness, and various productive chains. This true race against time involves a series of actions that scarcely reach public knowledge, but are essential to preserve supply and ensure the normality of activities in the country, which imports 30% of the diesel it consumes.

These are strategies that require intensive capital mobilization – extra and unforeseen investments that became necessary given the scenario. This ranges from emergency expansion of logistics infrastructure, involving the activation of more ships, the expansion of tank storage and the contracting of insurance, to the absorption of financial impacts, such as the payment of extremely high premiums in the international market and managing the cost difference in relation to domestic product.

## Continuous Work

Surveillance and strategies will need to be maintained even with a potential ceasefire and full reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, as the average cycle time for negotiation and transport from that region to Brazil is approximately 45 days. It is projected that the global energy chain will face a persistent "logistics hangover," marked by bottlenecks, cargo accumulation, and other issues that may require months of restructuring until global stability is restored.

The scenario was already turbulent due to the conflict in Eastern Europe between Russia and Ukraine, which profoundly altered global petroleum derivative distribution routes, especially due to embargoes and sanctions applied to Russia. This reconfiguration forced nations around the globe, including Brazil, to seek alternative suppliers with new freight matrices, which strangled international supply and, combined with Middle East conflicts, has sustained structural increases in import parity prices.

## Price Surge

Since the beginning of the global shock caused by the Middle East conflict, the price of Brent crude oil surged to the US$120 range, resulting in an increase of approximately 65% in diesel prices on the international market. Additionally, imported fuel costs have shown high volatility, with significant daily variations up or down, which also contributes to making planning difficult.

The imported volume, purchased at inflated prices on the international market to cover the shortfall in the national quota, increases the final "blend" (the cost mixture between domestic and imported product) required to guarantee the completeness of supply in the country.

For all these reasons, anyone who credits the increase in diesel prices in Brazil to mere speculation is certainly poorly informed or acts in bad faith by ignoring all the circumstances described. Major distributors are absorbing the complexity of the structural crisis, yet the sector is often targeted, politicized, and incorrectly framed under the narrative of "inflation villains," with "abusive profits."

Another relevant point that needs to be known by the public is that the impact of distribution on the final price of diesel is around 5%, potentially varying up to 10%, depending on the region and logistics dynamics – in any case, it remains always as a minority fraction.
