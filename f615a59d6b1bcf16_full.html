# How BTG Entered the SpaceX IPO

Last week, BTG Pactual became one of 21 banks selected to participate in SpaceX's IPO. The news—a victory for the bank's ECM franchise—was the culmination of a process that began months ago. When it became clear that there would be a capital opening, André Esteves and bank executives approached the C-Level of Elon Musk's company.

"It wasn't a cold call, obviously. If it had been, it wouldn't have worked. There was already an older relationship between the bank and the company, and between Esteves and Musk," an executive close to Esteves told Brazil Journal. The two had met at international events and grew closer during and after Musk's visit to Brazil in 2022, when the businessman was received by former president Jair Bolsonaro. Esteves was part of the group of businesspeople who participated in the meeting.

At the end of 2025, there was a first virtual meeting between BTG and SpaceX's C-Level. In the conversation, Esteves, Guilherme Paes, who heads the investment bank, Fabio Nazari, the head of equity capital markets, and other BTG executives discussed the bank's track record and how it could help distribute SpaceX shares among institutional and wealth clients in Latin America.

In subsequent meetings, all conducted online, Esteves and executives detailed the international operations the bank had participated in. One was the IPO of a Pershing Square closed-end fund on Euronext in 2014. BTG was the exclusive distributor in the region and placed $200 million of a total offering of $3 billion. This year, the bank is acting in the IPO of Bill Ackman's investment firm and one of its funds. Beyond that, the bank participated in the IPOs of Spanish Cellnex Telecom and the Italian postal service, both in 2015, building goodwill with some global companies and showing clients the bank's access to such operations.

BTG also tried to demonstrate to SpaceX executives the importance of having an "institutional point of contact" in Latin America. This is what BTG pursued in its 2010 private placement, when it raised $1.8 billion from investors who could open doors for the bank in the future—including sovereign wealth funds from Abu Dhabi, Singapore and China, the Canadian pension fund Ontario Teachers Pension Plan, and the Rothschild and Agnelli families.

The kickoff meeting with the company was Monday night. According to Reuters, the plan is to conduct the roadshow in June and distribute up to 30% of shares to retail investors, above the typical range of 5% to 10%. SpaceX also plans to hold an event for 1,500 individual investors, betting that Musk's millions of followers are potential stock buyers.

The company—which in addition to rocket launch operations owns xAI, Starlink, and social network X—is expected to raise around $75 billion and seeks a valuation of approximately $2 trillion. SpaceX's numbers are not yet public, but according to The Information, the company generated $16 billion in revenue and $2 billion in free cash flow last year, mainly thanks to Starlink. The IPO proceeds will finance ambitious projects, such as developing new rockets, AI data centers in space, and a lunar base, in addition to fueling the cash burn of xAI, which recently merged with SpaceX.

"They're not pricing SpaceX based on fundamentals. They're pricing it based on supply scarcity and demand: there's only one SpaceX," analyst Aakash Gupta said on X. "There will never be another rocket monopoly with 10 million broadband subscribers, $24.4 billion in government contracts, and 165 orbital launches last year."

"The Starlink business alone is already generating stable recurring telecom revenue at monopoly scale. It's the foundation. But the premium above $1 trillion is pure optionality: Starship, the Golden Dome anti-missile defense system, orbital AI infrastructure… Investors are paying for futures that don't yet have revenue."

Beyond giving retail a larger slice of the IPO, Musk has another ally to make the offering take off at this stretched valuation. Nasdaq just changed its rules to allow companies with high market value to be included in the Nasdaq 100 after just 15 days of trading—before the change, this period was three months. Since there are numerous ETFs that track this index, being part of it generates additional (and mandatory) demand for shares.
