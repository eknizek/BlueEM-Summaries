# AI levels advertising agencies but does not eliminate human experience, repertoire and creativity

An independent agency, without the financial muscle of major global groups, has already created campaigns for Netflix, Havaianas and Uber. With artificial intelligence, it is gaining even more competitiveness, competing on equal footing with the largest agencies in the market. For Marcelo Rizério, CCO and co-founder of Euphoria Creative, one of the biggest transformations that technology is causing in the advertising market is not in the campaigns themselves, but in the starting point of each agency.

"Ten years ago, a large group agency paid millions for software and platforms. Today it's the same price for everyone," he said to Revolução IA, a NeoFeed program. This leveling, however, does not mean that any agency can deliver the same results. Given equal access to tools, what makes the difference is human experience, repertoire and creativity. The agency has already created 100% AI-produced campaigns, such as Ticket's, and hybrid productions, like Hellmann's, but in neither case was the choice made out of trendiness or to impress clients.

In the Ticket campaign, the script itself played with the concept of the artificial: everything was fake, except the benefits. In Hellmann's, a human hand was deliberately filmed to create a contrast with the rest of the AI-generated production. And although it provides more agility, the use of AI does not eliminate common steps in the production process, such as tests and casting. "The client heard '100% artificial intelligence' and thought it would be ready the following week. We had to sit down and explain: you're still going to choose if the character is male or female, tall or short. AI comes after all that," he says.

All these details are necessary, according to the advertiser, because we are in the era of attention economy, in which a brand does not compete only with its direct competitors. It competes for space with a neighbor's vacation photo, with a football championship result, or with anything that appears in a social media feed. In this scenario, being ignored is the worst possible outcome. Therefore, in Rizério's view, even a campaign that generates rejection serves a purpose, because it still generates conversation, keeps the brand on the radar and feeds the algorithm. It is better to be talked about than forgotten.

An example was the Volkswagen campaign, which brought Elis Regina "back to life" via AI alongside her daughter Maria Rita and which divided opinions in the market, although all the work was done with authorization from the family and rights holders. "Regardless of whether you are for or against it, it became a conversation, became a topic. And at the end of the day, that's what made the campaign and brand relevant," says Rizério.

The risk, however, lies in when technology is used carelessly. AI already allows recreating voices, faces and scenes with a perfection that makes it almost impossible to distinguish the real from the manufactured. "It became too easy to deceive anyone. We're going to enter a moment when we won't be sure anymore what is real and what is not. It's up to our generation to prevent this so the next one doesn't suffer," says Rizério.
