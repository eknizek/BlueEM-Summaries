# AI Levels Advertising Agencies but Does Not Replace Human Experience, Expertise, and Creativity

An independent agency, without the financial muscle of large global groups, has already created campaigns for Netflix, Havaianas, and Uber. With artificial intelligence, it is gaining even more competitiveness, competing on equal footing with the largest agencies in the market. For Marcelo Rizério, CCO and co-founder of Euphoria Creative, one of the biggest transformations that technology is bringing to the advertising market is not in the campaigns themselves, but in the starting point of each agency.

"Ten years ago, a large group agency paid millions for software and platforms. Today it costs the same for everyone," he said on Revolução IA, a NeoFeed program.

However, this leveling does not mean that any agency can deliver the same results. Given equal access to tools, what makes the difference is human experience, repertoire, and creativity. The agency has created campaigns 100% produced with AI, such as Ticket's, and hybrid productions, such as Hellmann's, but in neither case was the choice driven by trends or to impress clients.

In the Ticket campaign, the script itself played with the concept of the artificial: everything was fake, except the benefits. In Hellmann's, a human hand was deliberately filmed to create a contrast with the rest of the AI-generated production. And although it provides more agility, the use of AI does not eliminate common production process steps, such as testing and casting.

"The client heard '100% artificial intelligence' and thought it would be ready the following week. We had to sit down and explain: you still have to choose whether the character is male or female, tall or short. AI comes after all that," he says.

All these details are necessary, according to the advertiser, because we are in the era of the attention economy, where a brand does not only compete with its direct competitors. It disputes space with a neighbor's vacation photo, with a sports championship result, or with anything that appears on a social media feed. In this scenario, being ignored is the worst possible fate.

Therefore, in Rizério's view, even a campaign that generates rejection serves a purpose, because it still generates conversation, keeps the brand on the radar, and feeds the algorithm. It is better to be talked about than forgotten.

An example was the Volkswagen campaign, which brought Elis Regina "back to life" via AI alongside her daughter Maria Rita, and which divided opinions in the market, although all the work was done with authorization from the family and rights holders.

"Regardless of whether you are in favor or against it, that became a conversation, became a topic. And at the end of the day, that's what made the campaign and the brand relevant," says Rizério.

The risk, however, lies in when technology is used carelessly. AI now allows us to recreate voices, faces, and scenes with a perfection that makes it almost impossible to distinguish the real from the fabricated.

"It has become very easy to deceive anyone. We are going to enter a moment where we won't be sure anymore what is real and what is not. It is up to our generation to prevent this so that the next one does not suffer," says Rizério.
