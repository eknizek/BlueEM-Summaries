New open finance model set to revolutionize credit rules in Colombia with data-sharing requirements

13 April 2026 · 09:27 PM

# How the New Open Finance Model Will Work: The Silent Revolution Changing Credit Rules in Colombia

For years, banks in Colombia were the silent owners of their customers' financial data. That has just changed. With the entry into force of Decree 0368 of 2026, entities will be obliged to share that information—always with user authorization—with other actors in the system, opening the door to more competition, new services, and better conditions. It is a structural shift in the rules of the game.

However, amid this fundamental transformation, there is a data point that reveals the true challenge: only 3 out of every 10 Colombians have heard of open finance, the model that promises to redefine their relationship with money and those who manage it, according to a DataCrédito Experian study.

According to the Financial Superintendence, this new framework strengthens three key pillars of the market: free competition, the entry of new actors, and above all, information symmetry—an element that historically had been left behind. In practical terms, this means that data stops being "captive" in a single entity and becomes a user asset.

The most immediate impact will be seen in small and medium-sized enterprises. Today, many depend on a single bank, which concentrates all their financial information. With the new scheme, they will be able to authorize multiple entities to access their data and compete to offer them credit.

"Until now, the bank kept key information such as cash flows or the actual behavior of the business. That is precisely what changes," explains Nicolás Villa, manager of Platam, a fintech that works with transactional data to evaluate companies. This opens the door to more real competition, as multiple providers will evaluate the same business with complete information, which should translate into better rates, more tailored products, and more agile processes. Ultimately, access to credit stops depending on who owns the data and starts depending on how it is used.

## Trust Contract

Although public debate has focused on the technical aspect, the real challenge is trust. The open finance model only works if people are willing to share their information.

Today, that process is just beginning. Lack of awareness is high, but not necessarily negative. The same DataCrédito study shows that when users understand the benefits, their willingness to share data increases significantly, especially if this improves their access to financial products.

Still, concerns persist. According to a digital privacy sector analysis, 62 percent of users fear that their information will be used for purposes other than those authorized, and 43 percent believe they could not easily revoke access to their data. The gap, then, is not only technological, but also one of understanding.

For Nathalia Landeta, manager and founder of Certena, the problem lies in how consent has been understood over time. "For years it was reduced to accepting lengthy and difficult-to-understand terms and conditions. That created a gap between the amount of data circulating and people's actual capacity to control it," she explains.

In this context, the success of open finance will depend on something deeper than regulation: the ability to return real control of their information to users.
