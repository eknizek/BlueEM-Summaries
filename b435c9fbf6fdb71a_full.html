# Embrapa announces Agrishow partnership for free planting in exchange for carbon credits

RIBEIRÃO PRETO (SP) - The Brazilian Agricultural Research Company (Embrapa) announced on Monday, April 27, during Agrishow in Ribeirão Preto (SP), a partnership with a Canadian company that provides for offering free planting to São Paulo farmers aimed at generating carbon credits. The company is Soil and Sky Alliance, which brought to Brazil eight of its small-scale planting machines that promise savings in fertilizers and fuel. One of these machines will be tested at AgNest, an experimental farm maintained by Embrapa in Jaguariúna (SP) to develop, validate and demonstrate new technologies for agriculture.

The other seven pieces of equipment will be made available at zero cost to São Paulo farmers. It will be a win-win relationship, since producers will be enabled to generate carbon credits, which will be sold in Canada and the United States to large companies such as Amazon, Microsoft and Shell. According to Ailton Schoenberger, International Business Director of the company, each planter, which measures 1.5 meters, will have the capacity to plant up to 200 hectares per harvest. As there will be seven machines, the total area of 1,400 hectares should benefit 40 to 50 farmers initially. If results are evaluated as positive, the expectation is to import more machines.

Schoenberger also states that this model works in India and Mexico, but the Brazilian proposal brings a novelty. This is because, in those countries, the machines are sold to landowners, while in Brazil there will be no purchase or rental costs. "We chose Brazil to experiment with this format and Agrishow to make the official launch," explains the director. He also says that the planter is adapted for planting various types of crops.

The pilot program will begin operating during the 2026/27 harvest planting, between September and October this year.

## AgNest

After Agrishow, Schoenberger, who is Brazilian and lives in Canada, intends to spend a month in Jaguariúna to evaluate the results of the planter that will remain at AgNest, a project that began operating in 2024 through a partnership between Embrapa Environment and Impactability. The objective is for the experimental area of 66 hectares to be managed as a commercial farm, so that researchers experience the challenges and perceive the demands of the field on-site and, with that, direct efforts to develop innovations for the sector.

Initially, there were four partner companies. Currently, there are 22, including other Embrapa units, Banco do Brasil and entities such as the Faesp/Senar System, which monitor tests with 18 technological solutions.

Embrapa's president, Silvia Massruhá, states that the partnership is aligned with the institution's objective — which during Agrishow celebrates 53 years of existence — of inserting the generation of carbon credits as a tool to add value to the production system. "In this context, it is important to bring data about the role of innovation in agriculture. In 2025, we evaluated the impact of 200 technologies developed by Embrapa. And we found that for every R$ 1 invested, Brazilian society is rewarded with R$ 27. Furthermore, these technologies generated last year R$ 124 billion in economic impact, 17% of the national Agricultural GDP. Low-carbon agriculture helps show the world that Brazil is an agro-environmental power."

In addition to Soil, another company that became a partner of AgNest at Agrishow was Bosch. Mathias Schelp, Vice President of Intelligent Agriculture at the company, sees in the partnership a possibility of scientific-technological exchange, especially because Embrapa's farm is close to Campinas, where Bosch's development center is located. "Integration with an experimental project, such as AgNest, will allow us to contact real problems and, with that, anticipate market demands."

## Transgenic corn

Among other technologies, the institution presents BTMax, Brazil's first 100% Brazilian transgenic maize hybrid. The variety, which reached the market in 2022 and now gains scale, was developed in partnership with Hélio Sementes. The plant received a protein from the bacterium Bacillus thuringiensis, which has insecticidal properties. With this, it manages to reduce infestation by the fall armyworm, one of the main pests of the crop. According to Pablo Arantes, technical services coordinator at Biomatrix, one of the companies marketing the hybrid, the pest scrapes the leaf and ingests the protein, which assumes a toxic effect. Tests conducted during the development of the variety indicated efficiency of up to 90% in controlling the armyworm. The technology continues to be improved, with the goal of expanding this rate.
