# Puma Energy Launches $120 Million Investment to Replace Diesel Imports

International trader Trafigura, owner of the Puma Energy brand, will undertake a $120 million investment to modernize its refinery in Bahía Blanca, Buenos Aires, and achieve the substitution of diesel imports. The work will begin in October and will involve removing sulfur from diesel processing, thereby adapting it to current environmental and quality regulations—in line with European standards. Sulfur combustion is a potent generator of polluting emissions, and the need for diesel imports has drained part of the Central Bank's reserves (BCRA) in recent years.

The Bahía Blanca refinery currently processes approximately 40,000 barrels per day of crude oil, representing 7% of the country's total fuel processing. This represents a 50% increase from its 2018 level, when Trafigura purchased it from Pampa Energía. In February, Puma Energy sold approximately 117 million liters of gasoline and diesel across its more than 400 service stations distributed throughout the country. The brand is the fourth largest seller in Argentina, behind YPF, Shell—operated by Brazilian company Raízen—and Axion.

According to data from consulting firm Economía & Energía, imports of gasoline and diesel account for only 4% and 2% of market consumption, respectively, totaling slightly more than 50 million liters per month. However, these imports depend on the economic cycle and scheduled maintenance work at all refineries.

Trafigura attempted to purchase Raízen's refinery in Dock Sud and Shell's 892 service stations in Argentina, but lost to Swiss group Mercuria, partner of José Luis Manzano, in a transaction worth approximately $1.6 billion, which will close in the coming weeks.

As it is not an integrated company—Trafigura only refines fuels without producing oil in the country—crude purchases are made from various operators at international prices minus tariffs and a premium for physical supply in real time. In other words, it recently sourced crude at around $100 per barrel but sells it at service stations between $90 and $95, due to a private price freeze agreement until mid-May.

With the war continuing in the Middle East, crude has found its floor at around $90 and its ceiling near $110, so gasoline and diesel prices will remain unchanged for a long time. If the conflict extends and restrictions on transit through the Strait of Hormuz continue, oil companies will be forced to renegotiate agreements and, eventually, raise fuel prices again after May 15.

According to sources familiar with the sector, prices still lag by between 10% and 15%, despite increases of up to 25% in March.

As it faces this moment of market tension, Puma Energy launched Cleantec, its new additive, which guarantees "complete engine cleaning and maximum performance," and continues to promote its Puma Pris digital application. The app already has 700,000 registered users and is the platform for offering discounts to customers when refueling.
