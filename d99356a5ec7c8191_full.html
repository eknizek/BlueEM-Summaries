# Mosaic Halts Fertilizer Plant Operations in Brazil Amid Sulfur Price Spike

Fertilizer manufacturer Mosaic will halt production of approximately one million tons of phosphate in Brazil at a time when geopolitical risk in the Middle East has driven up prices of this key input. Mosaic will begin shutting down its facilities in Araxá and Patrocínio "as part of its efforts to reduce costs and reallocate capital," the company stated on Tuesday (April 7). The company also plans to proceed with the sale of its assets in Araxá.

The decision comes amid a spike in sulfur prices, which is used in the production of phosphate fertilizer. Mosaic said in December that it would halt production at its Fospar and Araxá plants due to increased input costs. Sulfur prices were already elevated due to increased demand from metal mining. But the Middle East conflict has driven them even higher, with spot prices in Brazil rising 40% since the beginning of the war last month, according to Bloomberg Green Markets data through April 3.

Nearly half of global sulfur trade is vulnerable to disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz. Although tensions diminished after the U.S. and Iran agreed on Tuesday night to a two-week ceasefire, with Iran committing to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, market turbulence is likely to continue. Normalization of maritime transport could take months, and shippers still await proof that transit will remain safe, which means interruptions in sulfur shipments are "structural, not temporary," according to Manuja Pandey, who covers fertilizers at ICIS, a commodity pricing company.

Mosaic's closures in Brazil represent a setback for the country's efforts to reduce its dependence on foreign agricultural nutrients. The agricultural powerhouse imports most of its fertilizers, including more than three-quarters of its phosphate consumption. Another phosphate producer, Itafos, halted new orders in Brazil last month while assessing its sulfur costs. CEO David Delaney said on Wednesday that the company remains "very cautious" and still expects sulfur prices to remain higher on a year-over-year basis.

Mosaic stated that its shutdowns should have limited impact on profits amid elevated sulfur costs. On Tuesday, Mosaic shares fell as much as 4.5% after news of the ceasefire, before trimming most of the losses.
