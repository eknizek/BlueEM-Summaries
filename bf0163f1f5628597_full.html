# Get into cherries and pistachios, raise cattle: provincial producers abandon wine and switch sectors

An inevitable process seems to be unfolding in the land of wine, where opportunities are emerging alongside concerns. The international crisis in consumption of the drink of the gods, with a market increasingly focused on quality, moderation, and healthy habits, continues to generate worry in the primary sector due to falling sales and low grape prices, with concrete movements taking place on the ground.

In this way, an increasing number of producers, especially small and medium-sized ones, are deciding to abandon the wine industry and dedicate themselves to other activities, such as cattle ranching and pasture cultivation, or reconvert to premium varietals or other products gaining ground, such as pistachios and cherries.

According to official estimates, with data provided to LA NACION by the Production Ministry of the Cuyo province, more than 10% of local producers have already begun the transformation process.

In a conversation with this publication, the minister in charge, Rodolfo Vargas Arizu, explained the situation being experienced on farms, with a complex context forcing farmers and industrialists to readapt or pursue new horizons. "There are opportunities every day. Many want the State to solve their problems. We see this a lot in wine production. It's time to understand the process and adapt to changes," said the official, who issued a call to producers: "Get into cherries, into pistachios, into pastures; raise cattle."

The global decline in wine consumption, low prices paid for grapes, lack of financing, quality demands, the impact of weather phenomena, and rising service costs, among other causes and expenses, have become a true headache in the vineyards. For this reason, some have abandoned their farms, failed to harvest, or decided to make a radical change, uprooting vineyards, primarily of Creole varietals, to replant high-end ones, such as Malbec and Cabernet Franc, among others.

Some producers are abandoning wine altogether and shifting to other sectors, including dried fruits like pistachios, almonds, and walnuts. Certainly, a difficult decision to make, but one that is slowly shaping the destiny of local production.

There is also sustained growth in pastures, especially alfalfa, and cattle ranching, as optimized irrigation systems open up more possibilities for long-term sustainability. "Even with better chances than in the humid pampas. In the south there is a project with more than 700 hectares of pastures with 20 irrigation pivots, which allows continued growth," noted Vargas Arizu.

Livestock in San Rafael and General Alvear continues to evolve, increasingly meeting the province's meat consumption demand, which currently reaches only 15%.

"The cow has the calf and we need to ensure it stays in Mendoza, which is why it's important to feed it and conduct early weaning. This also allows the cow to become pregnant again by avoiding the stress caused by calf separation," explained the official. "There is an outlet for the south of the province; we are already seeing investments," he highlighted.

Without a doubt, what shows the most movement in recent years is dried fruits. In this case, pistachio, walnut, and almond plantations are gaining strength. "We're seeing growth that has tripled in three years. Everything exportable generates greater interest," said the Production Minister.

There is also a shift toward other productions such as plums, garlic, potatoes, and tomatoes. In fact, the producer association "Tomate 2000" was created and achieved dollar-denominated contracts providing tomato sauce for export, which accounts for more than 4,000 hectares in Mendoza.

In the case of potatoes, new opportunities are emerging. For instance, the renowned Simplot company has announced that it needs 2,000 more hectares than it currently possesses. In fact, it currently imports "dirty potatoes" from the south, which commands a higher price per sack than regular potatoes.

From the Production Ministry, they also highlight the growth and opportunities presented by entry into the production and export market of hybrid seeds, onions, and carrots, which are increasingly gaining share of national cultivation, reaching 40%.

In this regard, Mendoza ranks among the regions with the strongest horticultural culture in the country, as one of the technically most viable and healthiest locations for seed production, similar to Australia, Mexico, and South Africa.

Another crop generating interest is fresh cherries, as the province's climatic conditions allow production in "early season" fashion, even before Chile, opening concrete export opportunities. Additionally, land values in Mendoza are cheaper, which encourages the arrival of new players who also value the province's legal security, according to local government sources. "There is a growing market for shipments to the United States and European Union," sources consulted indicated.

"As for wine, we have the best quality-to-price ratio in the world. We need to go out and sell, convince the buyer. We have much room to grow. It's important to adapt and prepare for what the world demands, especially high-end products, which is why we're seeing a change in varietal planting. We already have 2 million fewer quintals than last year, but we see, for example, that the 46,000 hectares that the Uco Valley has today are those that disappeared from the eastern zone," Vargas Arizu added.

There is also another situation linked to weather events, such as frost and hail, complicating producers in "high-risk zones," as those with consecutive disasters are excluded from the Unique Land Registry (RUT) and face increases in agricultural insurance, forcing them to pursue another path, especially in the south and east of the province. Hence the importance of continuing with some cultivation and investing in anti-hail netting, taking advantage of some official credit benefits.

"We shouldn't be monolithic, thinking of grandfather and ancestors. Things change so abruptly in the world; it's tough. Today, we're in the middle of the river, but we have to bet that we'll reach safe harbor," concluded Vargas Arizu, also alluding to the change in economic model and direction for Argentina, trusting that profitability will improve for those dedicated to production.
