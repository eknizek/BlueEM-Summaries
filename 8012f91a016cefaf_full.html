# Gold surge, high benchmark rates, and record debt drive jewelry pawning at Caixa

Since the pandemic, retired Clarice Almeida, 72, has viewed her gold jewelry collection differently. Overwhelmed with credit card debt, rings, bracelets, and chains became a lifeline. Rather than sell them, she found another solution: pawning them at Caixa Econômica Federal, the only financial institution in the country authorized to offer this type of credit.

"I was drowning in debt and couldn't pay my bills. But I've always had a lot of jewelry, and a friend of mine pawns items at Caixa here in Osasco [São Paulo metropolitan area] and said it was a good option, that the interest was low. I pawned some items and liked it too—the interest is much lower," she said in a video call.

Pawning works as a loan with collateral. Clients bring valuable items—jewelry, silverware, watches, or even pens adorned with precious metals—to an agency where a specialist evaluates them. Caixa can provide credit of up to 100% of the item's value, with money disbursed immediately. In return, clients pay interest of around 2.19% per month on contracts lasting up to six months, which can be renewed. Items are stored in Caixa's safe until the debt is paid off. If the contract isn't fully paid or renewed, items go to auction.

Item valuations don't consider luxury brands or design. Instead, prices are determined by technical appraisal, with the troy ounce gold quotation on the international market as a primary variable.

Over the past year, gold has surged more than 60%, setting successive historical records. In January, it was quoted at US$5,600 per ounce. "In reais, spot gold reached a maximum of R$900 per gram," says Mauriciano Cavalcante, a specialist at Ourominas. From there, affected by Middle East conflicts, the quotation fell to US$4,712 per ounce. "But the trend is still upward in the short term, potentially reaching previous record levels again," says Cavalcante.

The precious metal is viewed as a store of value during periods of economic and geopolitical instability. The rise began in late 2022 when the Ukraine War intensified and culminated in Russia's international reserves being confiscated. Last year, however, President Donald Trump's erratic trade policy changed gold's status: from safe haven to an essential asset in portfolios. The tariff shock to the global trade system was so significant that it shook confidence in the dollar and American securities, considered the safest in the world. Gold thus climbed from US$3,343 per ounce on April 1st, on the eve of "Liberation Day," to a peak of US$5,600 less than a year later.

With metal more valuable, jewelry is worth more—and demand for pawning has skyrocketed. According to Caixa, the pawning portfolio ended 2025 with a balance of R$3.2 billion, a 31.24% increase compared to the previous year. The institution states that gold appreciation is the main reason for the growth.

"As gold rose significantly, many clients began to have relevant assets sitting in home drawers," says Gustavo Trotta, partner at Valor Investimentos. "With this, pawning becomes a quick way to access credit, using a valued asset without needing to sell. The gold surge allows clients to borrow more money with the same collateral, improving the cost-benefit ratio of the operation."

There's another factor at play. Family indebtedness—the primary driver prompting Clarice to pawn even her wedding ring—has reached 80.4% of the population, a record in the historical series of research conducted by CNC (National Confederation of Commerce of Goods, Services and Tourism). Delinquency has also risen, affecting 29.6% of indebted families, driven by the Selic rate at its highest level in nearly two decades. At 14.75% per year today, the Selic rate guides interest rates practiced in the market, from credit card revolving rates to personal loans—and that's precisely where pawning gains ground.

"It tends to be cheaper than other traditional lines. Clients seek pawning as a competitive alternative," says Trotta. There's yet another appeal: unlike conventional loans, pawning doesn't require meticulous credit analysis. The collateral alone is sufficient. "It ends up being an entry door for people with negative records or difficulty getting approval on other lines," the specialist affirms.

For this same reason, however, pawning can be a silent indicator of greater indebtedness. Because it involves real collateral, the loan carries less weight in the financial system, explains Trotta, since it doesn't involve credit score analysis. "It can be a sign that families are turning to alternatives to manage their debt."
