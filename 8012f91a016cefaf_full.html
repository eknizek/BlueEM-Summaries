# Surging Gold Prices, High Selic Rates and Record Household Debt Boost Jewelry Pawning at Caixa Bank

Since the pandemic, retiree Clarice Almeida, 72, began viewing her gold jewelry collection differently. Drowning in credit card debt, rings, bracelets and chains became a lifeline. But instead of selling them, she found another way out: pawning them at Caixa Econômica Federal, the only financial institution in the country authorized to offer this type of credit.

"I was full of debts and couldn't pay my bills. But I've always had lots of jewelry, and a friend of mine pawned items at Caixa here in Osasco [São Paulo metropolitan region] and said it was good, that the interest was low. I pawned mine and liked it too—the interest is much lower indeed," she said in a video call.

Pawning works as a guaranteed loan. The customer brings a valuable item to the branch—jewelry, silverware, watches or even pens adorned with precious metals—and a specialist evaluates it. The credit provided by Caixa can reach up to 100% of the piece's value, and the money is available immediately. In return, the customer pays interest of around 2.19% per month, in contracts of up to six months and renewable. The items are kept in Caixa's vault until the debt is paid off, and if the contract is not fully paid or renewed, they go to auction.

The value set for the pieces does not account for luxury brands or design. The price is actually based on technical evaluation, with one of the main variables being the troy ounce price of gold on the international market.

Over the past year, gold has advanced more than 60%, setting successive historical records. In January, it was quoted at US$5,600 per ounce. "In reais, spot gold reached a maximum of R$900 per gram," says Mauriciano Cavalcante, specialist at Ourominas. From there, affected by the Middle East conflict, the quote fell to US$4,712 per ounce. "But the trend is still upward in the short term, potentially reaching previous record levels again," says Cavalcante.

The precious metal is viewed as a store of value during moments of economic and geopolitical instability. The rise began in late 2022 when the Ukraine War intensified and culminated in the confiscation of Russia's international reserves. Last year, however, President Donald Trump's erratic trade policy changed gold's status: from safe haven to indispensable asset in portfolios. The tariff shock to the global trade system was so significant that it shook confidence in the dollar and American securities, considered the safest in the world. Gold thus rose from US$3,343 per ounce on April 1st to the peak of US$5,600 less than a year later.

With metal more valued, jewelry is worth more—and demand for pawning has skyrocketed. According to Caixa, the pawning portfolio ended 2025 with a balance of R$3.2 billion, growth of 31.24% compared to the previous year. The institution says gold appreciation is the main reason for the growth.

"As gold rose significantly, many customers came to have relevant assets sitting in home drawers," says Gustavo Trotta, partner at Valor Investimentos. "With this, pawning becomes a quick way to access credit, using a valued asset without needing to sell. The gold increase allows the customer to get more money with the same asset as collateral, improving the cost-benefit ratio of the operation."

There is another factor at play. Household debt—the main driver for Clarice to even pawn her wedding ring—reached 80.4% of the population, a record in the historical series of research conducted by CNC (National Confederation of Commerce of Goods, Services and Tourism). Delinquency also rose, affecting 29.6% of indebted families, driven by the Selic rate at its highest level in nearly two decades. At 14.75% per year today, the Selic sets interest rates practiced in the market, from credit card revolving to personal loans, and that's precisely where pawning gains ground.

"It tends to be cheaper than other traditional lines. The customer seeks pawning as a competitive alternative," says Trotta. There is yet another attraction: unlike a conventional loan, pawning does not require thorough credit analysis. The asset provided as collateral is already sufficient. "It ends up being a gateway for people with negative credit or difficulty getting approval on other lines," the specialist affirms.

For this same reason, however, pawning can be a silent indicator of greater indebtedness. Since it involves real collateral, the loan carries less weight in the financial system, explains Trotta, as it does not involve analysis of the customer's credit score. "It can be a sign that families are resorting to alternatives to manage their debt."
