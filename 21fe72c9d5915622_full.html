# Unemployment in Greater Buenos Aires poses greatest political risk to Government, says economist Arriazu

The words of economist Ricardo Arriazu are gospel for the business world. In a select webinar before sophisticated investors convened by the asset management fund Black Toro, Arriazu warned that the greatest challenge for the economic program lies in the labor market of the suburban area.

In his view, we are facing a profound structural change, but "the current change process produces more job destruction than creation. This situation can generate political noise and transfer to the electoral arena. The challenge is the election in Greater Buenos Aires next year."

Here are his other key points:

"In my life I have never seen this new dynamic of capital-intensive sectors like energy, mining and agriculture that generate a large amount of foreign currency but absorb little labor.

"Industry, construction and commerce are going through a downturn and maintain their epicenter in Greater Buenos Aires.

"The businessman is not to blame for having invested where he invested; they were the incentives that governments gave. People are also not to blame for having moved to Greater Buenos Aires; those were also the incentives the Government gave them. And now the rules of the game change and some are left very damaged.

"Argentina has an artificial productive structure, the product of almost a century of penalizing the sectors in which we have comparative advantage and subsidizing sectors where we don't have comparative advantage.

"Vaca Muerta is possibly the best rock in the world. At minimum, equal to Permian in the United States. The break-even point in Argentina should be at $42 per barrel. Argentina is in full development process, it has more gas than oil. Production is growing; it was lacking infrastructure.

"By 2030, Argentina could reach energy exports of $32 billion, driven by Vaca Muerta development, expansion of transport infrastructure and increased production of gas liquids. This year 135 million barrels of oil will be exported and 225 million next year.

"Agriculture, if export taxes are eliminated, will contribute an additional $20 billion.

"These benefits are not yet fully reflected in current activity and the transition process implies bottlenecks that must be managed.

"At this moment, in my opinion, the Central Bank should lower the interest rate. It's the same as in 2024. The Fund said it had to be raised, most said it had to be raised, we said it had to be lowered. This time we believe it should be lowered because people think in dollars. Banks are afraid because their non-performing portfolio increased.

"The economic program currently has a 50% probability of success. Last year I thought it would be 30%, but it is conditioned on the ability to pass the next electoral test without major social deterioration."
