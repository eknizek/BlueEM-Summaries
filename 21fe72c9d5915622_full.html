# For Arriazu, unemployment in Greater Buenos Aires is the biggest political risk for the Government

The economist warned that the current change process produces more job destruction than creation. "This situation can generate political noise and translate into the electoral arena," he stated.

The word of economist Ricardo Arriazu is the bible for the business world. In a select webinar before sophisticated investors convened by the asset management fund Black Toro, Arriazu warned that the greatest challenge of the economic program lies in the labor market of the greater Buenos Aires area. In his view, we are facing a profound structural change, but "the current change process produces more job destruction than creation. This situation can generate political noise and translate into the electoral arena. The challenge is the election in Greater Buenos Aires next year."

Here are his other observations:

"In my life I have never seen this new dynamic of capital-intensive sectors like energy, mining, and agriculture that generate a large amount of foreign currency but absorb little labor.

"Industry, construction, and commerce are undergoing a downturn and maintain their epicenter in Greater Buenos Aires.

"The businessman is not to blame for having invested where he invested; those were the incentives that governments gave. People are also not to blame for having moved to Greater Buenos Aires; those were also the incentives the Government gave them. And now the rules of the game are changing and some are being severely damaged.

"Argentina has an artificial productive structure, the product of almost a century of punishing sectors in which we have comparative advantage and subsidizing sectors where we have no comparative advantage.

"Vaca Muerta is possibly the best rock in the world. At minimum, equal to the Permian in the United States. The equilibrium point in Argentina should be at US$ 42 per barrel. Argentina is in the midst of development; it has more gas than oil. Production is growing; it lacked infrastructure.

"By 2030, Argentina could reach energy exports of US$ 32 billion, driven by the development of Vaca Muerta, the expansion of transport infrastructure, and increased production of gas liquids. This year 135 million barrels of oil will be exported and 225 million next year.

"Agriculture, in the event that export taxes are eliminated, will contribute an additional US$ 20 billion.

"These benefits are not yet fully reflected in current activity and the transition process implies bottlenecks that need to be managed.

"At this moment, in my opinion, the Central Bank should lower interest rates. It's the same as in 2024. The Fund said rates had to go up, most said they had to go up, we said they had to go down. This time we believe they need to come down because people think in dollars. Banks are afraid because their irregular portfolio increased.

"The economic program currently has a 50% probability of success; last year I thought it would be 30%, but it is conditioned on the capacity to get through the next electoral test without greater social deterioration."
