Family Office Ghia Cultivates R$5B in AUM by Serving Agribusiness Wealth Management

23 April 2026 · 07:55 PM

# How a Family Office "Harvested" R$5 Billion Under Management by "Watering" Agribusiness Investors

The maturation of agribusiness is creating new demand for wealth management outside the traditional axis of wealth management. And Ghia Multi Family Office has been leveraging this transformation to scale its expansion thesis. With approximately R$5 billion under management, the firm founded in 2010 in the city of Uberlândia in Minas Gerais has been growing, on average, between 30% and 35% per year by specializing in families whose wealth comes directly or indirectly from the agricultural sector. Now, it wants to use this avenue to reach R$10 billion by 2028 and approach R$20 billion in the five years that follow. Its clientele consists of families with a minimum ticket of R$5 million, many of them still in the first or second generation of wealth formation. In addition to its headquarters in Uberlândia, the company maintains offices in Goiânia and Cuiabá and a presence in Ribeirão Preto, all with the same profile of business families linked to agribusiness.

"The rural producer saw that he made money with land and that he knew how to make money with it. So the most obvious option was to increase his position in something he had experience with. But the recent agricultural crisis showed that there is great risk in this concentration," says Gabriel Cestari, co-founder of Ghia Multi Family Office.

The firm's assessment is that this client, for a long time, flew under the radar of the traditional large-fortune industry because much of their wealth was tied up and not in liquidity to invest in the financial market. This type of investor always thought about buying more land with the money they earned. And they weren't wrong in doing so, after all, the properties appreciated far more than the market average. A survey by Scot Consultoria shows that the average nominal price of a hectare of pasture, intended for cattle raising, in the Midwest jumped from R$7,579.17 in December 2019 to R$21,270.83 in December 2025, a 181% increase. In the same period, agricultural hectares rose from R$16,834.78 to R$47,604.17, an appreciation of 183%. For comparison, in the same period the CDI accumulated about 73%, while the Ibovespa advanced 39%.

However, the same boom in appreciation proved to be a trap. The sharp increase in land prices combined with the favorable cycle of commodities, especially in the post-pandemic period, expanded the wealth of these families and increased the complexity of managing it. The rural business became more valuable, but also more expensive, more volatile, and more dependent on governance. This shift gained momentum in recent years. In addition to asset appreciation, increased agricultural operating costs and volatility in prices, exchange rates, and inputs began to demand more professionalization in business management. And with commodity prices falling, the challenge became greater.

Cestari states that, after years of robust margins, the fall in commodity prices and the liquidity squeeze exposed more clearly the risk of wealth concentration. "The lack of liquidity began to weigh heavily," he says. "This producer began to have to dispose of something. And when you need to sell and can't wait or negotiate better for that asset, you sell for whatever price the market is willing to pay."

According to Ghia MFO's co-founder, it was this environment that grew the perception among families that it was not enough to merely accumulate wealth in land. It was necessary to start thinking about liquidity, protection, and succession. This process led families to look more carefully at planning outside the farm. Often, the demand started with holding structures, but ended up advancing to topics such as tax and succession planning, local and international diversification, and broader wealth organization. Tax changes also helped accelerate this movement. The increase in some taxes, the rise in ITCMD in states like Goiás, and the perspective opened by tax reform were factors that reinforced the search for structuring and governance.

To make this possible, in many cases what occurred was an interruption of the automatic reinvestment cycle in the business itself. Resources that would previously be allocated to purchasing new land began to be directed toward more liquid alternatives, including overseas. "It's funny that this investor jumps straight from CDI investment to a diversified offshore. But it makes sense because in their minds everything is in dollars," says Cestari.

This assessment also helps explain why Ghia sees room to win clients who today are still poorly served by banks, advisors, and private banking. According to Cestari, there are families with over R$10 million in assets who remain in traditional retail or concentrate almost their entire position in very conservative products, without an integrated view of investments, succession, tax issues, and corporate structure.

Until now, Ghia has expanded only organically, benefiting from the asset appreciation of its clients and from referrals among families of similar profile. But in its next phase, the company is already willing to look at acquisitions in markets considered strategic. The firm's assessment is that this movement is still in its infancy. For Ghia, agribusiness remains concentrated in a stage of raw material production, but should open space in the coming years for a cycle of greater industrialization, new businesses, and new demands for wealth management. "We are still in the production phase, of raw materials, and we should see a movement toward greater industrialization, with this wealth originating other businesses. It's a market that will grow a lot and needs guidance for it," says Cestari.
