Ecopetrol board governance under scrutiny as S&P raises concerns

11 April 2026 · 02:09 PM

# What's Happening with Ecopetrol's Board of Directors? Its Members Under Scrutiny

Ecopetrol's internal situation has taken a critical turn following a recent downgrade in its global credit rating from BB to BB- by Standard & Poor's (S&P). While the company initially attributed this adjustment to country risk behavior, an investigation by EL TIEMPO's Investigative Unit revealed that the rating agency's report contains direct questions about the management and composition of the state oil company's board of directors.

## Poor Performance and Government Pressure

The S&P document, which will be subject to mandatory analysis at the board meeting scheduled for April 21, points to a deterioration in the company's governance. According to EL TIEMPO's Investigative Unit, the agency rates the state company's governance as "negative," identifying the National Government, as the majority shareholder, as a source of constant financial pressure exercised through the board.

One of the greatest concerns lies in the independence of its members. The report highlights that of the nine board members, only two are considered independent. This lack of rotation and the high presence of profiles directly linked to the Executive branch have been warned by experts as a risk to the corporate governance that guides the company.

## Red Figures and Dividend Policy

S&P's financial scrutiny also detected resource consumption significantly higher than projections. The state oil company consumed nearly 9 trillion pesos more than expected. In practical terms, this meant that instead of generating free cash of 2.95 trillion pesos, the company ended up with an additional consumption of 5.9 trillion, impacting a total debt that already exceeds 118 trillion pesos.

Adding to this scenario is the dividend distribution policy. EL TIEMPO's Investigative Unit highlights that in 2024, Ecopetrol distributed 15.6 trillion pesos in dividends, a figure that exceeded its net profit of 14.9 trillion. Additionally, at the initiative of the Ministry of Finance, the dividend per share was raised from 110 to 121 pesos, which according to S&P, limits the financial flexibility of the company in facing its fiscal challenges.

## The Ricardo Roa Factor and International Oversight

Juan Carlos Hurtado's debut as the new head of the company was marked by the need to respond to these observations. Hurtado assumed the position replacing Ricardo Roa, who is on vacation and unpaid leave while facing criminal proceedings for alleged violations of campaign spending caps and influence trafficking.

President Gustavo Petro himself has publicly expressed his discontent with the board, calling some representatives "timid" for not sustaining the defense of Roa against judicial accusations. However, the real fear among board members, according to sources consulted by EL TIEMPO's Investigative Unit, is that S&P's findings could lead to administrative or judicial investigations by authorities in the United States. Given that Ecopetrol is listed on U.S. stock exchanges, its executives could face scrutiny if it is demonstrated that they did not act to protect the financial and reputational stability of the oil company.
