# Private equity seeks exit door to resume fundraising and buying

Private equity in Brazil is going through one of the most challenging cycles of the last decade, with high interest rates, restricted liquidity, and a more selective market for divestments. This situation has made new fundraising difficult and extended the holding period of assets in funds. On the other hand, the same environment has opened a favorable window for new investments, with more attractive prices and greater negotiating power for funds with available capital.

According to sector executives, a reduction in interest rates will contribute to the gradual reopening of IPOs and greater participation of strategic investors in operations, accelerating exits and reducing the imbalance between the pace of new investments and divestments.

Priscila Rodrigues, president of the Brazilian Association of Private Equity and Venture Capital (Abvcap), states that the Brazilian market, being smaller, is more dependent on strategic investors and, more recently, on secondary transactions (operations between funds). Still, she emphasizes the importance of an open capital market that provides greater predictability for exits. "The fundraising window depends on exits. There are managers with many companies in their portfolio," she says. "Portfolio recycling is needed to free up capital for investors, so the industry will focus more on divestments."

A Bain & Company survey, in partnership with Abvcap, shows that private equity funds made 32 new investments last year, compared to 24 in 2024. Exits, however, totaled 11 operations, below the 17 recorded in 2024. This slower exit flow has prolonged the holding period of companies in portfolios. In practice, investors are taking longer to receive their capital returns. This mismatch between investments and exits has led managers to adjust their focus between portfolio management and new allocations, prioritizing exits from mature investments and being selective in new asset investments.

At Pátria Investimentos, this dynamic appears more clearly through the coexistence of distinct cycles within the same manager. Funds IV and V, which are older, are focused on the divestment phase. The most recent exit was SmartFit, a company invested since 2010 and fully divested through a series of operations over recent years. Luiz Felipe Cruz, a partner in Pátria's private equity division, believes the environment is still challenging for exits but emphasizes that operations with strategic investors remain viable, especially for higher-quality assets. For him, normalization of the interest rate scenario and reduction of geopolitical uncertainties tends to gradually improve the divestment environment, with subsequent effects on fundraising.

Funds VI and VII, meanwhile, operate in the investment and value creation phase. The most recent fund, whose fundraising was completed in the second half of 2024, has approximately half of its $1.2 billion already committed to new allocations, particularly in healthcare, food and beverage sectors. "With less competition and more available capital, we are finding premium assets at very attractive prices," says Cruz. The most recent investment was in Banmédica, a healthcare operator with operations in Chile and Colombia. The transaction, worth approximately $1 billion, involved fund resources and co-investor participation, expanding total allocation capacity.

Gustavo Camargo, a partner and leader of private equity at Bain & Company, evaluates that the industry is experiencing a temporary imbalance following a very strong fundraising and investment cycle during the pandemic, followed by difficulties in selling assets in subsequent years. Looking ahead, expectations are for a constructive long-term scenario but with short-term uncertainties, especially due to factors such as the global environment, interest rate levels, and political dynamics.

According to him, the decision by managers to avoid sales at unfavorable moments is natural, but this behavior has a negative consequence. "This decision is obvious and correct, but when all managers do the same, the market practically stops," he states. This lack of portfolio recycling is present in other economies. Sovereign funds and other institutional investors have resources—the so-called "dry powder"—but it has not yet been directed to new investments due to these uncertainties and the cost of capital that reduces risk appetite. In Brazil, however, there is a relatively more constructive perception. The combination of still-depreciated assets, less competition, and high interest rates has made the investment environment more attractive for those already holding capital.

This scenario is already reflected in the strategies of managers. Such is the case with Vinci Compass, which raised R$ 3.2 billion in its fourth fund last year, with about half of that amount still available. In the assessment of the firm's private equity co-heads, Gabriel Felzenszwalb and Carlos Eduardo Martins, the moment demands more discipline, selectivity, and execution capacity. For those who can get through this period, the expectation is for a more promising harvest in the future. Felzenszwalb states that the company's strategy remains centered on long-term structural theses, less dependent on economic cycles—such as population aging, healthcare, wellness, digitalization, and convenience. "These are themes that will grow for 10, 15, 20 years, not depending so much on the economic cycle," he says.

Martins highlights that, despite current restrictions, especially with still expensive credit, there are opportunities for capitalized funds. "The only certainty is the entry price, and today we are seeing larger companies with lower multiples," he explains. Piero Minardi, a partner at Lazuli Partners, holds the same view. Fundraising should continue to be difficult in the short term, but lower company valuations open space for investments. In this context, Minardi sees a relevant change on the part of investors, who seek more flexible models, such as co-investments. This makes the process more selective and labor-intensive for managers. "The investor wants to look at the manager, the company, and the thesis before investing. The power is more in their hands," he says.

In terms of strategy, Lazuli has been looking at assets in consumption and education but with a more disciplined approach. The firm prioritizes already-established companies with growth potential and, in some cases, the need for balance sheet restructuring. For Roberto Haddad, a co-leading partner of private equity at KPMG, Brazil should benefit because it has a more neutral geopolitical position, entering the radar of global investors. For him, there is greater preference for more mature assets, co-investments, and smaller deals.
