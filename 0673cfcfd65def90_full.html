# Private equity seeks exit strategies to resume fundraising and acquisitions

Private equity in Brazil is going through one of the most challenging cycles of the last decade, with high interest rates, restricted liquidity, and a more selective market for divestitures. This situation has hindered new fundraising and extended the holding period for assets in funds. On the other hand, the same environment has opened a favorable window for new investments, with more attractive prices and greater negotiating power for funds with cash on hand.

According to sector executives, the reduction in interest rates will contribute to the gradual reopening of IPOs and greater participation of strategic investors in operations, accelerating exits and reducing the imbalance between the pace of new investments and the realization of divestitures.

Priscila Rodrigues, president of the Brazilian Association of Private Equity and Venture Capital (Abvcap), states that the Brazilian market, being smaller, is more dependent on strategic investors and, more recently, on secondary transactions (operations between funds). Nevertheless, she emphasizes the importance of an open capital market that provides greater predictability for exits. "The fundraising window depends on exits. Some managers have many companies in their portfolios," she says. "Portfolio recycling is needed to release money to investors, so the industry will focus more on divestitures."

A Bain & Company survey, in partnership with Abvcap, shows that private equity funds made 32 new investments last year, compared to 24 in 2024. Exits totaled 11 operations, below the 17 recorded in 2024. This slower exit flow has prolonged the holding period for companies in portfolios. In practice, investors are taking longer to receive their capital returns. This mismatch between investments and exits has led managers to adjust their focus between portfolio management and new allocations, prioritizing the exit of more mature investments and being selective in new asset contributions.

At Pátria Investimentos, this dynamic is clearer due to the coexistence of different cycles within the manager itself. Funds IV and V, older funds, are focused on the divestiture phase. The most recent exit was SmartFit, a company invested since 2010 and completely divested through a series of operations over recent years. Luiz Felipe Cruz, partner in private equity at Pátria, evaluates that the environment is still challenging for exits, but emphasizes that operations with strategic investors remain viable, especially in the case of higher-quality assets. For him, the normalization of the interest rate scenario and reduction of geopolitical uncertainties tend to gradually improve the divestiture environment, with subsequent effects on fundraising.

Funds VI and VII operate in the investment and value generation phase. The most recent fund, whose fundraising was completed in the second half of 2024, has about half of the US$ 1.2 billion already committed to new allocations, especially in the health, food, and beverage sectors. "With less competition and more capital available, we are finding premium assets at very attractive prices," says Cruz. The most recent investment was in Banmédica, a health operator operating in Chile and Colombia. The transaction, of approximately US$ 1 billion, drew on fund resources and participation from co-investors, expanding total allocation capacity.

This model also reflects a change in investor behavior, which has sought specific theses. Gustavo Camargo, partner and private equity leader at Bain & Company consultancy, evaluates that the industry is experiencing a temporary imbalance after a very strong fundraising and investment cycle during the pandemic, followed by difficulties in selling assets in subsequent years.

Looking ahead, the expectation is for a constructive scenario in the long term, but with uncertainties in the short term, especially due to factors such as the global environment, interest rate levels, and political dynamics. According to him, the managers' decision to avoid sales in unfavorable moments is natural, but this behavior has a negative consequence. "This decision is obvious and correct, but when all managers do the same, the market practically stops," he states.

This lack of recycling is present in other economies. Sovereign funds and other institutional investors have resources, the so-called "dry powder," but it has not yet been directed to new investments due to these uncertainties and the cost of money that reduces risk appetite.

In Brazil, however, there is a relatively more constructive perception. The combination of still-depreciated assets, less competition, and high interest rates has made the investment environment more attractive for those who already have committed capital.

This scenario is already reflected in the strategies of managers. Such is the case with Vinci Compass, which raised R$ 3.2 billion in its fourth fund last year, and about half of that amount is still available. In the assessment of the manager's co-heads of private equity, Gabriel Felzenszwalb and Carlos Eduardo Martins, the moment demands more discipline, selectivity, and execution capacity. For those who can navigate this period, the expectation is for a more promising harvest in the future. Felzenszwalb states that the house's strategy remains centered on long-term structural theses, less dependent on the economic cycle—such as population aging, health, wellness, digitalization, and convenience. "These are themes that will grow for 10, 15, 20 years, not so dependent on the economic cycle," he says.

Martins highlights that despite current constraints, especially with credit still expensive, there are opportunities for capitalized funds. "The only certainty is the entry price, and today we are seeing larger companies with lower multiples," he explains.

Piero Minardi, partner at Lazuli Partners, shares the same view. Fundraising should remain difficult in the short term, but lower company valuations open space for investments. In this context, Minardi sees a relevant change on the investor's part, which seeks more flexible models, such as co-investments. This makes the process more selective and labor-intensive for managers. "The investor wants to look at the manager, the company, and the thesis before investing. Power is more in their hands," he says.

In terms of strategy, Lazuli has focused on assets in consumption and education, but with a more disciplined approach. The house prioritizes already-established companies with growth potential and, in some cases, balance sheet restructuring needs.

For Roberto Haddad, partner-leader of private equity at KPMG, Brazil should benefit because it has a more neutral geopolitical position, entering the radar of global investors. For him, there is greater preference for more mature assets, co-investments, and less focus on other factors.
