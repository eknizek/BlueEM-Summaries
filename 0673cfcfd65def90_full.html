# Private Equity Seeks Exit Route to Resume Fundraising and Buying

Private equity in Brazil is going through one of the most challenging cycles of the last decade, with high interest rates, restricted liquidity, and a more selective market for divestments. This scenario has made new fundraising difficult and extended the length of time assets remain in funds. On the other hand, the same environment has opened a favorable window for new investments, with more attractive prices and greater negotiating power for funds with cash on hand.

According to sector executives, a reduction in interest rates will contribute to the gradual reopening of IPOs and greater participation of strategic investors in operations, accelerating exits and reducing the imbalance between the pace of new investments and the realization of divestments.

Priscila Rodrigues, president of the Brazilian Private Equity and Venture Capital Association (Abvcap), states that the Brazilian market, being smaller, is more dependent on strategic investors and, more recently, on secondary transactions (operations between funds). Still, she emphasizes the importance of an open capital market that provides greater predictability for exits. "The fundraising window depends on exits. There are managers with many companies in their portfolios," she says. "There needs to be recycling to free up money for investors, so the industry will focus more on divestment."

A Bain & Company survey, in partnership with Abvcap, shows that private equity funds made 32 new investments last year, compared to 24 in 2024. Exits totaled 11 operations, below the 17 recorded in 2024. This slower exit flow has prolonged the time companies remain in portfolios. In practice, investors are taking longer to get their capital returns. This mismatch between investments and exits has led managers to adjust their focus between portfolio management and new allocations, prioritizing the exit of more mature investments and being selective in new asset contributions.

At Pátria Investimentos, this dynamic is clearer due to the coexistence of different cycles within the same manager. Funds IV and V, older ones, are focused on the divestment phase. The most recent exit was SmartFit, a company invested since 2010 and fully divested through a series of operations over recent years.

Luiz Felipe Cruz, partner in the private equity area at Pátria, evaluates that the environment is still challenging for exits, but emphasizes that operations with strategic investors remain viable, especially for higher-quality assets. For him, normalization of the interest rate scenario and reduction of geopolitical uncertainties tend to gradually improve the divestment environment, with subsequent effects on fundraising.

Funds VI and VII operate in the investment and value creation phase. The most recent fund, whose fundraising was completed in the second half of 2024, has about half of the $1.2 billion already committed to new allocations, especially in the healthcare, food, and beverage sectors. "With less competition and more available capital, we are finding premium assets at very attractive prices," says Cruz.

The most recent investment was in Banmédica, a healthcare operator operating in Chile and Colombia. The transaction, of approximately $1 billion, included fund resources and co-investor participation, expanding total allocation capacity. This model also reflects a change in investor behavior, which has sought specific theses.

Gustavo Camargo, partner and leader of private equity at Bain & Company consulting, evaluates that the industry is experiencing a punctual imbalance following a very strong cycle of fundraising and investments during the pandemic, followed by difficulties in selling assets in the following years. Looking ahead, the expectation is for a constructive scenario in the long term, but with uncertainties in the short term, especially due to factors such as the global environment, interest rate levels, and political dynamics.

According to him, managers' decision to avoid sales at unfavorable times is natural, but this behavior has a negative consequence. "This decision is obvious and correct, but when all managers do the same, the market practically stops," he states.

This lack of recycling is present in other economies. Sovereign funds and other institutional investors have resources, the so-called "dry powder," but have not yet been directed to new investments due to these uncertainties and the cost of money that reduces risk appetite. In Brazil, however, there is a relatively more constructive perception. The combination of still-depreciated assets, less competition, and high interest rates has made the investment environment more attractive for those who already have committed capital.

This scenario is already reflected in managers' strategies. This is the case with Vinci Compass, which raised R$ 3.2 billion in its fourth fund last year and about half of that amount is still available. In the assessment of the manager's co-heads of private equity, Gabriel Felzenszwalb and Carlos Eduardo Martins, the moment requires more discipline, selectivity, and execution capacity. For those who can get through this period, the expectation is for a more promising harvest in the future.

Felzenszwalb states that the house's strategy remains centered on long-term structural theses, less dependent on economic cycles—such as population aging, health, wellness, digitalization, and convenience. "These are themes that will grow for 10, 15, 20 years, not so dependent on the economic cycle," he says.

Martins highlights that, despite current restrictions, especially with credit still expensive, there are opportunities for capitalized funds. "The only certainty is the entry price and today we are seeing larger companies with lower multiples," he notes.

The same vision is shared by Piero Minardi, partner at Lazuli Partners. Fundraising should remain difficult in the short term, but lower company valuations open space for investments. In this context, Minardi sees a relevant change on the investor's part, seeking more flexible models, such as co-investments. This makes the process more selective and labor-intensive for managers. "The investor wants to look at the manager, the company, and the thesis before investing. The power is more in their hands," he says.

In terms of strategy, Lazuli has looked at assets in consumption and education, but with a more disciplined approach. The house prioritizes already-established companies with growth potential and, in some cases, need for balance sheet restructuring.

For Roberto Haddad, partner-leader of private equity at KPMG, Brazil should benefit because it has a more neutral geopolitical position, entering the radar of global investors. For him, there is greater preference for more mature assets, co-investments, and lower frequency of leveraged buyouts.
