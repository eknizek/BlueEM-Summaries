# Avianca Activates Plan to Repatriate Passengers Following Spirit Airlines Bankruptcy

The Colombian airline offers return options for travelers affected by Spirit's closure, which leaves millions of passengers stranded.

By Laura Lesmes Díaz

May 2, 2026, 8:07 AM

The bankruptcy of Spirit Airlines, one of the most representative low-cost airlines in the United States, triggered an immediate response in the region. Avianca announced a plan to assist passengers affected by the airline's cessation of operations, which left millions of travelers without flights as of May 2, 2026.

The Colombian airline announced that it will make available its network of routes and available capacity on its flights to facilitate the return of those left stranded. The measure is adopted voluntarily and joins other similar actions the company has implemented during previous sector crises.

The plan provides that passengers who already began their journey with Spirit can return to their original destination without paying the airline fare. This alternative applies to those who have a return ticket with the U.S. airline and have been affected by flight cancellations. Rebooking will be subject to availability and will be handled directly at airports. Users must present themselves the same day of their scheduled flight or up to one day before, and seats will be assigned on a first-come, first-served basis. The measure covers tickets issued for travel dates between May 2 and 16.

Avianca clarified that, although it will not charge the airline fare, passengers must assume taxes, airport fees, and other mandatory charges defined by authorities. It also noted that additional services will be subject to operational availability.

The announcement comes amid an unprecedented collapse in recent commercial aviation. Spirit Airlines, considered the eighth largest airline in the United States, ceased operations after entering its second bankruptcy following months of financial difficulties. The company had already faced structural problems before the international context worsened its situation.

Rising fuel prices, driven by geopolitical tensions such as the war with Iran, further pressured its operating costs and limited its maneuvering capacity. In an attempt to avoid closure, Spirit sought an agreement with the U.S. administration to obtain last-minute financial rescue. However, a key group of creditors rejected the proposal, which precipitated the decision to liquidate operations.

With 34 years in the market, the airline was a pioneer of the low-cost model in the United States and transformed the way people travel by offering reduced fares in exchange for basic services. Its exit marks the first bankruptcy of a major U.S. airline in 25 years for financial reasons.

The immediate impact is reflected in the total cancellation of flights, closure of customer service channels, and instructions to passengers not to go to airports. The company reported that customers will receive refunds and must arrange new trips with other airlines.

"We are proud of the impact our low-cost model has had on the industry over the last 34 years and hoped to continue serving our passengers for many years to come," Spirit said in a statement. "We deeply regret to announce that, as of May 2, 2026, Spirit Airlines initiated an orderly cessation of its operations, with immediate effect."

The decision also has labor consequences. Approximately 17,000 employees will lose their jobs following the airline's closure, a significant blow to the U.S. aviation industry. Meanwhile, analysts anticipate that Spirit's exit could result in increased fares in the market due to reduced supply in the low-cost segment. The company had been a key player in downward price pressure, especially on domestic routes.

In this context, Avianca reiterated the importance of strengthening supervision of the aviation sector. The airline called on authorities to exercise preventive controls that allow for anticipating situations of financial risk and avoiding abrupt interruptions in service.
