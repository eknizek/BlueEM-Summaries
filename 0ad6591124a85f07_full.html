# Avianca Activates Plan to Repatriate Passengers Following Spirit Airlines Bankruptcy

The bankruptcy of Spirit Airlines, one of the most representative low-cost airlines in the United States, triggered an immediate response in the region. Avianca announced a plan to assist passengers affected by the cessation of operations of the company, which left millions of travelers without flights as of May 2, 2026.

The Colombian airline reported that it will make available its network of routes and available capacity on its flights to facilitate the return of those who were stranded. The measure is adopted voluntarily and adds to other similar actions it has implemented in previous sector crises.

The plan provides that passengers who have already started their journey with Spirit can return to their original destination without paying the airfare. This alternative applies to those who have a return ticket with the U.S. airline and have been affected by flight cancellations. Rebooking will be subject to availability and will be carried out directly at airports. Users must present themselves on the same day of the scheduled flight or up to one day before, and spaces will be assigned on a first-come, first-served basis. The measure covers tickets issued with travel dates between May 2 and 16.

Avianca clarified that although it will not charge the airfare, passengers must assume taxes, airport fees, and other mandatory charges defined by authorities. It also noted that additional services will be subject to operational availability.

The announcement comes amid an unprecedented collapse in recent commercial aviation. Spirit Airlines, considered the eighth largest airline in the United States, ceased operations after entering its second bankruptcy, following several months of financial difficulties. The company was already facing structural problems before the international context worsened its situation.

The increase in fuel prices, driven by geopolitical tensions such as the war with Iran, further pressured its operating costs and limited its room for maneuver. In an attempt to avoid closure, Spirit sought an agreement with the U.S. administration to obtain last-minute financial assistance. However, a key group of creditors rejected the proposal, which precipitated the decision to liquidate its operations.

With 34 years in the market, the airline pioneered the low-cost model in the United States and transformed the way people travel by offering reduced fares in exchange for basic services. Its exit marks the first bankruptcy of a major U.S. airline in 25 years for financial reasons.

The immediate impact is reflected in the total cancellation of flights, the closure of customer service channels, and instructions to passengers not to go to airports. The company reported that customers will receive refunds and must arrange new trips with other airlines.

"We are proud of the impact our low-cost model has had on the sector over the last 34 years and hoped to continue serving our passengers for many years to come," Spirit said in a statement. "We deeply regret to announce that, on May 2, 2026, Spirit Airlines initiated the orderly cessation of its operations, effective immediately."

The decision also has labor consequences. Around 17,000 employees will lose their jobs following the airline's closure, a significant blow to the U.S. aviation industry. In parallel, analysts anticipate that Spirit's exit could translate into an increase in fares in the market, due to reduced supply in the low-cost segment. The company had been a key player in downward price pressure, especially on domestic routes.

In this context, Avianca reiterated the importance of strengthening supervision of the aviation sector. The airline called on authorities to exercise preventive controls that would allow them to anticipate situations of financial risk and avoid abrupt interruptions in service.
