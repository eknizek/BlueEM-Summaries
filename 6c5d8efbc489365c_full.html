Energy deficit and 'Super El Niño' phenomenon increase pressure on Colombia's electrical system

12 April 2026 · 10:17 PM

# Energy deficit and 'Super El Niño' phenomenon increase pressure on Colombia's electrical system

Colombia's energy system faces increased pressure due to a growing deficit in energy supply combined with the possibility of an El Niño phenomenon. The alert intensifies following confirmation from the U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) that current conditions are neutral, but with high probability of transition toward El Niño in the coming months.

According to the most recent report from the Climate Prediction Center from April 9, ENSO-neutral conditions would be maintained between April and June 2026 with an 80% probability. However, between May and July, the probabilities increase that El Niño will develop, with 61%, and that it will extend until the end of the year. This behavior aligns with models warning of a progressive increase in the phenomenon's intensity toward the second half of the year.

To these projections is added the analysis of Sergio Cabrales, professor at Los Andes University and expert in mining and energy issues, who warns that the probability of the phenomenon occurring is 92%, while the possibility that it reaches strong or very strong intensity reaches 51%. As he explained, subsurface ocean warming already shows signs of strengthening, a condition that usually anticipates intense El Niño episodes.

The climate outlook intersects with signals of weakening in the electrical system's capacity. The most recent report from XM, presented to the National Operations Center (CNO) in January 2026, shows deterioration in energy balances compared to projections from 2025.

## Energy Deficit

XM updated its projections and raised the energy deficit for 2026 to 2.7%, compared to the 1.6% estimated previously. For 2027, the shortage is located at 4.4% and for 2028 at 2.5%, while for 2029 a deficit of 4.8% is projected. These figures reflect a reduction in available firm energy, which corresponds to maximum generation capacity under critical conditions such as droughts. The decrease in this capacity is associated, in part, with a 2.5% drop in Firm Energy for the Reliability Charge (ENFICC), explained mainly by variations in the performance of thermal plants.

This adjustment implies that in low hydrology scenarios, the system would have less backup to cover demand. In a drought context associated with El Niño, the system could face a deficit close to 272 megawatts of firm power, equivalent to the consumption of a city like Bucaramanga. This scenario becomes more relevant considering that approximately 62% of electrical generation in Colombia depends on hydroelectric sources.

Reduced rainfall directly impacts reservoir levels, which limits hydroelectric generation and forces greater participation from thermal plants, which operate with gas, coal, or liquid fuels.

The El Niño phenomenon is characterized by sustained increase in Pacific Ocean temperature. As explained by Natalia Gutiérrez, president of the Colombian Association of Electrical Energy Generators (Acolgen), it occurs when that temperature exceeds at least 0.5 degrees Celsius above its average for three consecutive months, which alters climate behavior.

"In the electrical sector, the El Niño Phenomenon translates into lower reservoir levels due to reduced rainfall," she noted. This condition directly impacts hydroelectric generation, which is the foundation of Colombia's energy system.

Gutiérrez explained that the country has managed to face at least eight El Niño episodes in more than three decades, partly thanks to the design of the electrical system following the 1992-1993 blackout. This model allows that when hydroelectric generation decreases, other sources can compensate for supply.
