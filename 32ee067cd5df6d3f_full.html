# Middle East Uncertainty Poses Current Threat to Insurance Industry Business

The Middle East war will increase insurance prices around the world. The logic is simple: the greater the risk, the higher the price to cover the potential for claims.

Insurance companies work with statistical tables and actuarial calculations that allow them to price their insurance with precision. The company does not know to whom it will pay claims, but it knows how many claims it should have in a given period and what their average value is, minimizing the margin of error by applying a surcharge based on a curve for upward deviations that could impact its results.

Insurance companies' experience is based on the past. On the numbers that impact their business, on the pure value of the insurance price and on the charges necessary to manage the business operations. Based on this, the insurance company arrives at an average price, to which it adds or subtracts the values needed to balance each policyholder in relation to the whole, composed of a fund formed by the proportional contribution of each member in relation to each risk.

At this moment, the Middle East is the outlier, the unknown that threatens the insurance business, first by the direct and indirect effects caused by the reduction in the supply of oil, gas and agricultural inputs around the planet; and second, by the direct risk of ships and cargo (basically oil) being hit by attacks from any of the parties involved.

In the second case, what we see is a direct increase in the insurance price. Insurers are recalculating their prices taking into account the potential risk stemming from the war. Since this increase is significant, the market will certainly dilute it across other portfolios and policies, in insurance and reinsurance contracts around the world, in addition to insurance for ports, business facilities, transportation and vessels in risk areas in the Middle East.

That is, due to the direct costs of the war, insurance in general will cost more and this increase will be shared around the planet, especially in reinsurance contracts.

But there is another variable that interferes with insurance prices. The reduction in economic activity and the increase in inflation are already priced in and will have an impact on the prices of goods and services in all countries, including those that have no direct relationship with the war. No one doubts that prices will rise. And this also impacts insurance prices.

For example, even if priced at the same rate, insurance for a popular car is cheaper than insurance for a luxury car. The reason is simple: the luxury car is more expensive than the popular car. This is what will happen. The price of insurance for more expensive products is higher because multiplying the rate by the price will give a higher result. And this additional price will impact the final price of the good or service, since it will be added to the costs that compose it. It is a vicious cycle, this pattern is inexorable.

Insurance around the world will cost more, which will further increase the final price of products and services, including in Brazil.
