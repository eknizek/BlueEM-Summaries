Multiplan CEO on End of 6×1 Schedule: 'No Country Evolves Working Less'

# Multiplan CEO on End of 6×1 Schedule: 'No Country Evolves Working Less'

Eduardo Peres, CEO of Multiplan Group, expressed concerns about fiscal imbalance and the proposal to reduce working hours from 44 to 40 hours per week. He believes this change, defended by the government, could harm Brazil's growth, especially in the retail sector, where costs would increase and consumer prices could rise.

A recent study by the National Confederation of Commerce of Goods, Services and Tourism shows that a possible adjustment to the new rule could generate R$ 122.4 billion in annual costs for commerce and increase payroll by 21%. The price increase passed on to consumers could reach 13%.

Peres argued, in an interview with NeoFeed, that productivity would not improve with reduced working hours and that Brazil would be exporting inefficiency. Multiplan has focused on expansions and revitalizations of shopping centers, with significant investments, while new construction greenfield projects remain off the radar due to the economic scenario.

Morumbi Shopping, after its sixth expansion delivered in March, became the company's leading sales unit, surpassing Barra Shopping in Rio de Janeiro. This year, three more expansions will be delivered (BH Shopping, Barra Shopping, and ParkShopping Brasília), with investments of R$ 365 million.

Two concerns are on businessman Eduardo Peres' radar. One is the fiscal imbalance of public authorities. The other, and more current, is the progress of discussions about reducing the 6×1 work schedule, moving from 44 hours to 40 hours per week. For Peres, if the project being discussed in Brasília and defended by the Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva government advances, Brazil's growth will be affected. The businessman believes the issue needs to be better discussed by society, with detailed analysis by sector. In the case of retail, according to the CEO, the impact will be very negative.

The Planalto's idea is to advance the proposal by the end of May, to serve as an electoral showcase, while business leaders have pressured for the measure to only be voted on after the October election and preferably in 2027, with a new composition of the National Congress.

"No country can evolve working less and without investing in education. Shopping opens every day. If a person wants to work every day, they can't? It's not the reduction of working hours that will bring productivity to the country," says Peres, in an interview with NeoFeed, after a public meeting of the company on Tuesday, April 7.

According to Peres, in the shopping center sector, the direct impact will be precisely the increase in prices by store operators if the measure really advances and is implemented. "If costs increase, they will increase prices. And, if costs increase, will Brazil become more efficient? No," he states.

With this scenario and uncertainty about the prospect of a real interest rate decline cycle, aggravated by the Iran-United States war, Multiplan has concentrated on expansion and revitalization projects, which are faster and generate more profitability for shopping centers. With this, greenfield projects for new construction remain off the radar.

On March 18, Multiplan delivered the sixth expansion of Morumbi Shopping, with an additional 13,000 square meters of gross leasable area (GLA) and 40 new stores distributed over two floors. The investment was R$ 400 million. With the new area, the shopping center in São Paulo's South Zone surpassed Barra Shopping in Rio de Janeiro and for the first time in history became the company's leading sales unit. During the period, revenue growth was 30%.

For 2026, the company should still deliver expansions of BH Shopping (on June 2), with an additional 2,000 m² of GLA and capex of R$ 30 million; Barra Shopping (in the third quarter), with an additional 2,000 m² of GLA and investments of R$ 35 million; and ParkShopping Brasília (on November 18), with an increase of 9,000 m² of GLA and capex of R$ 300 million.

In 2027, expansions of ParkShopping São Caetano (an additional 9,000 m² of GLA), JundiaíShopping (an additional 8,000 m² of GLA), and another BH Shopping expansion (13,000 additional m² of GLA) will be carried out.

Between 2023 and 2025, the company invested R$ 540 million in modernizing 19 of its 20 shopping centers. Over the past five years, the company allocated R$ 7.8 billion, distributed in improvement investments, expansions and new works (R$ 3 billion), and dividend payments and share buybacks (R$ 4.8 billion).

Multiplan closed 2025 with Ebitda of R$ 2 billion (an increase of 8.4%) and R$ 25.9 billion in tenant sales. Net profit was R$ 1.14 billion, maintaining levels above R$ 1 billion for the third consecutive year. Over the accumulated 12 months, MULT3 shares registered appreciation of 40.8% on B3. Multiplan has a market value of R$ 16.3 billion.

**Work Schedule Reduction**

"I am very concerned about this work schedule reduction agenda. This will affect everyone. It's not just my business. It will affect everybody. No country can evolve working less and without investing in education. I don't know of any. I don't think this can be done this way, quickly. It needs to be studied. Can it be done? Can it be done? Can all sectors be framed and work this way? In my view, the way the government is putting it, it will be bad for Brazil. My sector doesn't close. Shopping opens every day. If a person wants to work every day, they can't? It's not work hour reduction that will bring productivity to the country."

**Impact on Tenants**

"What I've been hearing from the retailers operating in our group's shopping centers is that, if this is implemented, it will increase prices. If costs increase, they will increase prices. And, if costs increase, will Brazil become more efficient? No. We are exporting inefficiency. When we reduce our working hours, it's the Chinese who work, it's the American who works. The CNI says that the reduction could decrease GDP by R$ 76 billion. Many people will pass it on. There's no way around it. We will contribute, as a sector, to discussions in Brasília, trying to bring elements so that such a decision is not made abruptly and will affect the entire country. For the shopping center sector, this is very bad."

**Interest Rates and War**

"I don't know if we will have a Selic rate cut cycle. Especially because now we have an impact from the Iran-United States war. It's difficult for us to imagine where it will go. It seems that the United States underestimated the impact of the war. It's lasting much longer than they wanted. And there are already major impacts. Aviation fuel rising, construction costs increasing. There has not yet been an impact on consumption, but, if the war continues, it will arrive. And then it reaches the shopping center. But I don't believe it should last much longer."
