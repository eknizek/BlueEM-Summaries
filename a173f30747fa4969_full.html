# Prévias: The Startup That Regularized Prediction Markets in Brazil

Bet, no: prediction pool. That's the premise of Prévias, a prediction markets startup founded by Arthur Farache and Leonardo Rebitte, partners at alternative investment platform Hurst Capital. Rebitte joined Hurst last year with the idea of creating his own prediction markets company – which was beginning to explode in the United States with the popularization of Kalshi and Polymarket. "When he came to me with this idea, I said I'd only do it if there wasn't a legal gray area," Farache told Brazil Journal. That's when the regulatory engineering began.

While in the United States this type of market is structured as a derivative and is allowed to the general public, here the format would only permit participation from qualified investors. In other words: in practice, it would be limited to Faria Lima – eliminating the scalability thesis. The solution found by Prévias was to frame the product as collective investment. To do this, the founders bought InvestWeb, a company already authorized by the CVM to operate as crowdfunding, but which was inactive. Based on that license, Prévias began to be structured as a collective investment pool and to operate on the same model as prediction markets: all participants put money into the same "pot" and, at the end, the value is redistributed among winners, minus platform fees. (In the case of bets, there is an odds definition set by the house itself and most lost bets go to the bank.)

"In practice, we regularized the prediction pool," said Farache. According to him, this architecture solved two problems at once: it kept the product within CVM regulation (avoiding classification as a bet) and allowed Prévias to be offered to retail customers.

Now the startup is trying to scale the business, which is still small: the platform has 1,500 users and has moved only R$ 35,000 since it started two months ago. Most bets on the platform are politics-related, such as the chances of President Lula's reelection and favorites in state races; others are more unorthodox, like the probable winner of a bodybuilding championship.

Farache believes the company's numbers should grow rapidly in the coming months thanks to partnerships with financial institutions and influencers. In the case of influencers, the idea is to encourage them to create their own "pools" in exchange for a fixed slice of the amount – about 6%. According to him, Prévias is close to finalizing the integration of its platform into a major digital bank's app – and is in talks with another. "There are millions of users who will have access to the platform," he said.

It's a model similar to what XP did with Kalshi, in a strategic partnership announced last month. With one detail: Clear brand customers have access to prediction markets only in XP's offshore environment. "Here we do everything in real," said Farache.

At the end of last month, BTG Pactual also launched BTG Trends, its derivatives contract platform that is currently only available to investors with aggressive profiles.

Meanwhile, betting companies are moving to prevent this market from growing. The Brazilian Responsible Gaming Institute (IBJR) said that prediction markets are nothing innovative, and that allowing them to operate outside the betting regime could generate consequences such as "unfair competition, weakening consumer protection, threat to sports integrity, and loss of tax revenue." "International experience reinforces this reading. In the United Kingdom, such products are clearly treated as bets," said André Gelfi, president of IBJR.

For Farache, the comparison is misguided because prediction markets companies do not operate against the investor; they only function as infrastructure. "Betting today in Brazil mixes sports betting with casinos, and much of the revenue comes from that," said Arthur. "Here there's no casino, no slots: it's an operation within the capital markets, like any investment."

But sometimes it really does look like a bet.
