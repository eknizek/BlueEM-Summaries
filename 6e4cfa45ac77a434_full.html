Opinion: The causes of INSS benefits' shocking growth

13 April 2026 · 09:50 AM

# Opinion: The causes of INSS benefits' shocking growth

The granting of new benefits by the National Social Security Institute (INSS) and the General Social Security System (RGPS) has increased exorbitantly since 2023, resulting from various measures that expanded opportunities for fraud in the system. These benefits include not only retirement and survivor pensions, but also temporary disability benefits (sick leave), maternity allowance, BPC/LOAS, and others.

There has been a break in the pattern observed over the last three decades, in which the growth of benefit grants occurred at a rate relatively close to that of the elderly population. In 2022, for example, 5.2 million INSS benefits were granted. The number jumped to 7.6 million in 2025 (an accumulated increase of 47%). This behavior corresponds to an average annual increase of 13.6% per year between 2023 and 2025 – a departure from the 3.8% annual rate that prevailed in the 2022 to 1995 comparison.

The granting of RGPS benefits jumped from 4.5 million in 2022 to 7 million in 2025 (an increase of 55.4%). This increase generated an average growth in the 2023 to 2025 period of 15.8% per year, again departing from the average of 3.3% per year in the 2022 to 1995 comparison.

The change in trend from 2023 onwards is unequivocal. A peculiar aspect of this sharp increase in benefit grants is that it occurred simultaneously with a significant increase in the so-called INSS queue, which grew from about 1.5 million applications in December 2023 to 3 million in December 2025. This queue represents a potential (and billionaire) pension liability that tends to pressure spending starting in 2026.

In the past there have been annual rates of growth in benefit grants at high levels, but often related to processes of clearing the queue, which had grown due to strikes or management problems. This time, however, there is a high growth of benefit grants over several consecutive years, and simultaneously with a sharp increase in the queue.

A factor that certainly weighed on this change in trend, but not necessarily was its only cause, is the so-called Atestmed, which significantly altered the modus operandi of granting temporary disability benefits. Very briefly, to make life easier for applicants (and thus reduce the queue), Atestmed allowed the granting of sick leave benefits based solely on the analysis of compliance with private medical certificates, eliminating the need for in-person medical examination.

A first conceptual aspect that needs to be debated is that illness, by itself, does not necessarily mean work incapacity. There is a need for analysis with minimum rigor to assess whether the illness generates work incapacity or not. The granting via only compliance analysis by medical certificate seems to reinforce this logic that, necessarily, illness is synonymous with work incapacity. In practice, it ended up being a disguised outsourcing to the private sector, with possible generation of moral hazard and potentially inadequate incentives.

Of course, the adoption of this measure aimed at a necessary and important objective of reducing the INSS queue. However, there was no effectiveness from this point of view. Apparently, despite faster granting, the program encouraged an increase in applications, as it facilitated obtaining the benefit, stimulating demand.

Annual granting of temporary disability benefits jumped from 2 million to 4.1 million in the 2022 to 2025 comparison, an increase of 104.7% – and an average annual growth of 27% per year. The granting of temporary disability benefits increased about 104.7% between 2022 and 2025, while the total number of RGPS contributors increased only 8.2% in the same period. Another account in Brazil that doesn't add up.

Spending on temporary disability benefits grew from R$ 27.6 billion in 2022 to R$ 43.4 billion in 2024, an increase of 57.6% in just two years.

In evaluating Atestmed, the Court of Accounts of the Union found "insufficiency of fraud detection mechanisms and control of compliance with benefit grants through documentary analysis, as well as lack of merit analysis by the Federal Medical Examination, which may lead to improper granting of social security benefits, generate rework and encourage (by low expectation of control) the filing of applications based on false or gratuitous medical certificates."

Moving in the right direction, the federal government began adjustments to Atestmed through Law 15,265/2025, which determined that the medical-expert examination for sick leave could continue to be carried out through documentary analysis, but establishing that in these cases, the benefit could not extend for more than 30 days. However, this duration period could be made exceptional by Executive action, in a justified manner and for a specified period. A succession of ordinances had maintained the period at 60 days. But in the opposite direction to the adjustment, recently the government issued another ordinance – this time extending the period to 90 days. It also began requiring the opinion of an expert doctor on the certificate submitted by the applicant.

In summary, Atestmed, in its current form, also creates the moral hazard that individual contributors who provide services to individuals can continue to engage in informal activity while filing or receiving temporary disability benefits.

The most important lesson here is that when it comes to an expense with social security benefits that reached R$ 1.027 trillion last year – the largest component of the Union's primary spending – one must be very cautious with any measure, as management changes can easily result in a billion-real increase in spending.

**Rogério Nagamine Costanzi** was Undersecretary of the General Social Security Regime from 2019 to 2022. He is a career server linked to the Ministry of Management.
