# Key points from the first global conference on phasing out fossil fuels held in Santa Marta

The first global conference led by Colombia and the Netherlands in Santa Marta to seek a way out of fossil fuel energy has come to an end, but what was advanced on the agenda in Santa Marta, a coal port on the Caribbean coast? Here are some conclusions.

## Roadmaps

France drew attention at the opening of the event by publishing a "roadmap" with details of its plan to eliminate all fossil fuels by 2050. Analysts said no other country had published such a clear and comprehensive plan, sending an important signal from one of the world's most advanced economies about its goals in this regard.

The commotion caused some discomfort in Santa Marta since there is no officially defined roadmap, and France did not announce a new policy, but rather existing commitments under a different title. Other countries noted that they also have calendars prepared for eliminating coal and other fuels, plans for decarbonizing heavy industry, and objectives for renewable energy use.

Leo Roberts, from the think tank E3G, said that a roadmap should be guided by science and make the phasing out of fossil fuels a "central planning principle" around which other policies revolve.

## Science

One of the main results was the creation of an expert scientific panel to advise governments, cities, and regions in planning their own transition routes away from fossil fuels. Carlos Nobre, a renowned climatologist and scientist from Brazil and one of the panel's promoters, told AFP that "this will provide all the solutions, to implement them and to finance them." The Scientific Panel for Global Energy Transition was humorously nicknamed the "Spaghetti" group by its English acronym: SPGET.

## Fossil Fuel-Free Zones

Driven by civil society, the concept of "Fossil Fuel-Free Zones" is gaining supporters in international meetings and found fertile ground in Santa Marta. The goal of these zones is to designate territories that, due to their ecological importance—from the Amazon to the Congo Basin and Indonesian rainforest—are protected by governments to prohibit any hydrocarbon exploration and extraction in them.

The Earth Insight expert group estimates that there are 58 protected areas of this type worldwide. Colombia, for example, banned hydrocarbon extraction and mining in its Amazon region last year to "stop the expansion of the extractive frontier," said Environment Minister Irene Vélez Torres, host of the conference.

## Next Conference in Tuvalu

Colombia passed the torch to Tuvalu, a small island state in the remote Pacific Ocean, which will host the next conference on phasing out fossil fuels in 2027. Tuvalu is seriously threatened by rising sea levels and has become a leading voice on the international stage for impoverished countries endangered by the consequences of climate change.

"This journey that began here, in a coal port of the Caribbean Sea, now heads to the Pacific Ocean," said Tuvalu's Climate Change Minister, Maina Talia.
