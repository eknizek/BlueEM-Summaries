End of Movistar Dominance in Fixed Internet?: Two Fiber-Optic Operators Target Lima

23 April 2026 · 01:00 PM

# Two Fiber-Optic Operators Target Lima: End of Movistar's Dominance in Fixed Internet?

In telecommunications, one of the segments showing the greatest growth and an increasingly competitive environment is residential internet. This dynamism not only responds to the expansion of the service, but also to the entry of operators with strong regional presence who are beginning to gain ground in Lima, which has attracted the interest of investment funds. Is a reshuffling among the main players approaching? What is expected for 2026?

The latest study by DN Consultores and TelcoETF, to which Gestión had access, offers a radiography of Peru's residential internet market. According to the report, this segment closed 2025 with revenues of S/ 3,600 million, a total of 4.4 million household connections, and an ARPU (average revenue per user) of S/ 69 monthly.

These figures reflect the growing relevance of the service within the telecom sector. "The fixed residential internet service shows quite robust growth in recent years and has been consolidating. Today its revenues are around S/ 3,600 million, within a total market that reaches approximately S/ 18,000 million," said Carlos Huamán, founding partner of DN Consultores.

In that line, he highlighted that the segment already exceeds 20% of total telecom sector revenues. "It is significant progress considering that a decade ago it represented around 10%. That is, its share has doubled in the last ten years," he explained to Gestión.

The growth of residential internet service had a clear turning point. "There is a before and after the pandemic. If you compare the results of 2019 with those of 2021, you can see a jump in both revenues and connections," the expert noted.

According to the research, revenues went from approximately S/ 2,200 million in 2019 to S/ 2,700 million in 2021, while connections surpassed 3 million to reach the current 4.4 million.

Huamán expressed that this boost responds mainly to changes in consumption habits. "Demand for speed has grown significantly because users access more and more digital applications," he noted.

Adding to this is the price factor. "The price per megabit has been reduced to its minimum expression. So, although ARPU remains relatively stable, what lies behind is an increase in the amount of megabits offered," he commented.

In that sense, he detailed that average speed went from 20 Mbps (megabits per second) in 2019 to nearly 400 Mbps in 2025. However, he warned that this model has limits. "The way to protect ARPU has been to offer more and more megabits, but that cannot be sustained indefinitely. Going forward, we will see other service differentiators," he stated.

Another factor that has driven sector growth is technological advancement. Currently, approximately 80% of residential internet connections correspond to fiber-optic to the home (FTTH), a proportion that is above the regional average, around 70%.

According to the report, the fixed internet market closed 2025 with 3.6 million FTTH connections, representing 18% growth compared to the 3 million recorded in the previous year. "Fiber is today the main engine of innovation in the sector and in Peru it already has a higher weight than the regional average. While there are more advanced markets, such as Uruguay, where penetration reaches 94%, in general terms the country is already above average, also driven by competition from independent ISPs (internet service providers)," he stated.

Will these results be maintained in 2026?

Eduardo Bisetti, founding partner of TelcoETF, believed that the growth trend in residential internet, especially driven by fiber, would continue in the short term. "Fiber deployment has been accelerating because it allows greater speeds and cost efficiencies, both in CAPEX and OPEX for telcos. Today investments are clearly focused on this technology," he emphasized.

He also indicated that greater competition, driven by independent ISPs, has pressured down the price per megabit and expanded market offerings. "This has generated a much broader and more competitive offer," he added.

However, he recalled that the mobile business remains the main one globally. "Mobile networks continue to be the largest telco business, and that proportion could be maintained," he clarified.

In that context, he projected that the residential internet segment would remain above 20% of telecom sector revenues this year.

He also warned that there is still significant room for growth in the country. "Today billing is concentrated in only half of households; the other half still does not have a fixed internet connection," he explained.

Regarding the role of new technologies, he considered that 5G will not replace fiber in the residential segment; on the contrary, 5G can facilitate access in areas where there is no infrastructure, although he recalled that it does not reach the quality of service offered by fiber to the home.

Another finding of the DN Consultores and TelcoETF report is linked to the advance of independent ISPs (internet service providers that do not belong to major operators) that have gained prominence in the market in recent years. While in 2015 they were practically nonexistent and by 2019 accounted for fewer than 200,000 connections combined, by 2025 they had reached 2.2 million connections.

With this, their share of the total market went from just 8% to 51% in that period, evidencing a structural change in segment competition.

Will Lima be the focus of ISPs?

What explains the greater competition in the residential internet segment is, to a large extent, the expansion of independent ISPs, driven by relatively low entry barriers. "In this business, initial investment is not so high, especially for operators that already came from cable."
