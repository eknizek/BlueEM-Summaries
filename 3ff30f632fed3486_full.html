# Easter 2026 Boosts Sales of Inflatable Pools and Water Safety Products

Easter is the peak sales season for inflatable pools and life jackets featuring bold designs like crocodiles, flamingos, ducks, and even hot dogs, according to Jorge Frauenberg, sales manager of Intex Latam, one of the leading Chinese-origin companies in this industry. In an interview, Frauenberg explained that to have inflatable products ready in stores across the country, orders are placed more than six months in advance, so this year prices would not be affected by the tariff increases that Mexico has applied to Chinese products since January.

The executive stated that despite the tariffs, "we sell pleasure, we sell an experience of being at home, being inside the pool, with something to drink, children jumping and playing, so it's something that will continue to work as long as someone wants to be happy."

However, he acknowledged that tariff rates would be noticeable until the 2027 vacation season, as many customers placed orders in advance. "Prices this Easter will likely be quite similar to what we've seen historically, but surely later on there will be a variation," Frauenberg warned.

Although the entire category will suffer from the tariff blow, the company is developing strategies to prevent the increase from straining Mexican families' budgets. "We try to talk with our clients and try to support them... A five-year-old child won't understand what a tariff is. The child wants their toys, wants to enjoy the pool."

Despite commercial challenges and the proliferation of low-quality Asian products without safety certifications sold at "ridiculously cheap" prices in street markets, Intex maintains aggressive expansion. In Mexico, the company projects sustained annual growth of 25%. This momentum comes not only from small children's pools but from a pandemic-inherited phenomenon: the adoption of large-volume structural pools (up to 12,000 liters) and inflatable mattresses, a category that already represents 40-45% of its sales in the country.

With rising import costs, the idea of installing a factory in Mexico is not ruled out. However, the company owner has not given the green light to this project. As an immediate alternative to overcome supply challenges, Intex is evaluating the creation of a logistics hub in Latin America, possibly in Panama, that would allow them to restock clients in less time.
