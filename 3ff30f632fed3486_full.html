# Easter 2026 boom: inflatable pools and floats sales surge with novelty designs

From hot dogs to ducks! Easter 2026 drives sales of inflatable pools and floats. Easter is the peak sales season for inflatable pools and floats with bold designs such as crocodiles, flamingos, ducks, and even hot dogs, according to Jorge Frauenberg, sales manager of Intex Latam, one of the leading Chinese companies in this industry.

In an interview, Frauenberg explained that to have inflatable products ready in stores across the country, orders are placed more than six months in advance, so this year prices would not be affected by the increase in tariffs that Mexico applies to Chinese products since January.

The executive stated that despite the tariffs, "we sell pleasure, we sell an experience of being at home, being inside the pool, with something to drink, children jumping and playing, so it's something that will continue to work as long as someone wants to be happy."

However, he acknowledged that tariff rates will be noticed until the 2027 vacation season, as many customers made orders in advance. "It's very likely that Easter 2026 prices will be quite similar to what has happened historically, but surely for later there will be a variation," warned Frauenberg.

Although the entire category will suffer from the tariff impact, the company is devising strategies to prevent the increase from suffocating Mexican families' budgets. "We try to talk with our clients and try to support them... A five-year-old child won't understand what a tariff is. The child wants his toys, wants to enjoy the pool."

Despite commercial challenges and the proliferation of low-quality Asian products without safety certifications sold at "ridiculously cheap" prices in street markets, Intex maintains an aggressive expansion pace. In Mexico, the company projects sustained annual growth of 25 percent.

This momentum comes not only from small children's pools but from a phenomenon inherited from the pandemic: the adoption of large structural pools (up to 12,000 liters) and inflatable mattresses, a category that already represents between 40 and 45 percent of its sales in the country.

With rising import costs, the idea of installing a factory in Mexico is not ruled out. However, the company's owner has not given approval for this project.

As an immediate alternative to overcome supply challenges, Intex is evaluating the creation of a logistics hub in Latin America, possibly in Panama, which would allow them to resupply customers in less time.
