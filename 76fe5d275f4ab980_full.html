# Wealth Tax: What Will Happen to Companies That Haven't Paid While Awaiting Court Decision?

Today marks the deadline for companies with assets exceeding 10.474 billion pesos to pay the second installment of the wealth tax established by the last emergency decree of the Gustavo Petro government, related to the winter weather crisis.

Although initially more than 15,000 companies were obligated to pay it, the Constitutional Court halted collection from foundations, private universities, and companies in liquidation. Justice provisionally suspended payment for this group of companies while it determines whether the tax is constitutional or not.

However, all companies were required to have paid the first installment to the National Tax and Customs Authority (Dian) on April 1st. The problem is that although the Government projected the tax on large companies (a general rate of 0.5 percent and 1.6 percent for financial and mining-energy activities) would raise 8 billion pesos—4 billion and 4 billion, respectively—the first installment only collected 2.43 trillion pesos. This means that 1.57 trillion pesos expected in April were not received.

"This could have been due to uncertainty about the legality and constitutionality of the decree, which in many cases may have led some taxpayers to file without payment or not file at all, while waiting for the Court's final decision on the matter. This considering that multiple requests for suspension and unconstitutionality of the parent decree and related tax decrees were already evident, and recently, for example, did result in suspension for a group of companies," stated Juan Pablo Díaz, senior counsel at Holland & Knight.

Until April 14th, according to former Dian director Lisandro Junco, who challenged the tax, the largest revenues came from financial activities, which collected more than 1.1 trillion pesos. Far behind are collections from commerce, transport and storage companies (368.612 billion pesos), manufacturing (237.892 billion pesos), and electricity, gas, steam and water (236.235 billion pesos).

## Is the Suspension an Absolution?

According to Juan David Velasco, tax partner at Baker McKenzie, for active companies, the obligation remains in effect; therefore, non-payment generates late-payment interest from the due date, and Dian could proceed with coercive collection if the declaration was filed without payment.

"If no declaration was filed, penalties are incurred for non-filing, plus interest, and if the Court later declares the tax unconstitutional, the non-payment will be protected. On the other hand, if it confirms constitutionality, penalties and interest remain. This tax has generated strong institutional tensions and has gone against the legitimate confidence of taxpayers," he explained.

Similarly, the Holland & Knight expert states that Dian could currently advance respective audit work as well as coercive collection proceedings for the first and/or second installment of companies that did not make payments while awaiting the ruling.

For the specific case of non-profit entities and companies in liquidation processes, which were covered by the Court's provisional suspension, Velasco indicates that the tax installment remains on hold and, therefore, generates neither late-payment interest nor penalties while the Court rules.

"If the Court declares it constitutional, the obligation is reactivated; if unconstitutional, non-payment is protected," he indicates.
