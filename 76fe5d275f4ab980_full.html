# Wealth Tax: What Will Happen to Companies That Haven't Paid While Awaiting the Constitutional Court's Decision?

Today marks the deadline for companies with assets exceeding 10.474 billion pesos to pay the second installment of the wealth tax established during the last emergency decree by President Gustavo Petro's government, related to the winter weather crisis.

Although initially more than 15,000 companies were obligated to pay it, the Constitutional Court halted collection from foundations, private universities, and companies in liquidation. The Court provisionally suspended payment for this group of companies while it determines whether the tax is constitutional. However, all of them were required to have paid the first installment to the National Tax and Customs Directorate (Dian) on April 1st.

The problem is that although the Government projected the tax on large companies (a general rate of 0.5 percent and 1.6 percent for financial and mining-energy activities) would raise 8 billion pesos, split into 4 and 4 billion respectively, only 2.43 trillion pesos were collected in the first installment. This means 1.57 trillion pesos fell short of April's projections.

"This could be due to uncertainty about the legality and constitutionality of the decree, which in many cases may have led some taxpayers to file without payment or not file at all, pending the Court's final decision on the decree and related tax matters," stated Juan Pablo Díaz, senior counsel at Holland & Knight.

Until April 14, according to former Dian director Lisandro Junco, who sued over the tax, the largest collections came from financial activities, which generated over 1.1 trillion pesos. Far behind were collections from commerce, transport and storage companies (368.612 billion pesos), manufacturing (237.892 billion pesos), and electricity, gas, steam and water (236.235 billion pesos).

## Is the Suspension an Absolution?

According to Juan David Velasco, tax partner at Baker McKenzie, for active companies, the obligation remains in force, and non-payment generates late payment interest from the due date, and Dian could proceed with forced collection if the company filed without paying.

"If no return was filed, penalties for non-filing are incurred, plus interest, and if the Court later declares the tax unconstitutional, the non-payment will be protected. On the other hand, if it confirms constitutionality, the penalties and interest remain. This tax has generated strong institutional tensions and has worked against legitimate taxpayer confidence," he explained.

Similarly, the Holland & Knight expert notes that Dian could currently proceed with audits and forced collection actions for the first and/or second installments of companies that did not make payments while awaiting the ruling. For non-profit entities and companies in liquidation liquidation processes, which were covered by the Court's provisional suspension, Velasco indicates the tax installment remains on pause and therefore generates neither late payment interest nor penalties while the Court decides. "If the Court declares it constitutional, the obligation is reactivated; if unconstitutional, the non-payment is protected," he states.
