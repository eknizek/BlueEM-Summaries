# Fintech UY3 seeks R$ 3 billion to accelerate private payroll-deduction lending growth

Central Bank data showed that private payroll-deduction credit reached a balance of R$ 101.6 billion in March, representing a 142.4% year-over-year growth in the modality. This surge was driven by the Worker's Credit program, launched by the federal government exactly one year earlier. And it's also shaping the strategies of UY3, a credit-as-a-service fintech that is in the process of raising R$ 3 billion to finance operations in this segment.

"The more dispersed our operations are, the better, but Worker's Credit is where we're placing the most effort," says Querandy Acosta, co-founder and director of UY3, to NeoFeed. "I believe these R$ 3 billion will be raised quickly, in about six months."

At this pace, the company's goal is to close 2026 with a monthly origination rate of R$ 1 billion in this area. Currently, the company records a volume of approximately R$ 300 million per month in these operations.

To turn – and accelerate – this wheel, the R$ 3 billion fundraising is being conducted through Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs), with different partners. Among them, XP, which structured two issuances of R$ 400 million each, already completed.

As part of its model, in all these vehicles, UY3, which provides credit infrastructure and funding for these operations to fintechs, financial institutions, retailers, and segments such as dealership networks and franchises, assumes the junior tranches of the FIDCs.

"We connect these partners with Faria Lima and take the risk alongside them," says Acosta. "Because often these companies don't have the financial capacity or access to the capital market."

On the supply side at the center of these issuances, he emphasizes that Worker's Credit still needs adjustments, as is typical of the evolution of initiatives of this scale. Which, initially, caused many banks to pump the brakes on this offering.

"But as these institutions test and learn, they open the tap more. And when they feel comfortable, they put their foot on the gas," says Acosta. "I think that in six months, this will be something crazy and this competition will be beneficial because it will drive down rates."

Operating in the program since the beginning, UY3 arrived in the market in 2021, when Acosta and brothers Tabaré and Caracé – the trio has Uruguayan ancestry, hence the startup's name – left the Plata Group, a family company that operated as a securitizer, to create the fintech focused on the clan's credit DNA.

"Many competitors went into Banking as a Service, but we understood that was a commodity," says Acosta. "BaaS is a bicycle, not much technology, but it runs well. Credit as a Service, on the other hand, is a motorcycle, with more value, but it needs combustion – and funding – to run."

Speaking of funding, so far, UY3 has already raised a R$ 50 million round from NVA Capital, a manager created by Marcelo Maisonnave, Pedro Englert, Eduardo Glitz, and Victor Knewitz, former XP partners, and with Vectis Partners. In addition to another R$ 200 million with funds from Vinci Compass.

With these resources, the startup consolidated a portfolio that, in addition to Worker's Credit, operates modalities such as FGTS advance, public payroll deduction, buy now pay later, and car equity. With this package, the company closed 2025 with over R$ 6 billion processed on its platform. For 2026, the projection is to exceed R$ 11 billion.

As part of this journey, Acosta says that UY3 already has a defined and active strategy to debut in the M&A arena. In its sights are credit fintechs operating in ecosystems such as vehicles and healthcare. And the appetite, it seems, is large.

"The time has come to make this inorganic move," he states. "Today, we already have one hundred companies mapped. And from here on, these deals will start to pop."
