# Credit Today, a Weak Engine for Driving Consumer Spending

The Economy Minister is a firm advocate for Argentines taking out loans; he recommended it to his friends and even urged officials on his team to take out loans from Banco Nación. Following data on the decline in economic activity in February (2.6% compared to January and 2.1% year-on-year), the official need to find "green shoots" grew by leaps and bounds. The need to extend the horizon of economic discourse became evident. In that sense, the shift by Luis Caputo was noticeable. Unlike what he had been saying for months, he now assures that there is no "Kuka risk" (that is, that Kirchnerism could return to power). Trying to calm and build confidence is a difficult task, especially with the coexistence of a war in the Middle East, with Brent oil spiking at $113 a barrel (the price that governs Vaca Muerta) and when the Government aims to lower inflation after the poor 3.4% result in March.

The Government highlights the fiscal and external surplus (consulting firm Abeceb says exports would reach $100 billion this year, with energy, agriculture and mining as the star sectors) and that it will continue with its plan. President Javier Milei's statement during EFI Expo closing week—"Those who don't adapt, break"—is sufficiently categorical.

Amid this host of definitions about what may come, two worrying data points emerged about the most battered part of the economy (industry, construction, commerce), which in turn generates the most jobs. One of those data points, according to Empiria, is that as of January there was a fifth consecutive decline in families' disposable income and that in the new composition of family spending, fixed expenses (water, electricity, transport) represent 23% of income.

That's when the jump in loan default rates appeared, which set off alarm bells on banking boards and for Santiago Bausili, president of the Central Bank (BCRA), who blames banks for "blindly granting" a first wave of loans. In the latest BCRA banking report, family loan delinquency reached 11.2% in February, quadrupling from a year ago. Bausili maintains that the worst of delinquency has already passed and banks claim to have provisions for that part of their portfolios in irregular situations for regulatory reasons. Will virtual wallets also have them backed up?

Carlos Pérez, director of the Capital Foundation, focuses on the fact that peso credit to the private sector has maintained real declines since the beginning of the year. "Credit to the private sector in pesos does not reach 10% of GDP," he says and adds that in neighboring countries this ratio is up to three times higher. In a country with a Gross Domestic Product around $700 billion, having a total credit between pesos and dollars for an approximate calculation of $90 billion (13%) constitutes a small engine to contribute to growth.

The opening of external financing by more solid private companies has been very decisive. Nearly $10 billion came in. In April, there were $1.7 billion from placements by Vista, Edenor and Chubut province. Additionally, according to consulting firm 1816, dollar loans were generated in banks that, not counting credit cards, represented $1.149 billion.

Eduardo Costantini, founder of Nordelta and the Malba museum, stated at EFI Expo that the much-sought mortgage credit "is for 30 years" and that conditions are not in place today: "The new real estate market is narrower than the used market" and that is why there is a record of deeds on what is already built. The dollar decline so far this year and rising costs have led construction along a narrow path that now advocates for greater caution in the financial system for financing projects and families squeezed by falling real wages and disposable income.

One key remains what Argentines who can do so will do with their savings. In that sense, an important data point from 1816's analysis is that in the first quarter "the entire trade balance was taken by the formation of external assets" including card payments abroad. Many more dollars are entering Argentina today, but it remains to be seen how many go to banks to expand credit and investments and how many go under the mattress. This is a very important result, the product of more or less confidence.
