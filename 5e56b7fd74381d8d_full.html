# From Brazil to Turkey, Emerging Markets Return to Global Bond Issuance in April

Emerging market bond issuances are regaining momentum after last month's stagnation, as issuers from Brazil to Turkey capitalize on market recovery to raise new funds. Sales of dollar and euro-denominated bonds from developing countries this month have already surpassed volumes from April last year by approximately 200%, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

A total of $46 billion was raised through Friday, as governments and companies rushed to take advantage of improved market sentiment amid hopes for a peace agreement between the United States and Iran.

The recovery follows a decline in issuances last month, when the war reduced appetite for higher-risk assets. Now, with markets actively buzzing again and stock exchanges on the verge of recovering pre-war losses, emerging markets have returned to being the favorite investment for yield-seeking investors.

"The emerging market bond market appears to have largely overcome war risk for now," said Laura Reardon, portfolio manager at MFS Investment Management.

"Deals that were in the pipeline before the conflict and were suspended are now coming to market, and investors have cash to deploy after the recent market stabilization."

Reardon highlighted the recent track record of resilience among emerging economies — which recovered quickly from crises such as the pandemic and the war in Ukraine. Although many countries were severely hit by the spike in oil prices last month, a large group of energy-producing nations in Africa and Latin America benefit from elevated prices.

Additionally, the artificial intelligence investment boom is seen as a favorable factor for the developing world. Investors absorbed bonds from energy exporters Brazil and Qatar, while issuances from Turkey and Poland were also well-received, as new bonds typically offer yields slightly higher than existing issuances.

Among companies, well-known names like Banco do Brasil returned to the market, alongside smaller companies such as Eldik Bank from Kyrgyzstan, which took advantage of the more favorable environment to make its debut in the dollar market.

All were encouraged by declining funding costs since Iran and the United States agreed to a two-week ceasefire. The average yield premium required by investors to hold emerging market sovereign dollar bonds relative to Treasuries fell to around 245 basis points — below levels observed shortly before the war began.

"With spreads where they are, it makes sense to issue," said Carmen Altenkirch, emerging market sovereign analyst at Aviva Investors. "Sovereigns usually wait for periods of relative calm to issue, especially regular issuers like Turkey."

April's total issuances include nearly $19 billion from emerging market companies, up 88% from the same period last year, according to the data. Markets remained open on Monday, ignoring an escalation in war tensions, while Kazakhstan's railway operator hired banks for a two-tranche issuance.

The operation followed those by Korea Ocean Business Corp and Chinese group Shuifa, which began marketing dollar bonds earlier in the day.

## Cash on Standby

The recovery in issuances means the year-to-date volume already exceeds levels from the same period last year by nearly 20%, reaching $281 billion. The recovery is similar to the increase in activity observed in developed markets. Sovereigns such as Italy and the United Kingdom recorded record demand for their new bonds, but subordinated debt issuances — generally considered higher risk — also advanced strongly this month.

One reason is that many investors want to deploy cash accumulated during the March liquidation, as any agreement to end the war is likely to trigger an even greater recovery in global markets.

"Emerging markets are far from the only place where conditions appear relatively immune to geopolitics," said Kieran Curtis, head of emerging market local currency debt at Aberdeen Group. "I have the impression that portfolio managers raised a lot of cash last month. Resource outflows were actually very limited, so now investors need to deploy and issuers are taking the opportunity."

Brazil capitalized on the moment to conduct its largest bond issuance in history, raising 5 billion euros in its first euro-denominated issuance in over a decade. Fund managers also agreed to lend to Turkey, despite its vulnerability to higher energy prices, and absorbed its $2 billion issuance with orders three times the amount offered.

Nevertheless, buyers extracted from Turkey an issuance premium of approximately 15 to 20 basis points, well above the five basis points obtained in previous issuances, according to estimates by Nimrod Mevorach, strategist at UBS Group. Poland, another energy importer, paid a premium of 12 to 15 basis points when it raised $6 billion in early April, he said, in contrast to virtually no premium in its previous issuance.

An even more remarkable indicator of demand was the debut of the Democratic Republic of Congo with a $1.25 billion issuance. Investors placed orders four times the amount, likely attracted by yields of 8.75% and 9.5% in the two tranches of the operation. Manuel Mondia, portfolio manager at Aquila Asset Management, said that while the DRC is fragile, it has low debt levels and exports large volumes of copper, an essential input for AI-related investments.

"It was a good deal for bond holders, at a time when these spreads are becoming increasingly rare," said Mondia, who purchased the paper. "The search for yield is overcoming fear of conflict and contagion."
