# From Brazil to Turkey, Emerging Markets Resume Global Bond Issuances in April

Emerging market bond issuances are gaining momentum again after last month's stagnation, as issuers from Brazil to Turkey take advantage of market recovery to raise new capital. Sales of bonds denominated in dollars and euros from developing countries this month already exceed volumes recorded in April last year by roughly 200%, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

A total of $46 billion was raised through Friday, as governments and companies rush to capitalize on improved market sentiment amid hopes for a peace agreement between the United States and Iran.

The recovery comes after a decline in issuances last month, when war reduced appetite for higher-risk assets. Now, with markets again in intense activity and stock exchanges on the verge of recovering losses from before the conflict, emerging markets are back as the investment of choice for yield seekers.

"The emerging market bond market seems to have broadly overcome war risk for now," said Laura Reardon, portfolio manager at MFS Investment Management.

"Deals that were in the pipeline before the conflict and were suspended are now coming to market, and investors have cash to allocate after the recent market stabilization."

Reardon highlighted the recent track record of resilience among emerging market economies — which have recovered quickly from crises such as the pandemic and the war in Ukraine, for example. Although many countries were hard hit by last month's oil price surge, a large group of energy-producing nations in Africa and Latin America benefits from elevated prices.

Furthermore, the AI investment boom is viewed as a favorable factor for the developing world. Investors absorbed bonds from energy exporters Brazil and Qatar, while issuances from Turkey and Poland were also well received, as new bonds typically offer yields slightly higher than existing issuances.

Among companies, well-known names like Banco do Brasil returned to the market, alongside smaller companies like Eldik Bank from Kyrgyzstan, which capitalized on the more favorable environment to make its dollar market debut. All were encouraged by the decline in borrowing costs since Iran and the United States agreed to a two-week ceasefire.

The average yield premium required by investors to hold emerging market sovereign bonds in dollars relative to Treasuries fell to around 245 basis points — below levels observed shortly before the war began.

"With spreads where they are, it makes sense to issue," said Carmen Altenkirch, emerging markets sovereign analyst at Aviva Investors. "Sovereigns usually wait for periods of relative calm to issue, especially regular issuers like Turkey."

April's total issuances include nearly $19 billion from emerging market companies, up 88% compared to the same period last year, according to the data. Markets remained open on Monday, ignoring a resurgence in war tensions, while Kazakhstan's railway operator hired banks for a two-tranche issuance. The operation followed those by Korea Ocean Business Corp and Chinese group Shuifa, which began marketing dollar bonds earlier in the day.

The resumption of issuances means the year-to-date accumulated volume already exceeds levels from the same period last year by nearly 20%, reaching $281 billion. The recovery is similar to the increase in activity observed in developed markets. Sovereigns like Italy and the United Kingdom recorded record demand for their new bonds, but subordinated debt issuances — generally considered higher-risk — also advanced strongly this month.

One reason is that many investors want to deploy cash accumulated during March's liquidation, as any agreement to end the war tends to trigger even greater recovery in global markets.

"Emerging markets are far from the only place where conditions seem relatively immune to geopolitics," said Kieran Curtis, head of emerging markets local debt at Aberdeen Group. "I have the impression that portfolio managers raised a lot of cash last month. Resource outflows were actually quite limited, so now investors need to allocate and issuers are seizing the opportunity."

Brazil seized the moment to conduct its largest bond issuance in history, raising 5 billion euros in its first euro-denominated issuance in over a decade. Fund managers also agreed to lend to Turkey, despite its vulnerability to higher energy prices, and absorbed its $2 billion issuance with orders three times the offered amount.

Nevertheless, buyers extracted a pricing premium from Turkey of approximately 15 to 20 basis points, well above the five basis points obtained in previous issuances, according to Nimrod Mevorach, strategist at UBS Group. Poland, another energy importer, paid a premium of 12 to 15 basis points when it raised $6 billion in early April, he said, in contrast to virtually no premium in its previous issuance.

An even more striking indicator of demand was the debut of the Democratic Republic of Congo with a $1.25 billion issuance. Investors placed orders four times the amount offered, possibly attracted by yields of 8.75% and 9.5% in the two tranches of the operation.

Manuel Mondia, portfolio manager at Aquila Asset Management, said that the DRC, although fragile, has a low debt level and exports large volumes of copper, an essential input for AI-related investments.

"It was a good deal for bondholders, at a time when these spreads are becoming increasingly rare," said Mondia, who purchased the paper. "The search for yield is outweighing fear of conflict and contagion."
