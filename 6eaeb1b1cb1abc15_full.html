# Japan Could Intervene 30 Times More to Support Yen, Says Goldman Sachs

Japan has the firepower to intervene 30 times in currency markets at the scale of last week, according to analysts at Goldman Sachs Group Inc (GS), though authorities are expected to conserve their reserves and intervene at more effective moments.

Authorities likely spent around 5 trillion yen (US$31.3 billion) last Thursday to shore up the yen after it weakened beyond 160 against the dollar, wrote Yuriko Tanaka, economist at the Wall Street bank, in a report released late Friday.

The intervention during relatively moderate volatility suggests that policymakers consider that level as the "line of defense," she added.

"Given the finite source of funds for currency intervention, we expect the MOF to aim at maximizing the impact of each intervention, and carefully select the most effective timing, such as when the yen is weakening rapidly," she added.

Japan has previously attempted to stem the yen's decline. The intervention came after repeated warnings from officials and was the first since July 2024, when the yen had also surpassed the 160 barrier, and triggered a rebound in the currency.

High energy costs and expectations that the spread between official U.S. and Japanese interest rates will not narrow soon have pressured the currency.

Looking ahead, the probability and magnitude of new moves to shore up the yen will depend on the pace of currency depreciation, market volatility and absolute levels, Tanaka said.

Japan holds approximately US$1.2 trillion in foreign exchange reserves as of late March, including US$161.7 billion in foreign currency deposits that can be used for intervention, with the remainder held in foreign securities.
