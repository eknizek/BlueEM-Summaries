# Asset Managers Unite to Pressure Tenda to Shut Down Alea 'Home Factory' After Years of Losses

Investment management firms have met with Tenda's board of directors to request the closure of Alea, a subsidiary that has accumulated losses and consumed cash while Tenda itself shows positive results. Alea, which was supposed to reach breakeven in 2025, now projects this for 2027, with accumulated losses of R$ 500 million over five years. AZ Quest, one of the managers present, expressed concern about continuing to invest in a project that has not proven viable, questioning Tenda's governance. Despite criticism, Tenda defends Alea as a long-term investment opportunity, representing only 1% of its revenue. Tenda's stock has appreciated, but the Alea situation raises questions about the company's capital discipline and the effectiveness of its strategic decisions.

Following Squadra's escalation against Hapvida's management and Mak Capital's call for the removal of Oncoclínicas' board, a group of investment managers is now uniting to pressure Tenda to discontinue Alea, the construction company's subsidiary operating in the off-site segment with wood frame construction systems. NeoFeed learned that after Tenda's results announcement in early March, J.P. Morgan arranged a meeting between Cláudio Andrade, board chairman, and a group of dissatisfied buy-side managers regarding Alea's performance. Present at the meeting were Absolute, AZ Quest, Ibiuna, Kinea, Vinci, among others. There was direct pressure regarding discontinuation of the project, which operates a factory in Jaguariúna in São Paulo's interior, but has never justified the investment, according to minority shareholders.

"It's not a consensus within the company either. Management itself knows this is holding back the stock price," says a manager who participated in the meeting but requested anonymity. Andrade, a partner at Polo Capital, Tenda's reference shareholder with nearly 20% stake, suggested that investors needed the same patience the market had with Elon Musk's ideas. He stated that "2-3% losses in a billion-dollar company should be viewed as R&D."

"They are destroying value with our money," says Welliam Wang, equity manager at AZ Quest, to NeoFeed. The manager's reaction—the firm manages over R$ 39 billion—was to dedicate its March investor letter to the topic. AZ Quest's question is "why do some companies insist on investments that no longer make sense?" Although not naming Alea directly, NeoFeed confirmed with Wang that the criticism is directly related to the poor performance of Tenda's subsidiary.

On the surface, Tenda is experiencing a recovery. The company ended 2025 with net income of R$ 505.7 million, improved adjusted gross margin to 36.8%, and cash generation of R$ 243.8 million. The company's core business of popular housing construction is again operating efficiently, with better operational indicators and greater predictability. However, when looking at Alea's performance, it appears to be an outlier.

Created as a bet on industrializing civil construction, Alea was born with a narrative of gaining scale, reducing costs, and transforming popular housing production in Brazil. In practice, the opposite occurred. The operation accumulates recurring losses, consumes cash, and has required successive revisions of expectations. In the fourth quarter of 2025, for example, Alea recorded losses of R$ 50.2 million, with negative gross margin (-29.4%) and negative EBITDA (R$ -56.1 million).

The original plan for Alea was to reach breakeven last year, but the new projection was postponed to 2027. In the latest statement, the company reports that "Alea's cash consumption in the second half of 2025, annualized, points toward annual consumption in 2026 already heading toward guidance of R$ 60 million to R$ 80 million."

For AZ Quest, what was a growth thesis became, in practice, a continuous adjustment process. In the letter, the manager identifies a recurring pattern in the market and describes the moment when investment logic begins to shift. "When results are consistently below expectations, accumulating losses of over R$ 500 million over 5 years [...] what was supposed to pay for itself becomes 'R&D' with no timeline to end."

In Alea's case, the repositioning was reflected in the balance sheet. The company states it traded accelerated expansion for restructuring; shifted from scale gains to efficiency; and moved away from promised results toward actualizing adjustments. Strategy or insistence?

AZ Quest's letter goes further, questioning the rationale behind the bet itself. In discussing the concept of efficiency, the manager uses the same figure chosen by Tenda's board chairman. In discussing Musk, the text observes that businesses with high operational complexity and structurally low margins tend to destroy value—especially when they require high execution levels. It draws a direct parallel with industrialized civil construction. "Elon Musk would never enter structurally complex, low-margin businesses [...] such as the attempt at industrialized production of low-income houses that have even negative margins," write AZ Quest's managers.

The implicit reading is that Alea may not be merely an execution problem but a conception problem. Despite negative results, the operation continues to demand capital and attention from the company. This persistence raises the central question of whether it makes sense to continue allocating resources to a thesis that has not yet been proven. According to AZ Quest, the answer involves governance.

The manager, without naming names, describes a scenario where power structures and incentives can lead to the maintenance of "investment decisions despite unsatisfactory results." At this point, the manager demonstrates part of the dissatisfaction present in the J.P. Morgan meeting: governance ceases to function as a control mechanism and instead acts as a shield for the decision itself. "When the response to legitimate shareholder questions becomes 'if you disagree, sell,' who is being protected?"

Alea's story impacts not just Tenda. It reveals corporate behavior. As the letter itself states: "In the short term, the market functions as a voting machine, but in the long term, as a balance scale."

For Tenda, the outcome of this story goes beyond a subsidiary. It will help define how the market perceives its capital discipline. On B3, TEND3 stock has accumulated a gain of 35.5% for the year and 129.5% over 12 months. Tenda's market value is R$ 3.9 billion.

When contacted by NeoFeed, Tenda provided the following statement: "The construction company reports that its Board of Directors monitors Alea's evolution and understands that the initiative has potential for value generation for the company in the long term, by operating in the homebuilding market and addressing the labor shortage in the sector through industrialization."
