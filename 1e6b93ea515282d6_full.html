# Asset managers unite to pressure Tenda to shut down Alea "home factory" subsidiary after years of losses

Investment managers met with Tenda's board to request the shutdown of Alea, a subsidiary that accumulates losses and drains cash, while Tenda presents positive results. Alea, which was supposed to reach breakeven in 2025, now projects this for 2027, with cumulative losses of R$ 500 million over five years. AZ Quest, one of the present managers, expressed concern about the continuity of investments in a project that has not proven viable, questioning Tenda's governance. Despite criticism, Tenda defends Alea as a long-term investment with potential, representing only 1% of its revenue. Tenda's stock has appreciated, but Alea's situation raises questions about the company's capital discipline and the effectiveness of its strategic decisions.

After Squadra escalated tensions against Hapvida's management and Mak Capital called for the removal of Oncoclínicas' board, a group of asset managers is now uniting to pressure Tenda to discontinue Alea, the construction company's subsidiary that operates in the off-site segment with wood frame construction systems. NeoFeed learned that, after Tenda's results announcement in early March, J.P. Morgan organized a meeting between Cláudio Andrade, chairman of the board of directors, and a group of dissatisfied buy-side asset managers regarding Alea's performance. Present at the meeting were Absolute, AZ Quest, Ibiuna, Kinea, Vinci, among others. There was direct pressure to discontinue the project, which operates a factory in Jaguariúna, in the interior of São Paulo, but has never justified the investment, according to minority shareholders.

"Within the company it is not a consensus either. Management itself knows that this holds back the stock's rise," says a manager who participated in the meeting but asked not to be identified. Andrade, who is a partner at Polo Capital, a reference shareholder with almost 20% stake, suggested that investors needed to have the same patience the market had with Elon Musk's ideas. And that "2% to 3% losses in a billion-dollar company should be seen as R&D."

"They are destroying value with our money," says Welliam Wang, equity manager at AZ Quest, to NeoFeed. The response from the manager, which holds more than R$ 39 billion under management, was to dedicate its March investor letter to the topic. AZ Quest's doubt is "why do some companies continue insisting on investments that no longer make sense?"

Although not mentioning Alea's operation by name, NeoFeed confirmed with Wang that the provocation is directly related to the poor performance of Tenda's subsidiary. At first glance, Tenda is experiencing a moment of recovery. The company ended 2025 with net profit of R$ 505.7 million, an improvement in adjusted gross margin to 36.8% and cash generation of R$ 243.8 million. The company's core business, which is popular housing construction, has returned to operating efficiently, with better operational indicators and greater predictability. When looking at Alea's performance, however, it seems like an outlier.

Created as a bet on construction industrialization, Alea was born with a narrative of gaining scale, reducing costs, and transforming popular housing production in Brazil. But in practice, the opposite occurred. The operation accumulates recurring losses, drains cash, and has required successive revisions of expectations. In the fourth quarter of 2025, for example, Alea recorded a loss of R$ 50.2 million, with negative gross margin (-29.4%) and EBITDA also in the red (R$ -56.1 million). The original plan for Alea was to reach breakeven last year, but the new projection was postponed to 2027. In the last balance sheet, the company states that "Alea's cash consumption in the second half of 2025, annualized, points to annual consumption in 2026 already heading toward guidance of R$ 60 million to R$ 80 million."

For AZ Quest, what was a growth thesis became, in practice, a continuous adjustment process. In the letter, the manager identifies a recurring pattern in the market. And describes the moment when investment logic begins to shift. "When results consistently fall short of expectations, accumulating losses of more than R$ 500 million over 5 years [...] what was supposed to pay for itself becomes 'R&D' with no timeline to end."

In Alea's case, the repositioning was placed on the balance sheet. The company says it swapped accelerated expansion for restructuring; shifted from gaining scale to efficiency; and left the promise of results for the execution of adjustments. Strategy or persistence?

AZ Quest's letter goes further and questions the very rationale behind the bet. In discussing the concept of efficiency, the manager uses the same figure chosen by Tenda's board chairman. Speaking of Musk, the text observes that businesses with high operational complexity and structurally low margins tend to destroy value—especially when they require high execution levels. And it makes a direct parallel with construction industrialization. "Elon Musk would never enter structurally complex businesses with reduced margins [...] such as, for example, the attempt at industrialized production of low-income homes that have even negative margins," write AZ Quest's managers.

The implicit reading is that Alea may not just be an execution problem, but a conception one. Even with negative results, the operation continues to demand capital and attention from the company. Persistence in this context raises the central question of whether it makes sense to continue allocating resources to a thesis that, thus far, has not proven itself. The answer, according to AZ Quest, lies in governance. The manager, without naming names, describes a scenario where power structures and incentives can lead to the maintenance of "investment decisions maintained despite unsatisfactory results."

At this point, the manager demonstrates part of the dissatisfaction that was present at the J.P. Morgan meeting: governance stops functioning as a control mechanism and starts acting as a shield for the decision itself and "when the answer to legitimate shareholder questions becomes 'if you disagree, sell,' who is being protected?"

Alea's story does not impact only Tenda. It reveals something about corporate behavior. As the letter itself states: "in the short term, the market functions as a voting machine, but in the long term, as a scale."

For Tenda, the outcome of this story goes beyond a subsidiary. It will help define how the market sees its capital discipline. On B3, TEND3 stock has accumulated gains of 35.5% this year and a rise of 129.5% in 12 months. Tenda's market value is R$ 3.9 billion. When contacted by NeoFeed, Tenda forwarded the following statement: "The Construction Company informs that its Board of Directors monitors the evolution of Alea and understands that the initiative has potential for value generation for the company in the long term, by operating in the home development market and addressing the shortage of skilled labor in the sector through industrialization."
