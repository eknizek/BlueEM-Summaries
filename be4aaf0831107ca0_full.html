# Wheat Acreage Projected to Fall 3% as Input Costs Surge

The Buenos Aires Grain Exchange projected wheat planting for the 2026 campaign at 6.5 million hectares, representing a 3% year-over-year decline, though it remains 2.8% above the five-year average. The input-product ratio emerges as one of the central axes of the campaign. During 2026, deterioration has deepened compared to previous years: acquiring one ton of urea requires 55% more wheat than in the previous cycle, while diammonium phosphate has increased by 29%.

Regional behavior reflects this context. In the Northwest and Northeast regions, a positive trend is observed in planting intention, driven by good moisture availability, though subject to price evolution and input availability. In the center-west, particularly Córdoba, a tendency to maintain area predominates, with possible adjustments in the technology package or incorporation of alternatives such as rapeseed or camelina. Conversely, in the center-east of the agricultural area, including major production centers Santa Fe and Entre Ríos, a negative trend is recorded, primarily driven by high fertilizer costs, with urea values near $1,000 per ton.

In the south, in Buenos Aires and La Pampa, wheat area retraction is also observed, with greater inclination toward barley. From a climate perspective, the scenario forecasts a transition toward conditions associated with a Niño event. During winter, precipitation near normal levels and lower frost risk are expected, while spring could see above-average rainfall, favoring crop development.

Internationally, wheat develops in a context of greater global supply. According to the USDA, world production would reach 844.15 million tons, with stock recomposition raising the stock-to-consumption ratio to 34.7%. This scenario tends to moderate prices, though volatility factors persist linked to geopolitical tensions.

In the local market, wheat price at harvest stands around $217 per ton, with a slight 3% year-over-year improvement, but still below the five-year average. In this context, the deterioration of the input-product ratio consolidates as one of the main variables explaining producer caution. In concrete terms, this deterioration implies that larger wheat volumes are needed to cover major production costs. The urea ratio shows a 55% year-over-year increase, while diammonium phosphate rises 29%. Meanwhile, diesel presents a ratio approximately 41% higher than the previous campaign, raising labor costs, and herbicides also reflect less favorable conditions.

According to the report, this scenario forces adjustments, either through reduced input use, especially fertilizers, or through shifts toward crops with lower requirements, consolidating a more defensive approach in broad productive regions.
