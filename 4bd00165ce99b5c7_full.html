Mexico: Second Global Destination for Cybernetic Money Laundering

19 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Mexico: Second Global Destination for Cybernetic Money Laundering

The digital security landscape has reached a critical dimension for the Mexican economy and the integrity of its financial institutions. According to the most recent report from the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) of the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI), Mexico has climbed positions to become the second global recipient of fraudulent international bank transfers.

The 2025 Internet Crime Report recently presented details that, through various deception schemes, cybercriminals have found in the national financial system a strategic gateway for capital of illicit origin, surpassing nations with traditionally vulnerable financial infrastructures in the Asian region.

"Mexico received 1,782 money transfers from victims of internet scammers after these individuals instructed them to make deposits abroad," the annual report from the U.S. agency points out precisely. This figure positions the country only behind Hong Kong, displacing nations such as Vietnam, Indonesia, and Canada.

The data reflects unprecedented sophistication in money routes, where transnational criminal groups take advantage of Mexican banking interconnectivity to triangulate funds obtained through investment fraud schemes and corporate identity impersonation.

On a global scale, the IC3 report for 2025 reveals a multibillion-dollar crisis that appears to have no ceiling: "Total losses from cybercrimes amounted to 20.8 billion dollars, representing an alarming increase of 26% compared to the previous year," stated José A. Pérez, Director of Operations of the Criminal and Cybernetic Branch of the FBI.

The FBI is emphatic in its diagnosis by stating that "fraud related to investments was again the main component of these losses, with a 65% increase compared to the previous year, closely followed by business email fraud (BEC)."

For the Mexican economy, this global increase translates into direct pressure on its money laundering prevention (PLD) mechanisms, as it is a preferred destination for the dispersal and "laundering" of these assets before being integrated into the legal system.

The data suggest that as digital assets become more mainstream, participants are being increasingly exposed to complex fraud schemes, including the use of unregulated cryptocurrency exchanges.

## Complaints on the Rise

Regarding the incidence of direct reports, the country also maintains an upward trend in the volume of complaints filed with the FBI, which evidences greater exposure of local users. "Mexico submitted 1,654 complaints, placing it 11th on the list of countries with the highest number of external complaints received by the IC3, ranking below Germany and France."

This data underscores that Mexicans not only act as involuntary intermediaries in the money laundering chain but are active victims of crimes ranging from phishing to digital extortion, in an environment where artificial intelligence has begun to play a disruptive role in creating difficult-to-detect scams.

The report highlights that the use of crypto-assets has exacerbated the technical difficulty of tracing funds, reaching losses of 11.36 billion dollars in this specific sector. In this regard, Pérez emphasizes that "the data suggest that as digital assets become more mainstream, participants are being increasingly exposed to complex fraud schemes, including the use of unregulated cryptocurrency exchanges."

This warning is particularly relevant for the Mexican market, where the adoption of digital wallets has grown exponentially among the young population, without financial education and advanced cybersecurity layers advancing at the same pace as technological innovation.

## Deceived

Another critical point highlighted by the IC3 is the refinement of social engineering techniques applied to the business sector. The report indicates that "the business email compromise (BEC) scheme continues to evolve, with scammers using compromised email accounts from legitimate suppliers to divert payments to accounts under their control."

In Mexico, where supply chains are deeply integrated with the U.S., this method represents a systemic risk for small and medium-sized enterprises lacking two-step verification protocols for international transfers.

The FBI also warned of a generational vulnerability that affects all reported countries across the board, jeopardizing families' patrimonial savings. "People aged 60 and older recorded the highest losses of any age group, totaling 7.7 billion dollars in 2025," the report states starkly.

This segment of the population, which in Mexico's case possesses a significant portion of real estate assets and retirement funds, has become the main target of so-called "technical support scams" and romantic fraud, consolidating a threat that is no longer merely technical but a latent risk to the social and financial stability of Mexican households.
