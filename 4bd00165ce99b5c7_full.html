# Mexico, Second Global Destination for Cyber Money Laundering

The use of cryptoassets has exacerbated the technical difficulty in tracking funds.

The digital security landscape has reached a critical dimension for Mexico's economy and the integrity of its financial institutions. According to the most recent report from the Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) of the Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI), Mexico has climbed positions to become the second global recipient of fraudulent international bank transfers.

The Internet Crime Report 2025, recently presented, details that through various deception schemes, cybercriminals have found in the national financial system a strategic arrival point for capital of illicit origin, surpassing nations with traditionally vulnerable financial infrastructures in the Asian region.

"Mexico received 1,782 money transfers from victims of internet scammers, after these victims were given instructions to make deposits abroad," the annual report from the U.S. agency notes precisely. This figure positions the country only behind Hong Kong, displacing nations such as Vietnam, Indonesia, and Canada.

The data reflects an unprecedented sophistication in money routes, where transnational criminal groups exploit Mexican banking interconnectivity to triangulate funds obtained through investment fraud schemes and corporate identity theft.

On a global scale, the IC3 report for 2025 reveals a multimillion-dollar crisis with no apparent ceiling: "Total losses from cybercrime amounted to $20.8 billion, representing an alarming 26% increase compared to the previous year," said José A. Pérez, Director of Operations of the Criminal and Cybernetic Branch of the FBI.

The FBI is emphatic in its diagnosis, declaring that "fraud related to investments was once again the main component of these losses, with a 65% increase compared to the previous year, followed closely by business email fraud (BEC)."

For Mexico's economy, this global increase translates into direct pressure on its money laundering prevention mechanisms (PLD), as it is a preferred destination for the dispersal and "laundering" of these assets before being integrated into the legal system.

"The data suggests that as digital assets become more common or mainstream, participants are being increasingly exposed to complex fraud schemes, including the use of unregulated cryptocurrency exchanges," noted José A. Pérez, Director of Operations of the Criminal and Cybernetic Branch of the FBI.

## Rising Reports

Regarding the incidence of direct reports, the country also maintains an upward trend in the volume of complaints filed with the FBI, evidencing greater exposure of local users. "Mexico filed 1,654 complaints, placing it 11th on the list of countries with the highest number of external complaints received by the IC3, ranking below Germany and France."

This data underscores that Mexicans not only act as involuntary intermediaries in the money laundering chain but are active victims of crimes ranging from phishing to digital extortion, in an environment where artificial intelligence has begun to play a disruptive role in creating fraud schemes that are difficult to detect.

The report highlights that the use of cryptoassets has exacerbated the technical difficulty of tracking funds, reaching losses of $11.36 billion in this specific sector. In this regard, Pérez emphasizes that "the data suggest that as digital assets become more common or mainstream, participants are being increasingly exposed to complex fraud schemes, including the use of unregulated cryptocurrency exchanges."

This warning is particularly relevant for the Mexican market, where the adoption of digital wallets has grown exponentially among the youth population, without financial education and advanced cybersecurity layers advancing at the same pace as technological innovation.

## Deceived

Another critical point highlighted by the IC3 is the refinement of social engineering techniques applied to the business sector. The report indicates that "the business email compromise (BEC) scheme continues to evolve, with scammers using compromised email accounts of legitimate suppliers to divert payments to accounts under their control."

In Mexico, where supply chains are deeply integrated with the U.S., this modality represents a systemic risk for small and medium-sized enterprises that lack two-step verification protocols for international transfers.

The FBI also warned about a generational vulnerability that affects all reported countries across the board, jeopardizing family savings. "People aged 60 and older registered the highest losses of any age group, totaling $7.7 billion in 2025," the report states bluntly.

This sector of the population, which in Mexico's case holds a significant portion of real estate assets and retirement funds, has become the main target of so-called "technical support scams" and romance fraud, consolidating a threat that is no longer merely technical but a latent risk for the social and financial stability of Mexican households.
