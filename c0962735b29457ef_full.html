# Minabus, Extreme-Condition Mining Transport Specialist, Targets Accelerated Growth and Plans to Double Workforce by 2026

Transporting people by bus from Iquique to mining operations located over 4,000 meters high, traveling nearly 200 kilometers, requires operating with high levels of control. In this context, Minabus began in 2025 betting on technology and safety as the axis of its operation. The company, founded by José Luis Tobar, currently operates with around 23 people and nine to 10 vehicles on route.

The company organizes its operations with a focus on safety, relying on technological tools and controls that begin before each shift and are maintained throughout the service. "We are positioning safety as a competitive advantage, not as an obligation, not as an accessory," says Tobar, general manager of Minabus.

## Real-Time Monitoring

Among the most relevant controls is real-time speed monitoring, fatigue detection through state-of-the-art cameras, and a pre-service declaration system in which each driver confirms whether they are in physical and mental condition to operate and whether they have consumed any medication or substance. The model is similar to the briefing that airline pilots conduct before each flight.

The use of this type of technology, especially cabin cameras, recently opened a debate in the industry. At the end of 2025, Union No. 2 of Minera Sierra Gorda SCM filed a complaint with the Antofagasta Labor Inspection for violating truck drivers' privacy, detecting systems that operated permanently during work hours with continuous recording and without consent, which was classified as constant and invasive surveillance.

In Minabus's case, the system functions under different logic. According to Tobar, the use of these cameras is agreed upon with drivers and does not imply permanent monitoring, but rather activates in the face of risk events. "It is consensual with the drivers and they allow it. We are not constantly visualizing behavior; the camera works when there is an alert," Tobar explains.

The technology combines cameras, sensors, and artificial intelligence to analyze driver behavior in real time. The system keeps the camera focused on the cabin, but only generates records when it detects conduct considered risky, such as drowsiness or, for example, prolonged blinking, repeated distractions, cell phone use, or failure to use a seatbelt. In those cases, an automatic alert is activated and a video clip is sent to the monitoring platform.

"If the driver has a blink longer than usual, takes their cell phone or gets distracted, the camera emits an alert. Everything depends on the driver's behavior," he details.

Additionally, the system incorporates proximity sensors and front cameras, which allows monitoring to be complemented with information about the vehicle's surroundings. This approach, according to Minabus, seeks to intervene only when there is a risk, avoiding constant supervision of the worker. This is complemented by an alcohol and drug control protocol that not only monitors but also includes internal prevention campaigns. Tobar acknowledges that the regional context makes this point especially critical.

"Beyond controlling it, we promote it. We have these alcohol and drug campaigns. We know that the issue, especially here in the area, is quite serious."

## Digitalization and In-House Development

Parallel to external tools, the company developed proprietary software called "Integra," which digitizes internal operational processes. The platform also seeks to eliminate paper use, a commitment to sustainability that Tobar defines as part of the company's identity. Additionally, it works with the Labor Department to digitize attendance records, a practice that is still done with physical books in the private passenger transport sector.

"Today there is no digitalization for private passenger transport in the industry. Only attendance books are used, which in my view are obsolete."

All this technological investment is self-funded. Tobar clarifies that mining companies demand certain standards, but the development of Integra and the digitalization of attendance are initiatives that go beyond what is required.

## Expansion

The scenario, however, is not without pressure. The rise in fuel prices has directly impacted personnel transportation, forcing a review of current contracts. In Minabus's case, several agreements include adjustment formulas linked to the CPI and diesel prices, which in the current context has generated significant increases. According to the company, some adjustments can reach up to 25%, which has led to opening conversations with clients to absorb the impact gradually.

The strategy has been to advance agreements that allow sustaining operations without suddenly transferring costs.

In parallel, the company projects accelerated growth. It currently operates with 23 workers and expects to close 2026 with at least 50, in line with its participation in new bidding processes, mainly in the Antofagasta Region. "It is a good year of growth for the sector we are in, because given the mining investments being published, it is attractive," says Tobar.

With less than a year of operation, Minabus already provides services to Collahuasi and Quebrada Blanca, the two main mining companies in the region. Its good performance allowed it to be invited to new processes, opening space for expansion outside Tarapacá. As part of that process, the company will be present at Exponor 2026 in Antofagasta, where it already has meetings scheduled with potential clients, in a scenario marked by new mining investments that continue to drive demand for specialized services in northern Chile.
