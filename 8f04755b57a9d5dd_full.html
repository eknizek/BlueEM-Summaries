# Dual Minister Mas Names Five Directors Joining Enami Amid Ongoing Restructuring

During the Senate's Mining and Energy Commission, dual minister Daniel Mas revealed the names of five directors who must be renewed by the current José Antonio Kast government on the board of the National Mining Company (Enami). "The process is not yet 100% complete, but the names are already defined," he told parliamentarians.

These are Álvaro Iriarte, Max Larraín, Rolando Cubillos, Danielle Laport, and Dieter Wunder, who will serve as replacements and join the 10-member board, which includes the board president, a position that falls—by law—to the Mining Minister.

According to subsequent confirmations made to DF, Iriarte, Larraín, and Cubillos will be appointed by President Kast himself and will replace Nancy Pérez, Valentina Santelices, and Leandro Voisin. Danielle Laport represents Corfo, while Dieter Wunder is the director designated by the Ministry of Finance, replacing Rodrigo Wagner.

Among the new directors, distinct profiles stand out. Álvaro Iriarte is a lawyer and Master of Liberal Arts from the University of Chicago. He served as legislative advisor for two years to then-deputy Sofía Cid and currently works as director of content for the Res Publica Institute, a center for political studies and training.

Max Larraín is familiar to Enami: he served as prosecutor for the state company between 2018 and 2022 and has an extensive legal background linked to the mining sector. Today, he is the legal manager of Aclara Resources, the Canadian firm developing a rare earth project in Penco.

Rolando Cubillos has 35 years of experience in the mining sector, having held executive positions in Chile at gold mining giants such as Barrick and Kinross, as well as other positions at Lundin Mining's Candelaria.

Danielle Laport is a civil industrial engineer with an MBA from PUC. Her career has been linked to finance positions, and she currently serves as Corporate Management Manager at the Chilean Construction Chamber.

Dieter Wunder is an economist from the University of Chile and holds a doctorate in Economics from Boston University. He was economic advisor to the Ministry of Public Works under Alfredo Moreno (2018-2019), director of the Investment Committee at the Ministry of Economy under Juan Andrés Fontaine (2010-2013), and also served on Enami's board between 2011 and 2013. He has been director of various companies and is currently an independent consultant for the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB).

Dual minister Mas indicated that all appointees will assume their positions on the Enami board next week.

## Mas Presents Enami's Situation

During his Senate intervention, dual minister Mas stated that "the Enami we received is an Enami that owes $220 million, so only for debt service, we have to pay on the order of $10 million annually. It is a truly significant figure for the budgets the company manages."

In any case, the state secretary said that in past years the debt had even exceeded $600 million and that during 2025 the company registered profits. Still, he noted that according to the budgets received, it is estimated that this year the state company will record "a small loss" and there is "a slightly positive cash position."

"The previous government disposed of certain assets: it sold its percentage in Quebrada Blanca to cover this hole of over $600 million accumulated, closed the iconic operation that was the smelter (Paipote), and today we are looking at assets that are truly not part of operations. The first is the large quantity of mineral tons we have, which as obvious as it may seem to have to sell them, has not been done for a long time, so we want to prioritize that (...). Rather than sell, what we want is to strengthen and make that something real in the short term," said the minister.

The issue that remained pending—and for which a special session was convened—was the lithium project in High Andean Salt Flats being developed by the state company (49%) with Rio Tinto (51%). "To maintain that participation we will have to invest a large amount of resources, the question is where from? we don't have an answer," said Mas, to which senator Daniel Núñez (PC) countered saying that a large portion of the investments for said initiative would come from the Anglo-Australian mining company.

Mas responded: "We have put several tens of millions of dollars. Enami has indeed put money from its coffers into this investment."

It should be noted that, according to information provided by both companies when confirming the alliance, Rio Tinto would be the financier and operator of the project, with an initial ownership stake of 51%.
