# From Cochabamba to the Salar de Uyuni: Bolivia accelerates tourism to revive economy

Bolivia has stood out as a mining giant since the colonial era and later became a major natural gas exporter. Now, as it emerges from two decades of leftist rule, the Andean nation is seeking to make its mark as a tourism destination. The new government hopes that the country's diverse geography, rich history, and cultural depth can help it attract foreign travelers and, with them, the dollars that Bolivia's ruined economy desperately needs.

Its tourist attractions range from high, snow-capped mountains to lush and biodiverse tropical forests. There are Incan settlements, Jesuit missionary cities, and a city once known as the world capital of silver that still houses an active 500-year-old mine. And of course, there is the salar—an enormous salt plain that is home to one of the world's largest lithium deposits.

Developing this will be crucial for the country's future prosperity. But capturing a significant slice of the battery metals market is a long-term project, so President Rodrigo Paz's government hopes the travel sector can offer a quicker solution. Last year, Bolivia recorded 1.1 million foreign visitors and received $854 million in tourism revenue, representing just under 5% of gross domestic product.

Having taken the initial step of elevating tourism to a full ministry, Paz's government aims for 2.6 million visitors and $3 billion in income by 2030, with the sector's contribution to total economic output growing to 7%.

Janette Simbron, who runs the luxury tourism company Bolivia Milenaria and has more than 30 years of experience in the sector, said this is the right time for the government to finally capitalize on tourism's potential.

"The raw materials are there: the natural resources, the attractions, and the iconic Salar de Uyuni," she said. "However, if we don't create the right conditions, we could end up killing the goose that lays the golden eggs."

Hotel and ground transportation infrastructure will need to be expanded and improved. But the air sector represents the biggest challenge, as Bolivia remains one of Latin America's most difficult destinations to reach due to limited connectivity with major airlines.

Paz's government, elected in November, is working on a new brand for the country and is prioritizing six destinations, including Uyuni and Bolivia's famous Cerro Rico. It is also coordinating with multilateral creditors to channel investments to the sector.

"Tourism has been identified as a strategic sector within the new productive matrix, given its potential to diversify an economy historically dependent on hydrocarbons and mining," Tourism Minister Cinthya Yañez said in a written response.

Beyond the area containing the salt flats, the former silver mining mecca of Potosí and the constitutional capital of Sucre, Bolivia hopes to promote its wine region of Tarija; the de facto capital of La Paz—situated in a dramatic canyon 3,600 meters above sea level—and nearby Lake Titicaca; as well as Rurrenabaque, the gateway to the Amazon and Madidi National Park. Cochabamba, a gastronomic hub near a huge Christ statue, and Torotoro National Park are also on the list, along with the commercial center of Santa Cruz de la Sierra and the semiarid savannas to the east and west.

"Tourism is one of Bolivia's most immediate and scalable opportunities to generate foreign currency, drive growth beyond major economic centers, and help the country return to a path of long-term prosperity," wrote economists Tim Freeman and Ricardo Hausman in an April report from Harvard University's Growth Lab. Funded by billionaire Marcelo Claure, the analysis argues that international tourism can offer gains to the Bolivian economy "without the long investment cycles required in some other tradable sectors."

Mining Minister Marco Antonio Calderon agrees that tourism could generate more dollars than lithium mining in the short term. "Therefore, we must protect the areas of the Salar de Uyuni where tourism is carried out," he said in an interview, adding that there is no rush to industrialize the battery metal. "We have to do this correctly, that is what matters most."

The Bolivian government is now working on a zoning process in the salt flats to ensure space for tourism activities, protected from mining areas where lithium extraction could be developed long-term, according to Yañez.

Previous leftist administrations in Bolivia repeatedly attempted to extract the metal used in electric vehicle batteries on a large scale from Bolivia's highlands, mainly through a state company. The landlocked country has twice the resources of neighboring Chile, but they are still not considered commercially viable. The deposits have high levels of magnesium, which makes lithium production more expensive. And the nearest port is over 300 miles away.

Governments invested money in evaporation tanks in the Uyuni salt flats, but a lithium industrial plant opened in 2023 is operating at only 15% of capacity. Former President Luis Arce tried to shift production to direct extraction in an attempt to accelerate the process, signing contracts with companies from China and Russia that the new government is now reviewing.

The decisions of Paz's predecessors are also behind Bolivia's lag in air connectivity. Governments favored the public airline over private companies, according to Jorge Valle, president of the national association of airlines. "I've never seen anything like it in my 52 years of experience in the sector," he said.

The new government promised an "open skies" policy to improve connectivity and attract investments. Although more legal reforms are needed, the government took the first step to allow foreign airlines to transport passengers and cargo between Bolivia and other countries. The government is also in negotiations with Canadian construction giant Aecon Group to expand Santa Cruz airport and transform it into a regional hub.

Analysts agree that developing tourism will take time. Paz's push is starting "from a very low base in terms of awareness," said Brian Seel, portfolio manager focusing on Latin America at Artisan Partners in Boston. But "if we don't start today, it will never happen."

The government, for its part, hopes its changes will help the country diversify its economy and generate much-needed revenue. "We already have the tourism pipeline, now we need to open the valve with air connectivity," said Deputy Tourism Minister Andrés Aramayo.
