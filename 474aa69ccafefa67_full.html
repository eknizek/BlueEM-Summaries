# Rising Food Costs: 'Perfect Storm' Pressures Food Prices and May Impact Central Bank's Inflation Target

Beyond the shock from the effects of the war, particularly on fuel prices, another group is beginning to appear on the radar as a source of inflationary pressure greater than anticipated this year and next. According to economists interviewed by Estadão/Broadcast, the benign dynamics of food prices observed in 2025 may give way to two consecutive years of increases above the Broad Consumer Price Index (IPCA), imposing even greater difficulty on the Central Bank's task of bringing the indicator to its 3% target.

More than the escalation in fertilizer prices caused by the conflict between the United States and Iran, which blocked the flow of a significant portion of the input's production, climate events that have the potential to cause a perfect storm for inflation are also factoring in. First, there is a non-negligible probability that the 2026 El Niño will be strong. The event, which occurs in mid-year, would coincide with the dry season in the Southeast region. This would be a combination that, in estimates such as Warren Investimentos' extreme scenario, could add up to 2 percentage points to IPCA increases over the two-year period.

Food and beverages, the largest group in the official inflation index measuring price variations in a consumption basket for families earning between 1 and 40 minimum wages, represent more than one-fifth (21.3%) of the IPCA. This percentage rises to 24.3% in the National Consumer Price Index (INPC), whose basket covers families with monthly income between one and five minimum wages.

Gustavo Cruz, chief strategist at RB Investimentos, reports that the institution's agribusiness clients are very concerned about the fertilizer increase. "They are already trying to lock in prices at higher levels. Part of it comes from reduced profits, and the other part is passed on. We will have more expensive food ahead because there is no way to escape the rise in this input."

According to a Warren study, six items top the list of greatest weight that, in addition to having high sensitivity to oil price increases, show rapid transmission of price increases within up to one month of the shock. They are: meat, processed fish, poultry and eggs, milk and dairy products, baked goods, and oils and fats. However, there are also food items in groups with medium-speed transmission, which occurs between two and four months, such as cereals, legumes, and food outside the home, and also in the slow transmission group, which occurs five months or more after the shock: flour, starches, pasta, and beverages and infusions.

According to Andréa Angelo, inflation strategist at the investment platform, these prices represent a significant portion of the total impact—calculated at 1.7 percentage points through 2027—of the group of prices sensitive to increases in Brent crude oil barrels in the IPCA. She explains that the effects come from the costs of transporting products, particularly milk, and also from the rise in fertilizers—which, in Warren's scenario, are more concentrated in 2026 than in the following year. "Natural food products capture the effect of more expensive fertilizers quickly. But, in the grains and commodities segment, depending on how much the 2026/2027 harvest becomes more expensive due to the shock, the impact could be more significant in 2027," she noted.

This is the view of Sergio Vale, chief economist at MB Associados, who is more concerned with food dynamics next year than this year. In his estimates, the IPCA for food and beverages will barely decelerate between 2026 and 2027, from 5.4% to around 5%, but the risks are upward. "This year we will have normal seasonal pressure on food, but this combination that could affect 2027 is more concerning, with the additional effect of fertilizers that we will need to monitor, because it depends on how the war develops," said Vale.

Should El Niño indeed escalate to strong—a possibility considered by entities such as the World Meteorological Organization (WMO), a UN agency, and the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts—and coincide with water deficit in the critical phase of second-crop corn from April to June, and the currency depreciates, there would be an impact of 0.39 to 0.49 percentage points on monthly inflation, estimates the economist. In this case, the accumulated 12-month inflation for food could reach 10% still this year, he says. "It is not yet the most likely scenario, but there is a non-trivial possibility."

Even the base scenario is challenging for the Central Bank, he pointed out, as it adds yet another element of risk to an already pressured IPCA.

Luis Otávio de Souza Leal, partner and chief economist at G5 Partners, points out that, according to his calculations, years with El Niño formation registered average annual food inflation of 11.6%. Without the climate phenomenon, the figure was 6.1%. "Short-cycle crops, such as vegetables, greens and fruits, can be impacted. And then we will indeed have higher food inflation in the second half," he predicts. If the event begins in June, the effect on prices will be more concentrated this year, says Leal. Should it begin in August, natural food production would be more affected in 2027. According to G5's estimates, food inflation at home will advance from 5% this year to 7% next year.
