# Monitored by the IMF, the Government advances cautiously with stabilisation measures

Luis Caputo took note of the International Monetary Fund's recommendations and in recent weeks has advanced with some difficulties in discreet measures to dismantle the monetary squeeze, with one eye on the dollar and inflation. The emergency scheme implemented last year before the elections caused interest rates to soar, made credit more expensive, and caused a slowdown in activity. In recent days, the head of the Central Bank, Santiago Bausili, told a group of bankers privately that the priority is to "stabilize rates."

Although officials deny they will modify the money supply control adopted after the 2024 run on banks, following the cases of Israel and Peru, they acknowledge that "perfecting" the system is intended to "facilitate credit and activity." With 2027 elections in mind, the Government needs to boost consumption by families and businesses, currently at record default levels in 20 years.

After last year's rate spike, the Central Bank reduced bank reserve requirements, encouraged lower rates on loans, and two weeks ago reinstated a kind of interest rate corridor, although it continues to operate cautiously. As Clarín anticipated, unofficially, the BCRA reactivated active repurchase agreements with a maximum rate of around 25% nominal annual, which was added to the existing floor of 20% nominal annual for passive repurchase agreements. In this way, it seeks to eliminate liquidity squeezes at the window that banks use to lend money to each other and where the Central Bank can take or deliver pesos.

The rates that banks pay savers for deposits have stabilized around 20% or even less (Banco Nación pays 18%)—well below an expected inflation of 30%—and the rates they charge for personal loans have fallen to 63%. From this spread, financial institutions take more than 40 basis points in margin, but are reluctant to lend at similar rates for more than 12 months.

According to a banker who met with Bausili, the changes are because "next year, with elections, it will be more difficult and they seek to give banks guarantees of rate stability." "They want to avoid volatility and make credit cheaper," he summarized.

Another purpose would be to keep the carry trade alive to make returns in pesos and prevent harvest dollars from leading to excessively low rates and, therefore, a weaker dollar. The rate corridor was implemented two days after the technical agreement reached on April 15 with the IMF staff. As part of the understanding, the organization requested measures to contain rate volatility alongside strict monetary policy, greater accumulation of reserves, and maintaining controlled spending, amid falling tax collection.

The problem is that the adjustments have begun to encounter limits, starting with the low demand for pesos by companies and high household default rates. Another obstacle is the dollar. According to 1816, the Government would have sold dollar-linked bonds on Monday to stop the retail exchange rate from rising. Thus, after rising 4% in seven trading sessions to $1,445, it closed at $1,410 on Thursday. There was another fact reflecting efforts to mitigate a dollar rise that would further delay inflation decline: in the last 20 days until April 26, effective monthly rates for wholesale fixed-term deposits taken by banks grew in real terms by almost 1 point, although they remain below the pace of prices, according to Quantum, consultant Daniel Marx's firm.

For its part, the Central Bank has already purchased some US$7 billion in reserves. Milei had told Bausili that dollars would come out of his ears. That said, according to 1816, during the first quarter the entire balance of trade foreign exchange was taken up by dollar purchases and card expenses, which makes the inflow of financial dollars with private or public debt more important.

Meanwhile, the market sees erratic monetary policy. On one hand, the BCRA expands peso issuance when it buys foreign currency, and on the other, the Treasury places bonds above maturities, restricting the peso supply. "They want to maintain discretion; depending on the moment, they want less inflation or more activity, they change everything all the time," complained someone at a private bank.
