# Under IMF Monitoring, Government Cautiously Advances Stabilization Measures

Luis Caputo took note of the International Monetary Fund's recommendations and in recent weeks has advanced with some difficulties on discrete measures to dismantle monetary tightening, keeping a close eye on the dollar and inflation. The emergency scheme implemented last year before the elections caused interest rates to skyrocket, made credit more expensive, and caused a slowdown in activity. In recent days, Central Bank President Santiago Bausili told a group of bankers in private that the priority is to "stabilize rates."

Although officials deny they will modify the money supply control adopted after the 2024 currency run, following the cases of Israel and Peru, they acknowledge that the "refinement" of the system is to "facilitate credit and activity." Facing the 2027 elections, the Government needs consumer spending by families and businesses to rebound, currently at record default levels in 20 years.

After last year's rate spike, the Central Bank reduced bank reserve requirements, encouraged a decline in credit rates, and two weeks ago reinstated a sort of rate corridor, although it continues operating with caution. As Clarín anticipated, unofficially, the BCRA reactivated active repos with a maximum rate of around 25% nominal annual, which added to the floor that already existed of 20% nominal annual for passive repos. This way, it seeks to eliminate liquidity squeezes at the window that banks use to lend money to each other and where the Central Bank can take or deliver pesos.

The rates banks pay savers for deposits stabilized around 20% or even less (Banco Nación pays 18%)—well below a forecast inflation of 30%—and rates charged for personal loans dropped to 63%. From that difference, the entities take more than 40 basis points in margin, but are reluctant to lend at similar rates for more than 12 months.

The changes, according to a banker who met with Bausili, are because "next year, with elections, it will be harder and they want to give banks guarantees of rate stability." "They want to avoid volatility and cheapen credit," he summarized. The other purpose would be to keep the carry trade alive to make rates in pesos and prevent harvest dollars from leading to rates that are too low and, therefore, to the dollar.

The implementation of the rate corridor came two days after the technical agreement reached on April 15 with the IMF staff. As part of the understanding, the organization requested measures to contain rate volatility along with strict monetary policy, greater accumulation of reserves, and keeping spending controlled, amid the decline in tax collection.

The problem is that the adjustments began to find limits, starting with the low demand for pesos from companies and high default rates among households. The other obstacle is the dollar. According to 1816, the Government would have sold dollar-linked bonds on Monday to stop the retail exchange rate from climbing. Thus, after rising 4% in seven trading sessions to $1,445, it closed at $1,410 on Thursday. There was another data point reflecting efforts to mitigate a dollar increase that would delay inflation decline even further: in the last 20 days through April 26, effective monthly rates for wholesale fixed-term deposits taken by banks grew in real terms by almost 1 point, although they remain below the pace of prices, according to Quantum, consultant Daniel Marx's firm.

On the other hand, the Central Bank has already bought about US$ 7 billion in reserves. Milei had told Bausili that dollars would come out "by the ears." That said, according to 1816, during the first quarter all the foreign exchange balance from goods trade went to dollar purchases and card spending, which makes the inflow of financial dollars with private or public debt more important.

Meanwhile, the market sees erratic monetary policy. On one hand, the BCRA expands peso issuance when it buys foreign currency, and on the other, the Treasury places bonds above maturities, restricting peso supply. "They want to maintain discretion; depending on the moment, they want less inflation or more activity, they change everything all the time," complained a private bank official.
