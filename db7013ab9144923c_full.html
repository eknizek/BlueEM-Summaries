Nueva EPS-Famisanar merger poses portfolio and payment risks to health providers

21 April 2026 · 11:30 PM

# Merger Between Nueva EPS and Famisanar Would Increase Portfolio Risks and Payment Delays to Health Centers

Health providers warn that integration could deepen illiquidity and affect resource flows in the sector. Famisanar accelerates closure of complaints and reduces backlog in care for affiliates by 25.2%.

The Union of Health Service Provider Institutions (Unips) warned about the financial risks of a potential merger between Nueva EPS and Famisanar, noting that it could worsen portfolio management and delay payments, directly affecting clinics and hospitals. The statement comes amid the structural crisis in the health insurance system, where providers warn that this type of reorganization, although seeking efficiencies, can generate negative impacts if not executed with technical rigor.

According to the statement, the integration of two insurers with operational and financial difficulties implies a high risk of liability concentration, which could translate into longer payment times and additional deterioration of cash flows. This situation is especially sensitive for the provider network, which already faces liquidity problems. The accumulation of obligations and uncertainty in portfolio auditing could deepen a scenario that sector stakeholders describe as critical.

## Financial Risks and Pressure on IPS Liquidity

Unips warns that merger processes typically generate prolonged periods of administrative transition, during which failures occur in the filing, auditing, and recognition of billing, affecting the financial stability of providers. In this context, it is noted that this situation "could deepen the already critical illiquidity of IPS," compromising their operational capacity, payment of health workers, and continuity in the provision of essential services.

The impact is significant given that delays in resource flows directly affect the sustainability of institutions, especially those with lower financial capacity and greater dependence on timely payments from the system. Additionally, the absence of clear rules regarding debt assumption and payment mechanisms increases uncertainty, creating a complex environment for financial planning by providers who depend on these revenues for daily operations.

## Contractual Uncertainty and Risks to the Service Network

From the perspective of approximately 3,707 providers linked to Famisanar, one of the main risks lies in the lack of guarantees regarding the management of existing and future obligations within the integration process. The statement warns that the absence of clear definitions about contractual continuity and financial commitments creates an environment of legal insecurity that could affect the stability of relationships between insurers and providers. This is compounded by the possibility of redefining the provider network based on financial criteria, which could result in the exclusion of some actors, service concentration, and the weakening of the hospital network in various regions.

This type of decision, according to Unips, can impact the system's problem-solving capacity and increase pressure on more complex services, especially in territories where care supply is already limited.

## Operational Challenges and Call for Process Oversight

On the operational level, integrating care models, contracted networks, and information systems between both EPS represents a considerable technical challenge, with risks associated with interoperability and process duplication. The statement notes that these difficulties can result in interruptions in care, access barriers for patients, and disarticulation of comprehensive care pathways, which is especially critical for high-risk populations.

Facing this panorama, Unips called on the National Health Superintendence to proceed with "maximum technical rigor, transparency, and a focus on system protection" if the process is finalized. It also requested the implementation of clear monitoring mechanisms, payment guarantees to the provider network, and contingency plans to ensure service continuity in an environment where system stability depends on the solidity of its actors.

The underlying message from the industry is that the sustainability of the health system cannot be built on the weakening of its provider network, so any reorganization process must prioritize financial stability and service protection.
