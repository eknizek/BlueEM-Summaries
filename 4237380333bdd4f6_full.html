# Colombian aviation aligns with regional trend: modern fleet reaches 38% with potential 11% emissions reduction

Colombia is part of a broader transformation in Latin American and Caribbean aviation, where the transition toward net-zero emissions by 2050 is advancing under market-specific conditions. This is according to a study presented by ALTA, an industry group representing airlines in the sector, which analyzes possible routes to achieve this objective in the region.

The report, developed with technical support from ICF International, an international consulting firm specializing in public policy analysis, energy, transportation, sustainability, and aviation, highlights that the path toward decarbonization does not follow a single model. In countries like Colombia, the challenge lies in advancing without affecting key variables such as connectivity, accessibility, and economic development that depend on air transport.

"There is a firm commitment from the industry to achieve Net Zero, but also recognition that Latin America and the Caribbean face different structural conditions," said Peter Cerdá, CEO of the Latin American and Caribbean Air Transport Association (ALTA). The executive insisted that aviation plays a relevant economic role in the region. "Aviation is not a luxury, it is an engine of economic development: it contributes 3.6% of GDP and supports 2.9% of employment in this part of the world. Ensuring its growth and sustainability is not only key for the industry, but for the competitiveness and connectivity of our countries," he noted.

The study included policy review, country-by-country analysis, and meetings with industry stakeholders, which allowed the construction of scenarios toward 2050 based on operational and regulatory information from the region.

## Modern Fleet

One of the main advances identified in the region, with direct impact on markets like Colombia, is fleet renewal. According to the study, 38% of air capacity in Latin America and the Caribbean operates with next-generation aircraft, surpassing Europe and the United States, where the indicator stands at 34%. This process has involved investments close to $40 billion in approximately 1,100 aircraft, which translates into improvements in fuel efficiency and emissions reduction. For Colombia, this is reflected in progressive modernization of operations, aligned with international standards.

In parallel, operational efficiencies are consolidating as the most immediate tool. The report estimates that measures such as route optimization, reduction of ground time, and the use of digital technologies can generate emissions reductions of up to 11%. However, progress on this front depends on coordination between airlines, airports, authorities, and air navigation service providers. The study warns that without such coordination, the reduction potential might not be fully realized.

## SAF and Carbon Bonds: Challenges and Opportunities for Colombia

In the long term, sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) emerge as the main alternative to reduce emissions, although their implementation faces significant barriers. Their cost, between three and twelve times higher than conventional fuel, limits adoption without incentives. The study warns that massive adoption without state support could increase costs per seat by $43 and reduce air traffic by up to 30%. In the Colombian case, this poses challenges in terms of competitiveness and access to air transport.

Despite these obstacles, the region has potential to develop this industry thanks to the availability of raw materials. The challenge lies in building regulatory frameworks and infrastructure to support SAF production and implementation at scale.
