# Government Submits $171 Million Vulture Fund Settlement to Senate with 10-Day Approval Deadline

Ten years after paying a group of vulture funds that sued Argentina for the 2001 default, the Government is now aiming to finalize an agreement for $171 million with a handful of plaintiff funds that remained in litigation in American courts.

Through a bill that the Executive Branch sent to the Senate this Friday, the Casa Rosada requested Congressional authorization to pay this amount to a group of vulture funds. The operations are led by Bainbridge and another consortium headed by Attestor Value Master Fund. The lawyers representing Argentina in New York courts, where trials over bonds that defaulted in 2001 were conducted, reached an agreement with the litigants to complete the definitive payment that closes this legal front. It includes a discount on the disputed capital.

According to the official text, the transaction contemplates a discount exceeding 30% on the disputed amounts and sets payments of $67 million for Bainbridge and $104 million for the Attestor group, under a "delivery versus payment" mechanism—meaning the Government receives the bonds in question for their cancellation. Additionally, legal costs are borne by the creditors.

The agreement was reached in March but has a specific deadline for payment before the possibility of judicial claims could potentially reopen. The bill mentions the "urgency of legislative treatment" since "the agreements establish April 30, 2026, as the closing deadline ('Outside Date')."

"The lack of parliamentary approval after that date would cause automatic resolution of the transactions, obliging the Argentine Republic to resume litigation under significantly more burdensome financial conditions," stated the document, signed by Justice Minister Juan Bautista Mahiques and Chief of Staff Manuel Adorni.

Another point the Government highlighted as decisive is that approval of this bill will eliminate embargo claims on Argentine assets. Among other provisions, it includes the termination of lawsuits that involved attempted seizures of state stakes in companies such as YPF, Banco Nación, and Aerolíneas Argentinas, as well as the closure of asset investigation processes ("discovery") that creditors had been pursuing.

As specified in the bill, payment to the two funds in question will be made from the budget allocated for debt service payments.

In April 2016, the Ministry of Finance, then headed by Alfonso Prat Gay, announced the closure of an agreement with the main vulture funds that sued Argentina in New York courts over unpaid bonds from the late 2001 crisis. The plaintiffs had favorable judgments in first and second instances, and the U.S. Supreme Court had rejected reconsideration of the appeals court ruling, so justice determined the country had to negotiate an exit through an agreed payment with the funds. One of the negotiators was Luis Caputo, then Secretary of Finance. The two main funds were NML Capital and Aurelius Capital Management.

To complete the payment, Congress approved the Sovereign Payment Law and the repeal of the "lock" and "sovereign payment" rules that prevented improving the conditions offered in previous exchanges. To obtain these funds, the Mauricio Macri Government issued bonds in international markets for approximately $16.5 billion, in what was one of the largest placements by an emerging country at that time. It represented Argentina's return to the international debt market.

The payment was completed on April 22, 2016, and agreements with holdouts for approximately $9.3 billion were settled. A group of smaller funds chose to remain outside that understanding and continued in courts to seek a more favorable agreement. The vulture fund business involved buying defaulted securities at very cheap prices after their price collapse and then litigating in New York to claim full payment. According to official estimates, at the time the agreement closed, the operation yielded up to 1000% profit for the holdouts.
