# Government sends Senate bill to pay US$171 million to vulture funds, with 10-day deadline

Ten years after paying a group of vulture funds that sued Argentina over the 2001 default, the Government is now moving to finalize an agreement for US$171 million with a handful of litigant funds that remained in litigation in American courts.

Through a bill that the Executive Branch sent to the Senate this Friday, the Casa Rosada requested Congressional authorization to pay that amount to a group of vulture funds. The operations are led by Bainbridge and another consortium headed by Attestor Value Master Fund. The lawyers representing Argentina in New York courts, where trials over bonds that defaulted in 2001 were conducted, reached an agreement with the litigants to carry out the definitive payment that closes this judicial front. It includes a haircut on the disputed capital.

According to the official text, the transaction contemplates a haircut exceeding 30% on the disputed amounts and sets payments of US$67 million for Bainbridge and US$104 million for the Attestor group, under a "delivery versus payment" mechanism—meaning the Government receives the bonds in question for cancellation. Additionally, legal costs are borne by the creditors.

The agreement was reached in March but has a specific deadline for payment before the possibility of judicial claims could potentially reopen. The bill mentions the "urgency of legislative treatment" since "the agreements establish April 30, 2026 as the closing deadline ('Outside Date')."

"The lack of parliamentary approval after said deadline would cause the automatic resolution of the transactions, obliging the Argentine Republic to resume litigation under significantly more burdensome financial conditions," stated the document signed by Justice Minister Juan Bautista Mahiques and Chief of Staff Manuel Adorni.

Another point the Government highlighted as decisive is that approval of this bill will eliminate attachment demands on Argentine assets. Among other points, the termination of lawsuits is foreseen, including attempts to attach state stakes in companies such as YPF, Banco Nación, and Aerolíneas Argentinas, as well as closing of asset discovery processes that creditors had been promoting.

According to the bill, payment to the two funds in question will be made from the budget allocated for debt service payments.

**Payment to vultures and exit from default: ten years later**

In April 2016, the Ministry of Finance, then headed by Alfonso Prat Gay, announced the closure of an agreement with the main vulture funds suing Argentina in New York courts over unpaid bonds from the late 2001 crisis. The claimants had, at that time, favorable rulings in first and second instances, and a rejection from the U.S. Supreme Court to reconsider the appellate ruling, so the courts determined that the country needed to negotiate an exit through an agreed payment with the funds. One of the negotiators was Luis Caputo, then Secretary of Finance. The two main funds were NML Capital and Aurelius Capital Management.

To carry out the payment, Congress approved the Sovereign Payment Law and the repeal of the "lock" and "sovereign payment" regulations that prevented improving the conditions offered in previous exchanges. To obtain these funds, the Mauricio Macri government issued bonds in international markets for approximately US$16.5 billion, in what was one of the largest placements by an emerging country at that time. It represented Argentina's return to the international debt market.

The payment was completed on April 22, 2016, and agreements with holdouts were canceled for approximately US$9.3 billion. A group of smaller funds chose to stay out of that understanding and continued in courts seeking a more favorable agreement.

The vulture funds' business was to buy defaulted securities at very cheap prices after their prices collapsed and then litigate in New York to claim full payment. According to official estimates, at the time the agreement closed, the operation earned the holdouts up to 1000% profit.
