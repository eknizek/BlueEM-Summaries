# Anbima Updates Certification Models for Financial Market Professionals

Paulo Vieira

São Paulo

Anbima, an association of financial and capital market entities representing banks, asset managers, brokerages, distributors, and administrators, is implementing a profound change in its certification policy for workers seeking to enter and remain in the sector. This certification is mandatory for those who deal with investors or specialize in investments.

The three main certifications required since 2002—CPA-10, CPA-20, and CEA—were replaced in 2026 by three new ones: CPA, C-Pro R, and C-Pro I. The new exams began on February 19, following a postponement due to the high volume of tests from the old model administered in December. Through March 31, according to Anbima data, 4,746 exams were administered, with 3,795 being the CPA certification alone. The pass rate, considering the three new certificates, reached 58%—above the 2025 average of 50% with the old certifications.

The new exams have a duration of 2.5 hours, one hour less than previously required for the CEA exam. The change was designed to address the complexity of financial products currently available in the market, a scenario very different from what existed at the beginning of the century, and the popularization of personal investment platforms and apps.

The goal is now also to assess applicants' behavioral skills, their ability to understand clients' main demands, and to relate to them appropriately. The exams, previously composed exclusively of multiple-choice questions, now also require discursive answers and include case studies and decision-tree structures, opening directed pathways based on the candidate's prior selection. They also no longer demand accumulated knowledge, as was the case with CEA certification, instead limiting themselves to what is truly necessary for the desired certification.

According to the entity, by December 31, 2025, 434,000 people held the old certificates, which could be revalidated. Of the total certifications, 85% refer to the more basic CPA-10 and CPA-20, granted almost equally. There is no gender difference among certified professionals, and São Paulo, with about 15% of those certified, and Rio, with 5.64%, are the cities that stand out most.

The change, as expected, shook the market for preparatory courses for this segment, through which more than 80% of candidates pass—only 17% of them prepare autonomously for the exams, according to Anbima, which recently launched a free online study platform, Anbima Edu. The pass rate, however, is similar: 48% for those studying independently, 51% for those who opt for preparatory courses.

For Kleber Stumpf, CEO of TopInvest, one of these preparatory course companies, the changes are welcome. "They are quite aligned with the market and now explore basic aspects of the activity, client relationships, and understanding of investments," he says. For Stumpf, there is also more fairness in the new model: "If previously candidates memorized what would appear on the exam, which could have impacts later on professional performance, now it should reward those who truly study, who really prepare."

Tiago Feitosa, from T2 Educação, told Folha that he completely remodeled his course materials due to the new exam model. He praises Anbima for listening to the preparatory courses throughout the process and disclosing the methodology for creating the new questions. For him, the evaluations were distant from the "day-to-day" work of professionals, something that tends to change now. "Since the student could, and still can, retake the exam if they failed, they would go, take a practice test and, even with new questions, would end up passing."

Although the businessman considers the advent of Anbima's educational platform positive, he justifies his business by offering candidates "curation and organization" of the knowledge that will be evaluated on the exams. Rafael Toro, from the "finance academy" that bears his name, also sees the change as positive, with the condition of improving professional qualifications. "It was very common for candidates to be approved for having memorized questions, but then, at the job interview, they couldn't get hired. Sometimes, they didn't have the ability to understand economic movements, the relationship, for example, between interest rates, exchange rates, and inflation."

Marcelo Billi, superintendent of sustainability, innovation, and education at Anbima, sees the new model in line with international best practices and designed to evaluate more complete professionals, especially regarding behavioral skills. However, unlike what some preparatory course founders heard by the reporting understand, Billi does not believe the old model certified professionals with technical deficiencies. The requirement for annual updates to maintain certification addresses, in his view, this possible technical deficit. As for the dynamics of adjusting exams to improve them, he says the model allows identifying questions that prove easier or more difficult, suppressing or altering them if necessary, immediately. A possibility, he says, that was also in effect in the previous model.
