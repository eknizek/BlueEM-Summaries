# Anbima Updates Certification Models for Financial Market Professionals

Anbima, an association of financial and capital market entities representing banks, asset managers, brokerages, distributors, and administrators, is implementing a profound change in its certification policy for workers seeking to enter and remain in the sector. This certification is mandatory for those who deal with investors or are investment specialists.

The three main certifications required since 2002—CPA-10, CPA-20, and CEA—were replaced in 2026 by three new ones: CPA, C-Pro R, and C-Pro I. The new exams began on February 19, after being postponed due to the high volume of tests using the old model conducted in December.

Through March 31, according to Anbima data, 4,746 exams were administered, with 3,795 in the CPA certification alone. The approval rate, considering the three new certificates, reached 58%—above the 2025 average of 50% with the old certifications.

The new exams last two and a half hours, one hour less than previously required for the CEA exam. The change was designed to address the complexity of financial products available in today's market, a scenario quite different from what existed at the beginning of the century, and the popularization of personal investment platforms and apps.

The new model also aims to evaluate candidates' behavioral skills, their ability to understand clients' main demands, and their capacity to relate to clients appropriately. The exams, previously composed exclusively of multiple-choice questions, now also require essay responses and include case studies and decision tree structures, creating directed pathways based on candidates' prior selections. They no longer require accumulated knowledge, as was the case with the CEA certification exam, instead focusing on what is truly necessary for the desired certification.

According to the entity, as of December 31, 2025, 434,000 people held the old certificates, which could be revalidated. Of the total certifications, 85% refer to the most basic CPA-10 and CPA-20, granted in nearly equal proportions. There is no gender difference among certified professionals, and São Paulo, with approximately 15% of those certified, and Rio, with 5.64%, are the cities that stand out most.

The change has naturally disrupted the market for preparatory courses for this segment, through which more than 80% of candidates pass—only 17% prepare autonomously for the exams, according to Anbima, which recently launched a free online study platform called Anbima Edu. The approval rate, however, is similar: 48% for those studying independently, 51% for those using preparatory courses.

For Kleber Stumpf, CEO of TopInvest, one of these preparatory course providers, the changes are welcome. "They are quite aligned with the market and explore basic aspects of the activity, client relationships, and understanding of investments," he says. For Stumpf, there is also more fairness in the new model: "If before candidates memorized what would appear on the exam, which could have later reflections on professional performance, now it should reward those who truly study, who truly prepare."

Tiago Feitosa, from T2 Educação, told Folha that he completely redesigned his course materials due to the new exam model. He praises Anbima for having listened to preparatory course providers throughout the process and for disclosing the methodology for creating the new questions. For him, the previous assessments were far from the "daily reality" of professionals, something that tends to change now. "Since students could, and still can, retake the exam if they failed, they would go, take a practice test, and even faced with new questions, they would end up passing."

Although the entrepreneur considers the advent of Anbima's educational platform positive, he justifies his "business" by offering candidates "curation and organization" of the knowledge that will be evaluated on the exams.

Rafael Toro, from the "finance academy" bearing his name, also sees the change as positive, with the condition of improving professional qualifications. "It was very common for candidates to be approved for having memorized the questions, but then, during the job interview, they couldn't get hired. Sometimes they weren't able to understand economic movements, the relationship, for example, between interest rates, exchange rates, and inflation."

Marcelo Billi, Anbima's superintendent of sustainability, innovation, and education, sees the new model as aligned with international best practices and aimed at evaluating more complete professionals, especially regarding behavioral skills. But, unlike what some preparatory course founders interviewed by the reporter understand, Billi does not believe the old model certified professionals with technical deficiencies. The requirement for annual updates to maintain the certificate, in his view, compensates for any possible technical deficit.

Regarding the dynamics of adjusting exams to improve them, he says the model allows identifying questions that prove easier or more difficult, removing or changing them, if necessary, immediately. A possibility, he says, that was also in effect in the model being phased out.
