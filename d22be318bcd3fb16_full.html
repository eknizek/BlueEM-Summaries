# Tariff War with Ecuador Looms Over 2,800 Colombian Companies

The escalating tariff war between Colombia and Ecuador is beginning to reflect its effects on the real economy, with direct impact on approximately 2,800 Colombian companies that export to that country and which, for now, would be left in limbo due to the complications they must face to stay afloat.

In a conversation with Portafolio, Katherin Díaz, founding partner of V&V Asociados, discussed the issue and warned that the measures could completely sacrifice Colombian commerce going to Ecuador, with immediate consequences for productive sectors and employment.

## Tariff War with Ecuador Hits Trade and Exports

The first thing to keep in mind is that the commercial conflict has a greater dimension than it appears, since Ecuador is Colombia's third export destination in Latin America and the exchange between both countries rounds USD $3,000 million annually. In this context, the imposition of 100% tariffs represents a direct shock to that flow.

According to Díaz, this type of measure implies an immediate contraction of trade, especially affecting companies that depend on that market. "When we talk about tariffs of one hundred percent, we are completely sacrificing Colombian commerce going to Ecuador," Díaz explained.

The impact is not marginal and this is a consolidated market for multiple sectors, which means that closing this commercial door not only reduces exports but also destabilizes commercial relationships built over years between companies from both countries.

## Industrial Sectors and SMEs Face Greater Risk

The blow concentrates on key sectors of the Colombian economy. The expert noted that industries such as petrochemical, pharmaceutical, automotive, and agribusiness will be the first to feel the contraction. These sectors have high exposure to the Ecuadorian market, leaving them in a vulnerable position against the tariff escalation.

Furthermore, the impact has a relevant business component in which of the approximately 2,800 affected companies, a significant part corresponds to small and medium-sized enterprises that do not have sufficient financial capacity to absorb an abrupt drop in income.

"Many of these are SMEs, and they will not have the financial capacity to survive this major blow," Díaz warned, noting that this situation increases the risk of business closures or operational adjustments that could affect business continuity.

## Employment and Border Regions Under Economic Pressure

Beyond the business impact, the tariff war also has social implications. Employment emerges as one of the main risk fronts, especially in regions with high dependence on binational trade.

Departments such as Nariño and Putumayo could face greater pressure, given that their economic dynamics are closely linked to trade with Ecuador. Trade contraction in these areas could translate into job losses and reduced income for households.

In parallel, the conflict poses challenges from a legal and commercial perspective. Colombia and Ecuador are part of the Andean Community of Nations, a scheme that promotes free trade among its members. In this context, tariff measures can be interpreted as a breach of that principle.

The expert explained that this scenario could lead to legal disputes, to the extent that the actions adopted clash with the commitments of the regional bloc. However, she also warned that Ecuador could justify its position with arguments related to border security.

## How to Stay Afloat in the Tariff War?

Facing this panorama, companies are forced to react. One of the main recommendations is to advance in market diversification, taking advantage of current commercial treaties with other countries in the region, such as Peru, Mexico, and several Central American economies.

"What would be immediately activated is commercial expansion and seeking to enter new markets beyond Ecuador," Díaz said, emphasizing that Colombia is not fully taking advantage of the benefits of its commercial agreements.

Additionally, companies must review their contractual structures with Ecuadorian clients and suppliers. The inclusion or activation of clauses such as force majeure or hardship can allow renegotiating conditions or suspending obligations without incurring non-compliance while stabilizing the situation.

For companies with direct presence in Ecuador, the challenge is even greater, and in these cases, the recommendation is to review the tax and legal structure under existing agreements, in order to avoid additional impacts derived from double taxation.

The tariff war between Colombia and Ecuador goes beyond an exclusively political conflict to become an economic problem with concrete effects on companies, employment, and regions.
