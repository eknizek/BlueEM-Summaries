# Anatomy of Kast's reconstruction project: corporate tax rates and tax system reintegration in focus

This week, the government of President José Antonio Kast revealed the first details of the renamed bill for National Reconstruction and Economic and Social Development. Through a national broadcast by the Head of State on Wednesday night and, the following day, at a press conference by his Finance Minister, Jorge Quiroz, just over 20 of the 43 measures that will make up the miscellaneous initiative to be submitted to parliament next week have been revealed so far. According to President Kast's proposal, the measures will be organized around five axes: tax competitiveness, strengthening formal employment, regulatory facilitation, legal certainty, and public spending containment. The first contains probably two of the most relevant actions for the current administration and are related to the reduction of corporate tax and the reintegration of the tax system.

## Corporate income tax at OECD level

In his address to the country, the president stated that Chile currently has the highest corporate income tax in its history "and that gap costs the country investment, jobs, and future" and proposed that the project will seek to gradually reduce it from 27% to 23%, placing it at "the most competitive level in the last two decades." According to one of the memos circulating among authorities and parliamentarians this week, in practice it is proposed to lower the rate by 1.5 percentage points for income accrued or received in the 2027 and 2028 commercial years, and by 1 percentage point for 2029, for companies subject to the regime of Article 14 of the Income Tax Law (LIR). In the case of SMEs, it is proposed to maintain the current rate of 12.5% for 2026 and 2027, and 15% in 2028. From there, the current law regulating taxation for this business segment after the pandemic provides for an increase in the tax to 25% as of 2029. However, there are no signals from the Finance Ministry that this measure will be modified, which has generated concern among industry groups.

A report published a couple of weeks ago by the think tank Libertad y Desarrollo, entitled "The Urgency of Reforming Corporate Tax," defends the need to implement profound and urgent changes to promote long-term growth. According to the document, the Corporate Income Tax (IdPC) must have four attributes to not affect growth: sufficiency, simplicity, efficiency, and equity. In Chile's case, these are not being met.

Citing a study in which economist Claudio Agostini participated, among others, entitled "The Elasticity of Corporate Taxable Income in Different Countries," published in March of this year by the National Bureau of Economic Research, it is revealed that Chilean companies show high sensitivity to changes in taxes, so an increase leads to a reduction in the declared taxable base and incentivizes them, for example, to continue operating under the SME regime.

"The large gap between the tax rate paid by SMEs (currently 12.5%) and the general regime (27%), incentivizes hiding sales that exceed the limit and discourages growth," warns the Libertad y Desarrollo document. Another indication of the need to modify the Corporate Income Tax, according to Libertad y Desarrollo, is provided by the Marfán Commission, an instance convened in 2023 by the Finance Ministry, presided over by the former minister and which included experts such as Andrea Repetto, Andrea Tokman, Rodrigo Vergara, José Pablo Arellano, Ignacio Briones, and José De Gregorio. According to this body, the increase in IdPC from 2000 to date would be costing the country almost 8 percentage points of lower GDP, and that a reduction of 1 point in the rate would raise the GDP level by 0.65%, distributed over 10 years. All this, while acknowledging that the reduction in the rate would have an effect on the decline in tax collection that would not be fully compensated in the following years.

According to various economists in the market, the reduction of 4 percentage points would imply, in steady state, lower tax collection ranging between US$1.2 billion and US$2 billion. A third precedent provided by Libertad y Desarrollo is Chile's position in the ranking of the International Tax Competitiveness Index, published annually by Tax Foundation, which evaluates the tax systems of 38 OECD countries. Chile has experienced a sustained decline, dropping from 19th place in 2014 to 32nd in 2025.

"This deterioration in position is explained mainly by the increase in the rate, which translates to the tax burden—understood as total tax collection as a percentage of GDP—faced by Chilean companies being about 50% higher than what they face in the OECD," states the think tank directed by Bettina Horst.

## The debate over tax reintegration

The other central element of Kast's tax proposal is the gradual reintegration of the tax system—until reaching full reintegration in the 2031 tax year, according to the government memo—which would allow for a return to the model that existed before 2014, prior to former President Michelle Bachelet's tax reform. In this way, the president stated in his address, "the tax paid by the company becomes fully deductible from the tax of its owners, eliminating double taxation, which encourages the reinvestment of profits and job creation."

A document prepared by the Finance Ministry during the previous administration, with the support of academics and researchers from the University of Santiago, entitled "Report on Tax Statistics" indicated that among OECD members, only seven countries have integrated or semi-integrated systems. Among these are Australia and New Zealand (with 100% credit) and Chile (with 65% credit). The remaining 30 countries have dual systems and their variants. According to the document, the type of tax system has direct consequences on the administrative costs that companies face in meeting their tax obligations. A case in point: data from the World Bank Doing Business Report (2020) shows that companies in countries with integrated or semi-integrated systems dedicate, on average, 21% more hours per year to tax compliance than companies in countries with dual systems.

Furthermore, the document warns that there is a relationship between the type of tax system and the level of tax rates. Countries using an integrated or semi-integrated system maintain, on average, higher corporate tax rates (28.93%) than countries with dual systems (22.64%). "This relationship is not coincidental. In countries with integrated or semi-integrated regimes, the existence of credits that reduce the effective taxation of individuals means that effective collection comes primarily from companies."
