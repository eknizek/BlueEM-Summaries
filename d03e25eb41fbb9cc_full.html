Analysis of the heart of Kast's reconstruction project: corporate tax rate and reintegration of tax system in focus

18 April 2026 · 12:44 PM

# Analysis of the Heart of Kast's Reconstruction Project: Corporate Tax Rate and Reintegration of Tax System in Focus

This week, the government of President José Antonio Kast unveiled the first details of the renamed bill for National Reconstruction and Economic and Social Development. Through a national broadcast by the Head of State on Wednesday night and, the following day, through a press conference by Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz, more than 20 of the 43 measures that will comprise the miscellaneous initiative to be submitted to parliament next week have been revealed so far.

According to President Kast's proposal, the measures will be organized around five axes: tax competitiveness, strengthening formal employment, regulatory facilitation, legal certainty, and public spending containment. The first contains probably two of the most relevant actions for the current administration and are related to the reduction of the corporate tax rate and the reintegration of the tax system.

## First Category Tax at OECD Level

In his address to the country, the president noted that Chile currently has the highest first category tax in its history "and this gap costs the country investment, employment, and future" and proposed that the project will seek to gradually reduce it from 27% to 23%, placing it at "the most competitive level of the last two decades."

According to one of the minutes circulating among authorities and parliamentarians this week, in practice it is proposed to reduce the rate by 1.5 percentage points for income accrued or received in the 2027 and 2028 commercial years, and by 1 percentage point for 2029, for companies subject to the regime of Article 14 of the Income Tax Law (LIR).

In the case of SMEs, it is proposed to maintain the current rate of 12.5% for 2026 and 2027, and 15% in 2028. From there, the current law that regulates taxation for this business segment following the pandemic contemplates an increase in tax to 25% from 2029 onwards. However, there are no signals from the Finance Ministry that this measure will be modified, which has generated concern among business associations.

A report published a couple of weeks ago by the think tank Libertad y Desarrollo, entitled "The Urgency of Reforming the Corporate Tax," defends the need to implement deep and urgent changes to promote long-term growth. According to the document, the First Category Tax (IdPC) must have four attributes to not affect growth: sufficiency, simplicity, efficiency, and equity. In Chile's case, these are not being met.

Citing a study in which economist Claudio Agostini participated, titled "The Elasticity of Taxable Income of Companies in Different Countries," published in March of this year by the National Bureau of Economic Research, it reveals that Chilean companies show high sensitivity to changes in taxes, so an increase leads to a reduction in the declared taxable base and incentivizes them, for example, to continue paying taxes under the SME regime.

"The large gap between the tax rate paid by SMEs (currently at 12.5%) and the general regime (27%) incentivizes hiding sales that exceed the limit and discourages growth," warns the LyD document.

Another indication of the need to modify the First Category Tax, according to LyD, comes from the Marfán Commission, an instance convened in 2023 by the Finance Ministry, chaired by the former minister and which included experts such as Andrea Repetto, Andrea Tokman, Rodrigo Vergara, José Pablo Arellano, Ignacio Briones, and José De Gregorio. According to this instance, the increase registered by the IdPC from 2000 to date would be costing the country almost 8 percentage points of lower GDP, and a reduction of 1 point in the rate would raise the level of GDP by 0.65%, distributed over 10 years.

All this, acknowledging that the reduction in the rate would have an effect on the decline in tax collection that would not be fully compensated in the following years. According to various economists in the market, the reduction of 4 percentage points would imply, in steady state, a lower tax collection ranging between US$1.2 billion and US$2 billion.

A third background provided by LyD is Chile's position in the International Tax Competitiveness Index ranking, published annually by Tax Foundation, which evaluates the tax systems of 38 OECD countries, in which our country has had sustained decline, dropping from position 19 in 2014 to 32 in 2025.

"This deterioration in position is explained mainly by the increase in the rate, which translates into the tax burden – understood as total tax collection as a percentage of GDP – faced by Chilean companies being about 50% higher than what they face in the OECD," says the think tank led by Bettina Horst.

## The Debate Over Tax Reintegration

The other central element of Kast's tax proposal is the gradual reintegration of the tax system – until reaching full reintegration in the 2031 tax year, according to the government's minutes – which would allow a return to the model that existed before 2014, prior to former President Michelle Bachelet's tax reform.

In this way, the president stated in his address, "the tax paid by the company becomes fully deductible from the tax of its owners, eliminating double taxation, which encourages the reinvestment of profits and job creation."

A document produced by the Finance Ministry during the previous administration, with support from academics and researchers from the University of Santiago, titled "Report on Tax Statistics," indicated that among OECD members, only seven countries have integrated or semi-integrated tax systems. Among these are Australia and New Zealand (with 100% credit) and Chile (with 65% credit). The remaining 30 countries have dual systems and their variants.

According to the document, the type of tax system has direct consequences on the administrative costs faced by companies to comply with their tax obligations. A case in point: data from the World Bank Doing Business Report (2020) shows that companies from countries with integrated or semi-integrated systems dedicate, on average, 21% more hours per year to tax compliance compared to companies in countries with dual systems.

Likewise, the document warns that there is a relationship between the type of tax system and the level of tax rates. Countries that use an integrated or semi-integrated system maintain, on average, higher corporate tax rates (28.93%) than countries with dual systems (22.64%).

"This relationship is not casual. In countries with integrated or semi-integrated regimes, the existence of credits that reduce the effective taxation of individuals means that effective collection comes mostly from companies."
