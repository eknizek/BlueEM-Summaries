# Farm Loan Program Slows with High Rates, But Credit Continues Flowing, Says Banco do Brasil VP

RIBEIRÃO PRETO (SP) – The vice-president of Agribusiness and Family Agriculture at Banco do Brasil, Gilson Bittencourt, stated that the 9% drop in disbursements from the 2025/26 Farm Loan Program (Plano Safra) through March, compared to the previous harvest, mainly reflects a contraction in financing for investment by medium and large producers, not a general paralysis of rural credit. In an exclusive interview with Estadão/Broadcast Agro, following the opening ceremony of the 31st Agrishow 2026 in Ribeirão Preto (SP), he said the scenario is linked to the process of financial reorganization in the agricultural sector and the rising cost of credit due to the elevated Selic rate. Currently, the basic interest rate of the economy is 14.75% per year.

According to a survey by Estadão/Broadcast Agro, disbursements from the 2025/26 Farm Loan Program totaled R$ 259.879 billion between July 2025 and March of this year, 9% below the R$ 285.689 billion released in the same period of the previous harvest. When Rural Product Certificates (CPRs) financed by banks are also considered, however, the volume released for business agriculture reaches R$ 403.981 billion, an increase of 10% compared to the previous cycle.

"When we look at the Farm Loan Program, we must first look at rural credit specifically, plus CPRs financed by banks. You had a more significant reduction in rural credit, but there was an increase in financing via CPR," Bittencourt stated.

According to him, the main reduction is concentrated in investment lines, especially for medium and large producers, as well as free-rate operations. "What has been occurring is a financial reorganization by producers who are postponing certain investments, precisely to try to reorganize their cash flow," he said.

According to the executive, with the elevated Selic rate, many projects cease to be economically viable, while banks are also adopting stricter criteria for granting credit, with greater collateral requirements and more thorough analysis of repayment capacity. This caution can also explain the lower performance of credit releases in the 2025/26 harvest.

"But the money is flowing, credit is being released," he assured.

Bittencourt also highlighted that debt renegotiation measures help unlock new financing approvals. He cited Provisional Measure 1.314, issued in September 2025 to allow debt renegotiation for producers affected by climate events, such as drought, floods, and crop failure. The measure authorized the use of up to R$ 12 billion in financial surpluses and free resources from banks for settlement or amortization of rural debts. According to him, Banco do Brasil alone renegotiated R$ 36.5 billion through the measure, with R$ 33.2 billion in free-rate operations and R$ 3.2 billion in controlled rates, with postponement of operations for up to nine years.

"Any process that helps improve payment flow contributes to new approvals," he stated.

Regarding the project under discussion in Congress for a new round of rural debt renegotiation, which is being discussed in the Economic Affairs Commission (CAE) of the Senate, Bittencourt assessed that the impact on Agrishow business should be small. He noted that producers already in debt and seeking financial restructuring are not usually the main buying audience at Agrishow 2026.

"Those seeking new investments are producers who have their cash flow in order," he said.

He was careful to emphasize that most Brazilian producers remain current on payments and have the capacity to invest, which sustains expectations for good performance of the fair. For this year, Banco do Brasil projects up to R$ 3 billion in business proposals at Agrishow, a value slightly lower than recorded in 2025, reflecting precisely the lower demand for financing from medium and large producers.
