# Oncoclínicas Advances in Restructuring as Porto and Fleury Exit, Eyes Capital Raise

Oncoclínicas has initiated a turning point in its crisis by ending M&A negotiations with Porto and Fleury and filing a request for precautionary measures in court, paving the way for an extrajudicial restructuring. The decision reflects the impasse in negotiations, marked by the absence of a firm proposal and disagreements over the timing of restructuring — the potential buyers conditioned progress on immediate entry into extrajudicial recovery, which the company considered premature. Despite this, sources assess that extrajudicial recovery is inevitable, given the fragmented debt, especially in CRIs, which makes it difficult to obtain a quorum for direct renegotiation. With Porto and Fleury's exit, Mak Capital emerges as the only proposal on the table, with potential injection of up to R$ 1 billion, still without detailed terms. The crisis is aggravated by a structural problem: dependence on health insurance plans, which pay with delays and frequently deny claims in full, blocking up to 40% of billing.

The last 24 hours marked the beginning of a turnaround in the Oncoclínicas crisis. The exclusivity contract for an M&A with Porto and Fleury was terminated and the extrajudicial restructuring process was submitted to the judiciary. NeoFeed found that the company filed, on the morning of Tuesday, April 14, with a request for precautionary measures in the third court of São Paulo. The analysis is with substitute judge Fernanda Perez Jacomini. The move comes on the heels of BR Partners' arrival as sole financial advisor, taking over the work previously handled by Rothschild.

People close to Oncoclínicas told NeoFeed that the M&A impasse involved everything from the level of financial commitment to the degree of access to the company's information, a sensitive point in an increasingly competitive and vertically integrated sector. The perception is that there was misalignment between what Oncoclínicas needed — significant new capital — and what was being offered, in terms considered insufficient or conditional.

Without a firm proposal, the company chose not to extend negotiations that could, in addition to not resolving the liquidity problem, further expose its operations. "It's complicated to have exclusivity and ask for infinite evaluation time without putting money on the table. And without showing a gesture of good faith to continue looking at the numbers as a potential competitor," says a source close to the negotiations.

Another point of disagreement in negotiations was Porto and Fleury's requirement that the company directly enter into an extrajudicial restructuring process as a condition to advance the proposal. Internal assessment, however, was that this move would be premature, given the difficulty in coordination with creditors and the absence of a structured agreement.

Despite the disagreement over timing, people close to the company affirm that the request for extrajudicial restructuring is seen as inevitable, given the fragmented debt — especially in instruments like CRIs — and the difficulty in obtaining a quorum for renegotiation outside a formal process.

In a report signed by analysts Samuel Alves and Maria Resende, BTG Pactual classified the outcome as "expected," given the depth of due diligence and Oncoclínicas' financial challenges. The bank cited high debt levels and potential off-balance-sheet liabilities as insurmountable barriers for players with robust cash, such as Fleury and Porto, to inject capital on acceptable risk-return conditions. For BTG, the move favors incumbents like Rede D'Or, which has been capturing repressed demand — including from more than 6,000 patients who had cancer treatments delayed because of the Oncoclínicas crisis.

**New Opportunity**

With Porto and Fleury's exit from Oncoclínicas, attention turned to Mak Capital, which since the beginning of this year has taken the position of activist investor to block the M&A. The hedge fund manager signaled a possible injection of up to R$ 1 billion, in a structure similar to what was previously discussed with Fleury and Porto. The proposal, according to NeoFeed's investigation, is the only one on the table at the moment.

As NeoFeed revealed, Mak Capital sent two letters to Oncoclínicas' management and board of directors detailing the changes in corporate governance necessary to realize the capital injection offer. "Chances increased," said a source close to the hedge fund, after the exclusivity agreement with Porto and Fleury ended.

Before the end of the M&A analysis, Mak Capital's proposal (conditioned on the removal of the current board with the election of two members nominated by it) was classified by Oncoclínicas in an official communication to the CVM as "unfeasible," especially because of the required guarantees — the assembly scheduled for April 30.

On the afternoon of Tuesday, April 14, Mak Capital and BR Partners will meet so the new financial advisor can understand the conditions and begin its analysis. Among proposals and intentions in the air, the idea is to find who has "skin in the game." However, according to NeoFeed's investigation, the financial advisor's idea is to "sit down and draw up various options, from creditors who can self-finance to new investors."

In its communication to the market, Oncoclínicas said it "will continue evaluating proposals for potential financial operations, as well as any corporate operations that may address its economic-financial situation, including those that have emerged in recent weeks and could not be explored due to the exclusivity then in effect."

**From Sale to Survival**

Latin America's largest oncology treatment group is seeing its capital structure deteriorate rapidly. In 2025, it suffered a loss of R$ 3.67 billion, with gross debt of R$ 4.8 billion and had its operational continuity questioned by auditor Deloitte. In this complex situation, there is a silent villain that has become the company's Achilles heel: health insurance plans.

Oncoclínicas depends almost exclusively on health plan operators for its revenue. Operators pay for procedures in timeframes reaching around 270 days. If that's already a problem, chaos sets in with so-called "claim denials." In practice, if an operator contests a single procedure code from a R$ 500,000 invoice, for example, it simply refuses to pay the entire invoice and not just the contested item.

"Health plans don't pay correct codes, they deny the entire bill. The whole discussion starts over and they profit from my working capital," says a company source.

The estimate is that, in some cases, the percentage of billing blocked by these disputes reaches 30% or 40%. "There is no margin to sustain this," the source adds.

Making matters worse, contracts with health plans generally prohibit the assignment of these receivables as collateral with banks. In other words, Oncoclínicas cannot even advance the money it is due to receive to buy medications.

Internally, the assessment is that the problem is structural and has no short-term solution without regulatory intervention.
