# Soles or Dollars? The Savings Decision Facing Thousands of Peruvians

In Peru, many face a recurring question: is it better to save in soles or dollars? The choice can affect the final cost of important goals, especially when they are linked to goods or services priced in foreign currency.

"Rather than choosing a currency based on perception or trends, the recommendation is to analyze what that money will be used for and how to make it more profitable," said Iván Mostacero, Senior Manager of Passive Products Hub at Banco Pichincha del Perú.

Since each goal has different characteristics, the financial conditions of each currency also play a role. While instruments in soles typically offer higher nominal returns, the dollar maintains its function as the reference currency in international transactions and in certain goods markets. Thus, the decision is not limited to comparing interest rates, but to evaluating how much exposure one wants to have to possible exchange rate variations based on the stated objectives.

## Saving in Soles: Stability and Higher Nominal Returns

The sol is the currency in which most people earn their income and conduct their daily expenses. In that sense, Mostacero explained that saving in soles can be convenient when goals are short-term, there are no dollar obligations, higher interest rates in local currency are sought, and in certain periods, products in soles can offer superior nominal returns, which favors immediate goals or emergency funds.

## Saving in Dollars: Coverage and Diversification

The dollar, for its part, is typically used as an international reference currency. Saving in dollars can be useful when:

- There are future expenses linked to that currency (travel, housing, cars, education, international purchases)

- There is a desire to diversify part of savings

- Coverage is sought against possible exchange rate variations

Maintaining a portion of savings in dollars does not mean abandoning the sol, but rather distributing risks across currencies.

## Is It Worth Choosing a Single Currency?

Experts agree that in many cases, the best strategy is not to choose exclusively one currency, but to combine both according to personal objectives.

"The decision should be based on financial planning. If a person has goals in both soles and dollars, it can be reasonable to divide savings based on those needs," Mostacero said.

"The key is not in predicting market behavior, but in structuring savings coherently with personal goals. Diversification can help reduce exposure to sudden changes," he concluded.
