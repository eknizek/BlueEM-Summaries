# Dollar Falls to 890 Pesos as Copper Surges Following Strait of Hormuz Tensions

The dollar fell this Wednesday in Chile after Donald Trump announced an indefinite extension of the truce with Iran, while the latter continued to obstruct passage through the Strait of Hormuz—a mix of factors that supported copper prices.

The currency dropped $5.3 to $889.7 at the close of the local market. The Chilean peso was the most strengthened currency among the few emerging currencies that appreciated in the session, which in any case comes after suffering the biggest blow in Tuesday's trading, according to Bloomberg data.

Comex copper rebounded 2.1% to trade at $6.2 per pound, helping the peso at a time when Brent oil exceeded $100 per barrel and the dollar index—an indicator of the global dollar—recovered 0.2%.

**Indefinite Timeline**

Supply restrictions appear to have supported the red metal. "While the evolution of copper prices since the start of the conflict with Iran has largely reflected concerns about economic growth, with an initial decline followed by a recovery after the ceasefire, we observe risks to copper supply stemming from a possible shortage of sulfuric acid if the recovery of flows through the Strait of Hormuz is delayed further," said Goldman Sachs analysts cited by Bloomberg.

Trump announced Tuesday, after markets closed in Chile and the United States, that the current truce with Iran will not expire this Wednesday, as he had previously indicated, but will remain in effect until peace negotiations conclude. The president stated on his Truth Social network that Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif asked him to suspend the planned attack against Iran "until its leaders and representatives can present a unified proposal." The Persian regime is "seriously fractured," according to Trump.

However, subsequent news was discouraging for those hoping for a swift resolution to the conflict. Iran claimed to have detained two commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, which it took to its territorial waters for "endangering maritime security." Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said in a conversation with his Italian counterpart that the situation at Hormuz is "a direct result of the violation of laws" by Washington.

While continuing to impose a naval blockade on Iranian ports, the United States seeks to increase its presence in the Middle East. On its way to the region is the USS George HW Bush aircraft carrier, whose arrival would mark the largest naval deployment since the 2003 Iraq War, according to the Financial Times.
