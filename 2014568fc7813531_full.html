# Chilean Peso Closes Lower at 890 per Dollar as Copper Strengthens Following Ceasefire Extension and Strait of Hormuz Developments

The dollar fell this Wednesday in Chile after Donald Trump announced an indefinite extension of the ceasefire with Iran, while the latter continued to obstruct passage through the Strait of Hormuz—a mix of factors that provided support for copper prices.

The currency dropped $5.3 to $889.7 at the close of the local market. The Chilean peso was the most strengthened currency among the few emerging market currencies that appreciated in the session, which, in any case, comes after suffering the heaviest blow in Tuesday's trading, according to Bloomberg data.

Comex copper rebounded 2.1% to trade at $6.2 per pound, supporting the peso at a time when Brent oil exceeded $100 per barrel and the dollar index—a global dollar indicator—recovered 0.2%.

**Indefinite Term**

Supply restrictions appear to have supported the red metal. "While copper price movements since the start of the Iran conflict have largely reflected concerns about economic growth, with an initial decline followed by a recovery after the ceasefire, we see risks to copper supplies stemming from a possible sulfuric acid shortage if recovery of flows through the Strait of Hormuz is delayed further," Goldman Sachs analysts cited by Bloomberg said.

Trump announced Tuesday, after market close in Chile and the United States, that the current ceasefire with Iran will not expire this Wednesday, as previously stated, but will remain in effect until peace negotiations conclude. The president stated on his Truth Social network that Shehbaz Sharif, Pakistan's prime minister, asked him to suspend the planned attack against Iran "until its leaders and representatives can present a unified proposal." The Persian regime is "seriously fractured," according to Trump.

However, subsequent news was discouraging for those hoping for a quick resolution to the conflict. Iran claimed the seizure of two commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, which it took to its territorial waters for "endangering maritime security." Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said in a conversation with his Italian counterpart that the situation at Hormuz is "a direct result of the breaking of laws" by Washington.

While continuing to impose a naval blockade on Iranian ports, the U.S. is seeking to increase its presence in the Middle East. Heading to the region is the USS George HW Bush aircraft carrier, whose arrival would mark the largest naval deployment since the Iraq War in 2003, according to the Financial Times.
