Real estate funds adopt FIDC and debenture structures to compete with fixed income

# Real Estate Funds Adopt FIDC and Debenture Structures to Compete with Fixed Income

Real estate fund managers (FIIs) are adopting multi-class share structures to attract investors in a high-interest and volatile environment. In 2025, 37% of significant operations registered with the CVM involved funds with multiple classes, and this trend is expected to grow in 2026. This strategy, inspired by FIDCs and debentures, aims to offer predictability and stability—characteristics desired by investors. These FIIs offer senior shares with lower returns and greater security, and subordinated shares with higher risk and returns. The popularity of these funds reflects the search for fixed income alternatives, especially in a volatile market, according to managers interviewed by NeoFeed. Despite the growth, managers warn of associated risks, such as asset quality and subordination, emphasizing that senior shares do not guarantee total safety.

If they cannot beat fixed income in a high-interest and volatile environment, real estate fund managers have decided to join forces with this asset class. Amid lack of liquidity and a closed market for traditional issuances, managers have circumvented the liquidity shortage by launching funds with subclasses, adopting a structure established in FIDCs and debentures to offer what investors, especially retail investors, want most right now: predictability and stability.

These structures were practically nonexistent in 2024, but multi-class share funds grew strongly last year. A survey conducted by Daycoval Asset for NeoFeed shows that of the 45 most significant operations registered with the Securities Commission (CVM) in terms of volume in 2025, 37% of the amount raised was captured by vehicles with multiple classes. Considering feeders of multi-class funds, this total rises to 47%. The year closed with a total of 428 operations. Managers interviewed by NeoFeed expect the number of multi-class FII offerings to gain even more space in 2026.

Even with market improvement, with the real estate funds index (IFIX) accumulating a gain of 17.93% in the 12 months through April 17, the assessment is that investors are still very reluctant to leave fixed income. "We saw a recalibration of risk perception, spreads compressed somewhat, prices rose, but the scenario remains cautious," says Martim Fass, FII manager at Daycoval Asset. "This is reflected in new issuances, with very few traditional brick-and-mortar operations of pure equity, and many with senior shares."

Like FIDCs, CRIs, and CRAs, multi-class FIIs offer senior and subordinated shares, with the former having maximum priority in receipts but lower returns, while the latter bears a certain level of asset risk, absorbing defaults, in exchange for higher compensation.

The creation of multi-class FIIs was enabled by CVM Resolution 175, published in December 2022, which allowed a single fund to issue different classes of shares. The provisions only came into effect in April 2024, which explains why offerings gained traction in 2025.

FIIs with senior and subordinated shares have a set term, unlike a traditional fund, which has no end date. Generally, funds have a duration of five to seven years. At the end of the period, assets are sold so the proceeds can be used to compensate investors. In the case of senior shares, investors receive a predetermined return based on rents and the liquidity event at the fund's end, mimicking fixed income, while the subordinated shares receive the surplus.

According to Rodrigo Abbud, partner and head of real estate at Patria Investimentos in Brazil, this type of FII created in partnership with banks and distributors is capable of giving investors a sense of security at a time of great market volatility. "The investor is seeking predictability and stability in the distribution of returns at this moment. They are willing to give up the upside to avoid the downside at a time like this," says Abbud. "They say 'pay me inflation plus 8% and 9.5%, the range these operations are coming in at, that's great'. It's almost fixed income."

The fact that these funds are not listed means the share price does not vary much, an important aspect in psychological terms. "This structure became popular because you can mark it linearly on the client's portfolio. It became popular among those who don't like the 'rock and roll' of single shares, which keep going up and down," says Pedro Ferronato, partner and co-head of credit at Paladin in Brazil.

The combination of the fixed income aspect of the senior share and the Income Tax (IR) exemption on FII dividends is the sector's bet to attract investors back to the market.

Operations on the street

All these characteristics have helped unlock the market, especially for brick-and-mortar funds. JiveMauá was one of the first firms to carry out this type of FII offering. At the end of 2024, it launched MCLO11, a logistics fund, which raised R$ 1.2 billion. The senior share pays IPCA plus 9%. For Brunno Bagnariolli, partner and investment director of JiveMauá's real estate strategy, the decision to structure this vehicle occurred precisely because of the difficulty in making traditional fundraisings. According to him, the firm was only able to conduct traditional fund offerings for credit, not brick-and-mortar during the period. "Fundraising for brick-and-mortar funds, considering the current interest rate level, is happening either in the senior and subordinated model or in the share exchange model," says Bagnariolli.

Bagnariolli reports that the experience was quite positive, with JiveMauá raising what it expected in 13 days. The positive investor reception led the manager to make three other fundraisings with the same structure, raising approximately R$ 2.1 billion, all brick-and-mortar funds. The real estate vertical currently has a total of R$ 10.5 billion in assets under management and is considering eventually new funds of this type. "It's a very good way to deploy resources," says Bagnariolli.

Multi-class funds are also helping to enable other strategies in the real estate market. Patria, which has R$ 38 billion in assets under management in real estate, launched a fund called Patria Oportunidades to provide financing to companies, which has already raised R$ 1 billion for the senior shares. Amid a restructuring process, data on the fund's return were not disclosed.

Tivio Capital structured a multi-class fund to invest in other FIIs. The manager—recently consolidated by Bradesco and has R$ 4.03 billion in real estate mandates, with R$ 3.815 billion in FIIs—raised R$ 400 million from bank clients for an FII that buys shares of brick-and-mortar and credit funds. The senior share pays IPCA plus 9%. According to Adriano Mantesso, head of real estate at Tivio, the security that a share FII provides to investors helped enable an "opportunistic play" that the manager was seeing. "We saw a window of opportunity, with shares being traded at interesting discounts. And with interest rates falling again, these shares tend to appreciate, providing enough return to have a subordinated class."
