# Italian confectionery giant targets Brazilian market with homemade sweets expansion

When executives from Irca Group arrive in Brazil to get to know the local market, VP Plínio Freitas has a ritual: he takes them to a Chocolândia store in São Paulo, one of the major confectionery ingredient retailers spread across the country, and lets the scene speak for itself. "We normally have to bring people from outside to show them, because just explaining it they can't understand," Freitas said, Latin America vice president of the Italian confectionery giant, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

What these visitors find is a market that, despite being one of the largest in the world, has a characteristic much more diffuse and linked to domestic sweet production than what is seen in other countries. It is in this home baking that the company is betting as it begins its venture in Brazil.

The Italian multinational, founded in 1919, says it has annual revenue of approximately €1.5 billion and began in 2025 to set up its structured operation in Brazil, which was formalized earlier this year.

The initial investment is approximately €1 million applied to setting up a commercial team, logistics structure, and product adaptation for the local market. The preparation took almost a year — time necessary for company opening, import organization, and portfolio selection.

The entry point chosen by Irca says much about how the company reads Brazil. The company is, by nature, a B2B business, and supplies ingredients to professionals, never to the final consumer. But Brazil has an intermediate layer that doesn't fit easily into this logic.

"In Brazil, there's this informal market that we call home baking, which is a home-based professional. It sits in that gray zone," Freitas said. This segment is formed by confectioners, chocolatiers, and brigadeiro and other sweet producers who operate from home or on a small scale. In Irca's reading, it is simultaneously the most immediate and the most voluminous.

The division of efforts, at this first moment, is approximately 60% focused on these home producers and 40% on industry. "Right now we've focused quite a bit on home baking, especially because it's work with a somewhat shorter timeframe. You go to the shelf, do the work, and already start seeing turnover," Freitas explained.

The activation strategy combines digital marketing with physical presence. The company has already conducted tastings and demonstrations in stores in Campinas and São Paulo, and organized a presentation event for chefs, confectioners, and distributors. The first confirmed larger B2B client is the gelato chain Bacio di Latte.

For this initial phase, Irca selected 50 items from a global portfolio of more than 5,000 products. The selection prioritized four categories. The main one is the line of fillings and toppings (hazelnut, pistachio, caramel, and praline, among others) that represents the company's historical core. The second specializes in candied fruits, with a focus on the panettone market. The third is aimed at Italian ice cream shops, a segment in expansion in Brazil. The fourth is focused on ready-to-apply chocolate decorations.

"We want to position ourselves as a one-stop shop for confectionery, chocolaterie, gelateria. Whatever these professionals need, we're capable of supplying," Freitas said. According to him, however, no product in the country is as important as brigadeiro. Among the ingredients the company is betting on in the short term is pistachio, whose popularity Freitas assesses as lasting. "We believe it won't just be a fad. It will stabilize at a high level," he said.

The company has Europe's largest pistachio factory, located in Sicily, and brings to Brazil everything from pure pastes to ready-to-apply creams. However, adaptation for the Brazilian market began even before the products arrived. In Europe, fillings are normally sold in 5-kilogram packages. In Brazil, the sector's profile required smaller formats. "We bought machines in Italy to meet this demand from Brazil specifically," Freitas said. Packages of 1 kilogram or less are becoming the standard for local operations.

Despite this connection with homemade production, the brand's positioning in Brazil is aimed at the premium market. Because it's an imported product, the price will be higher than local competitors, but the company deals with this obstacle by betting on aspirational consumption. "It might be that our product costs 20 cents in production for a small company, but their margin can increase by 50 cents," Freitas said. For the home baker selling brigadeiros or chocolate eggs eaten with a spoon, using an Italian ingredient of recognized origin allows them to charge more for the final product, according to him. "It's premium, but it's not exorbitant, super expensive and inaccessible. And it really brings value to whoever uses the product," Freitas stated.

In the industrial segment, the cycle to consolidate in the local market is longer and more bureaucratic. The group has already mapped targets such as Cacau Show, Kopenhagen, Ofner, and Bauducco as potential clients. Globally, the company already supplies Unilever in the ice cream division and has worked with Franui on launching an ice cream line in Europe. The ambition is to replicate this model in Brazil, but Freitas acknowledges that this work "takes more time" and is "more bureaucratic."

Despite starting slowly, the goal for Brazilian operations is for it to be among the three largest in the group worldwide. "The path to get there combines organic growth in the first three years with the possibility of structural moves later. Irca has grown a lot through acquisitions. We have a bit of that in our DNA. So a possible acquisition in Brazil could also be a reasonable possibility," he said.
