# Asian stocks open lower after global tech selloff and AI doubts

Asian stock exchanges opened lower following a massive technology stock selloff that affected Wall Street, amid growing concerns about the profitability of the billions invested in artificial intelligence, ahead of earnings reports from numerous large-cap companies.

Stocks opened lower in Australia and South Korea, with Japan closed for a holiday, while sentiment toward technology showed signs of stabilization. Nasdaq 100 index futures rose 0.4% after the underlying indicator fell 1% on Tuesday following news that OpenAI failed to meet its own targets.

Treasury bonds fell on Tuesday, pushing yields to their highest levels in several weeks, as escalating oil prices drove up inflation expectations and dampened bets on Federal Reserve interest rate cuts ahead of the central bank's monetary policy announcement on Wednesday. Spot Treasury bond trading is closed during Asian trading hours due to Japan's holiday.

Brent crude opened Wednesday with a 0.5% decline to $110.75 per barrel, breaking seven days of gains. Renewed concerns about artificial intelligence investments and rising oil prices have created a crucial trading day, as large-cap technology companies will report their earnings while investors await clues from the Fed about the direction of interest rates.

Technology companies have contributed to the global equities rally in recent weeks to erase losses stemming from the Middle East conflict, making Wednesday's earnings announcements crucial to maintain the rebound.

"The most important question for investors is whether artificial intelligence will be able to continue driving the market," said Dennis Follmer of Montis Financial.

The large technology companies, which represent around a quarter of the S&P 500's value, are preparing to publish their earnings. Alphabet Inc., Microsoft Corp., Amazon.com Inc. and Meta Platforms Inc. report on Wednesday, followed by Apple Inc. a day later.

The technology sector's earnings have been largely protected from the disruptions of the Iran war. The sector's results are expected to have grown 41% in the first quarter, according to data compiled by Bloomberg Intelligence.

Additionally, the United Arab Emirates will leave OPEC next month, dealing a significant blow to the group that raises questions about its future. The UAE's departure on May 1 after six decades of membership is the latest sign of how the conflict is reshaping global energy markets.

Meanwhile, President Donald Trump said Iran has asked the U.S. to lift its naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz as both sides negotiate an end to the two-month war. Mediators in Pakistan expect Tehran to present a revised proposal in the coming days, CNN reported.

Traders will also be watching Australian economic data to be released later Wednesday. According to Bloomberg Intelligence, inflation is likely to show strong acceleration in the first quarter, driven largely by increased fuel costs as a consequence of the Iran war's impact.
