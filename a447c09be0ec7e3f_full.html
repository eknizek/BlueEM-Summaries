# Asian stocks open lower after global tech selloff and AI concerns

Asian stock exchanges opened lower following a massive selloff in technology stocks that hit Wall Street, amid growing concerns about the profitability of billions invested in artificial intelligence, ahead of earnings reports from numerous large-cap companies.

Stocks opened lower in Australia and South Korea, with Japan closed for a holiday, while sentiment toward technology showed signs of stabilization. Nasdaq 100 index futures rose 0.4% after the underlying index fell 1% Tuesday following news that OpenAI failed to meet its own targets.

U.S. Treasury bonds fell on Tuesday, pushing yields to their highest levels in several weeks, as soaring oil prices boosted inflation expectations and dampened bets on Federal Reserve rate cuts before the central bank's monetary policy announcement on Wednesday. Spot Treasury bond trading is closed during Asian hours due to Japan's holiday.

Brent crude opened Wednesday with a 0.5% drop to $110.75 per barrel, breaking a seven-day winning streak. Renewed concerns about artificial intelligence investments and rising oil prices have set up a crucial day in which large-cap technology companies will report earnings, while investors await clues from the Fed about the direction interest rates will take.

Technology companies have contributed to the global equity rally in recent weeks to erase losses stemming from the Middle East conflict, making Wednesday's earnings announcements crucial for sustaining the rebound.

"The most important question for investors is whether artificial intelligence will continue to drive the market," said Dennis Follmer of Montis Financial.

The large tech companies, which represent roughly a quarter of the S&P 500's value, are preparing to report earnings. Alphabet Inc., Microsoft Corp., Amazon.com Inc., and Meta Platforms Inc. report on Wednesday, followed by Apple Inc. a day later.

Technology sector earnings have been largely shielded from disruptions caused by the Iran war. The sector's results are expected to have grown 41% in the first quarter, according to data compiled by Bloomberg Intelligence.

Meanwhile, the United Arab Emirates will exit OPEC next month, a significant blow to the group that raises questions about its future. The UAE's departure on May 1 after six decades of membership is the latest sign of how the conflict is reshaping global energy markets.

President Donald Trump said Iran has asked the U.S. to lift its naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, as both sides negotiate an end to the two-month war. Mediators in Pakistan expect Tehran to present a revised proposal in the coming days, CNN reported.

Traders will also watch Australian economic data set to be released later Wednesday. According to Bloomberg Intelligence, inflation is likely to show sharp acceleration in the first quarter, driven largely by increased fuel costs as a result of the Iran war's impact.
