Sleeping budget: 1,442 districts have not used a quarter of funds for public works

20 April 2026 · 11:29 PM

# Sleeping Budget: 1,442 Districts Have Not Used a Quarter of Funds for Public Works

In Peru, the calendar advances, but public management does not move at the same pace. Gestión reviewed the spending capacity of Peru's 1,891 municipalities and discovered that budget execution for local projects is setting off alarm bells. It is not only a matter of slow work: in some cases it is nonexistent as of 2026 so far.

To date, the districts and provinces have S/ 23,147.9 million for their projects. As of April 17, they have invested 19.1% of this amount. The data comes from the Economic Transparency platform, a digital tool from the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), which allows monitoring how public entities in the country use money.

## Zero Spending on the Radar

A critical fact is that 97 of the 1,891 municipalities in the country have not executed a single sol by April. Their spending level is zero in a context where demand for basic services, infrastructure, and security continues to increase. Amazonas, Áncash, Apurímac, Arequipa, Ayacucho, Cajamarca, Huancavelica, Junín, Lambayeque, Lima, Pasco, Piura, Puno, and San Martín are home to these municipalities.

But there are more numbers in "red": when expanding the view, 1,442 municipalities were detected that do not exceed 25% budget execution through the evaluated period. That is, 76.26% of Peru's municipalities register a harmful spending pattern: money paused at the beginning of the year and accelerated at the end.

Karla Gaviño, professor of Public Management at the University of the Pacific (UP), explains this trend, but first points out its implications: "We are in April. That there are municipalities with 0% budget reduction means they are not even paying advances for technical files, designing, or convening for the physical execution of works, which means we are quite far from them being able to make a financial move."

She adds: "This leads us to a recurring scenario each time there is a change of government, in which during the first months there is no major spending execution, but in the last months there is a kind of race to use all the money possible. The punishment is concentrated in the quality of execution."

Álvaro Cubas, economist at the Private Council for Competitiveness (CPC), agrees: "Unfortunately, it is habitual behavior among municipalities, but one always tries to think that, being the last year of management, they should go against the current: spend more to close more, but that scenario is not being seen."

He adds: "We could say that rushed execution also translates into low-quality execution. For example, in a hypothetical case, they gave S/ 1 million to a project, but between November and December S/ 800,000 was spent. Then, it is understood that, during the course of the year, the demands that the work could have had were not met. [...] Delayed spending will also cause the project's progress in general to be 'kicked down the road.'"

## The "Race" Before Year-End

When there is a change in subnational governments, the probability increases that ongoing projects will be paralyzed because each administration usually pushes its own agenda. "The risk is greater when there is a change of authority. According to what we saw at the CPC, that is when the number of discontinued projects is largest. [...] It will weigh heavily whether the incoming mayor has the political commitment to continue the project that the previous authority left. What we end up having as citizens is the risk that what was promised will not be fulfilled, that the spending that was done will not translate into works or well-being," Cubas warns.

Gaviño is emphatic on this point: "There is a very perverse objective, which is just to close the year because the administration ends. Then, in the last months, the spending that is executed is financial spending that does not necessarily correspond with physical execution of work."

"This leads us to infer, potentially, that we are talking about works done with considerable haste and with reduced quality. And not only with reduced quality, but also with spending converted into simplistic expenses." She refers to the fact that the urgency to show results usually tilts the balance toward interventions that do not address deeper deficiencies: "They are not necessarily going to be works that generate greater impact on social gaps, but rather those more populist ones that will be remembered in the voter's memory."

## Geographical Patterns

The lag, moreover, has a marked geography. Áncash, with 132 municipalities, and Lima, with 126, lead the list with the largest number of municipalities that fail to execute a quarter of their budget in the first months of the year. They are followed by Junín, with 102, Ayacucho, with 100, and Cajamarca, with 95.

Cubas makes a note on this: "A large part of the municipalities are wealthy municipalities [...]. The saddest part of all this is that, beyond the delays, when a project takes time to execute and years pass, its cost will increase due to reactivation. A project that cost S/ 100 million will cost S/ 300 million. And it is not that the scope of the project increased, but rather S/ 200 million more is being spent simply because it was paralyzed and requires other additional expenses, administrative ones especially, so that it gets back on track."

Meanwhile, the academic emphasizes that the situation is especially concerning in regions that receive canon revenue, such as Áncash or Cajamarca. Having more resources and having the numbers against you reflects a lack of technical capacity. "It is not healthy that Áncash and Cajamarca do not have accompaniment in budget allocation, there is neither political priority nor technical spending capacity. They are regrettable indicators."

"And there is another additional fact. It is a cross-cutting problem throughout the entire country [the lack of strategy in municipal budget execution]. We cannot talk about focusing on a particular area. [...] Hence the importance of the MEF, which must provide support from the leadership of public investment and budget."
