Sleeping Budget: 1,442 Districts Have Not Used One Quarter of 2026 Spending Allocation

20 April 2026 · 11:29 PM

# "Sleeping" Budget: 1,442 Districts Have Not Used Even a Quarter of Their Money for Works

In Peru, the calendar moves forward, but public management does not keep pace with it. Gestión reviewed the spending capacity of Peru's 1,891 municipalities and discovered that budget execution for projects at the local level sets off an alarm signal. It's not just slow work: in some cases it's nonexistent through what has transpired in 2026.

To date, districts and provinces have S/ 23,147.9 million available for their projects. As of April 17, they have invested 19.1% of this amount. The data comes from the Economic Transparency platform, a digital tool of the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), which allows monitoring how public entities in the country allocate money.

## Accelerated Execution on the Radar

A critical fact is that 97 of the 1,891 municipalities in the country have not executed a single sol through April. Their spending level is zero in a context where demand for basic services, infrastructure, and security continues to increase. Amazonas, Áncash, Apurímac, Arequipa, Ayacucho, Cajamarca, Huancavelica, Junín, Lambayeque, Lima, Pasco, Piura, Puno, and San Martín harbor these municipalities.

But there are more numbers in "red": when broadening the perspective, 1,442 municipalities were detected that do not exceed 25% budget execution through the evaluated period. That is, 76.26% of Peru's municipalities register a harmful spending pattern: money paused at the beginning of the year and accelerated at the end of it.

Karla Gaviño, professor of Public Management at the Universidad del Pacífico (UP), explains this trend, but first points out its implications: "We are in April. That there are municipalities with 0% budget reduction means they are not even paying for advances on technical reports, designing, or calling for the physical execution of works, so we are quite far from being able to make a financial movement."

She adds: "This leads us to a recurring scenario every time there is a change of government, in which during the first months there is little budget execution, but in the last months there is a kind of race to use all the money possible. The punishment is concentrated in the quality of execution."

Álvaro Cubas, economist at the Private Council for Competitiveness (CPC), agrees: "Unfortunately, it is habitual behavior for municipalities, but one always tries to think that, being the last year of management, they should go against the current: spend more to accomplish more, but that scenario is not being seen."

He adds: "We could affirm that rushed execution also translates into low-quality execution. For example, in a hypothetical case, they gave S/ 1 million to a project, but between November and December S/ 800,000 was spent. Therefore, it is understood that, throughout the year, the demands the work could have had were not met. [...] Delayed spending will also cause the project's overall progress to be 'kicked down the road.'"

## The "Race" Before Year-End

When there is a change in subnational governments, the probability increases that ongoing projects will be paralyzed because each administration usually pushes its own agenda. "The risk is greater when an authority changes. According to what we saw at the CPC, that is when the number of discontinued projects is largest. [...] It will weigh heavily whether the incoming mayor has the political commitment to continue the project that the previous authority left behind. What we end up having as citizens is the risk that what was promised will not be fulfilled, that the spending that was done will not translate into works or well-being," Cubas warns.

Gaviño is emphatic on this point: "There is a very perverse objective, which is precisely to close the year because the administration ends. Therefore, in the last months, the expenses that are executed are financial expenses that do not necessarily correspond with physical execution of works."

"This leads us to infer, potentially, that we are talking about works done with considerable haste and with reduced quality. And not only with reduced quality, but also with expenses turned into simplistic spending."

She refers to the fact that the urgency to show results usually tips the balance toward interventions that do not address deeper needs: "Works will not necessarily be done that generate greater impact on social gaps, but rather those that are more populist and remembered in the voter's memory."

## Lima

In Lima province, two districts registered 0% progress: Barranco and San Bartolo. The jurisdictions have S/ 1.8 million and S/ 1.9 million for their projects, respectively. In the case of Metropolitan Lima, which has S/ 886.1 million for its works, 33.4% has already been executed.

## Cross-Cutting Diagnosis

The lag, moreover, has a marked geography. Áncash, with 132 municipalities, and Lima, with 126, top the list with the highest number of municipalities that do not execute a quarter of their budget in the first months of the year. They are followed by Junín with 102, Ayacucho with 100, and Cajamarca with 95.

Cubas makes a note on this: "A large part of the municipalities are wealthy municipalities [...]. The saddest part of all this is that, beyond the delays, when a project takes time to be executed and years pass, its cost will increase due to reactivation. A project that cost S/ 100 million will cost S/ 300 million. And it's not that the project's scope was increased, but rather S/ 200 million more is being spent simply because it was paralyzed and requires other additional expenses, administrative especially, so that it gets back on track."

For her part, the academic emphasizes that the situation is especially concerning in regions that receive canon revenue, such as Áncash or Cajamarca. Having more resources and having numbers against you reflects a lack of technical capacity. "It is not healthy that Áncash and Cajamarca lack guidance in budget allocation; there is neither political priority nor technical spending capacity. These are lamentable indicators."

"And there is another additional fact. It is a problem transversal to the entire country [the lack of strategy in municipal budget execution]. We cannot speak of a focus on a particular area. [...] Hence the importance of the MEF, which must provide support from the governance of public investment and budget."
