# Travel is no longer a pleasure: reasons why bus fares rise but service deteriorates

Fares are increasing and travel is getting worse. This widespread feeling among public transportation passengers in the Buenos Aires Metropolitan Area (AMBA) is driven by several economic reasons. The crisis began at the start of this century, worsened ten years ago, and finally exploded after the pandemic in 2022, when inflation accelerated.

The problems with transportation are most noticeable in buses: the available vehicle fleet is smaller, frequencies are stretched, lines multiply, and travel becomes increasingly crowded, especially during peak hours. The fourth business day of each month is a moment of tension: amid widespread austerity and lack of fiscal resources, the Nation and the province of Buenos Aires must transfer money to bus companies for salaries. If the money doesn't arrive or payments are delayed, service on dozens of lines is at risk.

**Bus fares, subsidies, and costs**

According to various surveys by the Interdisciplinary Institute of Political Economy (IIEP) of UBA and Conicet, and the Argentine Association of Automotive Transport Entrepreneurs (AAETA), the real cost of passenger transportation by bus is between $2,083 and $2,129 per trip. However, companies only receive $1,559. The difference amounts to about $64.5 billion in total, money that the private sector stops investing and is reflected in a loss of service quality, reduced frequencies, less vehicle renewal, and greater public safety risks.

The economic situation worsened in March due to a 23% average increase in diesel prices, motivated by rising crude oil prices due to the war in the Middle East. The entire bus system consumes on average about 35 million liters of fuel per month. To address the March increase, the Nation, the City, and the province of Buenos Aires should inject extra subsidies of $17.5 billion—according to IIEP—or apply a 16% fare increase. Meanwhile, each user pays a minimum of between $700 and $871, depending on the jurisdiction administering the line, barely 35% of the real cost.

Instead of integrating the metropolitan transportation system between the Nation, the City, and the province of Buenos Aires, each regulator establishes its own criteria and fares, as if the Riachuelo or General Paz Avenue were a border.

**Why buses are malfunctioning**

Facing this scenario, the adjustment happens through quantities. In March, approximately 16,989 buses circulated in AMBA, 742 fewer units than in November of last year. The comparison yields worse results compared to November 2019, six and a half years ago, when approximately 19,348 cars were on the street; the decline is 12%.

Throughout last month, the number of frequencies was reduced by 4% compared to the same month last year, and the number of passengers fell by 11%. Meanwhile, in the first half of April, frequencies dropped 18% and passengers fell 21%.

Luciano Fusaro, president of AAETA—which represents Metropol lines, the second-largest company in AMBA, behind DOTA—explained that these numbers are related to "the fall in economic activity in Buenos Aires, worse service, and higher fares in relation to wages."

A combination slow-cooked has resulted in bus travel no longer being a pleasure.
