# Travel is no longer a pleasure: reasons why bus fares rise but service deteriorates in Buenos Aires

Fares are increasing and travel is getting worse. This widespread feeling among public transportation passengers in the Buenos Aires Metropolitan Area (AMBA) is motivated by several economic reasons. The crisis began in the early 2000s, worsened ten years ago, and finally exploded after the pandemic in 2022, when inflation accelerated. The problems are most noticeable in buses: the available vehicle fleet is smaller, frequencies are stretched, lines multiply, and travel becomes increasingly crowded, especially during peak hours.

The fourth business day of each month is a moment of tension: amid widespread budget cuts and lack of fiscal resources, the national government and Buenos Aires province must transfer money to bus companies for wages. If the funds don't arrive or payments are delayed, service on dozens of lines is at risk.

## Fares, subsidies, and bus costs

According to various surveys by the Interdisciplinary Institute of Political Economy (IIEP) at UBA and Conicet, and the Argentine Association of Automotive Transport Entrepreneurs (AAETA), the real cost of passenger transportation by bus is between $2,083 and $2,129 per trip. However, companies only receive $1,559. The difference amounts to about $64.5 billion total, money that the private sector stops investing and is reflected in a loss of service quality, lower frequencies, less vehicle renewal, and greater public safety risks.

The economic situation worsened in March due to a 23% average increase in diesel prices, motivated by the rise in crude oil due to the war in the Middle East. The entire bus system consumes an average of about 35 million liters of fuel per month. To deal with the March increase, the national government, the City, and Buenos Aires province should inject extra subsidies of $17.5 billion—according to IIEP—or apply a 16% fare increase. Meanwhile, each user pays a minimum of between $700 and $871, depending on the jurisdiction administering the line, barely 35% of the real cost.

Instead of integrating the metropolitan transportation system among the national government, the City, and Buenos Aires province, each regulator establishes its own criteria and fares, as if the Riachuelo or General Paz Avenue were a border.

## Why buses work poorly

Faced with this scenario, the adjustment happens in quantities. In March, approximately 16,989 buses circulated in AMBA, 742 fewer units than in November of last year. The comparison shows worse results compared to November 2019, six and a half years ago, when about 19,348 cars were on the street; the decline is 12%. Throughout last month, the number of frequencies was reduced by 4% compared to the same month last year, and the number of passengers fell by 11%. In the first half of April, frequencies declined 18% and passengers dropped 21%.

Luciano Fusaro, president of AAETA—which represents the lines of Metropol, the second largest company in AMBA behind DOTA—explained that these numbers are related to "the fall in economic activity in Buenos Aires, worse service, and the increase in fares in relation to wages." A combination slowly cooked has caused traveling by bus to no longer be a pleasure.
