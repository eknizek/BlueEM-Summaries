Brazil top court upholds curbs on foreign-linked land ownership

24 April 2026 · 01:30 PM

Brazil's Federal Supreme Court (STF) ruled on Thursday (23) to uphold rules restricting the acquisition of land by Brazilian companies owned by foreign capital, as established under Law No. 5,079/1971. In a unanimous decision, the justices followed the opinion of the rapporteur, retired Justice Marco Aurélio Mello, maintaining the limitations set out in the legislation.

The law provides that the purchase of rural land in Brazil by Brazilian companies with foreign capital is subject to the same treatment as acquisitions by foreign companies. Each municipality may have no more than 25% of its territory owned by foreign individuals or companies. In addition, a single foreign nationality may not hold more than 10% of a municipality's total area.

The judgment had been suspended in March after Justice Alexandre de Moraes requested additional time for review. When the case resumed on Thursday (23), he aligned with the rapporteur, stating that Brazilian legislation does not discriminate against foreign companies or prevent foreign investment, but rather establishes a regulatory framework that prioritizes national sovereignty while allowing for development.

"This is not about restricting access or imposing discriminatory treatment, but about upholding national sovereignty. It allows the country, in a regulated and reasonable manner—without discrimination but with differentiation—to govern land acquisition in a way that supports national development," Moraes said.

Justice Luiz Fux followed, emphasizing the need to balance economic development with national sovereignty. Justices Dias Toffoli and Chief Justice Edson Fachin also voted in favor.

The Court reviewed two cases concerning the validity of the rules for Brazilian companies owned by foreign investors. In constitutional legal action ADPF 342, the Brazilian Rural Society (SRB) sought to overturn the restrictions, arguing that treating such companies the same as fully foreign entities undermines financing for agricultural activities and could discourage investment in the country. In civil action ACO 2463, the federal government and the National Institute for Colonization and Agrarian Reform (INCRA) requested the annulment of a legal opinion issued by São Paulo's Court of Justice oversight body, which had exempted notary offices from complying with the contested rules.
