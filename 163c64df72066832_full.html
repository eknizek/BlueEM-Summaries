# Oil could rise further, says JP Morgan: "simple mathematics"

Global oil consumption has plummeted more since the start of the war than during the peak of the 2009 financial crisis. Although this data is striking, the drop in supply was far worse, signaling that even greater "demand destruction" is needed to bring the market back into balance, according to JP Morgan analysts.

In practice, this scenario creates room for an additional increase in Brent barrel prices, the bank's research team said, assessing that current quotations are not at "extreme" levels given the historical context and magnitude of current events. The report's title sums up the thesis: "simple mathematics."

The oil supply disruption from the conflict in Iran, which has practically paralyzed the Strait of Hormuz, is estimated at nearly 14 million barrels per day (bpd). To put the scale of the problem in perspective: Saudi Arabia, the second-largest producer, was extracting 10 million barrels daily before the crisis triggered by U.S. and Israeli attacks on Iran.

On the demand side, there was a drop of 2.8 million barrels in March and 4.3 million in April, compared to about 2.5 million during the 2009 recession, according to JP Morgan's calculations.

Even assuming an aggressive drawdown of 8 million barrels per day from global inventories, it would still be necessary to eliminate 2 million barrels from demand—either through lower consumption or increased inventory use—to return to balance, the analysts said.

"This is a very large volume to be absorbed solely by emerging markets. In practical terms, Europe and the U.S. will also need to participate. For this to happen, prices will probably need to rise further."

Oil prices surged following the war to above $100 per barrel, and fuel derivatives prices nearly doubled. "Still, prices don't appear high enough to explain, by themselves, such a magnitude of demand drop, or one so rapid. This suggests that much of this is not traditional demand destruction via prices, but forced losses due to supply shortage," the analysts said.

For now, this physical scarcity has been concentrated in the Middle East and Asia. Africa has also been heavily hit, but consumption there is less significant, according to the bank. "The Americas are relatively more isolated in the short term thanks to greater flexibility in domestic supply and inventory buffers. Still, prices at the pump are beginning to reduce discretionary vehicle use in the U.S., and rising prices are starting to reduce demand for air travel."

The market imbalance becomes even more complex because countries with spare production capacity, such as Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates, are unable to rapidly increase supply due to the Strait of Hormuz blockade, which paralyzes barrel transport. The U.S. in theory could increase shale oil production, but this would not happen immediately, while Russia has some spare capacity but is under Ukrainian attacks against its energy infrastructure that have reduced production.
