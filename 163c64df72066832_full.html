# Oil Could Rise Further, Says JP Morgan: "Simple Math"

Global oil consumption has already plummeted more since the start of the war than it did during the peak of the 2009 global financial crisis. Although this data is striking, the drop in supply was much worse, signaling that "greater demand destruction" is still needed to bring the oil market back into balance, according to JP Morgan analysts.

In practical terms, the scenario opens space for additional increases in Brent barrel prices, the bank's research team said, assessing that quotations are not at "extreme" levels given the historical context and magnitude of current events. The report's title summarizes the thesis: "simple math."

The oil supply disruption from the Iran conflict, which has practically paralyzed the Strait of Hormuz, is estimated at nearly 14 million barrels per day (bpd). To put the scale of the problem in perspective: Saudi Arabia, the second-largest producer, had been extracting 10 million barrels daily before the crisis triggered by U.S. and Israeli attacks on Iran.

On the demand side, there was a drop of 2.8 million barrels in March and 4.3 million in April, compared to around 2.5 million during the 2009 recession, according to JP Morgan's calculations. Even assuming an aggressive drawdown of 8 million barrels/day from global inventories, it would still be necessary to eliminate 2 million barrels from demand—either through lower consumption or greater use of stockpiles—to return to balance, the analysts said.

"This is a very large volume to be absorbed solely by emerging markets. In practical terms, Europe and the U.S. will also need to participate. For this to happen, prices probably need to rise even further."

Oil prices surged following the war, above $100 per barrel, and derivative prices such as fuel nearly doubled. "Still, prices don't seem high enough to explain, by themselves, such a large or such a rapid drop in demand. This suggests that much of this is not traditional demand destruction via prices, but forced losses due to supply shortage," the analysts said.

For now, this physical scarcity has been concentrated in the Middle East and Asia. Africa has also been heavily impacted, but consumption there is less relevant, according to the bank. "The Americas are relatively more isolated in the short term thanks to greater supply flexibility from domestic sources and inventory buffers. Still, prices at the pump are beginning to reduce discretionary vehicle use in the U.S., and rising prices are starting to diminish demand for air travel."

The market imbalance becomes even more complex because countries with production spare capacity, such as Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates, are prevented from rapidly increasing supply due to the Strait of Hormuz paralysis, which halts barrel transportation. The U.S. could theoretically increase shale oil production, but this would not happen immediately, while Russia has some spare capacity but is under Ukrainian attacks on its energy infrastructure that have reduced production.
