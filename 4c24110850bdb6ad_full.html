# JiveMauá Debuts in Agribusiness with R$ 500 Million Fiagro Fund

JiveMauá launched its first Fiagro, the JiveMauá Agronegócio Fiagro (JMAG), in a challenging scenario where 16 funds face problematic assets. With R$ 25 billion under management, the manager specialized in distressed assets seeks to capitalize on market failures to become a creditor in agribusiness. The JMAG has an initial portfolio of R$ 500 million, with a senior quota offering a target return of 15% per year. The fund's 15% subordination structure, equivalent to R$ 75 million, serves as protection for senior investors. The fund enters the market with 22 operations and a pipeline of R$ 800 million, focusing on real guarantees and governance. Despite the adverse environment, JiveMauá believes that discipline in risk selection is a differentiator.

One year ago, sixteen Agricultural Production Chain Investment Funds (Fiagro) carry some type of problematic asset. And seven of the nine most liquid funds on B3 live with delays or guarantee misalignment. It is in this scenario that JiveMauá decided to debut in the market and launch its JiveMauá Agronegócio Fiagro (JMAG) to be traded on Cetip. With R$ 25 billion under management, the manager specialized in distressed assets will capitalize on others' mistakes to become a creditor in agriculture.

"We are entering operations with real guarantees and taking advantage of a more negative cycle to better select risks. It becomes much easier to make decisions," says Samer Serhan, Chief Investment Officer of private credit, infrastructure, agribusiness and pension funds at JiveMauá, to NeoFeed.

The JMAG is born with a portfolio of over R$ 500 million. The senior quota—accessible to the general investor—has a target return of 15% per year, net of costs, in fixed-rate series with a six-year term. The public offering is coordinated by XP.

But what helps explain JiveMauá's Fiagro differential is the fund's subordination structure. This subordinated quota equals 15% of assets (R$ 75 million) and functions as a "line of defense." This means that all structural costs and any initial losses pass through it before any impact on the senior quota. In practice, the investor only starts losing after the manager has fully absorbed this amount.

"The senior quota holder only loses R$ 1 if JiveMauá loses the R$ 75 million first," states Paulo Fleury, agribusiness manager at JiveMauá.

With a 15% subordination structure, the JMAG is in the upper range of the industry, where many managers operate between 10% and 12% and, in some cases, even below 5%. There are funds with higher levels on paper, of 20% or 25%, but without the manager's own capital absorbing the first loss. It is at this point that JiveMauá placed its differential: the R$ 75 million are capital from existing house funds assuming the first impact.

The JMAG also differentiates itself by how it enters this market. Instead of seeking future diversification, the fund debuts with a portfolio of 22 operations and a pipeline exceeding R$ 800 million, with expectations of full allocation within 60 to 80 days. The operations are distributed among different instruments, such as FIDCs, agricultural receivables certificates (CRAs), rural producer credit (CPRs), debentures and sale and leaseback structures, across agribusiness subsectors, from inputs and machinery to rural producers, food industry and distributors.

The entry into agribusiness also reflects an internal movement at JiveMauá. The private credit vertical, created in 2022, has grown rapidly and already totals approximately R$ 5.5 billion. To have a dedicated agribusiness desk, the manager assembled a dedicated team with over 20 professionals including lawyers and financial specialists. And at the end of last year, it brought Fleury to lead the strategy—he worked at houses such as Valora, Solis and eB Capital, in addition to having been manager of FGAA11, from FG/A, one of the few Fiagros that crossed the recent period without relevant credit problems.

"There isn't an ideal moment that we will hit a perfect seasonality to enter [private credit for agribusiness]. That matters less. But how we are entering, that, yes, is our great differential," says Serhan.

**Market Environment**

Launching a Fiagro in 2026 may seem counterintuitive. The sector faces falling commodity prices, high costs and a recent sequence of judicial recoveries. But it is precisely the type of environment that favors a more disciplined strategy.

"Those who had to have problems already did. Now we can structure operations with guarantee, governance and term," says Fleury.

To understand the timing of JiveMauá's fund, one must look at what went wrong in the Fiagro industry. At the end of 2024, the Fiagro IAGR11, from SFI Invest, became a symbol of a problem the market preferred to ignore. Launched at R$ 11 per quota, with promises of tax-exempt dividends and controlled risk, the fund saw its assets melt when four major agribusiness debtors entered judicial recovery almost simultaneously. In the fund's portfolio, 60% was in CRAs from Três Irmãos, Piva, Mitre and Castilhos. The quota fell to R$ 3.97. More than 338,000 investors suffered significant losses, in some cases exceeding 30%.

The IAGR11 was not alone. The GCRA11, from Galápagos Capital, reached 34% of assets exposed to the same issuers. The first problem with these Fiagros was concentration, which showed that a poorly distributed portfolio can compromise an entire fund. The second was the fragility of guarantees. A significant portion of problematic Fiagros operated with pledges or guarantees difficult to enforce, which in practice placed the investor in the judicial recovery queue. And the third point was the lack of specialization. Amid the sector boom, generalist managers entered agriculture pressured by capital raising and ended up assuming risks they did not understand.

One of the pioneers in investments with the purchase of bad credits, restructuring of companies in judicial recovery or liquidation of assets to recover value, JiveMauá can use this reputation and DNA in the JMAG—even though the Fiagro is not itself a special situations fund.

"Now it is allowed for funds that go to the general public, like the one we are raising, to invest in companies that are in judicial recovery," says Serhan. The condition, he details, is that the judicial recovery is consolidated and the receivables are performing, that is, the company has exited the chaos and has predictable cash flow. The JMAG will be able to allocate a small portion to assets with this profile, as long as the senior quota holder protection structure is respected. But Serhan notes that this type of opportunity has more to do with the house's distressed fund than with the private credit Fiagro.

Today, the Fiagro market exceeds R$ 40 billion. "If we compare with real estate funds and the size of agribusiness, it is reasonable to project a market above R$ 200 billion in the coming years," says Fleury.
