# JiveMauá enters agribusiness with R$500 million fund

JiveMauá launched its first Fiagro, the JiveMauá Agronegócio Fiagro (JMAG), in a challenging scenario where 16 funds face problematic assets. With R$25 billion under management, the manager specialized in distressed assets seeks to capitalize on market failures to become a creditor in agribusiness. The JMAG has an initial portfolio of R$500 million, with a senior quota offering a target return of 15% per year. The fund's subordination structure of 15%, equivalent to R$75 million, serves as protection for senior investors. The fund enters the market with 22 operations and a pipeline of R$800 million, focusing on real guarantees and governance. Despite the adverse environment, JiveMauá believes that discipline in risk selection is a differentiator.

One year ago, sixteen Agricultural Production Chain Investment Funds (Fiagro) carry some type of problematic asset. And seven of the nine most liquid funds on B3 deal with delays or misalignment of guarantees. It is in this scenario that JiveMauá decided to debut in the market and place its JiveMauá Agronegócio Fiagro (JMAG) for trading on Cetip. With R$25 billion under management, the manager specialized in distressed assets will leverage others' mistakes to become a creditor in agriculture. "We are entering operations with real guarantees and taking advantage of a more negative cycle to better select risks. It becomes much easier to make decisions," says Samer Serhan, CIO of private credit, infrastructure, agribusiness and pension funds at JiveMauá.

The JMAG is born with a portfolio of over R$500 million. The senior quota—accessible to general investors—has a target return of 15% per year, net of costs, in a pre-fixed series with a six-year term. The public offering is coordinated by XP. But what helps explain the differential of JiveMauá's Fiagro is the fund's subordination structure. This subordinated quota equals 15% of assets (R$75 million) and functions as a "line of defense." This means all structural costs and potential initial losses go through it before any impact on the senior quota. In practice, the investor only starts losing after the manager has fully absorbed this amount. "The senior quota holder only loses R$1 if JiveMauá loses the R$75 million first," states Paulo Fleury, agribusiness manager at JiveMauá.

With a subordination structure of 15%, the JMAG is in the upper range of the industry, where many managers operate between 10% and 12% and, in some cases, even below 5%. There are funds with higher levels on paper, of 20% or 25%, but without the manager's own capital absorbing the first loss. This is where JiveMauá placed its differential: the R$75 million is capital from existing house funds assuming the first impact.

The JMAG also differentiates itself by how it enters this market. Rather than seeking future diversification, the fund debuts with a portfolio of 22 operations and a pipeline exceeding R$800 million, with expectations of full allocation within 60 to 80 days. The operations are distributed among different instruments, such as FIDCs, agricultural receivables certificates (CRAs), rural producer credit (CPRs), debentures, and sale and leaseback structures, across agribusiness subsectors, from inputs and machinery to rural producers, food industry, and distributors.

Entry into agriculture also reflects internal movement at JiveMauá. The private credit vertical, created in 2022, has grown rapidly and now totals approximately R$5.5 billion. To have a dedicated agribusiness desk, the manager assembled a dedicated team with over 20 professionals including lawyers and financial specialists. And at the end of last year, it brought in Fleury to lead the strategy—he has worked at firms such as Valora, Solis, and eB Capital, and was manager of FGAA11, from FG/A, one of the few Fiagros that came through the recent period without significant credit problems.

"There's no ideal moment when we'll get perfect seasonality to enter [private credit for agribusiness]. That matters less. But how we are entering—that is our great differential," says Serhan.

**Market Environment**

Launching a Fiagro in 2026 may seem counterintuitive. The sector faces falling commodity prices, high costs, and a recent sequence of judicial recoveries. But it is precisely the type of environment that favors a more disciplined strategy. "Those who were going to have problems have already had them. Now we can structure operations with guarantees, governance, and terms," says Fleury.

To understand the timing of JiveMauá's fund, one must look at what went wrong in the Fiagro industry. At the end of 2024, the IAGR11 Fiagro, from SFI Invest, became a symbol of a problem the market preferred to ignore. Launched at R$11 per quota, promising tax-exempt dividends and controlled risk, the fund saw its assets melt when four major agricultural debtors entered judicial recovery almost simultaneously. In the fund's portfolio, 60% was in CRAs from Três Irmãos, Piva, Mitre, and Castilhos. The quota fell to R$3.97. More than 338,000 investors suffered significant losses, in some cases exceeding 30%.

IAGR11 was not alone. The GCRA11, from Galápagos Capital, had up to 34% of assets exposed to the same issuers. The first problem of these Fiagros was concentration, which showed that a poorly distributed portfolio could compromise the entire fund. The second was the fragility of guarantees. Much of the problematic Fiagros operated with pledge or guarantees difficult to execute, which, in practice, placed the investor in the judicial recovery queue. And the third point was lack of specialization. Amid the sector boom, generalist managers entered agriculture pressured by fundraising and ended up assuming risks they didn't understand.

One of the pioneers in investments through buying bad credits, restructuring companies in judicial recovery, or liquidating assets to recover value, JiveMauá can use this reputation and DNA in the JMAG—even though the Fiagro is not itself a special situations fund. "Now it is allowed for funds going to the general public, like the one we are raising, to invest in companies in judicial recovery," says Serhan. The condition, he details, is that the judicial recovery be consolidated and the receivables be performing—that is, the company has already emerged from chaos and has predictable cash flow. The JMAG can allocate a small portion in assets with this profile, as long as the protection structure for the senior quota holder is respected. But Serhan notes that this type of opportunity relates more to the house's distressed fund than to the private credit Fiagro.

Today, the Fiagro market exceeds R$40 billion. "If we compare it with real estate funds and the size of agribusiness, it is reasonable to project a market above R$200 billion in the coming years," says Fleury.
