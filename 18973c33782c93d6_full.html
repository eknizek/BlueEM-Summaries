# Larroudé aims to be Tory Burch, Prada and Shein all in one

The women's shoe market in the US is divided into two extremes: products costing around $100 with low quality, and shoes above $600 that have quality but are inaccessible to most people. This was the insight of the couple Ricardo and Marina Larroudé, who founded the Larroudé brand six years ago precisely to fill this gap.

"We saw this 'white space' in the market and decided to create something with an accessible luxury profile and a digital approach, focused mainly on D2C," Ricardo told Brazil Journal.

The decision to start a business came at a turning point in their lives. Both have lived in the United States for over 20 years. It was the beginning of the pandemic, and Marina had just been fired from Arezzo, where she worked for eight months. Ricardo, in turn, had been at Ambev for three years, following a career in investment banking.

"We started thinking about a way to combine my finance experience with her creative experience to build our own business," he said.

The bet seems to have paid off. Larroudé grossed over $50 million last year, has about 700 employees, and its shoes are worn by celebrities like Taylor Swift, Selena Gómez, and one of Beyoncé's daughters. Last month, the run rate already hit $100 million, and the goal for this year is to gross $120 million.

According to Ricardo, the secret to success was correct positioning and product quality. He says Larroudé works only with pure leather, proprietary design, and a broad portfolio, ranging from sandals to boots, from flats to high heels. The focus is on women's shoes, but the brand has already launched a unisex sneaker called George, and has plans to enter men's shoes, starting with leather shoes and loafers.

Product creation is led by Marina, who heads a team of 20 designers. The brand also works with other creative directors who do sporadic projects for the brand. "We created a place where these people can truly create. They come in, create some collections, and those who are good we keep," said the CEO.

Shoes are sold basically through the brand's ecommerce, which accounts for 90% of revenue. The remaining 10% comes from multi-brand stores like Nordstrom and Bloomingdale's. Despite operating mainly in the United States, Larroudé's factory is located in Rio Grande do Sul, occupying a space of more than 10,000 square meters in the region's shoe-making hub. Larroudé also sells its products in Brazil, which accounts for a quarter of revenue. Unlike what happens in most cases, shoes are cheaper here than in the US. While in the US, prices range from $300 to $400, in Brazil they cost between R$ 600 and R$ 1,000.

"Since we have the factory in Brazil, there's no import tariff and logistics are cheaper. We also do more promotions in Brazil, while in the US we try to keep prices stable," said the CEO.

Since it was founded, Larroudé has raised only $7 million from angel investors, such as José Olympio Pereira, Martin Escobari, Ricardo Salmon, and VTEX founders Mariano Gomide and Geraldo Thomaz. Now, the company is preparing for a new round of up to $50 million to take the next step in its expansion.

The plan is to reach revenue of $400 million to $500 million in the next five years, which would already allow the company to consider an IPO. This expansion involves entering new markets and opening physical stores. Larroudé's plan is to enter Mexico and Colombia and, in the future, Europe, a much more fragmented and competitive market. Ricardo said the plan is to open few stores with a concept-store profile.

"We want the stores to bring some experience, similar to what Kith does, but adapted for our audience," he said. "It's impossible to put the website experience in stores, because shoes have many SKUs, with different sizes and colors. To do that we would have to manufacture a lot of things without knowing if they will sell, which doesn't make sense."

The funds from the round will be used to expand the number of factories to sustain the brand's growth, and to vertically integrate other links in the supply chain, such as leather and metal. Another investment will be in return centers for products in Brazil and the US.

Conversations for fundraising have already begun, with the focus mainly on family offices and asset managers. The pitch is that Larroudé is bringing together the best of several worlds. "We are a mixture of Tory Burch [an American accessible luxury brand] with Prada, but we are digital like Shein," said the founder.
