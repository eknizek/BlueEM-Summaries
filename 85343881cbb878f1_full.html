# Micro-pensions: Colombia's strategy to close the pension coverage gap amid pension crisis

Colombia has already put into practice what some countries are only beginning to discuss: how to include in the pension system millions of workers who will never contribute under traditional schemes. The program of Periodic Economic Benefits (BEPS) — based on flexible micro-savings and state subsidies — has become a regional reference for what micro-pensions can be in the digital economy.

The scheme allows for small, voluntary contributions adapted to unstable incomes. In a country where a large part of the population works in the informal sector or on digital platforms, this type of mechanism directly addresses closing the pension coverage gap. It is no coincidence that a study by the International Federation of Pension Fund Administrators (FIAP) highlights that "micro-pensions emerge as a solution to formalize and protect them."

However, the model is not without tensions, the report warns. While it expands coverage, it also faces clear limitations, such as low savings levels, difficulties sustaining contributions over time, and persistent distrust in the system. In other words, the same instrument that promises inclusion also reflects the structural fragilities of the labor market.

## A Made-to-Measure Model

According to experts, micro-pensions respond to a concrete problem: traditional systems were designed for formal workers with stable incomes, a reality that is increasingly less representative. The scheme incorporates key elements such as liquidity, flexibility, and a broad collection network.

The report defines them as schemes that allow "voluntary, flexible contributions of small amounts," breaking with the rigidity of mandatory contributions. In Colombia, BEPS allows contributions from minimum amounts and offers a 20 percent state subsidy, which incentivizes participation by low-income workers. Additionally, the scheme incorporates fundamental elements such as liquidity, flexibility, and an extensive collection network. This makes it possible for people outside the formal financial system to participate, expanding coverage in rural and urban areas.

The main contribution of micro-pensions is inclusion. They allow workers who previously had no protection to access, at least, income in old age. They also introduce efficiency through technology. International experiences show that digital tools reduce costs and facilitate savings. The study highlights that these solutions "have reduced costs and barriers for unbanked workers."

But the true value of the model is its capacity for adaptation. In contexts of high informality, flexibility is not an advantage but a necessary condition, the FIAP report states.

## The Barriers

Nevertheless, the challenge is profound. The main barrier is behavioral, since saving for old age competes with immediate needs. As the report notes, "workers tend to underestimate the importance of pension savings."

Institutional distrust also weighs heavily. Many informal workers do not believe they will receive real benefits, which limits their willingness to participate. This is compounded by structural problems, such as low, intermittent, and in many cases insufficient incomes to generate significant savings.

In that sense, micro-pensions do not solve the underlying problem, but they do mitigate it. The Colombian case gains relevance at a moment when the model of micro-pensions is gaining significance in Latin America given the debate over the sustainability of pension systems and the growing labor market generated by digital platforms.
