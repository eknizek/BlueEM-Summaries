# Micropensions: Colombia's bet to close the pension gap amid pension crisis

Colombia has already put into practice what some countries are only beginning to discuss: how to include in the pension system millions of workers who will never contribute under traditional schemes. The Economic Periodic Benefits program (BEPS) — based on flexible micro-savings and state subsidies — has become a regional reference for what micropensions can be in the digital economy.

The scheme allows small, voluntary contributions adapted to unstable incomes. In a country where a large part of the population works in the informal sector or on digital platforms, this type of mechanism aims directly at closing the pension gap. It is no coincidence that a study by the International Federation of Pension Fund Administrators (FIAP) highlights that "micropensions [...] emerge as a solution to formalize and protect them."

However, the model is not without tensions, the report warns. While it expands coverage, it also faces clear limits, such as low savings levels, difficulties in sustaining contributions over time, and persistent distrust in the system. In other words, the same instrument that promises inclusion also reflects the structural fragilities of the labor market.

## A Model to Measure

According to experts, micropensions respond to a concrete problem: traditional systems were designed for formal workers with stable incomes, a reality that is increasingly unrepresentative. The scheme incorporates key elements such as liquidity, flexibility, and a broad collection network.

The report defines them as schemes that allow "voluntary, flexible contributions of small amounts," which breaks with the rigidity of mandatory contributions. In Colombia, BEPS allows contributions starting from minimal amounts and offers a state subsidy of 20 percent, which encourages the participation of low-income workers. Additionally, the scheme incorporates fundamental elements such as liquidity, flexibility, and a broad collection network. This makes it easier for people outside the formal financial system to participate, expanding coverage in rural and urban areas.

The main contribution of micropensions is inclusion. They allow workers who previously had no protection to access at least some income in old age. They also introduce efficiency through technology. International experiences show that digital tools reduce costs and facilitate savings. The study highlights that these solutions "have reduced costs and barriers for unbanked workers."

But the true value of the model is its capacity for adaptation. In contexts of high informality, flexibility is not an advantage, but a necessary condition, the FIAP report notes.

## The Barriers

Still, the challenge is profound. The main barrier is behavioral, since saving for old age competes with immediate needs. As the report notes, "workers tend to underestimate the importance of pension savings."

Institutional distrust also weighs heavily. Many informal workers do not believe they will receive real benefits, which limits their willingness to participate. To this are added structural problems, such as low, intermittent, and in many cases insufficient incomes to generate significant savings.
