Aneel activates yellow tariff flag, electricity bills rise in May

24 April 2026 · 09:30 PM

# Aneel Activates Yellow Tariff Flag, Electricity Bills Rise in May

The National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) announced on Friday (24th) that it will activate the yellow tariff flag for the month of May. With this change, electricity bills will have an additional charge of R$ 1.885 for every 100 kilowatt-hours (kWh) consumed.

In a statement, the agency said the change is due to a reduction in rainfall during the transition from the wet to the dry season, which leads to lower hydroelectric generation and the activation of thermal power plants, which have higher costs.

Since January, the flag had been at the green level, meaning no extra charge for consumers, due to more favorable generation conditions.

The regulatory agency's decision is aligned with projections from consulting firms specializing in the electric sector, as shown by Valor. Ampere Consultoria predicts a yellow flag from May to July. Esfera Energia, in turn, estimates a yellow flag in May, activation of the red flag at both levels between June and September, and a yellow flag between October and December this year.

In a statement, the Federation of Industries of the State of Minas Gerais (FIEMG) evaluated the activation of the yellow flag with concern. "The impact is direct on electricity bills and company costs, especially for industry, which has energy as one of its main inputs."

## Tariff Flags

The tariff flag system was created in 2015 to signal to consumers the cost of energy generation in the country. The flags show the variable cost of energy production, based on the analysis of water resource availability, the advancement of renewable sources, and the activation of more expensive generation sources.

In practice, the green, yellow, and red 1 and 2 flags indicate whether or not there will be additional charges on electricity bills. The green flag, when there is no surcharge, means that the cost of generation is low. The yellow and red flags indicate increased costs and the need to use thermal sources, for example.

The model also reduced financial impacts on distributors. Previously, companies bore the costs of more expensive generation for months, which were only passed on to electricity bills in the annual tariff adjustment, with interest charges.
