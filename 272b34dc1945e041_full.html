Dollar Weakness Affects Corn Prices More Than Soy Prices

24 April 2026 · 07:45 PM

# Dollar Weakness Affects Corn Prices More Than Soy Prices

The dollar has been falling against the real due to external market conditions. The American currency is weaker in relation to the world's main currencies, and this is reflected in Brazil. Additionally, there is greater investment flowing into the country, which causes the dollar to fall even more than the decline generated by external conditions. Despite everything, Brazil remains a place that, compared to other countries at the same level, offers a certain security to foreign investors. This ends up attracting more speculative short-term capital.

The effect on agricultural products is a reduction in prices in reais received by producers and an increase for importers. However, Brazilian soy, since the country is the only viable exporter at the moment, is the cheapest in the world. The country is the major global supplier at the moment, says Daniele Siqueira, an analyst at AgRural. The dollar decline pressures the oilseed's price in real, and the value paid to producers becomes lower. With this, producers tend to withhold sales, and to guarantee the product, the importer pays an extra premium for soy. At this time of year, Argentina has a delayed harvest and will only export starting in May and June. Meanwhile, the United States is in an off-season, and the major importer of American soy is China. American soy, however, pays a fee to enter the Chinese market. To guarantee a supply of product, especially since this is the peak export period for Brazil, importers pay more, raising export premiums. "If they don't import from us, who will they import from? And that gives Brazilian product a certain margin for price increases," says Daniele. The premium partially offsets the dollar decline. In Sorriso (MT), a sack of soy, even with the dollar decline, remains close to R$ 103. The same occurs in Cascavel (PR), where a sack is between R$ 117 and R$ 118, according to AgRural monitoring.

The export premium is normally the decisive factor in relation to Chicago. It is a dollar value, negotiated daily at other trading points outside Chicago. Price formation begins at the Chicago Exchange, and the market determines a premium for the local price in relation to that Exchange. Sometimes it's above, sometimes it's below. It depends on the time and market situation, says Daniele.

As for corn, there is no problem at this time of year, since the country will only export larger volumes in the second semester. However, there is a major difference between the cereal scenario and that of the oilseed. Brazil is not alone in this corn market, and if this scenario persists until the second semester, Brazilian cereal will lose competitiveness. The United States and Argentina have record harvests, and Ukraine, despite the war, also maintains itself in this market. When the importer seeks corn worldwide, it will not increase the export premium for Brazilian product, since it can buy cereal cheaper in the United States and other markets. Therefore, corn receives a greater impact than soy in price formation via export parity with the dollar decline.

The Brazilian corn market, however, is no longer guided by export parity as soy is. The internal sector carries greater weight. Ethanol plants buy corn in advance, something that animal feed corn normally does not do. With this, the plants provide liquidity to the market, cause producers to sell in advance, and offer prices higher than export parity, says Daniele.

In the second semester, however, if this exchange rate scenario continues, the country may have more difficulty in exporting than it did last year. The export parity of Brazilian product will be a lower value than what is paid internally, making Brazilian cereal expensive compared to that from other exporting markets. For the United States, the scenario of a falling dollar in relation to other currencies is good. Their product becomes cheaper and they had a record harvest. American product prices are more competitive and they have better conditions for supplying cereal. Brazilian corn exports in the second semester will face a challenge they did not have last year. Iran, Brazil's main importer, depends on credit, and the country's economic and financial conditions have been affected by the war. Probably, Brazil will have to seek other markets for cereal, says Daniele.

At the bottom line, the export parity of soy and corn shows that the oilseed suffers less from the effect of the dollar decline. On March 27, soy was quoted at US$ 11.59 per bushel in Chicago, and the premium was negative at US$ 0.05 per bushel in Brazil. The exchange rate was at R$ 5.24. Discounting transportation costs from Rondonópolis (MT) to Santos (SP), the spot soy export parity was R$ 106.66 per sack in the Mato Grosso city. On the 17th of this month, soy rose to US$ 11.67 per bushel in Chicago, and the premium in Brazil, instead of falling, rose to US$ 0.29 per bushel. The exchange rate fell to R$ 4.90.
