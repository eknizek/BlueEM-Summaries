# Owner of Lili Pink among Colombia's top 1,000 companies by sales, profits, and assets

The judicial proceedings carried out by the Attorney General's Office at Lili Pink fashion stores have generated public uproar since, with around five hundred establishments in shopping centers across the country, it is one of the brands closest to consumers. This widespread presence of these stores in the country is reflected in the economic indicators of the business that the owning company, Fast Moda SAS, registers with the authorities, as can be verified in its reports to the Superintendence of Companies regarding income, profits, or assets.

According to the Superintendence of Companies, in 2024, the last year with available public reports, Fast Moda SAS ranks 528th in sales in Colombia. That year the company sold 518 billion pesos, and registered significant growth of 16 percent in its operating income.

With this level of business, its profits reached 5,578 million pesos during the fiscal year, with a 27 percent reduction compared to the previous year. Fast Moda's assets grew 9 percent in 2024 compared to the previous year, reaching 307 billion pesos.

## The profitability of the Lili Pink owner firm

Additionally, profitability that year was 1.82 percent, meaning that for every 100 pesos in assets it obtained profits of 1.82 pesos. Regarding equity—the value resulting from subtracting total debts from assets—this was 31,868 million pesos. Thus, return on equity (which shows the efficiency of the owners' investment) was 8.3 percent.

## The operation against Lili Pink stores

On Monday, the Attorney General's Office conducted a large-scale operation, raiding 62 Lili Pink and Yoi stores in a property forfeiture procedure against alleged money laundering and smuggling. The operation took place in Bogotá and other cities.

The operation was carried out after the net equity of Fast Moda SAS increased 5,531.29 times over the past five years, as in 2020 it was 10 million pesos. The company has five hundred stores.

According to the Attorney General's Office, "the material evidence indicates that the criminal organization would have structured a network composed of importers, distributors, and shell companies, through which it brought merchandise into the country such as clothing, toys, and cosmetic products, which were then distributed in the national market to give the appearance of legality to the operation."
