# Troubling parallels between 2008 and 2026: Is another financial crisis coming?

On September 15, 2008, Bobby Seagull arrived at his office in Canary Wharf, London, shortly before 6 a.m. It was the last time he would need to be punctual. He was an operator at Lehman Brothers, an American bank that was experiencing serious turbulence. "We had seen in the Sunday news, coming from the United States, that they were filing for bankruptcy. We weren't very sure of the implications for us in the United Kingdom. So we were just told to report as usual."

Lehman Brothers employees with cardboard boxes became an image of the 2008 financial crisis. Initially, it was "chaos," Bobby says. "There was no direct communication with our American colleagues. They weren't answering the phone. Some people were grabbing objects, like frames on the wall, and saying: 'They owe me shares.'"

Bobby sensed that disaster could happen and was well prepared. "Curiously, that summer, people noticed a certain unease. I spent my entire vending machine card, [worth] 300 pounds [R$2,021], on chocolates, because I realized that if the vending machine or the bank went broke, my vending machine card would become useless." Bobby, along with thousands of colleagues, put his entire career in a cardboard box and left the building. It was a striking image of the global financial crisis that saw thousands of companies fail and millions of people lose their jobs. It ushered in one of the longest and deepest recessions since World War II.

Now, various warning signs are emerging in the global economy, leading some to question whether we are at the beginning of another financial crisis. What would the next collapse look like? And, considering that international relations in 2026 are more unstable than in 2008, will policymakers have the necessary tools to prevent a collapse?

## Warning Signs

Before the crisis that ravaged the global economy in 2008, there were already warning signs in some parts of the financial system. In 2007, many investments in high-risk mortgage lending in the U.S. lost money, with high default rates among homeowners. Funds managed by Bear Stearns, BNP Paribas, and other banks had to freeze investors' ability to withdraw their money or liquidate the funds completely. These problems served as first warnings of a crisis that would prove much deeper.

As nervousness spread, even banks eventually stopped lending to each other for fear of not getting their money back, creating the so-called credit crisis. This caused a global financial crisis.

Now, let's return to today. Several funds that lend money have reported losses or restricted investors' ability to redeem their investments. BlackRock, Blackstone, Apollo, and Blue Owl faced billions in withdrawal requests from private credit funds—institutions that offer an alternative to traditional banks.

Banking regulators and veterans of the financial sector recognize the similarities between the two crises. Sarah Breeden is Deputy Governor of the Bank of England, the institution responsible for ensuring financial stability in the United Kingdom. She states that the new world of private credit has grown rapidly, has not yet been tested by financial adversity, and is poorly understood.

"There are echoes of the global financial crisis in what we are seeing now," she says. "Private credit has gone from zero to $2.5 trillion in the last 15 to 20 years. There is leverage [borrowed money], there is opacity, there is complexity, there are interconnections with the rest of the financial system. All of this resembles what we saw in the global financial crisis."

She is also concerned that much of the money lent by private credit funds has already been borrowed, creating layers of debt—or leverage—that can amplify any losses. "There is leverage on leverage on leverage. What we want to ensure is that everyone understands how this complex structure of leverage accumulates."

Mohammed El-Erian, chief economic adviser at German financial company Allianz and former CEO of PIMCO, the world's largest bond investor, agrees that the risk of another crisis is being underestimated. "There are certain similarities with 2007 that keep me up at night. The similarities are clear weaknesses in the financial system that are not being properly recognized."

In fact, he argues, it was the restrictions imposed on banks after the crisis that gave rise to this new private credit market. Banks were forced by new regulations to be more cautious. So funds that mimicked banks emerged to fill the gap.
