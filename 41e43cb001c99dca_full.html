# Biomethane trucks now move ethanol waste at Cocal

Cocal, which has two plants in the state of São Paulo and two in Mato Grosso do Sul, has entered into a partnership to replace its entire fleet of trucks that transport vinasse (a residue from ethanol production) at its São Paulo units with trucks powered by biomethane.

The decision by Cocal, an associate of Copersucar, represents one of the largest fleet replacement operations in Brazil aimed at replacing fossil fuel-powered trucks with biomethane-powered vehicles, and should help the company reduce both greenhouse gas emissions and expenses with diesel, whose prices soared due to the Middle East war.

There will be 44 Scania biomethane-powered trucks transporting vinasse, which is precisely the raw material Cocal uses to produce renewable gas, and which is also used as fertilizer in sugarcane fields. With the change, the company will stop consuming 19 million liters of diesel over five years, to use instead the biomethane it produces by biodigesters at its plants.

The deal was finalized with LOTS, a fleet management and logistics company that operates trucks from Scania and other brands and specializes in operations with lower carbon footprints. LOTS, which already manages part of Cocal's truck fleet, will operate the 44 new vinasse transport trucks, which will be leased from Scania. The contract establishes a five-year service provision.

"We are using biomethane to return vinasse to the field, closing a circular economy [system]," emphasizes André Gustavo Alves da Silva, commercial and new products director at Cocal.

The initiative aimed to reduce carbon emissions from the company's own production process (emissions scope 1 of the GHG Protocol), but it should already have an economic impact. The change will also allow Cocal to reduce its diesel consumption—one of the main costs of an agro-industrial operation—and replace it with fuel produced in-house by the company itself.

Cocal does not reveal how much it expects to save with the changes. But, if current diesel prices remain around the same level throughout the duration of the contract with LOTS, the savings would be more than R$100 million over the period.

But the transition involves more than just the replacement of trucks. LOTS invested around R$10 million to adapt Cocal's fuel stations to ensure greater efficiency in vehicle refueling. One adaptation was the investment in chillers, to cool the gas and prevent it from expanding and taking up too much space in the truck cylinders. Adaptations were also made to reduce refueling time.

"Today we can refuel in 18 minutes, but at the beginning it was 45 minutes," Alves says. As the trucks operate non-stop in three shifts, and in the case of those powered by biomethane, will have to refuel once a day, every five minutes makes a difference, he emphasizes.

Even vehicle maintenance will change. "Energy transition goes far beyond buying a truck, putting it at the gas station and turning the key. There are several factors that need to be worked on together with the manufacturer, the transport operator, and the customer for the transition to be viable," says Pedro Silvestrini, vice president of strategy and new business at LOTS.

According to him, the trucks used in this operation did not require any adaptation since they left the factory—unlike those that Copersucar is using to transport sugar from its São Paulo mills to the Port of Santos, which, due to the distance, require extra tanks.

Cocal will also begin testing the use of biomethane-powered trucks to transport sugarcane, but the operation will still be studied, since it is a much heavier load. But Silvestrini is confident that Scania's biomethane models have the power to efficiently replace diesel engines, he says.

According to the vice president of LOTS, who provides fleet management services to several clients within their facilities and on highways, the war in the Middle East has not yet directly resulted in increased demand for trucks powered by alternative fuels—but clients have been talking about the issue, he says.

"Demand has not intensified, but there is an increased concern about energy resilience, about reducing volatility, which has come to the fore," he adds.
