# CPFL's "Invisible" Fintech Now Rivals Digital Banks and Plans to Become a Platform

CPFL Energia has structured a financial business internally that does not appear in traditional analyses of the electricity sector, but delivers numbers like a digital bank. The combination of subsidiaries Alesta, a digital financial institution, and CPFL Total, a payment solutions company, achieved in 2025 an Ebitda of R$ 100 million, a growth of approximately 180% over four years since the pilot was launched. The return on equity (ROE) combined from both operations reached 33.2%, a level that few fintechs manage to deliver.

As the second-largest electricity distributor in Brazil, valued at R$ 59.1 billion on B3 with presence in São Paulo and Rio Grande do Sul, the company is at the maturity point of its embedded financial services platform, which uses the electricity bill as a distribution channel for credit, receivables anticipation, and financial product sales.

The embedded finance model, which originated to solve an internal default problem, is being prepared to be exported to other utilities, including water, gas, and even electricity distributors from other groups. "Just as I embed a distributor from the group, I can embed a distributor from another group," says Fernando Rocha Antonaglia, executive director of Alesta and CPFL Total, to NeoFeed. "And we are not restricted to energy alone. We can imagine water companies, gas companies, and so on. You have a very interesting range in utility lines."

The executive describes the business as "plug and play," ready to be embedded in other companies with an open architecture model—a step that will help reposition CPFL as a white label financial services platform for the infrastructure sector.

Other Brazilian utilities have similar projects, creating fintechs or partnerships to offer credit and payment solutions. One of the differentials of CPFL's operation, according to Antonaglia, is proprietary funding. Alesta operates with approximately R$ 100 million in its own capital, without needing to resort to Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) or external funding. Although Alesta is the only financial subsidiary, Voltz, from Energisa Group, is a fintech that offers digital accounts, cards, and credit and invoice negotiation options, with products aimed at both residential and supplier customers. Both have very similar operational proposals but with different legal and market positioning.

There are other initiatives from companies like Neoenergia and Sabesp with internal platforms for installment payment of overdue bills in partnership with payment companies—the São Paulo sanitation company developed a hub with Bemobi.

## Business Fronts

CPFL's financial operation is sustained by three business fronts, all anchored in the recurring relationship with the electricity customer.

The first and most well-known is the installment payment of overdue bills. Through Alesta, customers with electricity bill debt can extend payment up to 15 times. Between 2021 and 2025, 353,000 contracts were signed, totaling R$ 353 million in installments. Beyond solving the consumer's problem, the operation relieves the distributor's own default rate. In this period, R$ 207 million in debts were settled that, according to the executive, would have a high probability of resulting in energy cuts and additional operational costs.

The second front is receivables anticipation for suppliers. An operation previously handled internally by the corporate area was transferred to Alesta in 2022. Since then, the volume moved has jumped from R$ 933 million to R$ 1.5 billion in 2025, while the base of served suppliers grew from 60 to 600.

The third leg, handled by CPFL Total, is the offer of small insurance and assistance services charged to the electricity bill. Products such as residential insurance, funeral assistance, and residential services are marketed with average tickets between R$ 30 and R$ 40. The operation currently handles 1.3 million monthly charges and maintains a complaint rate below 40 per month.

## Limited Resources

Despite strategic optimism, Antonaglia demonstrates caution when discussing the model's limits. The operation deals with a vulnerable public—defaulted customers, often without relationship to banks or credit cards, seeking to extend debt to avoid electricity cuts.

The expected default rate in Alesta's credit portfolio is around 20%, a high level but priced into the business model. "I need eight out of ten to pay. It's predictable that you have high dependency, but completely absorbed by the volume and rate compound we put in the model," says the executive.

In the insurance and assistance products offering, the math also needs adjustment. Customers with average bills of R$ 120 to R$ 150 cannot commit much more income. For this reason, the group limits the offering to a maximum of two products per customer, with express and renewed authorization.

For the coming years, CPFL is working on accelerating the current portfolio, using artificial intelligence to improve credit scores and prospecting. Today, an installment is completed in about five minutes via mobile, compared to up to 12 minutes at the operation's start.

Another front is diversification. The group has tested new credit lines, such as offering cash lines to customers with good payment history. "The challenge here is to test new business lines for financing beyond the current two," says Antonaglia.

If the plan succeeds, CPFL will consolidate itself as a major infrastructure concessionaire and also as a financial services platform.
