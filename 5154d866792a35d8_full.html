Knowledge gap slows regenerative farming adoption in Brazil

27 April 2026 · 07:00 PM

# Knowledge gap slows regenerative farming in Brazil

Most Brazilian farmers already adopt at least one regenerative practice, but a lack of knowledge continues to limit the expansion and refinement of this type of management, according to a new survey by agtech company Agrosmart.

In a poll conducted among users of its free app BoosterAgro, 78.9% of respondents said they practice no-till farming, 75.3% reported using cover crops, and 66.4% said they adopt crop rotation. However, these practices are not necessarily integrated, often due to insufficient information.

The survey also found that 49.8% of farmers still use harrowing and plowing—methods not recommended under the no-till system, which requires not only direct planting on crop residue and crop rotation but also minimal soil disturbance.

Other regenerative practices remain less widespread. Composting is used by 24.7% of respondents, while integrated crop-livestock-forestry (ICLF) systems are adopted by 11.5% and agroforestry by 11%. By contrast, some more recently developed sustainable technologies, such as solar energy on farms, have gained broader traction, with 45.8% reporting adoption.

Mariana Vasconcelos, CEO of Agrosmart, said some regenerative practices are more widely adopted than others due to differences in how extensively they have been disseminated. "No-till farming has been discussed since the 1970s, while ICLF has gained attention only in the past five years. There is a gap in how widely these practices are understood and discussed," she said.

She added that "there are still few studies demonstrating the financial returns" of integrated systems. In some cases, adoption is driven by market maturity. Solar power is one such example, she noted. "There has been broad dissemination of the benefits of solar energy, and financing has expanded rapidly. In some areas, there are more than ten solar companies, which has helped spread the technology as understanding has grown that financing the energy transition is important. Today, everyone is approaching farmers [to offer solar panels]," she said.

Another advantage of solar power is that both suppliers and farmers have a clear understanding of the return on investment. "When we talk about reducing nitrogen fertilizers in favor of biominerals or green fertilization, there is far less information available on productivity gains and required investment," she added.

For solutions where returns are less clear and less widely communicated, Vasconcelos argues that incentives are needed to support adoption, such as premium payments or guaranteed purchase agreements.

Farmers also favor some form of economic incentive, even though they recognize the agronomic benefits of regenerative practices. Among those already adopting such practices, 70.8% said soil fertility improved, and 58.3% reported productivity gains. However, 50% of respondents said they still struggle to measure the exact impact of these practices.

As a result, most producers seek more immediate financial returns. According to the survey, 62.2% said they would adopt regenerative agriculture if market conditions were favorable, 17.9% would do so if they received a price premium above 10%, and 6.6% would adopt if the premium were up to 10%.

In Vasconcelos's view, the findings show that farmers are seeking "economic security" to transition their production models.

The survey covered a diverse sample of producers across 19 Brazilian states and 519 municipalities, ranging from large-scale to smallholders and spanning more than 90 different crops.
