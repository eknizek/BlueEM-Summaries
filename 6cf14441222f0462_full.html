# The unanimous decision that doesn't unify the market: What's behind Banco de la República's interest rate pause?

The Board of Directors of Banco de la República's decision to maintain the interest rate at 11.25 percent—when most expected another increase—not only surprised the market but also immediately sparked debate among analysts about what the Board really meant by that pause, following the aggressive adjustments of January and March, 100 basis points per month.

For some analysts consulted, it is a signal of technical prudence. For others, a truce amid political tensions. And for a third group, a mixture of both positions.

From the more technical front, the predominant interpretation is that the Bank decided to buy time. It came from an aggressive cycle of increases—up to 200 basis points so far this year—that, according to several analysts, already gave it room to pause and calmly observe what new data would show. This is the view from BBVA and Bancolombia, where they agree that the Issuer opted for a cautious stance amid high uncertainty and with still mixed signals from the economy.

Inflation, in fact, remains a cause for concern. Not only did it rebound in March (5.56 percent, according to Dane's report), but expectations remain outside the target range—between 6.2 and 6.4 percent for the end of 2026—which requires maintaining restrictive monetary policy. But at the same time, the economy shows no clear signs of weakness. Growth and employment remain relatively solid, which allows the Issuer to maintain high rates without needing to raise them immediately.

Even Banco Popular partially aligns with that reading, considering the pause correct because the previous adjustment provided space to observe, in an environment where information is still insufficient to make a definitive decision. In fact, analysts at this institution warn that inflationary pressures remain in force—from oil prices to the possible El Niño phenomenon—and that the rate could rise again in the second half.

That is, the pause is valid, but not definitive. In that scenario moves much of the market consensus, so a pause does not mean the cycle has ended.

However, differences begin to appear in the interpretation of the background to the decision.

**Other Perspectives**

For analysts like César Pabón, Executive Director of Economic Research at Corficolombiana, unanimity in Thursday's decision should not be interpreted as a political concession, but as a signal of independence and institutional maturity. In his view, the board achieved a "partial equilibrium" in a complex environment, supported by the fact that it had already made a significant adjustment in previous months.

Luis Fernando Mejía, president of Lumen Economic Intelligence, goes in the same direction, noting that more than complacency from the Issuer, what is observed is a convergence within the Board around the need to await new data. Unanimity, in that sense, reinforces the idea of technical consensus rather than external pressure.

But not everyone sees it that way. Felipe Campos, Investment and Strategy Manager at Grupo Alianza, has a different reading: a kind of "truce" between the Bank and the Government. From his perspective, what was achieved was an implicit non-aggression agreement for two months, in which neither party took its position to the extreme.
