# 'Innovation is not Just Technology, It's Being Open to What Works,' Says Luiza Trajano

**Interview with Luiza Helena Trajano**

**President of the Board of Magazine Luiza and Women's Group of Brazil**

Luiza Helena Trajano, president of the Board of Magazine Luiza and Women's Group of Brazil, has always had her eyes on innovation. The theme has been her focus of attention since the time she was leading the day-to-day operations of the chain of physical furniture and appliance stores, founded in the city of Franca, in the interior of São Paulo State. The company generated R$ 64.7 billion last year across its entire ecosystem and is now one of the country's retail giants in both physical and digital commerce.

"Innovation is a commodity: either you innovate or you fall behind," says Luiza. She recalls that Kodak, which in the past was synonymous with cameras, did not innovate and ceased to exist as a brand.

A conversation with the Women's Group of Brazil, led by Luiza Trajano, is part of the programming for São Paulo Innovation Week. The event takes place from May 13-15 at Arena Pacaembu and at Fundação Armando Alvares Penteado (FAAP).

According to Luiza, Magazine Luiza has always been open to changing cycles and innovating. In 1992, for example, the company created the first electronic stores without physically exposed merchandise, when e-commerce wasn't even discussed in Brazil. The traditional Liquidação Fantástica (Fantastic Clearance), held in the first week of January, which still exists today and changed the retail calendar, is also cited by the businesswoman as an example of innovation.

With the retailer's entry into online commerce, Lu was born—the world's largest digital influencer, award-winning at the Cannes Festival. She came to humanize electronic commerce.

Last year, the company created Galeria Magalu, a reinvention of the old department store. The unit that occupies the former Livraria Cultura location at Conjunto Nacional in São Paulo seeks to unite the physical store with the online experience. Recently, the company launched its own cloud and was the first national company to create a cloud for data storage available to the market.

Leading the Women's Group of Brazil, Luiza coordinates the first meeting of women in professions, scheduled for August. Currently, there are only 18% of women in politics. The goal is for women to occupy 50% of senior positions in various areas in the medium term.

"It's an innovation when we have 30%, 40% of women. Innovation is not just technology, it's being open to what works."

## Main excerpts from the interview:

**How do you see innovation and why is it so important for retail?**

Magazine Luiza has always been very open to innovation. In 1992, we created the virtual store, when nobody talked about digital. Then, in 2001, Frederico (Trajano, CEO of Magalu) created the website. We created those clearance sales that changed the January calendar, with long lines, which still remain after more than 30 years. Today I was reading about Kodak, which had 142,000 employees in 2007. It didn't innovate and no longer exists as a brand. And Kodak was synonymous with cameras. Innovation is no longer a requirement. It's a commodity. Either you innovate or you fall behind. And in retail it's the same thing. I think innovation took a bit longer to arrive in retail in general. Because retail is always very complex. Back then, you had to wait six months, eight months to implement a technology system. But retailers are very fast because retail demands that we be fast. So many things were done.

**How did the idea of the virtual store come about in 1992, when e-commerce didn't even exist in the country?**

In the past, Casas Pernambucanas had many stores in small interior cities of São Paulo—small cities I mean those with less than 100,000 inhabitants, 50,000 inhabitants. Suddenly, Pernambucanas started closing stores in these cities. And Magazine Luiza was strong in the region, in Franca, Ribeirão Preto, with stores of 800, 1,500 square meters. How would I open my stores if another company was closing? So we had the idea, using the slogan: 'Magazine brings to your city the electronic store Luiza'. We didn't even call it digital. The second electronic store was in Igarapava, then Batatais. But the most important was in Cássia, Minas Gerais, a city that still has 15,000 inhabitants today. Because one of my aunts, who was a partner in the company, had a farm there and people asked for a store. And all three stores still exist today, can you believe it? Of course, today it has another name—it's called Magalu virtual store.

**Did the electronic store emerge from a market opportunity?**

I had seen something similar in London. And we created it together. The store had a promotion center that the community itself generated revenue from, we brought technology courses, cooking courses with the Walita cooking school (small appliances brand) to have that dynamic. One of the risks we ran was comparison with other cities. Magazine Luiza brings to this city a store that doesn't even have products on display. Franca has a big store, Ribeirão Preto has three, four big stores. So we did educational marketing for three months. Magazine Luiza is arriving, it won't have products on display, but it's the store of the year 2000. And we did marketing both for rural areas, for city people, and for school children. Today we have 200 stores of this type with the name Virtual Store Luiza. We had difficult moments, because everything that is very modern and very ahead of its time has difficult moments, but it's a legitimate survival. But today this store has technology and it's not the way it was back then?

About two years later multimedia came to Brazil. So we had a CD recording center in Ribeirão Preto's Distribution Center, which already recorded the products and sent everything via computer. I pioneered multimedia because of the electronic store.

**How does the company treat innovation and how was that leap to Galeria Magalu?**

Galeria is a reinvention of the old department store. We are very open to changing cycles and innovating. We have today the world's largest digital influencer, a company that was born in the interior, that spent almost 50 years in physical operations and managed to have laboratories, create now the first completely Brazilian cloud for the market. We've always been open. Frederico (Trajano, CEO) has always been very focused on digital.
