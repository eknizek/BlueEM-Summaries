# Latin America Leads Recovery Among Emerging Markets; Goldman Sachs Highlights Brazil

The recovery of emerging market stocks following the April 7 ceasefire in the Middle East has been marked by clear regional differentiation. Latin America — along with Northern Asia and emerging Europe — is leading the recovery, in a context where capital flows are returning and valuations are adjusting after the volatility of the conflict.

In this context, Goldman Sachs strategists state that "emerging market stocks have recovered (+7%), led by a 'relief' rally in cyclical sectors, recovering most losses since the low to remain +12% year-to-date." The American bank further highlights that the recovery has been even more intense than in developed markets — excluding the United States. "Northern Asia, emerging Europe, and Latin America led the recovery, while oil-importing segments of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) and India lagged behind," the report stated.

Strategists consider this scenario will persist given current disruptions in the energy sector. Latin American markets are among the best-performing blocs in the post-conflict recovery, with gains around 5% to 6% in local currency in countries such as Brazil, while Chile exceeds 8% since April 7.

This dynamic fits into a broader pattern in which markets that suffered the largest corrections during the volatility period have led the recovery.

**Latin America Leads Recovery**

Among emerging markets, Latin America has reached new highs for the year, driven by Brazil's performance and the positive effect of commodity prices. The report highlights that "Latin America (led by Brazil) and emerging Europe (led by Hungary and Turkey) have reached new highs year-to-date," consolidating their leadership in the recovery phase.

Investor behavior also reflects a significant shift. After cumulative outflows of approximately $80 billion during the conflict period, the market registered net inflows of approximately $9 billion in the last week, alongside a valuation of MSCI EM of 11.6 times future earnings, below its ten-year average. In this context, the company maintains an optimistic view on this emerging asset. "Valuations and positioning have been significantly readjusted, and profit growth is likely to be solid," analysts stated.

Goldman Sachs has a profit growth forecast of 23% for 2026, of which 16 percentage points come from demand related to artificial intelligence.

**Brazil, Main Bet in the Region**

Preference for Latin America is concentrated in Brazil, where favorable factors converge both externally and internally. "In Latin America, we continue to prefer Brazil due to the commodity momentum," the bank's analysts affirm, in a context where the energy sector has accumulated gains of approximately 25% since the beginning of the conflict.

The recent performance of the Brazilian market has been mixed. While commodity-related sectors benefit from the price environment, the domestic stock market has suffered pressure due to high interest rates. Still, expectations are positive. "We expect Brazilian stocks to outperform the market given that they present valuations that, in our view, are attractive relative to the current level of rates, with expectations for further interest rate cuts."

Expectations regarding monetary policy reinforce this scenario. The bank's economists project a Selic rate of 12.75% by year-end, a level that could serve as a catalyst for valuation appreciation of rate-sensitive assets in the local market. This adjustment in rates occurs in a context of high sensitivity of the Brazilian stock market to financial conditions, which amplifies the potential for revaluation if expected rate cuts materialize.

**Flows, Rates, and Market Perspectives**

The recovery of emerging market stocks is also due to partial stabilization of rate markets after the energy shock. The ceasefire reduced the risk of new spikes in oil prices, allowing relief in local yield curves, especially in economies with initially higher sales. Still, the scenario continues to be marked by inflationary pressures. "The path is more difficult for yields to fully return to pre-conflict levels, due to increased energy prices and inflation in the spot market," the report stated, which limits the margin for additional easing in the short term.

In Latin America, this context translates into increased inflationary pressure, while in Asia the impact was partially contained by fiscal measures. However, the combination of high real rates and room for cuts maintains the region's relative attractiveness in the context of emerging markets.

With a target of 1,680 points for MSCI EM in 12 months, equivalent to a 20% return from the start of 2026, market attention is now focused on the evolution of corporate profits, the trajectory of energy prices, and the materialization of interest rate cuts — variables that will define the sustainability of Latin America's leadership in the current recovery cycle.
