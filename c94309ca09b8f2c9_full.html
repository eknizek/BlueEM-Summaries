# Latin America leads emerging market recovery, and Goldman sees Brazil as standout

The recovery of emerging market stocks following the April 7 ceasefire in the Middle East has been marked by clear regional differentiation. Latin America — along with North Asia and emerging Europe — is leading the recovery, in a context where capital flows return and valuations adjust after conflict-related volatility.

In this context, Goldman Sachs strategists state that "emerging market stocks have recovered (+7%), led by a 'relief' rally in cyclical sectors, recovering most losses since the low to stand at +12% year-to-date". The American bank further highlights that the recovery has been even more intense than in developed markets — excluding the United States.

"North Asia, emerging Europe and Latin America led the recovery, while oil-importing segments of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN) and India lagged behind," the report stated.

Strategists believe this scenario will persist given current disruptions in the energy sector. Latin American markets are among the best-performing blocs in the post-conflict recovery, with gains around 5% to 6% in local currency in countries like Brazil, while Chile surpasses 8% since April 7.

This dynamic fits into a broader pattern in which markets that suffered the largest corrections during the volatility period have led the recovery.

## Latin America leads the rebound

Within emerging markets, Latin America has hit new highs for the year, driven by Brazil's performance and the positive effect of commodity prices.

The report highlights that "Latin America (led by Brazil) and emerging Europe (led by Hungary and Turkey) have reached new highs year-to-date," consolidating their leadership in the recovery phase.

Investor behavior also reflects a significant shift. After cumulative outflows of approximately $80 billion during the conflict period, the market registered net inflows of approximately $9 billion in the last week, alongside an MSCI EM valuation of 11.6 times forward earnings, below its ten-year average.

In this context, the firm maintains an optimistic view on this emerging asset. "Valuations and positioning have been significantly readjusted, and earnings growth is likely to be solid," analysts stated.

Goldman Sachs has a 23% earnings growth forecast for 2026, of which 16 percentage points come from AI-related demand.

## Brazil, the region's main bet

The preference for Latin America concentrates on Brazil, where favorable external and internal factors converge. "In Latin America, we continue to prefer Brazil, given the commodity tailwind," the bank's analysts state, in a context where the energy sector has accumulated gains of around 25% since the conflict began.

The recent performance of the Brazilian market has been mixed. While commodity-linked sectors benefit from the pricing scenario, the domestic stock market has faced pressure due to high interest rates. Still, expectations are positive.

"We expect Brazilian stocks to outperform the market given they present valuations that, in our view, are attractive relative to the current level of rates, and with expectations of further interest rate cuts."

Expectations regarding monetary policy reinforce this scenario. The bank's economists project a Selic rate of 12.75% by year-end, a level that could serve as a catalyst for appreciation of rate-sensitive assets in the local market. This rate adjustment occurs in a context of high sensitivity of the Brazilian stock market to financial conditions, which amplifies the revaluation potential if expected cuts materialize.

## Flows, rates and market perspectives

The recovery of emerging market stocks is also due to partial stabilization of rate markets following the energy shock. The ceasefire reduced the risk of new oil price spikes, allowing relief in local yield curves, especially in economies with higher initial sales.

Still, the scenario continues to be marked by inflationary pressures. "The path is more difficult for yields to fully return to pre-conflict levels due to increased energy prices and spot market inflation," the report stated, which limits room for additional easing in the short term.

In Latin America, this context translates into increased inflationary pressure, while in Asia the impact was partially contained by fiscal measures. However, the combination of high real rates and margin for cuts maintains the region's relative attractiveness within emerging markets.

With a 1,680-point target for MSCI EM in 12 months, equivalent to a 20% return from the start of 2026, market attention is now focused on corporate earnings evolution, energy price trajectory, and materialization of interest rate cuts — variables that will define the sustainability of Latin America's leadership in the current recovery cycle.
