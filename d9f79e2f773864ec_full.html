# Queqén port conflict resolved after more than 15 days of strike

After more than two weeks of paralysis, the labor conflict that had virtually halted activity at Queqén Port has finally been resolved, ending the significant losses for the entire agro-export chain. The protest, led by self-organized cargo transporters, was primarily focused on updating freight rates due to sharp increases in operational costs, especially diesel fuel. The protest had resulted in blockades of port access, delays in loading ships, and a virtual shutdown of the terminal during peak harvest season.

According to estimates released by the export sector, the measure had left at least 17 ships waiting for cargo and approximately 347,600 tons of grain delayed, with losses calculated at around US$ 300 million. Some cargo even began to be diverted to other ports and even to Brazil due to lack of operational predictability.

The resolution came this Friday following a dialogue meeting held at the Queqén Port Management Consortium, where the parties reached an agreement that allowed the strike to be lifted. The understanding establishes a 16% increase over the last prevailing rate, applicable to the entire Queqén Port hinterland. Additionally, it was agreed that administrative expenses cannot exceed 2%, a significant reduction compared to the values that had been generating controversy.

The agreement also established that rates for the parking areas at Sitio 0 de Queqén S.A. will remain unchanged. It was also stipulated that, 24 hours after each truck with a quota enters the port zone, if the unit remains stopped, the transporter must pay the corresponding storage fee.

The agreement was reached with the mediation of interim president of the Management Consortium Mariano Carrillo; national deputy Jimena López; and the provincial director of Permits and Licensing for Cargo Transport, Miguel Ángel Bettili. Local media also noted the participation of agricultural representatives, grain traders, and operational port authorities.

With this resolution, normal truck traffic is expected to resume in the coming hours and export activity is expected to return to normal, bringing relief to one of the main grain terminals in southeastern Buenos Aires. The Queqén conflict had remained the last active focal point of a protest that had already been resolved at most Buenos Aires ports, such as Bahía Blanca. Its prolongation affected not only exporters and grain traders but also all regional logistics and the international reputation of the Argentine port system during peak commercial season.
