# Quequén Port conflict resolved after more than 15 days of strike

After more than two weeks of paralysis, the labor dispute that had practically halted activity at Quequén Port has finally been resolved, ending strong losses for the entire agro-export chain. The dispute, led by self-organized cargo transporters, centered on updating freight rates in response to a sharp increase in operational costs, especially diesel fuel. The protest had resulted in port access blockades, delays in ship loading, and a virtual shutdown of the terminal during peak harvest season.

According to estimates released by the export sector, the measure had left at least 17 ships waiting for cargo and approximately 347,600 tons of grain delayed, with losses calculated at around US$ 300 million. Some shipments even began being diverted to other ports and even to Brazil due to lack of operational predictability.

The resolution came this Friday following a dialogue meeting at the Quequén Port Management Consortium, where the parties reached an agreement that allowed them to lift the strike. The understanding establishes a 16% increase over the last applicable rate, applicable to all of Quequén Port's hinterland. Additionally, it was agreed that administrative expenses cannot exceed 2%, a significant reduction from the values that had been generating controversy.

It was also defined that tariffs corresponding to the parking areas of Sitio 0 de Quequén S.A. will remain unchanged. It was also stipulated that 24 hours after each truck with a quota enters the port zone, if the unit remains stopped, the transporter must pay the corresponding storage fee.

The agreement was reached with the mediation of the interim president of the Management Consortium, Mariano Carrillo; national deputy Jimena López; and the provincial director of Cargo Transport Permits and Licensing, Miguel Ángel Bettili. Local media also noted the participation of representatives from agriculture, grain handlers, and port operational authorities.

With this resolution, truck entry and export activity are expected to begin normalizing in the coming hours, bringing relief to one of the main grain terminals in southeastern Buenos Aires. The Quequén conflict had become the last active focal point of a protest that had already been resolved in most Buenos Aires ports, such as Bahía Blanca. Its prolongation affected not only exporters and grain handlers, but also the entire regional logistics and the international reputation of the Argentine port system during peak commercial season.
