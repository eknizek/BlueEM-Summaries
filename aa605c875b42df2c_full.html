From Brazil to Mexico: How the IMF Sees Iran War Impacts on Latin America

# From Brazil to Mexico: How the IMF Sees Iran War Impacts on Latin America

The war in the Middle East is deepening the differences between major countries in Latin America and the Caribbean. While oil-producing nations benefit from elevated energy prices — despite facing challenges — other economies are experiencing contractions in activity and show greater vulnerability to the conflict.

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) points out that oil-producing countries such as Argentina, Brazil, Colombia, Ecuador, Guyana, Trinidad and Tobago, and Venezuela are benefiting from higher energy prices. "The commodity shock is strengthening their balance of payments, supporting growth and helping public finances," the IMF states in its report "The Middle East War Will Have an Uneven Impact on the Western Hemisphere."

The document explains that although some of these countries also face more restrictive financial conditions, many of them should overall record net economic gains. Nevertheless, the IMF warns that "even in these oil-producing countries, it should not be overlooked that the most vulnerable will be most affected by increases in energy and food prices."

Beyond the possible winners, "for others a very different story is unfolding," the IMF says in its report. "The conflict has clearly negative economic impacts on both economic activity and the population."

Caribbean economies dependent on tourism are expected to be the hardest hit. Their debt is high and their net energy imports are significant, representing on average about 6% of Gross Domestic Product (GDP). Central America is also vulnerable to high energy prices and, in several cases, faces fiscal limitations that restrict its capacity to respond. The IMF notes, however, that advances in renewable energy in some countries offer some relief.

Countries with current account deficits and high dependence on external financing — including some energy exporters — also face difficulties, with higher financing costs and reduced access to markets in an environment of lower global risk appetite. "The war reduces investors' appetite for risk," the IMF emphasizes in its report.

According to the IMF, the effects of this scenario will vary between countries in the region and will depend largely on the duration of the conflict and associated disruptions. On Saturday (18th), Iran transmitted a message to ships in the Strait of Hormuz region that the maritime passage was closed again, one day after the country announced the complete reopening of the strait — maintaining an uncertain scenario.

## Inflationary Shock

While the impact on economic activity should vary widely among countries, the effect on inflation will be more uniform, the IMF assesses. "Inflation will be higher for everyone. The region will face much higher costs for fuel, transportation, food and other inputs," the IMF states in the document.

Among the countries analyzed in the region, inflation projected for 2026 is highest in Venezuela (220%), Bolivia (26.1%), and Argentina (25%). In Brazil, the estimate is 4.3%; in Chile, 3.6%; in Paraguay, 3.5%; in Ecuador, 3%; and in Peru, 2.5%. In Colombia, the projection is 6.3%, and in Uruguay, 4.5%.

In Central America, inflation would reach 4.8% in Honduras, 3.9% in Guatemala, 2.5% in El Salvador, and 1% in Costa Rica. In the Caribbean, the Dominican Republic would register 4.5%.

For Latin America and the Caribbean as a whole, the IMF projects inflation of 6.6% in 2026 and 4.2% in 2027. The fund warns that increased inflation will create difficulties especially for lower-income families, who are least able to absorb the rise in the cost of essential goods.

In this sense, the IMF assesses that the conflict represents a renewed and highly unpredictable challenge at a time when the region was still working to recover from the effects of COVID-19.

## Better Prepared Countries

The IMF indicates that countries with solid macroeconomic and institutional frameworks will be better positioned to absorb the shock — especially those with anchored inflation, fiscal discipline, and low debt.

Available fiscal space should be used cautiously, while more limited countries may be forced to tighten their fiscal and monetary policies. Energy exporters with fragile fundamentals should save extraordinary gains, the report suggests.

Central banks, which have already contained post-pandemic inflation, now must again ensure price stability. On the fiscal front, the IMF considers it essential to preserve progress in eliminating widespread subsidies and to resist political pressures to control prices administratively. Spending should be directed at vulnerable families, farmers, and small businesses.

Given the high level of indebtedness, "the region has little room to continue expanding fiscal deficits," the fund states. According to the report, the priority should be to reduce less essential spending or increase revenue collection from companies and families with greater ability to pay.

## Consequences Beyond the War

Oxford Economics consultancy predicts that the Middle East conflict will reduce global growth, raise inflation, and maintain disruptions in the energy sector, with effects expected to extend even after a ceasefire.

The consultancy's global GDP growth projection has been reduced by 0.4 percentage points since early March, to 2.4% in 2026. "The fragile ceasefire apparently reduces the risk of a worse scenario, but even if the truce holds, it will take time for energy production and maritime traffic to return to normal levels," said Ben May, Director of Global Macroeconomic Research at Oxford Economics.

Globally, the IMF cut its growth projection for this year to 3.1%, compared with 3.3% predicted in January, due to the impact of the Middle East war and the oil crisis. "Global prospects have darkened abruptly following the start of the Middle East war," the fund says. "Before the war, we were prepared to improve our global growth forecast," the institution added.
