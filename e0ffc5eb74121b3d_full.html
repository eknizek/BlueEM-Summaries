# Pernambuco Startup Seeks R$50M Revenue with "Agentic Assembly Line"

Based at Porto Digital, Volund uses agents in its own operations and has even created a Department of Human Resources and Artificial Intelligence

A Pernambuco startup wants to prove that it's possible to build complex corporate software in days, not months. Volund, officially founded in February 2026 and based at Porto Digital in Recife, was born to operate with what it calls "agentic engineering": a model in which artificial intelligence agents are not merely support tools, but actively participate in the entire software development cycle, from requirements gathering to final documentation. The revenue target is R$50 million, with a team expected to reach 40 or 50 people by year-end — currently ten employees with another ten in the hiring process.

"Vibe coding is cool, but it's not enough to operationalize a company. There's a technical gap. For example, how do you test at the same speed? Or, if you don't test, you don't launch or you launch poorly finished products. What we realized was that we needed to create an agentic assembly line, end-to-end," says Vinícius Guedes, CEO of Volund, in an interview with Startups.

## From Beyond to Volund

The company is an offshoot of Extreme Group, a technology ecosystem founded 12 years ago with around 2,000 employees and annual revenue of R$600 million. Vinícius also heads Beyond, another group company, created in 2019 with a focus on cloud technology and now operating as a venture builder. The model took shape within Beyond. In March 2025, the team created an initial version of the agentic assembly line and used it to rebuild all of the company's internal products. The process was refined until it became clear there was market demand, and that it made sense to create a separate company just for this.

"Unlike Beyond, where we're making an effort to transform into AI-first, remodeling processes, Volund is born in a logic where all processes were built to be operationalized by AI," Vinícius points out.

## Virtual Employees

In practice, the model functions like an automated production line. AI agents analyze meetings and documentation to gather requirements, generate technical and commercial proposals, decompose scope into tasks, write and review code, execute tests, and produce delivery documentation — all under human supervision at strategic points.

The stated result is an operation up to 15 times more productive than a conventional software factory. Projects requiring teams of eight to ten people over months are delivered with two to four professionals in strategic roles. Internally, the company also operates with an AI agent called Vitória, a kind of virtual chief of staff with access to emails, meetings, and complete operational context.

"She has the autonomy to read projects and, based on what we've taught her, she can provide answers that have always been correct so far," the CEO recounts. The idea is that she formally occupies an executive position within the company's organizational chart. Volund has also created a department called RHA (Human Resources and Artificial), based on the premise that in future work, both dimensions will be inseparable.

## Not SaaS

According to the CEO, Volund positions itself as a services company, not SaaS. The charging model is per delivery, and customers can choose to keep the system internally or contract for continuous evolution.

"Customers will continue to have a need for robust technology solutions. Now it's more viable to have a personalized solution, which wouldn't have been possible two years ago," Vinícius argues.

Being within the Extreme Group ecosystem is treated as a structural advantage: the company inherits relationships with major clients, mapped pain points, and immediate distribution capacity. The company currently has eight projects underway with five clients, focused on the public, healthcare, and financial sectors, and is looking at expansion in Latin America and the United States.

The startup is also open to investment rounds, but without urgency. "The business is already running on its own, but we understand that to take the next steps, raising a round could be part of our strategy. Right now, demand for the service has been so great that we're more focused on maturing our operations," says the CEO.
