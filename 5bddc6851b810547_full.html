# One of the largest oil market shocks hits aviation fuel and puts aviation on alert, warn S&P and IATA

The global oil market is experiencing one of the most disruptive episodes in recent years, according to Debnil Chowdhury, Executive Director, Head of Americas and Europe Fuels and Refining at S&P Global Commodity Insights, who argues that the combination of geopolitical tensions and structural changes in the industry has generated "one of the largest shocks in the global oil market."

During the Wings of Change Americas (WOCA) 2026 event taking place in Santiago, Chile this week, the executive explained that this scenario responds not only to circumstantial factors but to a deeper transformation in the supply chain. "We are seeing refinery closures in places like the west coast of the United States and Europe. This is increasing the distance between where the product is demanded and where the supply comes from," he stated.

Although part of this scenario was already on the radar, its timing amplifies the impact. "We had already anticipated that it would be very important to monitor refinery closures globally and how that impacts the market," he added.

**Jet fuel: the weakest link**

Aviation fuel has been the most affected by this disruption. Unlike other derivatives, its behavior has decoupled from traditional indicators. "Normally, diesel is a good indicator of where jet fuel prices are heading, but in this case that relationship has been distorted," Chowdhury explained.

The underlying issue is structural: Latin America and Europe increasingly depend on imports, while Asia and the Middle East concentrate production for domestic consumption. "This means that Latin America depends increasingly on imports, and in that context the United States—especially the Gulf Coast—is a key supplier," he indicated.

However, that supply faces additional pressures. "There is more competition for those supplies due to refinery closures in the United States (due to the transition toward clean energy, high regulatory adaptation costs, and projections of lower long-term fossil fuel demand)," he noted, recalling that the east coast has lost nearly 50% of its capacity.

The result: a decline of nearly 20% in global jet fuel supply and a contraction of up to 42% in maritime trade, which worsens the shortage.

**A logistics crisis with prolonged effects**

The shock responds not only to production but also to disruptions in key routes. The closure of corridors like the Suez Canal has complicated the flow of fuels from Asia and the Middle East to Europe and the Americas. "This implies a disconnect between the physical market and prices," Chowdhury warned.

Even in a normalization scenario, effects would not be immediate. "Even if the Strait of Hormuz reopens completely, it would take about 20 days to clear the accumulation of ships," he explained.

Adding to this is geopolitical uncertainty. "There are differences on key issues such as uranium enrichment, which could reignite the conflict," he added.

The impact is already reflected in inventories and prices. While initially stable, a significant decline is now projected.
