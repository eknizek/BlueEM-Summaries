# Crime and Insecurity: The Drag on Economic Growth in Latin America

Insecurity in Latin America has become a factor that directly impacts the economic performance and democracies of countries in the region. According to data cited by Rebecca Bill Chavez, CEO and president of the Inter-American Dialogue, the phenomenon represents a cost close to 3.5% of regional GDP. This is reflected in lower levels of investment and impacts on key sectors such as tourism, particularly in the Caribbean, Central America, and Mexico.

The expert shared her views on the eve of the event "Colombia: Addressing Citizen Insecurity from the Subnational Level," which will take place on Monday, April 27, 2026 at the University of Los Andes, organized by the Inter-American Dialogue, El Tiempo, and Portafolio.

**What are your expectations for the event that will take place in Bogotá next Monday?**

First, I should say that I am now president and CEO of the Inter-American Dialogue, but previously I worked as Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense for the Western Hemisphere in the Barack Obama administration. During my time at the Pentagon, I had the opportunity to work closely with Colombia's Ministry of Defense, during Juan Manuel Santos' government. It was one of the highlights of my career.

That experience allowed me to closely observe the advances the country achieved in terms of security. However, I must also point out that the situation has deteriorated in recent years. That is why the event in Bogotá represents a very important opportunity to bring together key actors and analyze not only the diagnosis but also possible solutions to these challenges that today affect Colombia and the entire region.

**What is the panorama you perceive and what do you think is behind this deterioration?**

I believe Colombia is not alone in this situation. Citizen insecurity is one of the largest and most urgent challenges in the hemisphere. It is not solely a public security problem; it is also a profound challenge for economic development and democratic resilience. From the Inter-American Dialogue, we launched an initiative called Hemispheric Security precisely because Latin America and the Caribbean register the highest homicide rate in the world. Furthermore, they present very high levels of organized crime. This is not limited to homicides: it includes kidnappings, extortion, and gender-based violence. It is a complex phenomenon that impacts multiple dimensions of social and economic life.

**What else have you found in your analyses?**

We have identified that insecurity has become the primary concern of citizens in several countries. For example, we saw this clearly in the recent elections in Chile, where the issue was at the center of public debate. It also occurs in Costa Rica, a country that historically had lower levels of violence, but where now citizens express growing concern. This reflects that the problem is not isolated or temporary, but structural and regional. The perception of insecurity is shaping politics and electoral decisions.

**Which countries generate more concern in this context?**

Central America has always been a region of high concern, as has the Caribbean. In the Andean region, Colombia continues to be an important focus, and there is also concern about what is happening in Venezuela. There we see the presence of multiple criminal groups, including FARC dissidents, the ELN, and other organizations that not only engage in drug trafficking but also in activities such as illegal mining. A critical problem in some countries is that the State does not have effective control over parts of its territory. That is clearly the case in Venezuela, and it represents an enormous challenge for governance.

**What impact does this situation have on economic development?**

The impact is significant. It is estimated that insecurity costs the region around 3.5% of GDP. This directly affects variables such as investment, economic growth, and job creation. Additionally, there are sectors particularly hard hit. In the Caribbean, in parts of Central America, and in Mexico, insecurity affects tourism, which is a key source of income. It also influences private investment decisions, as companies assess risk before expanding or establishing themselves in certain territories.

**Beyond local problems, what role do criminal networks that operate in several countries play?**

That is a fundamental point. Organized crime does not respect borders, so solutions cannot be exclusively national either. A coordinated regional response is required. We need more collaboration between countries, more intelligence sharing and joint strategies. That is why the conference we will hold will be multisectorial. We will have mayors, representatives from the private sector and civil society. The idea is to build comprehensive responses. In Latin America and the Caribbean, more than half of homicides are linked to organized crime. Furthermore, one in five citizens considers insecurity their primary concern. The human cost is enormous and its effects extend to all areas.

**What other effects do you detect from insecurity?**

Beyond economic impact, there is a direct effect on democracy. Although around 60% of the population considers democracy to be the best form of government, it is not a particularly high level. Almost 40% of citizens are not satisfied with how it functions. This is very much related to the perception that democratic governments have failed to guarantee public security. In that context, there is growing concern about the resilience of democratic institutions in the region.
