# Nubank Disputes Receivables with Merchants in Entrepay Case

Entrepay's liquidation exposes gap in payment arrangement by revealing duplicate billing

Since the end of March, Entrepay has faced a flood of lawsuits from companies that did not receive payments made by consumers through their card machines at the point of sale. In addition to merchants, financial institutions that advanced payments to the acquirer also did not receive their money back.

Although it is the first case of liquidation of a card acquirer by the Central Bank, the episode calls into question aspects of the current Brazilian payment arrangement, by exposing duplication — or even triplication — of charges and the lack of guarantees that debt will be honored when one of the links breaks, according to market participants heard by Pipeline.

The most emblematic case is Nu Financeira, which is claiming R$ 74 million for advancing funds to Entrepay. In the normal flow, the issuing bank is the one that has the credit relationship with the merchant and commits to transferring resources to the acquirer — such as Entrepay, Cielo, Rede, Stone, GetNet and PagBank — within D+30 or D+27, which then passes them on to the merchant.

When contacted by the reporting team, Nubank explained that it "carried out some receivable advance operations with Entrepay, within the scope of payment arrangement rules that are regulated, with specific operational and legal safeguards. This is an isolated case involving a single acquirer, with no material impact to the company."

"Nubank reiterates its commitment to the stability of the Brazilian Payment System and to the preservation of the regulated arrangements that sustain it. The institution believes that strict compliance with agreements between the links in the chain is fundamental for sector balance, and adopts the legal measures necessary to ensure the integrity and credibility of these relationships," the statement concludes.

Another bank with funds to receive is Daycoval, which said through its press office that it has requested from the liquidator, at the administrative level, the restitution of amounts "resulting from assignment operations in which the bank is the legitimate assignee," without confirming the amount, estimated at R$ 13 million by the market. South Korean KEB Hana (R$ 15 million) did not respond.

The problem is compounded because there are at least two parties claiming to own the same resource: the bank that purchased Entrepay's receivables and the merchant who should have received them. In many transactions, there are also sub-acquirers in the middle of the chain, to give capillarity to the acquirer.

On the list in São Paulo courts are: PJBank, TAL Soluções em Pagamentos, Parcelamos Tudo, Keypay and NetCred, the only one of them willing to comment on the matter. "NetCred's action charges Entrepay for the value of transfers not made as well as the resumption of future transfers that are scheduled. The company has already covered these overdue amounts to its customers, and now awaits the Judiciary and the Central Bank for the resumption of transfers and reimbursement of those not made," the company said.

By paying merchants, these sub-acquirers avoid loss of customer confidence — and the systemic risk — that could occur even without them being the original debtors. "With liquidation, the money simply stops flowing from the issuing bank," criticizes a market participant.

"It's a rupture in the entire chain, which creates an immediate constraint for merchants. Someone who had an Entrepay machine could end up in a critical situation," warns another participant, comparing it to the Will Bank case at the beginning of the year: although it was only the card issuer, money also stopped flowing at the source.

When contacted for this report, the Central Bank did not comment.

Visa, Mastercard, Elo and American Express also appear as interested parties in actions by other companies. Last week, São Paulo courts ordered the card brands to deposit in court R$ 49 million owed by Entrepay to Grupo Tauá Hotéis e Resorts, which did not respond to this reporting team's request for comment.

Visa and Mastercard forwarded the positioning request to the Brazilian Association of Credit Card and Services Companies (Abecs), which represents the companies and repeated the statement from the 27th, saying it is "monitoring developments" and has "already started dialogue with ecosystem participants and with the competent authorities."

Elo said it does not comment on ongoing legal proceedings. Amex did not respond.

The tourism sector stands out among creditors. From the BeFly group, Vai Voando and Queensberry Agência de Viagens confirmed that "given the absence of settlement of receivables from sales, an action was filed, seeking to recover the amounts that failed to be settled," R$ 13 million combined. Besides them, FlyPay reported that it received the R$ 8 million before liquidation.

Another relevant creditor on the list is Vivo, which claims R$ 31 million, plus R$ 96 million due in the future. The company says it does not comment on ongoing legal proceedings.

In total, the amounts of the cases add up to R$ 268 million, or R$ 431 million when including future flows expected by some of the contracts.

Most of the actions were filed before the extrajudicial liquidation decreed by the Central Bank in Grupo Entre — which also includes the companies Acqio Adquirência and Octa SCD — for commitment of financial situation, infringement of activity rules and "losses that subject creditors to abnormal risk."

The group of businessman Antonio Carlos Freixo Junior, known as Mineiro, was also the target of Operation Compliance Zero by the Federal Police in January, cited in a price manipulation scheme led by Master Bank owner Daniel Vorcaro, whom authorities suspect is the hidden owner of Entrepay.

Entrepay denies having "corporate, control or governance ties" with the former banker and does not comment on the liquidation, claiming it already has a person appointed by the Central Bank for the purpose — retired Central Bank official Cassio Haig Vartanian.

When contacted, the liquidator did not respond to requests for comment.
