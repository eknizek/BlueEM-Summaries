# Nubank Disputes Receivables with Merchants in Entrepay Case

Entrepay's acquirer liquidation exposes a gap in payment arrangements by revealing duplicate charges

In liquidation by the Central Bank since late March, Entrepay faces a flood of lawsuits from companies that did not receive payments made by consumers through its payment terminals at the point of purchase. In addition to merchants, financial institutions that advanced payments to the acquirer have also not received their money back.

Although it is the first case of a card acquirer's liquidation by the Central Bank, the episode puts into question aspects of the current Brazilian payment arrangement, by exposing duplication — or even triplication — of charges and the lack of guarantees that the debt will be honored when one of the links in the chain breaks, according to market participants heard by Pipeline.

The most emblematic case is that of Nu Financeira, which is claiming R$74 million for advancing funds to Entrepay. In the normal flow, the issuing bank is the one that has the credit relationship with the merchant and commits, in D+30 or D+27, to transfer the resources to the acquirer — such as Entrepay, Cielo, Rede, Stone, GetNet and PagBank — which then passes them on to the merchant.

When contacted by the report, Nubank explained that it "carried out some receivables advancement operations with Entrepay, within the scope of the rules of regulated payment arrangements, with specific operational and legal safeguards. This is an isolated case in a single processor, with no material impact for the company."

"Nubank reiterates its commitment to the stability of the Brazilian Payment System and to the preservation of the regulated arrangements that sustain it. The institution believes that strict compliance with agreements between the links in the chain is fundamental for sector balance, and adopts the necessary legal measures to ensure the integrity and credibility of these relationships," the statement concludes.

Another bank with amounts to receive is Daycoval, which said through its press office that it has requested from the liquidator, at the administrative level, the restitution of amounts "arising from assignment operations in which the bank is the legitimate assignee," without confirming the amount, estimated at R$13 million by the market. Korean bank KEB Hana (R$15 million) did not respond.

The problem is compounded because there are at least two parties claiming ownership of the same resource: the bank that bought Entrepay's receivables and the merchant who should have received them. In many transactions, there are also sub-acquirers in the middle of the chain to provide capillarity to the acquirer. Those named in lawsuits in São Paulo include: PJBank, TAL Soluções em Pagamentos, Parcelamos Tudo, Keypay and NetCred, the only one willing to comment on the matter.

"NetCred's action charges Entrepay for the amounts of transfers not made as well as the resumption of future transfers that are scheduled. The company has already covered these late amounts to its clients, and now awaits the Judiciary and the Central Bank for the resumption of transfers and reimbursement of those not made," said the company.

By paying merchants, these sub-acquirers avoid loss of client confidence — and systemic risk — that could occur even without them being the original debtors. "With the liquidation, the money simply stops flowing from the issuing bank," criticizes a market participant.

"It is a rupture in the entire chain, which creates an immediate restriction for merchants. Someone who had an Entrepay terminal could find themselves in critical situation," warns another, comparing it to the Will Bank case earlier in the year: although it was only the card issuer, money also stopped flowing from the source.

When contacted for this report, the Central Bank did not respond. Visa, Mastercard, Elo and American Express also appear as interested parties in actions by other companies. Last week, the São Paulo Justice required the card networks to deposit in court R$49 million owed by Entrepay to Grupo Tauá Hotéis e Resorts, which did not respond to this report's request for comments. Visa and Mastercard forwarded the positioning request to the Brazilian Association of Credit Card Companies and Services (Abecs), which represents the companies and repeated the statement from the 27th, that it continues "monitoring developments" and has "already initiated dialogue with ecosystem participants and with the competent authorities." Elo said it does not comment on ongoing lawsuits. Amex did not respond.

The tourism sector stands out among creditors. From the BeFly group, Vai Voando and Queensberry Agência de Viagens confirmed that "given the absence of settlement of receivables from sales, an action was filed, seeking receipt of amounts that were not settled," R$13 million combined. Besides them, FlyPay reported that it received R$8 million before liquidation. Another relevant creditor on the list is Vivo, which claims R$31 million, plus R$96 million due in the future. The company says it does not comment on ongoing lawsuits.

In total, the amounts of the cases add up to R$268 million, or R$431 million when including future flows expected by some of the contracts. Most of the actions were filed before the extrajudicial liquidation decreed by the Central Bank in the Entre Group — which also includes the companies Acqio Adquirência and Octa SCD — due to compromised financial situation, violation of activity norms and "losses that subject creditors to abnormal risk."

The group of businessman Antonio Carlos Freixo Junior, known as Mineiro, was also targeted by the Federal Police's Compliance Zero operation in January, cited in a price manipulation scheme led by Banco Master owner Daniel Vorcaro, whom authorities suspect of being Entrepay's hidden owner. Entrepay denies "corporate, control or governance ties" with the former banker and does not comment on the liquidation, claiming it already has a person appointed by the Central Bank for the purpose, retired Central Bank official Cassio Haig Vartanian.

When contacted, the liquidator did not respond to requests for comment.
