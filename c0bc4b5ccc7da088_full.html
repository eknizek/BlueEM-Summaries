# Nubank Disputes Receivables with Merchants in Entrepay Case

Nubank disputes receivables with merchants in Entrepay case. Payment acquirer liquidation exposes gap in payment arrangement by revealing duplicate billing.

By Felipe Frisch — São Paulo 24/04/2026 06:12 Updated 11 hours ago

In liquidation by the Central Bank since the end of March, Entrepay faces a flood of lawsuits from companies that did not receive amounts paid by consumers through its payment terminals at the time of purchase. In addition to merchants, financial institutions that advanced payments to the acquirer also did not receive their money back. Although it is the first case of liquidation of a card acquirer by the Central Bank, the episode calls into question aspects of the current Brazilian payment arrangement, by exposing duplication — or even triplication — of charges and the lack of guarantees that the debt will be honored when one of the links breaks, according to market participants heard by Pipeline.

The most emblematic case is that of Nu Financeira, which claims R$ 74 million for advancing funds to Entrepay. In the normal flow, the issuing bank is the one with the credit relationship with the merchant and commits, in D+30 or D+27, to transfer the resources to the acquirer – such as Entrepay, Cielo, Rede, Stone, GetNet and PagBank – which then forwards it to the merchant.

Contacted by the report, Nubank explained that "it carried out some receivables advance operations with Entrepay, within the rules of regulated payment arrangements, with specific operational and legal safeguards. This is an isolated case with a single acquirer, with no material impact for the company."

"Nubank reiterates its commitment to the stability of the Brazilian Payment System and to the preservation of the regulated arrangements that sustain it. The institution believes that strict compliance with agreements between the links in the chain is fundamental to the balance of the sector, and adopts the necessary legal measures to ensure the integrity and credibility of these relationships," ends the statement.

Another bank with amounts to receive is Daycoval, which said through its press office that it requested from the liquidator, in the administrative sphere, the restitution of amounts "resulting from assignment operations in which the bank is the legitimate assignee," without confirming the amount, estimated at R$ 13 million by the market. Korean KEB Hana (R$ 15 million) did not respond.

The problem is compounded because there are at least two parties claiming ownership of the same resource: the bank that bought the receivables from Entrepay and the merchant who should receive them. In many transactions, there are also sub-acquirers in the middle of the chain, to give capillarity to the acquirer. This list in São Paulo courts includes: PJBank, TAL Soluções em Pagamentos, Parcelamos Tudo, Keypay and NetCred, the only one that was willing to comment on the matter.

"NetCred's action charges Entrepay for the amounts of transfers not made as well as the resumption of future transfers that are scheduled. The company has already covered these overdue amounts to its clients, and now awaits the Judiciary and the Central Bank for the resumption of transfers and compensation of those not made," said the company.

By paying merchants, these sub-acquirers avoid the loss of client confidence – and systemic risk – that could occur even if they were not the original debtors. "With the liquidation, the money simply stops flowing from the issuing bank," criticizes a market participant.

"It is a rupture in the entire chain, which creates an immediate restriction for merchants. Anyone who had an Entrepay machine can find themselves in a critical situation," warns, comparing it to the Will Bank case at the beginning of the year: although it was only the card issuer, money also stopped flowing at the source.

Contacted for this report, the Central Bank did not comment.

Visa, Mastercard, Elo and American Express also appear as interested parties in lawsuits by other companies. Last week, São Paulo courts ordered the card brands to deposit in court R$ 49 million owed by Entrepay to Tauá Hotéis e Resorts Group, which did not respond to this report's request for comments.

Visa and Mastercard forwarded the positioning request to the Brazilian Association of Credit Card Companies and Services (Abecs), which represents the companies and repeated the statement from March 27, that it continues "monitoring developments" and that "has already begun dialogue with ecosystem participants and with the competent authorities."

Elo said it does not comment on ongoing lawsuits. Amex did not respond.

The tourism sector stands out among creditors. From the BeFly group, Vai Voando and Queensberry Agência de Viagens confirmed that "given the absence of liquidation of receivables from sales, a lawsuit was filed, aiming to receive the amounts that were not liquidated," R$ 13 million combined. In addition to them, FlyPay reported that it received R$ 8 million before liquidation.

Another relevant creditor on the list is Vivo, which claims R$ 31 million, plus R$ 96 million due. The company says it does not comment on ongoing lawsuits.

In total, the amounts in the cases add up to R$ 268 million, or R$ 431 million when including future expected flows from some of the contracts. Most of the actions were filed before the extrajudicial liquidation decreed by the Central Bank in the Entre Group – which also includes the companies Acqio Adquirência and Octa SCD – due to compromised financial situation, violation of activity regulations and "losses that subject creditors to abnormal risk."

The group of businessman Antonio Carlos Freixo Junior, known as Mineiro, was also targeted by Operation Compliance Zero, by the Federal Police, in January, cited in a price manipulation scheme led by Master Bank owner Daniel Vorcaro, who authorities suspect is the hidden owner of Entrepay.

Entrepay denies "corporate, control or governance links" with the former banker and does not comment on the liquidation, claiming it already has a person appointed by the Central Bank for this purpose, retired Central Bank official Cassio Haig Vartanian.

Contacted, the liquidator did not respond to requests for comments.
