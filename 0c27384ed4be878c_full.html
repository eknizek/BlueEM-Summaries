# Airline fares to rise up to 10% this year due to kerosene costs

The instability generated by the armed conflict between the United States and Israel against Iran will cause airline fares to rise up to 10% for the remainder of 2026, according to an analysis by Oxford Economics.

"The increase in fuel prices will have to be passed on, at least partially, to consumers through higher airline fares."

According to the report, long-haul flights are the most vulnerable since fuel represents a larger proportion of ticket prices.

Despite the fare increase, Oxford Economics does not anticipate a total collapse in demand outside combat zones. While the increase "will discourage some of the most price-sensitive travelers," global growth trajectory remains stable outside the Middle East and Africa.

The report highlights that aircraft fuel typically represents between 25% and 35% of an airline's operating costs. For some airlines, it can reach 60%, making the sector highly vulnerable to oil price fluctuations.

"In the second half of 2026, most, if not all, regional air activity is expected to resume, but persistent negative sentiment is likely to cause a more prolonged recovery of pre-war capacity and the propensity to travel to or through the Middle East."

According to the document, in all other regions, there is some loss of demand due to reduced capacity to, from, or through the Middle East, in addition to less favorable macroeconomic conditions that have slowed overall air passenger demand.
