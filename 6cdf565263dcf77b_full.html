# Foreign Hotel Investment Surpassed $11 Billion in Colombia Between 2019 and 2025

Hilton, Marriott, Hyatt and Accor lead expansion. Community tourism and river cruises expand offerings. Avianca will strengthen its network in the United States with more flights and new frequencies. The market closed 2025 with approximately 12,957 hotels and 297,487 rooms.

Since 2010, the entry of international chains has resulted in more than 335 hotels and over 37,000 rooms incorporated into Colombia, an advance that consolidated the country as a destination for foreign investment in hotel infrastructure, according to an analysis published by the Colombian Association of Travel and Tourism Agencies (Anato). The market closed 2025 with approximately 12,957 hotels and 297,487 rooms, driven by tourism growth, expansion of air connectivity, and an incentive scheme for new projects.

"Colombia, The Country of Beauty, is consolidating a tourism and hotel platform with clear opportunities for the international investor. The combination of growing demand, connectivity, incentives, and project availability allows for the development of infrastructure with long-term vision and global vocation," said Carmen Caballero, president of ProColombia.

The transformation of the sector has attracted global brands such as Radisson, ACCOR, IHG, ARIA Hotels, Sirenis, Hilton, Four Seasons, Marriott, Wyndham, and Hyatt, which have participated in the development of new hotel projects, especially during moments of greater investment dynamism. This presence reinforced the country's attractiveness for foreign capital in a market that diversified its offerings in key destinations and consolidated tourism as a large-scale business.

## Foreign Direct Investment Captured $1.104 Billion in 2025

Between 2019 and 2025, commerce, restaurant, and hotel activities received more than $11 billion in foreign direct investment, according to the Balance of Payments of the Bank of the Republic. In 2025 alone, these sectors recorded $1.104 billion, equivalent to 12.5% of the country's non-mining-energy foreign direct investment.

This capital flow accompanied the strengthening of tourism demand and sustained arrival of international visitors. In 2025, Colombia received 4.4 million foreign non-resident tourists, among them, the United States remained the main source market with 25.4% participation, while Mexico, Peru, Brazil, Spain, and Argentina showed notable performance among the main origins of travelers. Bogotá, Medellín, and Cartagena concentrated approximately 80% of international arrivals.

This greater flow of tourists allowed the sector to consolidate as the main generator of external income outside the mining-energy segment. Hotel infrastructure responded with projects of different scales and formats, from urban hotels to ecotourism and agritourism developments in prioritized territories.

## Tourism Generated $11.166 Billion in Foreign Currency with 9.4% Growth

Tourism generated $11.166 billion in foreign currency in 2025, with growth of 9.4% compared to the previous year. The country has more than 1,500 weekly air frequencies, connections with 30 countries through 30 airlines operating in the market with more than 300,000 seats per week.

Modernization and construction projects are advancing for five strategic airports through public-private partnerships, with an estimated investment of $3 billion and capacity to serve up to 90 million annual passengers. In addition, the country offers tax and operational conditions that include a special 15% corporate income tax rate for 10 years for hotels, ecotourism parks, and agritourism; the benefit of 0% tariffs for importing raw materials and capital goods not produced domestically; tourism free trade zones; and the Plan Vallejo scheme for projects linked to service exports.

## Community Tourism Expands Territorial Offerings

The opportunity is not limited to major capitals. Colombia has prioritized 170 PDET municipalities (Programs for Development with Territorial Focus), of which 80 are considered peace tourism territories, with potential for lodging projects, ecotourism, agritourism, entertainment, and new travel experiences. This agenda also includes formats such as community and rural tourism, sun and beach, urban hotels, and river cruises, a segment in which projects like those of AmaWaterways on the Magdalena River have already begun operations.
