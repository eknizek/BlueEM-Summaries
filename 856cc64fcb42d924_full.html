# Good Health News Could Be a Lesson for Education

Social Organizations (OS) are non-profit private institutions dedicated to social impact policies, such as health, education, and the environment. Law 9,637/98 regulates the management contracts of OS with the public sector.

An article to be published in the prestigious The Quarterly Journal of Economics, by Maíra Coube (Insper), Luiz Felipe Fontes (Insper), and Rudi Rocha (FGV/Eaesp), quantifies the productivity and quality gains in hospital management by health social organizations (OSS) when compared to hospitals under traditional public management. Hospitals managed by OSS are financed by public resources, and payments are linked to achieving targets. There is no patient billing or private capital. Access is universal, as in any SUS hospital. OSS introduce private management practices and have greater flexibility in hiring and personnel compensation.

Hospitals that began to be managed by OSS increased hospitalizations by 40% and bed occupancy by 14%. The average length of hospital stay fell 8%. There was no increase in patient mortality or readmissions. The profile of patients and diseases treated was maintained, indicating that OSS do not avoid complex cases. In municipalities with greater hospital scarcity, the entry of OSS led to a 6.4% drop in population mortality. Improvements did not come from technology investments, but from efficient use of available resources, especially in personnel management. Physicians with public servant contracts decreased while other types of contracts increased, such as CLT, with performance affecting compensation. There was also an increase in hiring specialist physicians. Lower-productivity ones were replaced, resulting in a 24% increase in admissions per professional. There was dispersion in results, with OSS with better governance and experience obtaining results well above average, indicating that proper selection and monitoring of OSS by the public sector are essential elements of the strategy.

Good results in health point to ways to improve basic education, where 100% state provision prevails. There are a few cases where infrastructure maintenance was outsourced, but educational service remains under direct administration. The state of Goiás attempted to implement education OS in 2015 but succumbed to actions by the Public Ministry and resistance from civil servant corporations.

Just as in health, the quality of education depends on the service provider at the front line (doctors/teachers). Eric Hanushek, using US data, shows that replacing an average-capacity teacher with an above-average one for four consecutive years is sufficient to eliminate the difference between students of different family incomes or to close the learning gap between Black and white students in 3.5 to 5 years. Each additional year of activity by an above-average teacher adds hundreds of thousands of dollars to students' lifetime earnings.

The flexibility of OS to reward good teachers and dismiss underperforming ones would be a key instrument for bringing basic education out of its backwardness. It is not a magic formula. We would need good OS, as seen in the health case, since it is the best ones that make a difference. The current model is exhausted, in which the priority is career advancement and civil servant stability, injecting ever more money into the system without impact on learning indicators.
