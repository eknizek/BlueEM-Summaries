# Hidrovia Concession Bidding Begins Today with US Attention Focused on Argentina

The Hidrovia concession is entering a critical phase: the Government will open this Wednesday, April 22 at 1 PM the technical proposals from the two competing companies still in the running for the concession of the Troncal Navigable Waterway on the Paraná River, commonly known as the "Hidrovia" — the Belgian dredging companies Jan de Nul and Dredging Environmental and Marine Engineering (DEME).

This process involves a significant portion of Argentina's logistics competitiveness, as between the confluence of the Paraguay and Paraná rivers and the outlet to the Atlantic Ocean through the Río de la Plata, 80% of the country's foreign trade transits. The Hidrovia also handles exports from Paraguay and a portion of external sales from Brazil and Uruguay, totaling 100 million tons per year.

## The Business and the Fight Over the Hidrovia

In a business promising to generate revenues of approximately $15 billion and profits of $3 billion over the next 25 years, the attention of global powers has intensified, particularly the United States and China. In recent weeks, DEME activated contacts with US Ambassador Peter Lamelas, claiming it has American partners and financing for the necessary work on dredging, maintenance, and signaling of the Hidrovia.

However, dredger Great Lakes Dredge & Dock and the financial group Clear Street, which would be DEME's partners, did not appear mentioned in "envelope 1" of the bidding, which contains the presentation and background of bidders; instead, Clear Street would appear in "envelope 3" as a guarantee of the economic proposal.

In response, the Government ratified through resolution 21 of the National Ports and Navigation Agency (ANPyN), published this Tuesday in the Official Gazette, that the only bidders are DEME and the Jan de Nul-Servimagnus consortium, led by Argentine businessman Alfredo Román, known for his dominance in the crane business.

Thus, at least formally, the American companies do not appear, though they claim to have financing from the World Bank and the US International Development Finance Corporation (DFC). Similarly, the American companies guaranteeing their backing and partnership with DEME indicated that Servimagnus conceals Chinese subcontractors, something explicitly prohibited by the bidding requirements. In response, Jan de Nul denied the accusation and noted that while Servimagnus worked with Chinese companies until 2020, its current proposal is to work exclusively with its own dredges.

The bidding explicitly excluded participation by state-controlled companies, a way to prevent China from entering—another gesture of alignment by Javier Milei's Argentina with Donald Trump's United States, which watches carefully to maintain strategic control of navigation in Western hands.

## A Third Competitor Excluded

The bidding had a third competitor, Brazilian DTA Engenharia, which was disqualified by the National Ports and Navigation Agency (ANPyN) for failing to present guarantees. From São Paulo, the company presented itself with multiple doubts, appearing to exhibit official favoritism toward Jan de Nul. DTA denounced "restrictions on competition derived from disproportionate technical and operational requirements, an evaluation scheme that favors previously positioned operators, and weak price competition, with direct impact on reasonable tariffs and logistics costs."

The process had broad support from exporters and governors, who had questioned the previous bidding for "lack of prior dialogue." Back then, amid political allegations, the competition failed with only one bidder, DEME, and the Government declared it void in February of last year.

Instead, the Nation engaged in conversations with all stakeholders from mid-2025. Former President Mauricio Macri, whom some linked to Boskalis due to his closeness to Dutch Queen Máxima Zorreguieta, did not activate contacts this time, but the PRO team warned that with the required minimum tariffs, the overcost to the Argentine economy will rise by $5 billion, translating into lost competitiveness.
