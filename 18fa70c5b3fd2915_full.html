# Argentina Begins Waterway Concession Process with US Oversight

The Hidrovia concession is beginning to take shape today, with the watchful eye of the United States. The Government will open the technical proposals this Wednesday from the two companies still in the race to concession the Hidrovia. The definition will impact 80% of Argentine foreign trade. The Hidrovia concession will generate income of approximately US$ 15 billion.

The bidding for the concession of the Troncal Navigable Waterway on the Paraná River, better known as the "Hidrovia," enters a critical phase: the Government will open this Wednesday, April 22 at 1 p.m. the technical proposals from the two remaining competitors in the race, the Belgian dredging companies Jan de Nul and Dredging Environmental and Marine Engineering (DEME).

This process involves a significant portion of Argentina's logistical competitiveness, since between the confluence of the Paraguay and Paraná rivers and the outlet to the Atlantic Ocean through the Río de la Plata, 80% of the country's foreign trade passes through. The Hidrovia also evacuates exports from Paraguay and a portion of external sales from Brazil and Uruguay, totaling 100 million tons per year.

## The Business and the Fight for the Hidrovia

In a business that promises to generate income of approximately 15 billion dollars and profits of US$ 3 billion over the next 25 years, the attention of global powers, such as the United States and China, has intensified. In recent weeks, DEME activated contacts with US Ambassador Peter Lamelas, claiming it has American partners and financing for the necessary dredging, maintenance, and signaling work on the Hidrovia.

However, the dredging company Great Lakes Dredge & Dock and the financial group Clear Street, which would be DEME's partners, did not appear mentioned in "envelope 1" of the bidding, which contains the presentation and background of the bidders; instead, Clear Street would appear in "envelope 3," as a guarantee of the economic proposal.

In response, the Government ratified through resolution 21 of the National Ports and Navigation Agency (ANPyN), published this Tuesday in the Official Gazette, that the only bidders are DEME and the consortium Jan de Nul - Servimagnus, owned by Argentine businessman Alfredo Román, known for his reign in the crane business.

Thus, at least formally, the American companies do not appear, which claim to have financing from the World Bank and the US International Development Finance Corporation (DFC). Similarly, the American companies guaranteeing their support and partnership with DEME indicated that Servimagnus hides Chinese subcontractors, something that is prohibited by the bidding documents. In response, Jan de Nul denied the accusation and pointed out that, although Servimagnus worked with Chinese companies until 2020, its current proposal is to work exclusively with its own dredges.

The bidding explicitly excluded the participation of companies controlled by a State, a way to prevent China from entering, as another gesture of alignment by Javier Milei's Argentina with the United States governed by Donald Trump, who is watching closely to maintain strategic control of navigation in Western hands.

## A Third Competitor, Left Out

The bidding had a third competitor, the Brazilian DTA Engenharia, which was disqualified by the National Ports and Navigation Agency (ANPyN) for not presenting the required guarantees. From São Paulo, the company presented itself with multiple doubts, to demonstrate alleged official favoritism toward Jan de Nul. DTA denounced "restrictions on competition resulting from disproportionate technical and operational requirements, an evaluation scheme that favors previously positioned operators, and weakness in price competition, with direct impact on tariff reasonableness and logistics costs."

The process had broad support from exporters and governors, who had questioned the previous bidding due to lack of prior dialogue. At that time, amid political allegations, the competition failed with only one bidder, which was DEME, and the Government declared it void in February of last year.

Instead, the Nation engaged in conversations with all stakeholders from mid-2025. Former President Mauricio Macri, who was linked to Boskalis due to his proximity to Dutch Queen Máxima Zorregueta, did not activate contacts this time, but the PRO team warned that with the required minimum tariffs, the additional cost for the Argentine economy would rise by US$ 5 billion, translated into a loss of competitiveness.
