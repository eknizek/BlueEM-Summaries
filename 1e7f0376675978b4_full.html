# Eni and Repsol Close Deal with Venezuela to Expand Gas Field, Sources Say

European energy multinationals Eni and Repsol plan to begin exporting natural gas from Venezuela by the end of 2031, after reaching an agreement with Caracas to resume a long-stalled effort to expand production from a massive offshore field. The agreement with interim president Delcy Rodríguez will allow the companies to more than double production from the field in the Gulf of Venezuela and export the fuel as liquefied natural gas from a floating terminal, according to people familiar with the matter who spoke with Bloomberg News.

Italian Eni and Spanish Repsol also received guarantees that they will be compensated for billions of dollars in gas they pumped to Venezuela over the years without being paid, according to sources who spoke with Bloomberg News.

This represents a significant victory for the European companies, which have long pushed to export gas from the offshore field, maintaining the 20-year project through years of political turmoil.

The agreement was struck as Iran's war has cut about 20 percent of global liquefied natural gas supply and as the Trump administration is easing sanctions on Venezuela to allow companies to rebuild its ruined energy infrastructure.

Eni, based in Italy, confirmed in a statement that the companies reached an agreement with Venezuela to increase gas production and begin exports, based on its previous experience with floating LNG terminals. Repsol, headquartered in Spain, declined to comment. Venezuela's state oil company Petróleos de Venezuela SA (PDVSA) did not respond.

The agreement was signed in mid-March, but details were not disclosed, according to sources familiar with the matter. Eni and Repsol plan to present a final development plan to PDVSA by June, according to people familiar with the matter who requested anonymity because the matter is private.

## Gas in Venezuela

For more than a century, Venezuela has focused on its oil reserves, which are among the world's largest in terms of energy production. But the country also has abundant offshore gas deposits.

Eni and Repsol discovered the enormous Perla field in 2009, in shallow waters near the border between Venezuela and Colombia. It is estimated to hold 481 billion cubic meters of gas, making it one of Latin America's largest gas discoveries.

The companies developed it jointly with the intention of exporting gas. However, their agreement with Venezuela stipulated that Eni and Repsol supply the country's domestic market from the field before sending any fuel abroad.

Over the years, the companies struggled to reach an agreement with Venezuela over the exact amount needed for the domestic market. Currently, the field produces approximately 16.6 million cubic meters of gas per day, supplying power plants, petrochemical facilities, factories, and homes in western Venezuela.

The new agreement between Eni and Repsol with the government allows the companies to begin exporting when they are supplying 18.26 million cubic meters per day for domestic use, according to people familiar with the matter.

Eni and Repsol plan to install two additional platforms in the field by 2028 and begin exporting when they reach 34 million cubic meters per day of production, according to sources.

The companies' agreement with Venezuela extends their lease contract to operate the field from 2036 to 2051, said the people.

Floating LNG export terminals are relatively rare, used mainly in remote locations or when there are obstacles to onshore construction. They can be faster to build but present complex engineering challenges that can make financing and development difficult. Eni has experience with this technology in Congo and Mozambique. The company is also developing a project with Argentina's YPF using two floating vessels.
