# Squirrel helicopter surprises Avantto with demand in 'agribusiness capital', CEO says

Avantto, one of the first aircraft-sharing companies in Brazil, discovered an unmapped business opportunity in Brazil's Center-West region. The Squirrel family helicopter emerged as unexpected demand in the company's expansion to Goiânia, revealing a growing market in national agribusiness.

"Our first pleasant surprise in the region was this demand for helicopters that the company had not initially mapped," revealed Rogério Andrade, CEO of Avantto, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

The company operates two single-turbine models from the Squirrel family: the Airbus H125 (up to five passengers plus pilot) and the H130 (quieter, up to seven passengers plus pilot). The fleet also includes the AW109 Grand, a twin-engine Leonardo helicopter (formerly AgustaWestland).

The discovery of the extra demand from Goiânia's capital happened during the implementation of the company's first operation outside the Rio-São Paulo axis, which began in the second half of 2024. Avantto chose Goiânia as a strategic gateway to the Center-West.

"Goiânia, we joke, is the capital of the rest of Brazil. Certainly it is the capital of agro," Andrade justified.

The Squirrel helicopter, assembled by Helibras in Brazil under license from Airbus Helicopters, with approximately 50% domestic content, offers specific operational advantages for agribusiness. Its ability to operate in reduced spaces and proximity to rural properties justifies the unexpected demand identified by Avantto. The decision was based not only on agricultural potential but on economic diversification in the region.

"Goiânia's economy is not based solely on agro. So you have a very strong pharmaceutical industry. The real estate sector is also very robust in Goiânia," he explained.

The Squirrel family: the H125, with exposed tail rotor, is the workhorse of agriculture and police; the H130, with enclosed rotor, is quieter and more executive.

The H125 helicopter has notable achievements on its resume. In 2005, the model broke the world record for landing on Mount Everest, at 8,848 meters, a feat accomplished by then-Eurocopter (now Airbus Helicopters), the aircraft manufacturer. "The model is the leader in the fleet of civil and semi-public turbine helicopters, occupying 15% of the global fleet. In Brazil, the H125 represents approximately 50% of the single-turbine helicopter fleet," Helibras reports.

The Epic is the flagship. The Squirrel helicopter complements the strategy centered on the Epic aircraft (a $6 million turboprop, chosen for operating on short and unpaved runways). "The Epic is the flagship of growth, but there is demand for another aircraft model as well," Andrade said about the need to diversify the fleet.

The inclusion of the Squirrel creates a unique competitive advantage in the regional market. "This makes Avantto differentiate itself even more in the region, because it will be the only sharing company that offers both airplanes and helicopters in the program," the CEO highlighted.

Avantto's integrated system allows operational flexibility between bases. "A helicopter shareholder in Goiânia, when arriving here in São Paulo, when arriving in Rio, has their helicopter available using one of the fleet's aircraft here," Andrade explained.

The demand for helicopters reflects the growth of Brazilian agribusiness and its specific mobility needs. The Squirrel serves rural properties with airport infrastructure limitations, offering capacity for up to six passengers and operation in restricted areas.

The discovery strengthens the company's geographic expansion strategy. "The more places the company operates, the more this multiplication effect, the network effect will be perceived by customers," the CEO evaluated regarding growth potential through operational reach.

Avantto maintains a partnership with Voar Aviação for hangarage and maintenance services in Goiânia. "We are a large customer of a hangarage service provider here in São Paulo, in Rio, and that is from Goiânia. So our natural choice was to continue with them in Goiânia," Andrade justified.

The expansion to the Center-West also fuels growth in traditional bases through synergy effects. "The fact that we are going to the Center-West region, offering Avantto aircraft in other places, ends up increasing demand here as well, in São Paulo, in Rio," the executive revealed.

The company identifies significant pent-up demand in the region. "The beauty of the Center-West region is that there is large pent-up demand there and the company is feeling it more accelerated now, increasingly feeling it," Andrade projected about market potential.

Avantto invests heavily in expansion, with an order for 34 Epic aircraft totaling $200 million (approximately R$1 billion at current exchange rates). The new aircraft will be "literally destined for the Center-West region," according to the CEO.

The company's numbers show an upward trajectory. "Avantto has grown between 20% and 30% annually for many years in revenue," Andrade reported.

Diversification of customers in Goiânia already shows positive results. "Usage there has been for agribusiness, but also for other purposes. Customers with other purposes of use. Even leisure," the CEO observed.

The regional client base is building a profile similar to traditional bases. "It is building a customer base with characteristics similar to what the company has in São Paulo. But I am sure that agribusiness will predominate," he predicted about the evolution of the local market.

The expansion strategy contemplates growth in the São Paulo-Goiânia corridor. "The idea is that the company expands operations to other cities in the Center-West region and in the Matopiba region (acronym covering Maranhão, Tocantins, Piauí and Bahia)," the CEO revealed about future plans for geographic expansion.

The entry of the Squirrel helicopter into Avantto's operations in the Center-West represents more than fleet diversification. For the CEO, it also symbolizes the evolution of the aircraft-sharing market in Brazil, expanding beyond major urban centers to meet specific demands of the productive interior of the nation.

Brazil's executive aviation fleet reached 11,239 aircraft in November 2025, up 6.5% in a year, according to a survey by Abag (Brazilian General Aviation Association) based on Anac (National Civil Aviation Agency) records, disclosed by Avantto in January. With more than 2,000 registered helicopters, Brazil has one of the largest fleets in the world. São Paulo concentrates more than 410, more than New York.
