# Tax reform: Drawback works well; therefore, Management Committee should not correct it

Tax reform was approved under a promise to simplify the Brazilian system. It was not, at least in theory, approved to create bureaucratic traps, nor to harm exporters.

For this reason, any attempt to restrict the drawback regime in the new tax system is concerning. Drawback is one of the few foreign trade instruments that have worked well for decades, allowing the suspension or elimination of taxes on inputs used in the production of goods that will be exported. No serious country exports taxes, and drawback is not a Brazilian oddity—it is a practice recognized by the World Trade Organization.

In Brazil, it has become essential for industrial, agricultural, and service sectors linked to global supply chains: more than 2,000 companies currently use it, more than $70 billion in exports last year with drawback, and less than 4% default rate over a quarter century.

Now, under the pretext of regulating tax reform, an undue restriction on drawback is being created. Decree 12,955 (art. 161) transforms drawback suspension—a usual instrument of tax neutrality—into a complex qualification regime with requirements typical of large structured companies. For the first time, to benefit from CBS suspension in drawback, the company now depends on qualification through a joint act of the Federal Revenue Service and the IBS Management Committee, with a long list of requirements: company audits, tax compliance of all entities, various certificates, computerized systems, installed capacity due diligence, barriers against new exporters, minimum annual exports, minimum input application.

The bureaucratic mess, in practice, amputates the drawback regime for small and medium-sized companies. It will be a club reserved for corporations with tax departments, sophisticated systems, and high net worth. Or, alternatively, it will force exporters into multiple litigation to maintain their basic constitutional right not to export taxes.

Legally, the decree illegally alters Secex's jurisdiction, creates double qualification, imposes restrictions without legal basis, conflicts with Complementary Law, violates constitutional principles—beyond, of course, the most basic logic derived from the saying "if it ain't broken, don't fix it."

The Management Committee's argument will be that in the new tax system, the exporter will not accumulate credits, thanks to compensation in the incidence that occurs in value-added taxes. That will be the typical argument from a bureaucrat, isolated in the Federal District's cerrado, who has never exported anything in their life. Drawback, dear Fiscal Theorist, is valuable precisely because it allows relief of cash flow for companies by not compromising funds for the production process. For a medium-sized company, even if compensation occurs in sixty days (an optimistic hypothesis yet to be tested), it amounts to two months of cash flow pressure in the face of one of the world's highest interest rates. This cash flow can, in the real world, lead to the inviability of export or the company's own operation. Small and medium-sized enterprises have tight margins, expensive credit, and reduced staff. By requiring multinational-level bureaucratic structure, the decree creates an insurmountable barrier to its use.

Drawback has always worked well because it is simple. If tax reform aims to unify, rationalize, and reduce litigation, it cannot multiply regimes, obscure conditions, and operational uncertainty. The exporter needs complete predictability, not an even larger tax maze.

It is hoped that the Management Committee, Congress, or the Judiciary pay attention to this issue and its costs for exporters. If the Management Committee has a central role in the new tax system, it is expected to act with institutional prudence. Its function is not to create more obstacles to exports, but to manage a system that favors growth, legal certainty, and competitiveness.

As in other cases, Decree 12,955 attempts to do with drawback what Brazilian bureaucracy frequently does: preserve the instrument in law but render it inviable in practice. A system that, without problems, has been in use since 1961.

Brazilian exporters face high logistics costs, expensive credit, currency bureaucracy, and deficient infrastructure. Adding one more tax uncertainty is making them give up.
