Dollar loses safe-haven appeal as Wall Street bets on currency decline

# Dollar loses safe-haven appeal as Wall Street bets on currency decline

Deutsche Bank and Wells Fargo are among the banks declaring that the dollar's appreciation as a safe haven, driven by the war, has likely come to an end, as a ceasefire between the United States and Iran leads investors to seek riskier assets. The American currency has erased all gains accumulated since the beginning of the U.S.-Iran war after Tehran announced on Friday (17th) that the Strait of Hormuz is "completely open" for commercial traffic. The development reduced demand for assets considered safe havens, such as the dollar, traditionally viewed as a refuge in times of crisis.

The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index fell as much as 0.6%, reaching its lowest level since February 27th. The index has accumulated a decline of about 1.9% since the United States and Iran agreed to a truce on April 7th. During the same period, currencies more sensitive to risk — led by those of Scandinavian countries, New Zealand, and Australia — are leading gains against the dollar, while the S&P 500 has recovered and reached new all-time highs this week.

Analysts argue that it's time to bet against the dollar, and global investors appear to be following that path. They have raised currency hedging protection rates to a two-year high, according to State Street Corp. In the options market, confidence in the dollar has withered, with positioning at the least optimistic level in recent weeks. With safe-haven appeal declining, investors are turning back to the headwinds that knocked the dollar down 8% last year — its worst performance since 2017 — including the prospect of interest rate cuts by the Federal Reserve.

"There is a clear rotation away from safe havens like the dollar toward risky assets," wrote Kathleen Brooks, research director at XTB brokerage in London, in an email. "If the conflict between the United States and Iran reaches a resolution soon, I see a prolonged period of weakness for the dollar ahead."

The strait in focus

The Strait of Hormuz is "declared completely open" for all commercial vessels during the remaining period of the ceasefire, said Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi on Friday. Oil prices retreated.

Some analysts warned, however, that it may be too early to bet on dollar weakness. Currency analysts at Citigroup said on Thursday that the risk-return relationship favored bets on dollar appreciation. Persistently high commodity prices will limit gains in risky assets, sustaining bond yields and the dollar, they said.

The easing of tensions has reignited distrust of the American currency that has shaped conversations about it since President Donald Trump took office last year. At Wells Fargo, strategists recommended buying the Swedish krona against the dollar. Deutsche Bank advised selling a broad measure of the American currency, with the prospect that the euro could eventually surpass $1.20 for the first time since January — versus about $1.18 today. JPMorgan Chase strategists said last week that "the dollar appears to come out worse in the medium term because of the conflict," in part due to high war spending.

Fed independence

Brooks from XTB lists some of the challenges to the American currency, beyond expectations that the Fed will eventually cut interest rates while markets anticipate hikes in other countries. She points to possible concerns about Federal Reserve independence, as Trump's threat this week to fire the institution's president, Jerome Powell, if he doesn't step down "in time," creates a possible scenario where the president appoints an ally as interim chairman while nominee Kevin Warsh awaits confirmation.

"This could bring us back to the theme of dollar depreciation, which weighed heavily on the currency last year," Brooks said.

Between the lines, there is also Wall Street's view that Trump would like to see a weaker dollar to support American exports, although the administration has repeatedly reaffirmed the traditional American policy of a "strong dollar."

Pessimistic view

With the shift in market sentiment, asset managers increased bets against the dollar in the early weeks of April, according to a Morgan Stanley model. A Bank of America survey conducted between April 3rd and 9th — a period coinciding with the start of the ceasefire — shows that the second highest conviction bet among fund managers this year, behind only buying bonds, was shorting the dollar. "Investors view the Iran war more as a change in the trajectory of the dollar in 2026 rather than as a change in trend," wrote BofA strategists, including Ralf Preusser and Meghan Swiber, in a note released this week.

The extent to which international investors eliminate currency risk from their positions in American assets — through derivatives betting against the dollar — is another possible trigger for currency weakness. Data from State Street, one of the world's largest custodian banks, shows they are rushing to this protection, raising hedging rates on the American currency to 63% in the wake of the ceasefire announcement.

"Markets are almost acting as if the conflict never happened," said Andrew Hazlett, currency trader at Monex Inc.

Moreover, a resolution of the war, by reducing concerns about economies outside the United States, could lead investors to buy more international assets. "The factors for the diversification movement are present underneath, but are being overshadowed by other concerns at the moment," said Beata Manthey, head of European equity strategy at Citigroup, in an interview with Bloomberg Television this week.
