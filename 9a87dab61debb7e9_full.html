# Stay home and let the tractor work alone in the fields

The arduous repetitive work of an agricultural machinery operator is becoming a thing of the past. From the coming years until probably 2030, practically all operations of soil preparation, planting, spraying and harvesting could be done remotely with machines interacting without the presence of operators. During harvest, as combine harvesters advance through soybean fields and their grain tanks are filled, tractors will fetch the harvested grains and take them to trucks that will transport them to warehouses. At the same time, other machines come behind planting corn. All without an operator. This is the near future promised by PTx, a brand that develops precision technologies for agriculture. Belonging to AGCO, the company is bringing the OutRun system to the Brazilian market, which will give a new level of autonomy to agricultural machinery.

Starting in 2027, Fendt tractors should be equipped to receive this technology, which will allow remote management of various agricultural activities. The tractor will have a package of equipment installed on the roof and other components inside the cabin, and through a tablet, the operator of a combine harvester, when the machine's grain tank is full, can remotely call the grain tractor for transfer of harvested grains. Without an operator, the grain tractor docks right next to the combine harvester to receive the grain discharge. Next, with the same tablet, the combine harvester operator sends the tractor to unload the grains into the truck, parked on the roadside. All autonomously, and without an operator in the tractor.

This system is already ready and being tested, and AGCO made a demonstration in a field at the IAC (Agronomic Institute) in Campinas, São Paulo, on Wednesday (8). In Canada and the United States, machines are already running with this system. Here in Brazil, however, legislation for this autonomous system is still not defined. The company expects a definition soon.

"Our objective is that by 2030, practically all field operations are available to the producer and already operational in the system. Not only soil preparation, but also harvesting, planting and spraying," says José Carlos Ferraz Bueno, commercial director of PTx for Latin America.

Each operation in the process is a challenge, according to Bueno. Brazilian agriculture is different from that in the United States and Europe. The producer here is also different from those in the United States. There, the farmer operates his own machine. Here, this only happens from Paraná downward. Besides, we have to adapt our products according to agriculture here. At the moment, the search is for safety, says Fernando Compagnon, product specialist at PTx. "It is important to highlight that anything that crosses the tractor's path, it detects and stops."

It follows a programming, and when it is poorly planned, it is blocked by the system itself through red screen messages on the tablet, says the specialist. The connectivity system of the tractor that will leave the factory is Starlink. But PTx will make the system, called OutRun, available for tractors already in use and from other brands, and in this case, the system can be 4G or 5G.

Bueno says that one of the major advantages of this technology is the increase in productivity that the producer will have. The system speeds up field activity, accelerates soybean harvesting and allows corn planting within the ideal window. Currently focused on corn and soybeans, the company should work with all crops, he states.

But when will the producer pay for this new technology? For now, the company says it is operating a pre-commercial system and does not yet have a defined price. Despite the product already being available for sale in other markets, such as the United States. The pricing there is very different, starting with Brazilian tax burden, which is 30% to 35%, says PTx's commercial director.

"We are already at a quite safe stage, but we will continue making validations to be ready starting next year," says Fabio Dotto, marketing director at Fendt, the company that will have the tractor coupled with the new technology. The commercial launch for some activities should occur in 2027. The technology will be important for the producer because he will have more efficiency, lower costs and better management of field operations, says Dotto.

## THE TRACTOR THAT TALKS

Will the weather be ideal for planting tomorrow? Something is wrong, why did you stop? When is your next maintenance? Is the speed we're going appropriate? These and other questions can be asked by the producer inside his tractor cabin, and an artificial intelligence system and the machine's telemetry will provide the answers.
