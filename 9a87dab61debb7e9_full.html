# Stay Home and Let the Tractor Work Alone in the Field

The arduous repetitive work of an agricultural machine operator is becoming a thing of the past. Starting in the coming years, likely by 2030, practically all operations of soil preparation, planting, spraying, and harvesting can be done remotely with machines interacting without the presence of operators. During harvest, as combine harvesters advance through soybean fields and have their grain tanks filled, tractors will collect the harvested grains and take them to trucks that will transport them to warehouses. At the same time, other machines follow behind planting corn. All without an operator. This is the near future promised by PTx, a brand that develops precision technologies for agriculture. Belonging to AGCO, the company is bringing the OutRun system to the Brazilian market, which will give a new level of autonomy to agricultural machines.

Starting in 2027, Fendt tractors should be equipped to receive this technology, which will allow remote management of various agricultural activities. The tractor will have a package of equipment installed on the roof and other components inside the cabin, and through a tablet, the operator of a combine harvester, when the machine's grain tank is full, can remotely call the grain tractor for the transfer of harvested grains. Without an operator, the grain tractor docks right next to the combine harvester to receive the grain discharge. Next, with the same tablet, the combine harvester operator sends the tractor to unload the grains into the truck parked at the roadside. All autonomously, and without an operator in the tractor.

This system is already ready and being tested, and AGCO held a demonstration in a field at the Agronomic Institute (IAC) in Campinas, São Paulo, on Wednesday the 8th. In Canada and the United States, machines are already running with this system. Here in Brazil, however, legislation for this autonomous system has not yet been defined. The company expects a decision soon. "Our goal is that by 2030 practically all field operations will be available to the producer already operational in the system. Not only soil preparation, but also harvesting, planting, and spraying," says José Carlos Ferraz Bueno, commercial director of PTx for Latin America.

Each operation in the process is a challenge, according to Bueno. Brazilian agriculture is different from that in the United States and Europe. The producer here is also different from those in the United States. There, the farmer operates his own machine. Here, this only occurs from Paraná southward. Furthermore, we have to tropicalize our products according to agriculture here.

Currently, the focus is on safety, says Fernando Compagnon, product specialist at PTx. "It is important to note that if anything crosses the tractor's path, it detects it and stops." It follows programming, and when poorly planned, it is blocked by the system itself through red screen messages on the tablet, according to the specialist. The tractor's connectivity system that will come from the factory is Starlink's. But PTx will make the OutRun system available to tractors already in use and from other brands as well, in which case the system can be 4G or 5G. Bueno says that one of the great advantages of this technology is the productivity increase the producer will have. The system speeds up field activity, accelerates soybean harvesting, and allows corn planting within the ideal window. Currently focused on corn and soybeans, the company should work with all crops, he states.

But when will the producer pay for this new technology? For now, the company says it is operating a pre-commercial system and does not yet have a defined price. Although the product is already being sold in other markets, such as the United States. The pricing there is quite different, starting with Brazilian tax burden, which is 30% to 35%, says PTx's commercial director. "We are already at a fairly safe stage, but we will continue validating to be ready from next year onwards," says Fabio Dotto, marketing director of Fendt, the company that will have the tractor equipped with the new technology. The commercial launch for some activities should occur in 2027. The technology will be important for the producer because he will have more efficiency, lower costs, and better management of field operations, says Dotto.
