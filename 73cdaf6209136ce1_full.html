# Brazil's Central Bank Tightens FGC-Backed Funding Rules, Requires Higher Quality Assets

The Central Bank announced on Thursday (23rd) a tightening of rules for fundraising with FGC (Credit Guarantee Fund) backing, requiring financial institutions to back resources with higher quality assets. So-called "reference assets" include public securities, credit operations, compulsory deposits, and other credit-like operations, among others.

From now on, if the value of fundraising guaranteed by the FGC exceeds the amount the institution has invested in quality assets, the excess must be allocated to federal public securities, considered safe investments.

The Central Bank's decision comes in the wake of the market crisis generated by Banco Master. Daniel Vorcaro's institution had a strategy of selling high-yield CDBs (Bank Certificates of Deposit), using FGC coverage as an attraction. With the funds raised, the bank purchased risky assets such as court-awarded claims, pre-claims, and company shares.

With the change, for example, if a bank raised R$ 100 million in FGC-covered CDBs but has R$ 80 million invested in reference assets, it must allocate the R$ 20 million excess to public securities, where the resources will be locked in.

The rule will take effect from June 1st, in a staggered manner. Initially, 5% of the amount will be required to be redirected. In the example above, this would be equivalent to R$ 1 million to be "sterilized" in public securities. The figure will rise to 15% in January 2027 and to 30% in July 2027. Six months later, in January 2028, it will be required to reach 60%, until hitting 100% in July 2028.

The new rules were approved by the CMN (National Monetary Council)—a board made up of Finance Minister Dario Durigan and Planning and Budget Minister Bruno Moretti, along with Central Bank President Gabriel Galípolo.

Internally, the measure is viewed at the Central Bank as a regulatory evolution that seeks to provide more security to the process, ensuring that fundraising based on FGC-linked risk is applied to more traditional and transparent assets.

"The measures complement the existing framework and aim to mitigate moral hazard associated with fundraising excessively anchored in the FGC guarantee and will take effect from June 1st this year," the Central Bank said in a statement.

In August of last year, it was defined that deposits in public securities would be required when the value of FGC-guaranteed fundraising exceeded ten times the financial institution's net equity. The proposal now approved brings a new trigger.

At that time, the Central Bank also announced it would double the contribution rate to the fund paid by banks wishing to raise larger volumes of resources using FGC guarantee as an attraction. It was decided that the regulation would only take effect on June 1st to give institutions time to make necessary adjustments to their portfolios.

As reported by Folha, the measure will function as a kind of penalty for increased fund risk and should mainly affect medium and small banks.

## Short-Term Liquidity Index

The Central Bank also decided to expand the requirement for compliance with the Short-Term Liquidity Index to S2 segment institutions of prudential regulation—those with assets accounting for 1% to 10% of GDP, such as BNDES (National Bank for Economic and Social Development), Citibank, XP, and others.

Until then, the rule applied only to the country's largest banks, from the S1 segment, with size greater than or equal to 10% of GDP. This is the case with Itaú, Bradesco, Santander, BTG Pactual, Banco do Brasil, and Caixa Econômica Federal.

This liquidity index must be greater than 1, meaning the institution is in a relatively safe position. The calculation measures the relationship between the stock of highly liquid assets and projected net cash outflows over a 30-day horizon, ensuring that institutions maintain sufficient reserves to face stress periods.

The agency will also implement a Simplified Short-Term Liquidity Index, applicable to S3 segment institutions (medium-sized, with assets accounting for 0.1% to 1% of GDP) and S4 (small-sized, with exposure below 0.1% of GDP), provided they raise funds from the public through deposits or securities issuance. This model will follow the previous logic but with simplified methodology suited to the size and complexity of smaller institutions.

Implementation of the new liquidity requirements will follow a transition schedule, seeking to ensure institutions adequate time to adapt their processes and systems. Between January 1st and June 30th, 2027, the minimum limit of indicators will be 90% (0.9), moving to 100% (1) from July 1st, 2027.

The indicators will not serve as automatic triggers for sanctions against institutions. In case of non-compliance with the required minimum limit, they must contact Central Bank supervision and present a recovery plan.

The topic was placed in public consultation by the Central Bank from September 18th to November 1st, 2025 to gather input for the new regulation. The change comes amid the liquidity crisis faced by BRB (Brasília Bank) following losses from fraudulent operations with Master. According to the Central Bank, the regulatory measures focus on risk reduction.
