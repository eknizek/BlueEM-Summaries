# The Ibáñez Atkinson Family's Dual Bet in London: Hotels and Prime Offices

Leicester Square is a pedestrian plaza in the heart of London's West End, 370 meters from Piccadilly Circus and surrounded by the theaters, cinemas, and restaurants that define the capital's nightlife. About 60 red-carpet film premieres are held there annually, and it is exactly there, at the corner of Charing Cross Road and Irving Street, that the Z Hotel Leicester Square opened a year ago: 95 rooms in a refurbished office building. It is the 15th property of Z Hotels, the British compact hotel chain that operates in London, Bath, Glasgow, and Liverpool under the concept of "compact luxury."

In January of this year, Z Hotels closed an £79 million deal—approximately US$106 million—with Metro Bank to refinance two of its properties in the city center: precisely the Leicester Square hotel and the Covent Garden hotel, 500 meters away. According to the group, the operation will free up cash flow to invest in portfolio growth.

Closely observing these movements is Felipe Ibáñez Scott, through Stars Companies, the business platform of his family branch, which has been listed as a major shareholder of the hotel chain since July 2022. The model aims to convert unused office buildings into efficient hotels with compact but high-standard rooms. Few in Chile know about this British hotel venture, but group executives have held seats on the board of the holding company that controls the chain and have even granted loans to companies related to other shareholders.

## No Windows

The idea was born from an ugly building. Bev King had spent months working on a plan to renovate the facade of the Tower Thistle, a hotel near Tower Bridge. He was COO of Thistle Hotels and had calculated that a small investment would generate considerable returns. But the company said no. That's when, after 25 years working for other companies, he decided to go independent.

King grew up in Cheddar, a village in Somerset where his parents managed pubs and later hotels. He trained in the Trusthouse Forte postgraduate program and worked at the Strand Palace Hotel, the Cumberland, and a hotel in Portugal before joining Thistle, a chain with more than 10 locations.

When he left, he was convinced that the sector's rules were wrong: for example, in a starred hotel, the bed cannot go against the wall, the bathroom must have a bathtub, and rooms need windows. But King questioned each of these rules. He started designing a functional 10-square-meter room. They removed the bathtub, put the bed against the wall, and even offered rooms without windows. The key was taking office buildings from the 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s—too small for the modern corporate market—and converting them into hotels in locations that normally only major four or five-star chains occupy. Each project cost an average of £5 million, financed through investment vehicles and bank debt. Among the first investors were former footballers, including David O'Leary, former Arsenal captain.

The first Z Hotel opened in November 2011 in Soho with 85 rooms. According to The Times, in 2015, with six hotels in operation, the chain billed £25 million and generated £7.5 million in EBITDA. Occupancy exceeded 90% and 40% of guests returned to some of its locations. At the Z Hotel Victoria, 71 of the 106 rooms have no windows (and they are the first to sell out). During the pandemic, Z Hotels employed 300 people and laid off fewer than 10. After reopening, they raised salaries by 10%, then 7%, and then 6%. All its hotel managers, except one, started as guest service assistants.

## Palena

Stars Companies' own website shows the Z Hotels logo in the private companies section, alongside Clínica Meds, Niu Foods, Glasstech, and Wivet, the network of veterinary clinics present in Chile and other countries in the region. But in British public records, the link is less direct. The connection passes through a chain of at least three vehicles in two jurisdictions.

Patricio Leighton, one of the executives closest to the family, is listed as director of the subsidiaries that operate the Covent Garden, Old Street, and City hotels. In most other group companies, Leighton left those positions in May 2025. In the parent company, Z Group Topco, he was replaced in May by Avon River Limited, a company incorporated just two months earlier. The first director of Avon River: Leighton himself. Its sole shareholder is Tay UK Holdco Ltd, a British entity created in 2022 by Palena S.A.R.L. SPF, a family wealth management vehicle domiciled in Luxembourg and linked to the Ibáñez Atkinson family.

## The Debt

The latest consolidated financial statements of Z Group Topco, signed on February 2, 2026, show revenue of £48.7 million (approximately US$65 million) and a loss of £4.1 million. Occupancy remained at 99%, a figure that the board itself described as exceptional and that exceeded the average for its segment in London. The loss—better than the £8.7 million from the previous year but still in the red—has an explanation: financial costs. Z Hotels paid £8.6 million in interest during the period, a figure that exceeds the £7.3 million it generated in operating cash flow. Total debt reached £103.6 million at year-end, of which £80.6 million was due within the following 12 months.

The financial statements also included a note of uncertainty: they stated that if lenders demanded early repayment, the group would not have the resources to respond without refinancing. That's why the March refinancing went in that direction. As occurs in the hotel industry, according to various consultants, Z's model is leveraged on real estate assets, so there are typically debts associated with those assets. In early 2026, the refinancing of real estate assets with Metro Bank was completed under favorable terms, significantly reducing the financial burden.

## Offices in Mayfair

Z Hotels is not the only bet by the Ibáñez Atkinson family in the United Kingdom, nor their largest. In February this year, Stars REI—the real estate arm of the investment platform—closed a £80 million credit line with Aareal Bank, a German bank specialized in real estate financing that trades on the Frankfurt stock exchange. The objective was to refinance its portfolio of offices in central London, where they control various Class A properties alongside Trinova, the London-based asset management firm that acts as their strategic partner in Europe. The refinancing occurred due to debt maturity, a routine operation in the real estate industry.

The two assets backing that operation are the building at 33 Kingsway in Covent Garden and 103 Mount Street in Mayfair, where they managed to close a lease contract at 172.5 pounds per square foot, a record for that stretch of the street. In that same building, in one of the most expensive districts of London, Antofagasta PLC, the mining company of the Luksic group, has offices.
