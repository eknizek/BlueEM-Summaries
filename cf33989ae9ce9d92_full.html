# The Ibáñez Atkinson Family's Double Bet in London: Hotels and Prime Offices

Leicester Square is a pedestrian plaza in the heart of London's West End, 370 meters from Piccadilly Circus and surrounded by the theaters, cinemas, and restaurants that define the nightlife of the British capital. About 60 red carpet premieres take place there annually, and it was there, at the corner of Charing Cross Road and Irving Street, that the Z Hotel Leicester Square opened a year ago: 95 rooms in a renovated office building. It is the 15th property of Z Hotels, the British chain of compact hotels that operates in London, Bath, Glasgow, and Liverpool under the "compact luxury" concept.

In January of this year, Z Hotels closed an £79 million deal—approximately US$106 million—with Metro Bank to refinance two of its properties in the city center: precisely the Leicester Square hotel and the Covent Garden hotel, 500 meters away. According to the group, the transaction would free up cash flow to invest in portfolio growth.

Watching these moves closely is Felipe Ibáñez Scott, through Stars Companies, the business platform of his family branch, which has been listed as a major shareholder of the hotel chain since July 2022. The chain's model aims to convert unused office buildings into efficient hotels with compact but high-standard rooms.

Few in Chile know about this British hotel venture, but group executives have held board seats in the holding company that controls the chain and have even granted loans to companies related to other shareholders.

## Without Windows

The idea was born from an ugly building. Bev King had spent months working on a plan to renovate the facade of the Tower Thistle, a hotel near Tower Bridge. He worked as COO of Thistle Hotels and had calculated that a small investment would generate considerable returns. But the company said no. That's when, after 25 years working for other companies, he decided to go independent.

King grew up in Cheddar, a village in Somerset where his parents managed pubs and later hotels. He trained in the Trusthouse Forte postgraduate program and worked at the Strand Palace Hotel, the Cumberland, and a hotel in Portugal before arriving at Thistle, a chain with more than 10 locations.

When he left his position, he was convinced that the sector's rules were wrong: for example, in a star-rated hotel, the bed cannot go against the wall, the bathroom must have a bathtub, and rooms need windows. But King questioned each of these norms.

He began designing a functional 10-square-meter room. They removed the bathtub, placed a bed against the wall, and even offered rooms without windows. The key was to take office buildings from the '60s, '70s, and '80s—too small for the modern corporate market—and convert them into hotels in locations normally occupied only by four- or five-star chains. Each project cost an average of £5 million, financed through investment vehicles and bank debt.

Among the first investors were former footballers, including David O'Leary, former captain of Arsenal. The first Z Hotel opened in November 2011 in Soho with 85 rooms. According to The Times, in 2015, with six hotels in operation, the chain billed £25 million and generated £7.5 million in EBITDA. Occupancy exceeded 90% and 40% of guests returned to some of its locations. At the Z Hotel Victoria, 71 of the 106 rooms have no window (and are the first to sell out).

During the pandemic, Z Hotels had 300 employees and laid off fewer than 10. After reopening, it raised wages by 10%, then 7%, and then 6%. All its hotel managers, except one, started as guest service assistants.

## Palena

Stars Companies' own website shows the Z Hotels logo in the private companies section, alongside Clínica Meds, Niu Foods, Glasstech, and Wivet, the veterinary clinic network present in Chile and other regional countries. But in British public records, the link is less direct.

The connection passes through a chain of at least three vehicles across two jurisdictions. Patricio Leighton, one of the executives closest to the family, appears as director of the subsidiaries operating the Covent Garden, Old Street, and City hotels. In most other group companies, Leighton left these positions in May 2025. At the parent company, Z Group Topco, he was replaced in May by Avon River Limited, a company established just two months earlier. Avon River's first director: Leighton himself. And its sole shareholder is Tay UK Holdco Ltd, a British entity created in 2022 by Palena S.A.R.L. SPF, a family wealth management vehicle domiciled in Luxembourg and linked to the Ibáñez Atkinson family.

## Debt

The latest consolidated financial statements of Z Group Topco, signed on February 2, 2026, show revenue of £48.7 million (approximately US$65 million) and a loss of £4.1 million. Occupancy remained at 99%, a figure that the board itself described as exceptional and surpassed the average for its segment in London.

The loss—better than the £8.7 million from the previous year but still in the red—has an explanation: financial costs. Z Hotels paid £8.6 million in interest during the period, a figure exceeding the £7.3 million it generated in operating cash flow. Total debt amounted to £103.6 million at year-end, of which £80.6 million was due within the following 12 months.

In the financial statements, a note of uncertainty was also issued: they stated that if lenders demanded early payment, the group would not have the resources to respond without refinancing. And that's why the March refinancing went in that direction. As occurs in the hotel industry, according to various consultants, Z's model is leveraged on real estate assets, so there are typically debts associated with those assets. In early 2026, the refinancing of real estate assets with Metro Bank was completed under favorable conditions, significantly reducing the financial burden.

## Offices in Mayfair

Z Hotels is not the Ibáñez Atkinson family's only bet in the United Kingdom, nor their largest. In February of this year, Stars REI—the real estate arm of the investment platform—closed a £80 million credit line with Aareal Bank, a German bank specializing in real estate financing that trades on the Frankfurt stock exchange. The goal was to refinance its portfolio of offices in central London, where they control various Class A buildings alongside Trinova, the London-based asset management firm that acts as their strategic partner in Europe.

The refinancing occurred due to debt maturity, a routine operation in the real estate industry. The two assets backing this transaction are the building at 33 Kingsway, in Covent Garden, and the one at 103 Mount Street, in Mayfair, where they managed to close a lease contract at 172.5 pounds per square foot, a record for that stretch of the street. In that same building, in one of London's most expensive districts, Antofagasta PLC, the mining company of the Luksic group, has offices.
