# Vaca Viva + Vaca Muerta: the strategy that can shield Argentina

The conflict in the Middle East can reorder key economic variables — especially oil and gas prices — and open a strategic window for the bioeconomy, according to recent analysis. Although the conflict is still unfolding and its final consequences remain uncertain, one conclusion appears clear: having productive and energy alternatives is no longer optional but inevitably crucial on future agendas.

But there is another point that is less visible and more structural. It is not only about energy; what happens "upstream" in agricultural production also matters, which is the central axis in food supply and other derivatives. In this area, the role of biotechnology as an engine of the bioeconomy is crucial.

In Argentina and much of Mercosur, this role was decisive: the incorporation of technologies such as genetically improved seeds and direct sowing allowed production to expand and efficiency to increase sustainably. Without that technological leap, the current scale of regional agriculture would hardly have been achieved. It also would not have been possible to sustain increasing levels of exports in increasingly volatile international contexts without the presence of this "living cow."

This point becomes especially relevant when linked to food prices. The 2008 crisis showed how shocks could quickly transfer from energy to food, with profound economic and social consequences. In that context, greater agricultural supply generated by biotechnology helped moderate the impact. Without that contribution, international prices would have been even higher and social tensions more intense, especially in countries that are net food importers.

Today, in a different but no less challenging scenario, shocks in energy and food intersect again. Market disruption is even greater than before, but at least for now, impacts on food prices have not reached the critical levels seen then. Multiple factors are at play: from changes in global demand to more active trade policies. But one of the most relevant is the consolidation of the bioeconomy as a production standard and biotechnology as its main component.

Applied to agriculture, this not only increases yields but also provides flexibility in changing contexts, improves input efficiency, and contributes to more stable supply. It builds bridges with energy through the development of biofuels, biomaterials, and certain ecosystem services. Additionally, applied to new value chains linked to biomass, they are beginning to gain scale and economic relevance.

On the energy side, a shift in many countries' strategies is also beginning to be observed, favoring reduced energy dependence, both as fuel or industrial input; "green chemistry" is beginning to consolidate in that direction. And all of this without sacrificing food security. The bioeconomy appears as a possible bridge between both needs, articulating production, technology, and innovation.

Of course, not everything is linear, nor is it free of tensions. What happened with fertilizer prices offers lessons on multiple aspects and forces us to look at the system as a whole. And it reaffirms that without the bioeconomy, options would be more limited and the impact of global crises much more severe.

Intuitively: the advance of the bioeconomy across a varied range of agricultural and industrial activities operates as insurance — in crises — and as a driver of long-term growth and sustainability — in booms.

From this emerge possible lessons for Argentina. It is clear that this is not about "Living Cow" or "Dead Cow." It is not about choosing, but about properly complementing both "corrals," knowing that each can contribute positively to different aspects (foreign currency, tax revenue, local anchoring, employment). The challenge is to integrate both strategies.

In this sense, a strategic path seems to be emerging where, on one hand, the country consolidates itself as a reliable supplier of fossil energy in a predictably demanding international context. And on the other, the bioeconomy is strengthened, attracting investments (in food, bioenergies, and their derivatives), adding value to exportable commodities, expanding the density of the productive matrix, and generating employment throughout the territory.

Global experience indicates that successful processes start from better leveraging competitive sectors, and this would be the case here. The sectors involved have strong competitive bases, but it is not just about leveraging them, but going further through building stable rules of the game, encouraging investment, and strengthening innovation systems.

The opportunity exists, but it is not automatic; for it to be effective, approaches must recognize the implicit characteristics of each sector. In the "dead cow" — where, perhaps, we should also include mining — significant progress has been made in recent times, which is already reflected in huge volumes of investment promises; in the "living cow," the issue is still a pending agenda, and we have not gone beyond celebrating record volumes of commodity production for export. Undoubtedly, a pending agenda requiring urgent attention.

In a more unstable world, that combination could be one of the country's main strengths. And, if well managed, a solid foundation for more balanced and sustained development over time.
