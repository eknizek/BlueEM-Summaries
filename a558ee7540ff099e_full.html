# Vaca Viva + Vaca Muerta: the strategy that could shield Argentina

The Middle East conflict may reorder key economic variables, with energy and food repositioned. They warn that without the bioeconomy, options would be more limited and the impact of global crises much more severe. The bioeconomy acts as insurance in crises—like the current one caused by the Middle East war—and as an inducer of growth and long-term sustainability during booms.

**By Eduardo Trigo and Roberto Bisang**

The Middle East conflict can reorder key economic variables—especially oil and gas prices—and open a strategic window for the bioeconomy, as we noted in a recent article. Although the conflict is still unfolding and its final consequences remain uncertain, one conclusion seems clear: having productive and energy alternatives is no longer optional but inevitably crucial in future agendas.

But there is another point less visible and more structural. It is not just about energy; what happens "upstream" in agricultural production also matters, which is central to food supply and other derivatives. In this area, the role of biotechnology as the engine of the bioeconomy is crucial.

In Argentina and much of Mercosur, that role was decisive: the incorporation of technologies like genetically improved seeds and direct seeding allowed production to expand and efficiency to increase sustainably. Without that technological leap, the current scale of regional agriculture would hardly have been possible. It also would not have been possible to sustain increasing levels of exports in increasingly volatile international contexts without the presence of this "live cow."

This point becomes especially relevant when linked to food prices. The 2008 crisis showed how shocks can quickly spread from energy to food, with deep economic and social consequences. In that context, greater agricultural supply generated by biotechnology helped moderate the impact. Without that contribution, international prices would have been even higher and social tensions more intense, especially in net food-importing countries.

Today, in a different but no less challenging scenario, shocks in energy and food intersect again. Market disruption is even greater than then, but at least for now, impacts on food prices have not reached critical levels as they did then. Multiple factors are at play: from changes in global demand to more active trade policies. But one of the most relevant is the consolidation of the bioeconomy as a productive standard and biotechnology as its main component.

Applied to agriculture, it not only increases yields but also provides flexibility in the face of changing contexts, improves the efficiency of input use, and contributes to more stable supply. It builds bridges with energy through the development of biofuels, biomaterials, and certain ecosystem services. Additionally, applied to new value chains linked to biomass, they are beginning to gain scale and economic relevance.

On the energy side, changes are also beginning to be observed in the strategies of many countries, in favor of reducing energy dependence, whether as fuel or industrial input; in that direction, "green chemistry" is beginning to consolidate. And all this without compromising food security. The bioeconomy appears as a possible bridge between both needs, articulating production, technology, and innovation.

Of course, not everything is linear, nor is it free of tensions. What happened with fertilizer prices teaches lessons on multiple aspects and forces us to look at the system as a whole. And it reaffirms that without the bioeconomy, options would be more limited and the impact of global crises much more severe.

Intuitively: the advance of the bioeconomy across a varied range of agricultural and industrial activities operates as insurance—in crises—and as an inducer of growth and long-term sustainability—in booms.

From this emerge potential lessons for Argentina. It becomes clear that it is not about "Live Cow" or "Dead Cow." It is not about choosing, but about properly complementing both "corrals," knowing that each can contribute positively to different aspects (foreign exchange, tax revenue, local anchoring, employment). The challenge is to integrate both strategies.

In this sense, a strategic path seems to be emerging where, on one hand, the country consolidates itself as a reliable supplier of fossil energy in a predictably demanding international context. And on the other, the bioeconomy is strengthened, attracting investments (in food, bioenergies, and their derivatives), adding value to exportable commodities, broadening the density of the productive matrix, and generating employment throughout the territory.

Global experience indicates that successful processes start from better leveraging of competitive sectors, and this would be the case here. The sectors involved have strong competitive bases, but it is not just about leveraging them but going further, through building stable rules of the game, encouraging investment, and strengthening innovation systems.

The opportunity exists, but it is not automatic; for it to be effective, the approaches must recognize the implicit characteristics of each sector. In the "dead cow"—where we might also include mining—there has been significant progress in recent times, which is already being reflected in enormous volumes of investment promises; in the "live cow," the issue is still a pending agenda, and we have not gone beyond celebrating record volumes of commodity production for export. Undoubtedly, a pending agenda requiring urgent attention.

In a more unstable world, that combination can be one of the country's main strengths. And, if well-managed, a solid foundation for more balanced and sustained development over time.
