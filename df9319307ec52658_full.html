# Gol seeks to join Azul-American antitrust process as third interested party

BRASÍLIA - Gol is expected to request admission as a third interested party in the operation involving Azul and American Airlines, which is under analysis by the General Superintendence of the Administrative Council for Economic Defense (SG-Cade), according to reporting by Estadão/Broadcast. When contacted, Gol did not comment.

The operation, which consists of American Airlines' acquisition of a stake in the Brazilian airline, was formally notified to the antitrust agency by the airlines in early April, approximately two months after Cade's plenary approved an increase in the minority stake held by United Airlines in Azul — which rose from 2.02% to approximately 8%.

Earlier, the Institute for Research and Studies on Society and Consumer (IPSConsumo) requested admission as a third interested party. The institute sees signs of premature integration among the companies — American Airlines, United Airlines, and Azul — an illegal practice known as gun jumping.

Among the signs of premature integration identified by IPS are the election of American executive Jeff Ogar to Azul's board of directors and strategic committee; the signing of a warrant subscription contract, which grants American the right to acquire equity stake; and public statements by Azul executives indicating prior participation by American and United in strategic decisions during the company's judicial recovery process (Chapter 11) in the United States.

Azul alleges in the proceedings that negotiations with American would be independent and uncertain.

IPSConsumo also requested the initiation of an administrative procedure to investigate the concentration act (Apac), with the application of a fine for the period when the American-Azul operation was announced to the market as completed without having been notified and approved by Cade.

Within the scope of the process, Cade will be responsible for assessing whether there was premature consummation of the operation before notification. If gun jumping acts are confirmed, the agency can apply sanctions, including fines to the companies involved.

When Azul's operation with United was approved by Cade's court in February this year, the case's rapporteur, currently interim president Diogo Thomson, presented some reservations involving governance and compliance commitments. He signaled that if and when American Airlines enters Azul's structure, Cade would conduct a more in-depth competitive analysis, including evaluation of the need to adopt mitigating measures as a condition for its approval.

"The competitive scenario could be substantially altered in the event of American Airlines' effective entry into Azul's corporate structure," he noted.
