# Pinheiro Family Surpasses 50% Stake in Hapvida and Will Sell Southern Operations

The Pinheiro family has increased its position in Hapvida, now holding over 50% of the company's capital. The family was already the practical controller of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares, but now also holds the stake formally through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The move was intended to consolidate control and try to stem the decline in the company's share price, but there is no intention to take the company private, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

In an effort to counter criticism and improve financial performance, the health operator has made changes to its administration and has decided to sell its operations in the South of the country. BTG has been hired to find a buyer for the region's assets.

The Southern operations consist of assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho, plus three hospitals built by the company. The total comprises eight hospitals, 21 clinics, and 490,000 lives. If segregated, this would make it the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure, paying R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus additional investments in developed facilities.

The divestment was already under debate on the board before the letter from the Squadra fund, sent to the company on April 1st, which described Hapvida as the "greatest destruction of shareholder value in the Brazilian capital market" and pointed to the sale of Southern operations as one way to simplify the company's structure and reduce leverage.

CEO Jorge Pinheiro responded with a letter to the market on April 6th, providing a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I acknowledge, however, that recent financial results have fallen short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he stated.

Pinheiro is stepping down as chief executive officer and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives, and a new pace," Pinheiro commented, noting he will remain on the board.

The company has also made changes in other strategic management positions, bringing executives from the market. On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
