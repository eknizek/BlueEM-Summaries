# Pinheiro Family Crosses 50% at Hapvida and Plans Southern Operations Sale

The Pinheiro family has increased its stake in Hapvida to over 50% in recent weeks as the company's stock has declined and its management has faced criticism from shareholder Squadra. The Pinheiros were already practical controllers of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares, but now also hold this position formally through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The increase was aimed at consolidating control and trying to contain the stock's decline, but there are no intentions to take the company private, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

In an effort to address criticism and improve financial performance, the health insurance operator has made administrative changes and has now decided to sell its Southern operations. BTG was hired to find a buyer for the assets in that region, according to Pipeline's reporting.

The Southern operation comprises assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho plus three hospitals built by the company, totaling eight hospitals, 21 clinics, and 490,000 lives. If segregated, it would be the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$4 billion in this structure, paying R$2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

Squadra had pointed to the sale of Southern operations as one alternative for Hapvida to simplify its structure and reduce leverage in an extensive letter sent the previous week describing Hapvida as the "largest destruction of value in the Brazilian capital markets." According to a source close to Hapvida, the divestment was already under board discussion before Squadra's letter was sent on April 1st.

On Monday, April 6th, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also sent a letter to the market offering a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I acknowledge, however, that recent financial results fell short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he assessed.

Pinheiro is stepping down from the position of chief executive officer and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives, and a new pace," commented Pinheiro, who remains on the board.

The company has also made changes to other strategic management positions, bringing in executives from the market. On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
