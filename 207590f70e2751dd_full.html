# Pinheiro family exceeds 50% stake in Hapvida; to sell southern operations

In recent weeks, with Hapvida's stock falling and its guidelines questioned by Squadra, the Pinheiro family increased its stake in the company, surpassing 50% of capital. The Pinheiros were already practical controllers of Hapvida as the largest shareholders with 42% of shares — but now they also hold formal control through a derivative with BTG Pactual bank. The move was to consolidate control and try to stem the decline in the company's shares, but there is no intention to close the capital, according to sources with knowledge of the matter.

In an effort to stem criticism and improve financial performance, the healthcare operator has already made management changes and has now decided to sell its southern region operations. BTG has been hired to find a buyer for the region's assets, according to Pipeline reporting.

The southern operation consists of assets acquired from Clinipam and Centro Clínico Gaúcho plus three hospitals built by the company — totaling eight hospitals, 21 clinics and 490,000 lives. If segregated, it would be the ninth largest operator in the market and the second largest in the region. Hapvida invested R$ 4 billion in this structure. The company paid R$ 2.6 billion for Clinipam in 2020 and R$ 1.06 billion for Centro Clínico in 2021, plus investments in developed units.

In the extensive letter written by manager Squadra last week — which described Hapvida as the "greatest destruction of shareholder value in the Brazilian capital market" — the sale of southern operations was pointed out as one of the company's alternatives to simplify its structure and reduce leverage. According to a source close to Hapvida, the divestment was already being debated on the board before Squadra's letter, sent to the company on April 1.

On Monday, April 6, CEO Jorge Pinheiro also expressed himself in a letter to the market, offering a more optimistic retrospective of the company's recent history. "I recognize, however, that recent financial results have fallen short of what we are capable of delivering. We could have done more and better," he assessed.

He is stepping down from the CEO position and will be replaced by current CFO Luccas Adib. "The choice of Luccas as the next CEO reflects our conviction that the future requires new competencies, new perspectives and a new pace," said Pinheiro, who remains on the board. The company has also made changes to other strategic management positions, bringing in market executives.

On Tuesday, the stock fell 3.9%. Over 12 months, the decline is 67%.
