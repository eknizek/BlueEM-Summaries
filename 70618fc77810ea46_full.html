# Roncador Lost Money in Its Mines. Then It Discovered 'a Periodic Table'

In the office of Pelerson Penido Dalla Vecchia, in the heart of Vila Madalena in São Paulo, samples of rocks and crushed stones are far more than mere decoration. Better known as Peleco, the rural producer and CEO of Grupo Roncador enthusiastically shows the differences between rocks, their geological characteristics—and, most importantly, the list of macronutrients they contain. If once Peleco was obsessed with what lay above the earth—and how to make agriculture more productive and sustainable—now he turns his attention to what lies below the soil.

Grupo Roncador will enter the fertilizer market later this year with a natural remineralizer extracted from its limestone mines in Mato Grosso, at a time when the input market is suffering from the instability of war in Iran.

For decades, Fazenda Roncador held the title of being "Brazil's largest," with 150,000 hectares in the municipality of Querência—an area equivalent to twice the size of New York City, including all five boroughs. The property was purchased in the late 1970s by Peleco's grandfather, Pelerson Soares Penido, who made his fortune with the construction company Serveng (which worked on Brasília's construction), founded the bus company Pássaro Marrom, and participated in creating CCR (now Motiva).

Located in the Araguaia Valley, a transition region between the Cerrado and the Amazon, the giant farm was also a symbol of extensive cattle ranching, when it had up to 100,000 head of cattle and 80% of its area with degraded soil. When he assumed leadership of the group in the early 2000s, Peleco implemented crop-livestock integration, a sustainable production strategy that combines both activities in the same area.

"Our agriculture doesn't exist without our cattle ranching. And our cattle ranching doesn't exist without our agriculture," the CEO told Brazil Journal.

The property also began using biological inputs and developing new soil recovery technologies. In 2021, following the death of Rosa Penido, Peleco's mother, Roncador was divided among the producer and his two siblings, Caio and Eduarda. Pelerson then received an area of 53,000 hectares and the group's infrastructure operations, including the two limestone mines, also in Mato Grosso.

Even before the group's division, mining was considered a bad business. The geological formation of the mines, in which other sedimentary rocks overlap in layers, made it difficult to extract high-grade limestone for soil correction, an input applied to almost all rural properties in Brazil. Roncador's partners decided in 2017 that it was better to sell the assets and delegated the task to Peleco.

"It was only costs. It made no money. To contain losses, we started making gravel for construction," Peleco recalls, who had to abandon his rodeo competitions after an accident. "On the other hand, we were looking for something that could replace potassium chloride, one of the most used fertilizers in Brazilian agriculture."

Much of the potassium chloride used in Brazilian agribusiness is imported and brings geopolitical instabilities into Brazilian farms. Russia is one of the world's largest potassium producers, and with the war in Ukraine in 2022, the price of potassium chloride rose 50% right at the beginning of the conflict. And in 2025, the Russians threatened to apply export quotas to give preference to the domestic market.

Another problem with potassium chloride is its carbon footprint. From extraction in Russia to the product delivered in Mato Grosso, which includes maritime transport and traveling long distances on Brazilian highways, the emissions from potassium chloride reach 570 kilos of CO2 equivalent per hectare.

It was then that Peleco decided to study the mines the group wanted to sell more carefully. In partnership with the Federal University of Goiás, the group conducted geochemical analyses and discovered that those rocks contained macronutrients such as potassium, magnesium, silicon, and phosphorus, in addition to calcium.

"We discovered almost a periodic table," Peleco said.

It remained to be seen whether those rocks, after being crushed and transformed into a very fine powder, would make a difference for agricultural activity. A first test with 500 hectares in 2020 brought surprising results: in areas where the rock powder replaced potassium chloride, nine more sacks of soybeans were harvested.

Since then, Grupo Roncador has tested different concentrations of "silicalc," as the product was registered in 2025, in total or partial substitution for potassium chloride. Today, the rock powder is applied to the 53,000 hectares of the Querência farm, on land the group leases in Mato Grosso and properties in the interior of São Paulo.

Today, the company harvests approximately 1.2 million sacks of soybeans per year. In Peleco's calculations, the cost of silicalc in soybean fields is R$700 per hectare compared to R$463 for potassium chloride. The difference is that the natural remineralizer has delivered between nine and ten more sacks of grain, which generates extra revenue, on average, of R$1,000 per hectare.

The application of the product in pasture areas also showed improvement in grass growth and quality, allowing an increase in the occupation rate of cattle areas and productivity per animal. In 2025, Roncador achieved 58 arrobas per hectare, compared to 14 arrobas in 2020. For context: the average for cattle farms in Brazil is 4.9 arrobas per hectare.

With a herd of 70,000 head of cattle, the company sold 55,000 animals for slaughter last year.

The result is not the exclusive merit of silicalc, but of its combined use with other regenerative technologies such as "forest soup"—a compound prepared from forest substrate and enhanced with silage in a biofactory within the farm itself.

"We are always seeking to reduce our risk matrix in relation to chemicals from large multinationals. In 2025, we reduced the quantity of active ingredients per hectare by 81% compared to chemicals," Peleco said.

The bet on silicalc led Peleco to pay R$30 million for two more mineral exploration licenses in areas near the current ones. Roncador is now prepared to produce 2 million tons of silicalc per year, and commercialization of the product begins in the second half.

Peleco believes his natural remineralizer will reach farther with the inauguration of the first section of the Mato Grosso State Railway, a Rumo project scheduled to begin operations in the second half.

"Many producers will take their soybeans to the railway's dry port and we can benefit from return freight, reaching regions up to 1,000 kilometers away," Peleco said. Besides Grupo Roncador, he has a stake in Raiar, an organic egg company, and invests in real estate projects by developer Idea!Zarvos.

In an agribusiness historically dependent on inputs from large multinationals, Peleco's bet is different.
