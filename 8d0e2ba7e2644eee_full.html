The "announced death" of the app law: the bill returns to square one and frustrates platforms

# The "Announced Death" of the App Law: Bill Returns to Square One and Frustrates Platforms

Congress, digital service platforms for transportation and delivery, and commercial sector companies have declared the "death" of the bill regulating app-based work in the country, following the government's withdrawal just before the vote in the Chamber, which was scheduled for Tuesday, April 14. As a result, the tendency is for the subject to be debated again only if the government bets on its own proposal, that is, from the Executive branch. And even if it sends a text to the Legislature before the elections, the resumption of the subject would still depend on the political scenario. In the event of a possible defeat for President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's (PT) reelection project, it would also be unlikely that the Executive would want to involve itself with the issue, according to sources who directly participated in the project's negotiations.

"The bill will no longer advance and has returned to square one. We will wait to see if the government sends a new project," Deputy Augusto Coutinho (Republicanos-PE), who was the bill's rapporteur in a special commission in the Chamber, told NeoFeed. "Since the government doesn't want it to move forward, let it send a project now. The lack of regulation is now on its account," he added.

Curiously, the government was betting on the project as a strategy to attract app delivery workers and drivers—more associated with Bolsonarism—to its political base. In 2024, it even presented a project (PLP 12) on the subject. Coutinho and Chamber President Hugo Motta (Republicanos-PB) had even closed an agreement to put the report to a vote this week. The idea was to vote on it by tomorrow both in the commission on the bill and in the plenary. But the government's leader in the House, José Guimarães (PT-CE), asked that the text be removed from the agenda. Coutinho also reported that the Ministries of Social Security and Labor were aligned in supporting the text regarding the social protection measures contained in the project.

"For the worker it was fantastic, but it was cheap populism from the government." There is an estimate of social security revenue of R$ 8 billion per year with the potential entry of these workers into the Brazilian social security system, according to calculations made by economist José Roberto Afonso from Finance Consultoria. The social security inclusion of approximately 2.5 million workers also pleased the Ministry of Finance, NeoFeed found. Ending informality for delivery workers and drivers was one of the objectives of the new law.

"The project is dead, forget about it," said a source involved in recent negotiations around the project.

Behind the withdrawal, the biggest opponent within the government was Minister Guilherme Boulos (General Secretariat). He did not give up on a fixed value of R$ 10 per ride, above what the rapporteur agreed to set in all versions of his report presented since last year. In his latest opinion, released last week, Coutinho maintained R$ 8.50 for short-distance rides and also proposed an alternative compensation method: by hours worked, which pleased the platforms and guaranteed some support for the vote, despite remaining resistance to certain parts of the text.

In a video released Tuesday morning, April 10, on his social networks, Boulos stated that the government did not support the bill because of five reasons: it excluded the R$ 10 minimum per ride and still left "loopholes" for platforms to pay by time; it did not guarantee a 30% night shift bonus for trips and bonuses for Sundays and holidays; the proposal keeps as "trade secret" how apps distribute deliveries and rides; it keeps confidential the amount charged to customers and the amount owed to workers; and it does not provide support points where drivers and delivery workers can shower, rest, and charge their phones.

"The Lula government does not support the final version of PLP 152 because simply it does not meet what was mainly requested by the category," Boulos stated. "We will continue standing with Uber drivers, iFood delivery workers, in the fight for work dignity and better compensation."

## Companies Struggling

Fernando de Paula, director of institutional relations at the National Restaurant Association (ANR), says he regrets that the project was not voted on, after practically three years of discussion with the government, platforms, and businesspeople, plus a working group and intense proceedings in Congress. For him, the lack of regulation for app-based work creates a vacuum in Brazilian legislation and leaves uncovered a sector associated with technology (delivery services) that depends on constant innovation.

According to de Paula, in the case of restaurants, for example, the delivery service has characteristics of flexible working hours and does not fit the traditional CLT model (signed employment). "The project was mature, but by a will of the government, which we don't quite understand well, they decided to withdraw support for the text. It's a very big disappointment," he says. "I believe it will be difficult to resume this bill from where it got to, but what cannot happen is the market continuing without regulation of labor."

The ANR, along with other business entities in the segment, recently signed a manifesto for the approval of the project reported by Deputy Augusto Coutinho. Meanwhile, Paulo Solmucci Júnior, executive president of the Brazilian Association of Bars and Restaurants (Abrasel), believes that even with the Planalto Palace's withdrawal, there is still room for political negotiation to save the project's approval, as long as certain points in the text are corrected. He also said he will meet with rapporteur Augusto Coutinho to discuss ways to keep the project alive.

"With the government's withdrawal of support, if we remove that delivery charge and focus on [delivery] by hour, provide social security and accident insurance, everything points to the process being approved," he pondered. "We are not giving up, not at all."
