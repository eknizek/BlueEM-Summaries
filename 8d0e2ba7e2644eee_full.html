Gig economy regulation bill faces uncertainty as Brazil government backs down before vote

14 April 2026 · 07:10 PM

# Gig Economy Regulation Bill Faces Uncertainty as Brazil Government Backs Down Before Vote

Congress, digital service platforms for transportation and delivery, and commercial sector companies have declared the "death" of the bill (PL) regulating app-based work in the country after the government withdrew its support on the eve of the vote in the Chamber of Deputies, which was scheduled for Tuesday, April 14.

As a result, the tendency is that the subject will only be debated again if the government bets on its own proposal, that is, from the Executive branch. And even if it sends a text to the Legislature before the elections, resuming the matter would still depend on the political scenario. In the event of a possible defeat of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's (PT) reelection project, it would also be unlikely that the Executive would want to get involved with the issue, according to sources who directly participated in the project negotiations.

"The PL will no longer advance and has returned to square one. We will wait to see if the government sends a new project," deputy Augusto Coutinho (Republicanos-PE), who was the rapporteur of the project in the special commission in the Chamber, told NeoFeed. "Since the government doesn't want it to move forward, let it send a project now. The lack of regulation is now his responsibility," he added.

Curiously, the government was betting on the project as a strategy to attract app delivery workers and drivers, more associated with Bolsonarism, to its political base. And in 2024, it even presented a project (PLP 12) on the subject.

Coutinho and Chamber President Hugo Motta (Republicanos-PB) had reached an agreement to put the report to a vote that week. The idea was to vote on it both in the commission on the PL and in the plenary by tomorrow. But the government's leader in the House, José Guimarães (PT-CE), asked that the text be removed from the agenda.

Coutinho also reported that the Ministries of Social Security and Labor were aligned in supporting the text regarding the social protection measures contained in the project. "For the worker it was fantastic, but it was cheap populism from the government."

There is an estimate of social security collection of R$ 8 billion per year with the potential entry of these workers into the Brazilian social security system, according to calculations made by economist José Roberto Afonso from Finance Consultoria. The social security inclusion of approximately 2.5 million workers also pleased the Ministry of Finance, NeoFeed found.

Ending informality for delivery workers and drivers was one of the objectives of the new law.

"The project died, forget about it," said a source involved in the negotiations around the project in recent weeks.

Behind the withdrawal, the biggest opponent within the government was Minister Guilherme Boulos (General Secretariat). He refused to give up a fixed value of R$ 10 per ride, above what the rapporteur agreed to set in all versions of his report presented since last year.

In his latest opinion, released last week, Coutinho maintained R$ 8.50 for short-distance rides and also proposed an alternative compensation method: by hours worked, which pleased the platforms and guaranteed some support for the vote, despite resistances at other points in the text.

In a video released on the morning of Tuesday, the 10th, on his social media, Boulos stated that the government did not support the PL because of five reasons: it excluded the R$ 10 per ride minimum wage and still left a "loophole" for platforms to pay by time; it did not guarantee an additional 30% night premium for trips and additional premium for Sundays and holidays; the proposal keeps as "trade secret" the way applications distribute deliveries and rides; it keeps secret the value charged to the customer and the value owed to the worker; and it does not provide support points for drivers and delivery workers to shower, rest, and charge their phones.

"The Lula government does not support the final version of PLP 152, because simply it does not meet what was mainly requested by the category," Boulos said. "We will continue alongside Uber drivers and iFood delivery workers, fighting for work dignity and better compensation."

**Companies on their heels**

Fernando de Paula, director of institutional relations at the National Association of Restaurants (ANR), says he regrets that the project was not voted on after practically three years of discussion with government, platforms, and entrepreneurs, in addition to a working group and intense processing in Congress.

For him, the lack of regulation for app-based work creates a vacuum in Brazilian legislation and leaves uncovered a sector associated with technology (delivery services) that depends on constant innovation. According to de Paula, in the case of restaurants, for example, the delivery service has characteristics of flexible working hours and does not fit with the traditional CLT model (formal employment).

"The project was mature, but by a will of the government that we don't understand very well, they decided to withdraw support for the text. It's a great disappointment," he says. "I believe it will be difficult to resume this PL from where it got to, but what cannot happen is for the market to continue without regulation of labor."

The ANR, together with other business entities in the segment, had signed a manifesto in recent days for the approval of the project reported by deputy Augusto Coutinho.

Paulo Solmucci Júnior, executive president of the Brazilian Association of Bars and Restaurants (Abrasel), believes that, even with the Palácio do Planalto's withdrawal, there is still room for political negotiation to save the project's approval, provided some points in the text are corrected. He also said he will meet with rapporteur Augusto Coutinho to discuss a way to keep the project alive.

"With the government's withdrawal of support, if we remove that charge per delivery and focus on [delivery] by hour, provide Social Security and accident insurance, everything is in place for the process to be approved," he considered. "We don't give up, no."
