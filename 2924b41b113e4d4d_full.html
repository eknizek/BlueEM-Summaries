# Gloria Defeats Laive in Indecopi: Tribunal Dismisses Unfair Competition Claim Over Yogurt

The dispute between Laive and Gloria over yogurt packaging design raises a recurring question in the market: when does similarity cease to be valid and become unfair competition? The highest tribunal of the National Institute for the Defense of Competition and Protection of Intellectual Property (Indecopi) revoked the sanction imposed on Gloria in the first instance and ruled out that there is a risk of consumer confusion. The decision not only closes the case but redefines the weight of visual elements against the brand and consumer behavior in evaluating such controversies.

## A Conflict Over Product Appearance

The case originated when Laive reported Gloria for redesigning its "Actibio" yogurt, claiming it replicated the visual concept of the "Sbelt" product. According to the complainant company, the combination of colors, graphic layout, and use of fruit images created a similar appearance capable of inducing error about the business origin.

In the first instance, the authority accepted this argument and concluded that there was a probability of confusion among the average consumer, imposing a fine of 21.20 UIT (S/ 116,600, at 2026 rates) and ordering modification of the questioned product packaging. However, the analysis was reviewed in a second instance.

## The Brand as the Axis of Differentiation

When evaluating the case, the Court of Competition Defense and Intellectual Property Protection centered its analysis on a key element: the brand. In both products, the brands "Laive" and "Gloria" appear prominently, accompanied by sub-brands "Sbelt" and "Actibio," respectively. This joint presence allows clear identification of each product's business origin.

Differences in denomination, typography, and graphic construction are sufficient to prevent the consumer from assuming they are from the same manufacturer. Additionally, both brands are considered to have significant market positioning in Peru, which strengthens their differentiation capacity.

## Similarities That Do Not Constitute Infringement

The tribunal also addressed the visual coincidences mentioned in the complaint. While acknowledging that both packages share elements such as the use of green color, white background, or fruit imagery, it concluded that these resources are common in the yogurt market. These are generic elements that cannot be exclusively appropriated by one company, especially in categories linked to healthy products.

Thus, it was clarified that evaluation should not focus on isolated coincidences but on the overall impression received by the consumer.

## The Consumer as a Point of Reference

Another determining aspect in the analysis was the consumer's perspective. The public for these products does not act automatically but can identify relevant differences, especially when brands are visible and recognized. Indecopi considered these to be consumers who value specific attributes, such as nutritional or functional benefits, which reduces the probability of error in purchase decisions.

From this logic, the existence of visual similarities does not necessarily imply a real or potential risk of confusion.

## A Change from the First Instance

Based on this comprehensive analysis, the Indecopi tribunal revoked the previous resolution and declared the complaint unfounded. Consequently, it vacated the imposed fine and the corrective measure that required Gloria to modify the product packaging. This reversal shows a difference in criteria from the first instance, which prioritized the visual impact of packaging, while the Court gave greater weight to the brand and consumption context.

## A Criterion on the Limits of Imitation

The resolution reaffirms that imitation is part of competitive play, as long as it does not generate confusion about business origin. In markets where certain designs tend to become standardized, differentiation falls primarily on distinctive signs. Thus, the case establishes that it is not enough to prove visual similarities to constitute an infringement; it is necessary to demonstrate that these actually affect the consumer's ability to identify who is behind the product.

## Brand Recognition and Consumer Behavior

Alex Sosa, partner at the Muñiz law firm, adds that brand recognition plays a determining role in decision-making. In his view, in mass consumption categories like yogurts, choice is usually previously influenced by the brand, which limits the possibility of error about business origin. In this context, he notes that brand recognition not only differentiates products but acts as a "filter" that guides consumer decisions even in environments with high visual similarity.

## Market Standards and Protection Limits

Finally, Sosa warns that extending protection over common elements—such as colors or images associated with health—could generate undue restrictions on competition. From his perspective, the ruling is relevant because it prevents the monopolization of visual resources that form part of the category standard.
