# From Marriage to Marriage: The Final Miscellaneous Mile of the Mega-Project in Finance Ministry

On Saturday, April 11, Finance Minister Jorge Quiroz attended the wedding of his right-hand man, Economic Regulation coordinator and head of advisors, Tomás Bunster. They had to balance the post-party festivities with the pressure of the frenetic final adjustments and skirmishes at Teatinos 120 in preparation for stamping the final version of the mega-bill on which the Kast administration has staked much of its fate. As of tomorrow or at the latest the day after, it will be submitted to that uncertain struggle in Congress.

While Bunster—they worked together at their consulting firm Quiroz and Associates—entered honeymoon mode this week, the team most involved in the final phase has spread across several fronts: drafting, editing, and final review of the bill; protecting against a possible constitutional challenge; fragile dealings with parliamentarians; vote polling; and an endless list of initiatives with as many different names as measures it contains.

Such effort was not enough to prevent another government stumble: the confusion over whether to include a measure limiting free higher education to those under 30 years old. After public contradictions between the Interior Minister (who had ruled it out) and the Finance Minister (who had confirmed it), it was only on Friday that it was announced that both, along with the Social Development Secretary, finally decided to leave it out. Chamber President Felipe Alessandri called them to "coordinate better and deliver a single message."

This mess exposed new differences between Quiroz and the duo of political ministers, Claudio Alvarado (Interior) and José García (Social Development). Consequently, rumors have swirled. As far as is known, the decision to exclude the measure was made in the "premium" Political Committee—the President and ministers, including Finance—on Monday the 13th. Hours later, as happens every Monday, lunch was held at Teatinos 120 with government legislators from those sector commissions. On Tuesday, the President, Alvarado, García, and Quiroz met again, this time with parliamentarians from the sector at the Cerro Castillo summit. The next day, Wednesday, Kast didn't even mention the controversial measure in his national address, in line with Monday's decision. While the broadcast aired his message, Quiroz attended the Cesco Week 2026 dinner. In the memo that Presidency sent to the media that night, the only mention of free education was in the final paragraph, but it referred to the "moratorium on entry of new institutions into free higher education," which it proposes to suspend "for four years." According to Finance, at the end of this whole soap opera, that is all that will remain in the package.

And we arrive at Thursday, when Alvarado announced that the age limit was out, while Quiroz said the opposite in his press conference. According to Finance, the "lack of coordination" occurred because when that conference was being prepared, the idea was to say things "different" from those in the national address... but they hadn't taken into account (or hadn't realized) that there was a prior agreement. Other suspicious minds at La Moneda believe this was because Quiroz never agreed with removing the age limit and that his statement at the conference was a way to test the waters to keep it. That led to the Finance-Interior-Social Development summit that same afternoon to resolve the problem.

As this edition closed, the bill had about 70 pages and 43 measures. They had tried to ensure that the final substantive changes wouldn't go past Wednesday the 15th, because otherwise they would have to also edit the financial report that accompanies the initiative. Those who have mainly worked on drafting the bill—besides Quiroz, who oversees many details—are Bunster; Alejandro Guin-Po Bon, macroeconomic coordinator; Bárbara Bayolo, legislative coordinator; Sebastián Vallebona, tax policy coordinator; and Rudy Canales, the minister's chief of staff. There have been days when they stayed until after 11 p.m. The financial report has been in the hands of José Ignacio Llodrá Vial, subdirector of Rationalization and Public Function at the Budget Directorate, and DIPRES itself, José Pablo Gómez. Perhaps given everything that has happened, Friday still had "versions circulating with tracked changes." By the end of this week, the bill has been subject to "revisions," by Finance and Social Development teams. There are about four people, potentially more than 10 depending on the subject needed. Before that, they had to work to ward off the danger that the opposition would have more arguments to file a constitutional challenge, invoking breaches related to the bill's core ideas and message. Although people experienced in this assure that this review is usually done, they acknowledge that this time they had to double that effort. And that if the opposition "hadn't raised this level of alerts, perhaps we wouldn't have reviewed it under this perspective."

Various people were involved in this task force, including Senator Arturo Squella and the minister's advisor (and former UDI deputy) Felipe Donoso. The President's summit with his congressmembers at Cerro Castillo on Tuesday the 14th was a very separate chapter, when they explained the bill's scope before the national address. It's very separate for various reasons. One is that right-wing legislators who expected to know the final bill exclusively had to settle for the same original text. Another is the commented verbal intervention of the First Lady, Pía Adriasola—among other things, she called for unity—an episode of which nothing similar is remembered in the sector. At the same dinner, Quiroz presented a PowerPoint about the bill and was accompanied by a group of his advisors, who addressed parliamentarians' questions and concerns. Some of these latter have repeatedly seen each other throughout the intense lobbying in Congress; another of the heavy parts of the week has been the back-and-forth with legislators. Although the burden of dealing with both their own and opposing congressmembers usually falls more heavily on Interior and Social Development, Finance has coordinated with both. There have been Bárbara Bayolo, Teatinos 120's legislative coordinator, and Quiroz's advisor Felipe Donoso, working side by side. The first is a lawyer and was legislative director of the Jaime Guzmán Foundation, and the second was a UDI deputy and as such knows how to deal with parliamentarians on both sides: he has spent hours talking with them, telephoning, and sending them memos... with varying results, they confess.

This miscellaneous week of experiences began to close with another wedding, Friday night: that of Alejandro Guin-Po Bon, macroeconomic coordinator. Tomorrow another season of this series begins.
