# United States Maintains Brazil on Intellectual Property Watchlist and Cites March 25 Deadline

The United States has kept Brazil on the intellectual property watchlist of the U.S. Office of the U.S. Trade Representative (USTR), released in a recent report. According to the document, the country remains on the "watch list," a category that brings together commercial partners requiring attention regarding intellectual property protection. The list also includes countries such as Chile, China, India, Indonesia, Russia, and Venezuela, which are on the priority watch list, a more critical level.

Brazil has appeared in the report for decades—at least since the late 1980s, according to records available on the USTR website. This year, the document states that piracy and counterfeiting continue to be central challenges in the country, driven by increased smuggling and the expansion of the volume of small packages entering national territory. The report cites Rua 25 de Março in São Paulo as one of the main markets for counterfeit products. Although recognizing the conduct of police operations, the USTR states that the absence of punishments with deterrent effect contributes to the continuity of illegal trade.

In addition to São Paulo's downtown area, the Port of Santos and the triple border region between Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina are pointed out as strategic points for the entry of goods that violate intellectual property rights.

The 2026 report also addresses streaming in the context of digital piracy and classifies it as one of the greatest challenges for intellectual property protection today. The report highlights the proliferation of piracy devices and services that illegally retransmit protected channels and content through portals and applications. For the U.S., these technologies represent a direct threat to content creators, sports leagues, and legitimate streaming and video-on-demand platforms.

In July of last year, the Trump government, through the USTR, opened a trade investigation against Brazil under Section 301. In opening the investigation, the document justified the need for analysis by citing Rua 25 de Março and criticizing alleged failures in the protection and proper enforcement of intellectual property rights. For the USTR, Rua 25 de Março remains, for decades, one of the largest markets for counterfeit products, despite operations directed at the area. The investigation is the same one that includes Pix, cited as a possible unfair practice by the country regarding electronic payment services. The process began in July of last year and typically lasts a year before the final result is released. However, people close to negotiations claim that it is possible the process could be concluded as early as May.

In the report released this Thursday on intellectual property in the United States, the focus of concern is the effectiveness of customs enforcement and the judicial system. The USTR highlights that an insufficient number of agents at borders and the slowness of judicial processes weaken the fight against crimes of this nature.

Despite positively evaluating an action by the Federal Revenue Service published at the end of 2025—which allows administrative seizures of counterfeit products without the need for judicial action by trademark holders—the report states that it is still unclear whether the measure will have an effective impact on increasing seizures.

In the field of innovation, the United States again criticizes the time for patent analysis in Brazil. Between 2020 and 2025, the average time for granting biopharmaceutical patents was 9.15 years, which, according to the report, undermines the effectiveness of protection for innovation. The document also demands greater protection against improper commercial use of test data for human medications, noting that Brazil already grants this type of protection for veterinary and agricultural products but not for the human pharmaceutical sector.

It also addresses digital piracy and international negotiations, encouraging Brazil to adhere to WIPO treaties on copyright and digital performances to strengthen the fight against illegal streaming content and devices. There is also mention of the agreement between the European Union and Mercosur, with concern that Brazilian rules on geographical indications could restrict the use of terms considered generic in the international market.
