Wall Street Journal strongly criticizes Milei's "radical change": "Corruption allegations, legal problems and economic malaise"

18 April 2026 · 01:33 PM

A recent and extensive article by the influential American newspaper The Wall Street Journal (WSJ) focuses on the current stage of Javier Milei's government, marked by an ambitious program of economic reforms and, at the same time, by signs of political and social wear. Among other factors, it points to corruption suspicions and mentions the Chief of Staff, Manuel Adorni, who appears with a photo in the article. The media outlet — a global reference in financial information and economic analysis — describes Argentina as a test case for free market policies in the region, but warns that the process faces obstacles similar to those that historically conditioned other governments.

Shock Therapy, Stabilization and Social Malaise

The report notes that the "shock therapy" strategy promoted by the Executive achieved concrete advances, such as the reduction of public spending and the slowdown in inflation. However, these achievements have not yet translated into tangible improvements for broad sectors of the population. "Corruption accusations. Legal setbacks. Economic malaise. Disastrous polling results," the article lists when describing the current scenario, in which factors converge that could jeopardize the continuity of the ruling project. In that vein, the WSJ cited specialist Benjamin Gedan, who warns: "Argentines have demonstrated unusual patience, but that patience is running out." The observation synthesizes one of the main concerns: political time versus economic timelines.

A Divided Economy

The analysis also underscores a growing duality in the economy. While exporting sectors such as energy and mining show dynamism, the domestic market remains weak, with falling employment and lagging wages. Economist Mauricio Monge defines it as "two distinct economies coexisting in one," where the growth of certain sectors fails to drive the whole. In turn, the article raises questions about some economic policy decisions, such as the exchange rate strategy, which allegedly limited export competitiveness. Nevertheless, it acknowledges that many of the current difficulties are foreseeable in processes of deep structural reforms.

Scandals and Political Fragility

On the political front, the report warns that corruption allegations and judicial investigations come at a delicate moment. The case linked to the cryptocurrency $LIBRA occupies a central place in this section. According to the article, the case could become a persistent axis of questioning toward the Government. Analyst Rafael Ch argues: "It is likely that the $LIBRA case will keep resurfacing (...) since it is one of the few issues on which the opposition has a specific line of attack." While corruption does not appear among the main social concerns, the text warns that any sign of political weakness could affect investor confidence.

Governing Without a Majority

Another highlighted point is the difficulty the ruling party faces in advancing its agenda in a context of parliamentary weakness. Despite some legislative achievements, the Government faces resistance from unions, courts, and opposition sectors. In that regard, they consulted former Macrist official Bernardo Saravia Frías, who summarized this limitation in a phrase: "These are the limits of governing without consensus and the consequences are already evident."

Regional Impact

The article also places the Argentine case in a broader geopolitical scenario. It mentions international support, particularly from Donald Trump, as part of a strategy to strengthen pro-market governments in Latin America against other global influences. In this context, Argentina's course appears as an experiment with impact beyond its borders.

The report was prepared by journalists Samantha Pearson and Silvina Frydlewsky, who construct a detailed portrait of a management that combines macroeconomic achievements with growing political challenges. The conclusion is clear: the success of the program will depend not only on its technical results, but on the Government's ability to sustain social support over time.
