# Former XP Executive Creates 'Brazilian Maldives' with R$5 Billion Northeast Brazil Investment

A partner from XP's early days as a small autonomous agents office in Porto Alegre, Julio Capua is transforming an as-yet underdeveloped stretch of Ceará's coastline into a luxury destination, complete with a five-star hotel, crystal-blue lagoons, architecturally-designed homes with high-end services, and an audience that already includes well-known names from the financial market.

A kitesurfing enthusiast, Capua left XP after the sale of its stake to Itaú in 2018, wanting to invest in something smaller at that time. Currently, Grupo Carnaúba spans 12 km² in Preá, 15 minutes from Jericoacoara airport, with plans to invest R$2 billion over the next two to three years.

Preá is considered one of the world's best destinations for kitesurfing—the sport combining board and kite—which led the investor to visit the region for the first time in 2014, become interested in the location, and discover opportunities to make the project viable. The first land was purchased in late 2019, a 500,000 m² area facing the beach, where Vila Carnaúba was born.

"Then the pandemic came. Many people put assets up for sale and I saw the opportunity to do something bigger. We bought 12 million m², part on the beach and part further back," the businessman recounts.

The first group of partners came from XP itself: friends Guilherme Benchimol, Gabriel Leal, Carlos Ferreira, and Eduardo Juaçaba. Then came institutional investors, real estate vehicles, and an XP fund with R$200 million in 2023.

"So far, we've used about 700,000 m² of the landbank. We still have over 11 million m² to develop. In 10 years, this could exceed R$5 billion, perhaps more than R$10 billion in total revenue," he calculates.

The first phase of the main condominium, Vila Carnaúba, launched in 2022, saw all 115 lots sold, with 10 already having completed homes and 45 under construction.

One of the challenges was the planned 60,000 m² of lagoons inspired by Lençóis Maranhenses. "At first, we didn't know how to do it. We went to test: digging, understanding water color, how to keep it blue with a white sand bottom. Today we know how to build lagoons at a cost of R$1,000 to R$1,200 per m²," Capua says proudly after completing construction of six of them in the first phase.

The idea came from local experience. "Once the wind picks up, the beach becomes a paradise for sailors but a nightmare for swimmers, reaching 30 knots (55 km/h). We needed to create a water attraction beyond the beach," he explains.

In the current phase, there will be two new lagoons with 32 surrounding lots, plus 24 branded residences managed by the luxury Anantara chain.

"The homes will have the style of their resorts in the Maldives, with decks on the waterfront," says the CEO. The complex's first hotel will also be from the Asian brand of Minor International—the brand's debut in Latin America, with 80 rooms and construction beginning in July.

"The operation stays with the flag, and we keep the asset," he details. The hotel property will be owned by the Wings fund, a R$280 million partnership between Carnaúba's manager and TG Core.

"We're in talks to bring other chains, such as Accor and Fasano. The idea is to consolidate Preá as a complete destination," says Capua, who also counts on subsidized financing from Banco do Nordeste (BNB) for up to 60% of the hotel project.

For those wanting to enjoy without the work of managing a home, Carnaúba Wind House entered operation last season—running from June through Carnival—a hospitality club with 60 rooms, five-star service, and complete leisure infrastructure. Among investors and visitors to the complex are Álvaro Garnero, Alexandre Birman, Pablo Marçal, Tallis Gomes, Flávio Canto, Claude Troisgros, and Felipe Bronze.

Next year, another residential product should be launched, with 200 apartments around R$2 million each.

To guarantee luxury status, with the number of staff it demands and containing irregular housing, Capua wants to launch a popular housing neighborhood to serve employees and potential service providers for the condominium. Vila Cajueiro should begin with a thousand residences in tiers 1 and 3 of the My House, My Life program, with wide streets, cycling path, and planned landscaping, 700 meters from Villa Carnaúba.

"The idea is to ensure that job opportunities are absorbed by people who have somewhere to live, preventing the formation of slums. This project could grow a lot over time and reach 40,000 popular homes over many years, since the land is large," he says.

For Capua, Preá is still in the beginning—and that's what makes the thesis interesting, he argues. "The region is underdeveloped and has much upside. Whoever comes falls in love with it."
