# Former XP Executive Creates Brazilian 'Maldives' with R$5 Billion Northeast Investment

A former partner from XP's early days as a small autonomous agent operation in Porto Alegre, Julio Capua is transforming an unexploited stretch of Ceará's coastline into a luxury destination, complete with a five-star hotel, crystal-blue lagoons, architect-designed homes with premium services, and an audience that already includes notable names from the financial market.

A kitesurfing enthusiast, Capua left XP after the company's stake was sold to Itaú in 2018, wanting to invest in something smaller at that time. Currently, Grupo Carnaúba encompasses 12 km² in Preá, 15 minutes from Jericoacoara airport, with a projected R$2 billion to be executed over two to three years.

Preá is considered one of the world's best destinations for kitesurfing—the sport combining a board and kite—which prompted the investor to visit the region for the first time in 2014, become interested in the location, and discover opportunities to make the project viable. The first land was purchased at the end of 2019, a 500,000 m² area facing the beach, where Vila Carnaúba was born. "Then the pandemic came. Many people put assets up for sale and I saw the opportunity to do something bigger. We bought 12 million m², partly on the beach and partly further back," says the businessman.

The first group of partners came from XP itself: friends Guilherme Benchimol, Gabriel Leal, Carlos Ferreira, and Eduardo Juaçaba. Institutional investors, real estate vehicles, and an XP fund with R$200 million followed in 2023. "So far, we've used about 700,000 m² of our landbank. We still have over 11 million m² to develop. In 10 years, this could exceed R$5 billion, perhaps more than R$10 billion in gross value," he calculates.

The first phase of the main condominium, Vila Carnaúba, launched in 2022, saw all 115 lots sold, with 10 already completed and 45 under construction.

One of the challenges was the planned 60,000 m² of lagoons inspired by the Lençóis Maranhenses. "At first, we didn't know how to do it. We tested: digging, understanding water color, how to make it blue with a white sand bottom. Today we know how to build lagoons at a cost of R$1,000 to R$1,200 per square meter," Capua says proudly after completing six in the first phase. The idea came from local experience. "Once the wind picks up, the beach becomes a paradise for sailors but a nightmare for swimmers, reaching 30 knots (55 km/h). We needed to create a water attraction beyond the beach," he explains.

In the current phase, there will be two new lagoons with 32 surrounding lots, plus 24 branded residences managed by luxury brand Anantara. "The homes will have the style of their resorts in the Maldives, with decks on the waterfront," says the CEO. The project's first hotel will also be from the Asian brand of Minor International—the brand's debut in Latin America, with 80 rooms and construction starting in July. "The operation stays with the brand, and we keep the asset," he details.

The hotel property will be owned by the Wings fund, a R$280 million partnership between Carnaúba's management company and TG Core. "We're in talks to bring other chains, like Accor and Fasano. The idea is to consolidate Preá as a complete destination," says Capua, who also counts on subsidized financing from Banco do Nordeste (BNB) for up to 60% of the hotel project.

For those wanting to enjoy without the work of managing a home, Carnaúba Wind House entered operation last season—which runs from June through the end of Carnival—a hospitality club with 60 rooms, five-star service, and complete leisure infrastructure. Among investors and complex visitors are Álvaro Garnero, Alexandre Birman, Pablo Marçal, Tallis Gomes, Flávio Canto, Claude Troisgros, and Felipe Bronze.

Next year, another residential product should be launched, with 200 apartments around R$2 million each. To guarantee luxury status, with the workforce it demands and containing irregular housing, Capua wants to launch a popular housing neighborhood to serve condominium employees and potential service providers. Vila Cajueiro should begin with a thousand residences in brackets 1 and 3 of the Minha Casa, Minha Vida program, featuring wide streets, bike lanes, and planned landscaping, 700 meters from Villa Carnaúba. "The idea is to ensure that job opportunities are filled by people who have a place to live, avoiding slum formation. This project can grow significantly over time, reaching 40,000 popular homes over many years, since the land is large," he says.

For Capua, Preá is still in its early stages—and that's what makes the thesis interesting, he argues. "The region is underdeveloped and has much upside. Whoever comes falls in love with it."
