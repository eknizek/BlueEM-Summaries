# Venezuela Signs Mining Agreements with US and Swiss Companies

Caracas | AFP

Venezuela signed raw materials and gold mining agreements on Friday (May 1st) with American company Heeney Capital and Swiss firm Mercuria, at a moment when it is opening the exploration of its vast reserves to foreign investors with Washington's approval.

Following the ousting of dictator Nicolás Maduro in a US invasion of Caracas at the beginning of the year, interim Venezuelan leader Delcy Rodríguez took office and anticipated profound changes in state management of natural resources, which had been imposed by Chavism. Rodríguez enacted a new mining law in April, which opens the door to foreign investors, after adopting a similar measure with the oil industry under pressure from the United States.

The Venezuelan Mining Corporation and Heeney Capital, a private equity firm based in New York, signed "a memorandum of understanding on mining matters" before American and Venezuelan officials in Caracas, according to images transmitted by state broadcaster VTV. James Gilbert, general director of investments at Mercuria, a Swiss multinational commodities company based in the Cayman Islands, was present at the ceremony.

On its official website, Mercuria reported having secured, in association with Heeney Capital, a series of strategic offtake agreements in the scope of bulk raw materials and gold mining projects in Venezuela. These are binding long-term contracts, signed before initiating a project, that ensure the acquisition of a significant portion of future production at an agreed price.

The Mercuria agreements, supported by associated investment commitments, are expected to generate approximately $2.2 billion (approximately R$ 11 billion) in annual mineral export value, according to its website.

Known for its enormous oil deposits, Venezuela is also a country rich in minerals such as gold, diamonds, bauxite, and coltan.

Mining activity in Venezuela is concentrated especially in a territory of 112,000 km², named the Mining Arc, an area considered dangerous where organized crime operates. Armed groups manage mining zones with the alleged complicity of authorities, according to various investigations.
