# Cade investigates 99Food following Keeta's complaint

The administrative authority opened an administrative inquiry on Tuesday night to investigate 99Food for suspected anticompetitive practices in the food delivery market, accepting a complaint filed by Keeta — the Chinese Meituan's arm for operating in other countries — in August. The competition authority will analyze exclusivity contracts between restaurants and 99Food, which established "banning clauses" to prevent these establishments from closing any type of commercial partnership with competitors such as Keeta and Rappi.

The market leader iFood is not mentioned in the contracts, which, in the complainant's view — Keeta — constitutes an attempt to form a duopoly in the country's food delivery market. The authority emphasized that there are indications of violations of economic order, including possible abuse of dominant position and restriction of competitors' access to strategic partners. In the preliminary assessment, Cade understood that the practices, if proven, could constitute market closure and hinder the entry or expansion of rivals, which justifies the opening of a formal investigation.

99Food — also Chinese-owned by DiDi — argues that it is a new entrant in the sector and that the clauses are part of a pro-competitive strategy, with incentives such as zero fees, discounts, and better conditions for restaurants and delivery workers. The company also argues that limiting its operations could reinforce iFood's dominance. In a statement, 99 states that Cade "follows the standard protocol of the authority in cases of this nature," "reiterates its commitment to ethical and pro-competition conduct" and will continue with investments in the sector.

Following the opening of the inquiry, Keeta states that "the food delivery segment urgently needs decisions that promote an open market and bring benefits to the entire ecosystem, enabling sustainable growth and innovation."
