Cade Investigates 99Food Following Keeta Complaint

Cade opened an administrative inquiry on Tuesday night to investigate 99Food on suspicion of anti-competitive practices in the delivery market, accepting a complaint filed by Keeta — the arm of Chinese company Meituan operating in other countries — in August. The competition authority will analyze exclusivity contracts between restaurants and 99Food, which included "ban clauses" to prevent these establishments from entering into any commercial partnerships with competitors such as Keeta and Rappi.

Market leader iFood is not mentioned in the contracts, which, in the view of the complainant Keeta, constitutes an attempt to form a duopoly in the country's delivery market. The agency highlighted that there are indications of violations of economic order, including possible abuse of dominant position and restriction of competitors' access to strategic partners. In its preliminary assessment, Cade determined that these practices, if proven, could constitute market foreclosure and hinder the entry or expansion of rivals, justifying the opening of a formal investigation.

99Food — also owned by Chinese company DiDi — argues that it is a new entrant in the sector and that the clauses are part of a pro-competitive strategy, with incentives such as zero fees, discounts, and better conditions for restaurants and delivery drivers. The company also contends that limiting its operations could reinforce iFood's dominance. In a statement, 99Food affirms that Cade "follows the authority's standard protocol in cases of this nature," "reiterates its commitment to ethical and pro-competitive conduct," and will continue its investments in the sector.

Following the opening of the inquiry, Keeta states that "the food delivery segment urgently needs decisions that promote an open market and bring benefits to the entire ecosystem, enabling sustainable growth and innovation."
