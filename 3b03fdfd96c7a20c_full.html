Prices in Metropolitan Lima Rose 4.01% Over 12 Months, Driven by Transportation: Here Are the Reasons

02 May 2026 · 08:14 PM

# Prices in Metropolitan Lima Rose 4.01% Over 12 Months, Driven by Transportation

In April, inflation in Metropolitan Lima increased 0.52% compared to the previous month, accumulating a variation of 3.72% so far this year and year-over-year growth of 4.01%, according to a report from the National Institute of Statistics and Informatics (INEI).

According to the report, the sector that recorded the largest increase was transportation, with a rise of 3.35%, driven mainly by the increase in airfare prices (4.4%), as well as road travel, including combi fares (9.5%), buses and microbuses (8%). Additionally, fuel remained 2.9% more expensive due to increases in oil and gasohol prices, although liquefied petroleum gas reduced its value after the Camisea crisis was resolved.

Other divisions that showed increases were restaurants and hotels (0.57%), diverse goods and services (0.24%), and furniture and household items (0.22%). In contrast, prices fell for non-alcoholic food and beverages (-0.26%), favored by cheaper meat such as eviscerated chicken (-8.2%) and other poultry cuts. Milk, cheese, and eggs also recorded declines.

## What drove inflation in April?

According to specialists consulted by El Comercio, this scenario is mainly due to external factors. In particular, the conflict between Israel, Iran, and the United States has generated a strong shock in oil supply, the impact of which has been passed on to transportation costs.

According to Jorge Gonzales Izquierdo, professor at the University of the Pacific, the transportation sector has accumulated a 15% increase over the past twelve months. "Given the war, the increase was foreseeable and is affecting all countries. Now the year-over-year rate in Metropolitan Lima exceeded 4%, which means we are well above what the Central Reserve Bank of Peru considers normal inflation for Peru," he noted.

For her part, Paola Herrera, senior economist at the Peruvian Institute of Economics, stated that the figure exceeded market expectations. "A maximum year-over-year inflation of between 3% and 3.5% was expected, but the increase turned out to be greater than anticipated. After March and following the resolution of the LPG crisis, the impact of international shocks is beginning to be reflected more clearly," she said.

## We could close 2026 above the target range

Meanwhile, Eduardo Jimenez, head of the Macroconsult Information System, estimated that once the conflict concludes, inflation linked to transportation—which has persisted for two consecutive months—could moderate and close the year near 3%, although there is also the possibility that the rate could be higher.

"There is a high probability that the BCR's expectations for year-end will not be met," he warned.

Concerns about ending December with inflation above the target range persist, because the escalation of the geopolitical conflict, even if it concludes, would not have an immediate effect on reducing oil prices. "This implies that monthly inflation in Peru will continue to be elevated for several months, which will affect the BCR's annual projection," added Gonzales Izquierdo.

If the situation continues, according to Herrera, the chances of adjusting the reference rate upward increase. Although, according to Jimenez, the BCR could take a more cautious decision in this regard, taking into account that it has been maintaining a cycle of cuts.
