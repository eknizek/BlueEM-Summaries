Lima Metropolitan Prices Rose 4.01% Over Past 12 Months, Driven by Transportation Costs

02 May 2026 · 08:14 PM

# Lima Metropolitan Inflation Rose 4.01% Over Past 12 Months, Driven by Transportation Costs

In April, inflation in Metropolitan Lima increased 0.52% compared to the previous month, accumulating a variation of 3.72% so far this year and year-over-year growth of 4.01%, according to a report from the National Institute of Statistics and Informatics (INEI).

According to the report, the sector that recorded the largest increase was transportation, with a rise of 3.35%, driven mainly by the increase in airline ticket prices (4.4%), as well as road travel, including combi fares (9.5%), buses and minibuses (8%). Additionally, fuel remained 2.9% more expensive due to increases in oil and gasohol prices, although liquefied petroleum gas reduced its value after the Camisea crisis was overcome.

Other divisions showing increases were restaurants and hotels (0.57%), miscellaneous goods and services (0.24%), and furniture and household articles (0.22%). In contrast, there was a price reduction in non-alcoholic food and beverages (-0.26%), favored by cheaper meat prices such as eviscerated chicken (-8.2%) and other poultry cuts. Milk, cheese, and eggs also registered declines.

## What Drove Inflation in April?

According to specialists consulted by El Comercio, this scenario is mainly due to external factors. In particular, the conflict between Israel, Iran, and the United States has generated a strong shock to oil supply, whose impact has transferred to transportation costs.

According to Jorge Gonzales Izquierdo, a professor at the University of the Pacific, the transportation sector has accumulated a 15% increase over the past twelve months. "Given the war, the increase was foreseeable and is affecting all countries. Now the year-over-year rate in Metropolitan Lima exceeded 4%, which means we are well above what Peru's Central Reserve Bank considers normal inflation for Peru," he stated.

For her part, Paola Herrera, senior economist at the Peruvian Institute of Economics, stated that the figure exceeded market expectations. "A maximum year-over-year inflation of between 3% and 3.5% was expected, but the increase turned out to be larger than anticipated. After March and following the resolution of the LPG crisis, the impact of international shocks is beginning to be reflected more clearly," she affirmed.

## We Could Close 2026 Above the Target Range

Eduardo Jimenez, head of Macroconsult's Information System, estimated that once the conflict concludes, inflation linked to transportation—which has persisted for two consecutive months—could moderate and close the year near 3%, although there is also the possibility of a higher rate. "There is a high probability that the BCR's expectations for year-end will not be met," he warned.

Concern about ending December with inflation above the target range persists, because the escalation of geopolitical conflict, even if it concludes, would not have an immediate effect on reducing oil prices. "This implies that monthly inflation in Peru will remain elevated for several months, which will affect the BCR's annual projection," added Gonzales Izquierdo.

If the situation continues, Herrera states, the chances of adjusting the reference rate upward increase. However, according to Jimenez, the BCR could take a more cautious decision in this regard, taking into account that it has been maintaining a cycle of rate cuts.
