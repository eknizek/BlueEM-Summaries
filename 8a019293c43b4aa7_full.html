# Former CEOs: From Climbing Corporate Ladders to Helping Startups Cross the River

Surrounded by Berni engravings in an office with beautiful views of the jacaranda trees on Figueroa Alcorta Avenue, six former CEOs have found their calling. Sergio Kaufman, who spent 36 years at Accenture and 12 as CEO, defined it this way: "In corporate life, you enter and climb ladders—first as a department head, then manager, then partner, and eventually the ladders run out. It's an inflection point. We decided not to climb ladders anymore, and we said to ourselves, let's build ladders instead."

The creation of Eolas Capital Partner followed swiftly. The name comes from Celtic mythology, which—unlike Greek tradition that presents Aeolus as the god of winds—attributes to Eolas the power of "knowledge, experience, and wisdom."

The team was assembled with Nerio Peitiado, who headed wholesale banking at Santander; Javier Goñi, who spent 8 years leading Ledesma; Gabriel "the bird" Martino, former CEO of HSBC; lawyer Guillermo Lipera; and PWC consultant and IDEA president Santiago Mignonè. They share a generation: all are between 60 and 65 years old. "We're vintage adolescents," they say with laughter.

When explaining their motivation, they elaborate: "A communion of values and complementary expertise and different experiences. In corporations, we went through deep strategic reflection processes and analogous situations, and we're very eager to help and make projects viable." They describe encountering spectacular ideas from young entrepreneurs that "sometimes won't work," so "guiding them in the right direction is very motivating."

For Eolas, they invested equally from their own pockets but are seeking partnerships with other funds. They claim their difference lies not only in offering capital to startups—something considered a commodity. "What's unique is the accompaniment. It's very difficult to hire someone with thirty years of CEO experience on the market to help you rethink a strategy or how to attack the market. That's our proposal. What matters is helping with implementation."

Another characteristic they offer is their extensive network of personal contacts, which facilitates access to various areas for those just starting out. "We try to go together and cross the first part of the river, where many drown. We're smart money because of the intelligence we bring. Entrepreneurs tend to be very focused on their ideas but get stuck choosing a provider or developing the app, and we establish a very fruitful relationship: they're constantly asking us about their real needs. There's a combination of energy and respect. It's a dynamic and collaborative relationship."

The former CEOs meet weekly to analyze projects. They decide unanimously and designate two partners as mentors for each project. They've already invested in two. And they're about to disburse capital in a third. One is Amar Mascotas, which is evolving into pet insurance in an Argentina where the human birth rate is declining rapidly, with cities like Buenos Aires having more pets than children. The other is SAVE, dedicated to purpose-driven savings. It specializes in savings strategies tailored to each person or household for a specific goal, for example attending the World Cup.

When asked what they look for when choosing projects, they respond: "The idea, the quality of leadership, the team, thinking big with Latin America as the first step. And the capacity to execute and implement those good ideas. We get involved."

Regarding the country's environment for project development, they note: "Today the conditions are in place for businesses to develop, even though we're all waiting for consumption to pick up. From our experience in leading firms, those who did well are the ones who kept going regardless of timing. Without overexposing themselves, without taking excessive risks, but those who truly built were always doing, regardless of context. We're not financial investors; we're knowledge investors."
