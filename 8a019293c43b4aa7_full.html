# Former CEOs: From Climbing Corporate Ladders to Helping Startups Cross the River

Surrounded by Berni engravings in an office with a beautiful view of the jacaranda trees on Figueroa Alcorta Avenue, six former CEOs have found their calling. Sergio Kaufman, with 36 years at Accenture and 12 as CEO, defined it this way: "In corporate life, you enter and climb ladders—first as an area manager, then as a manager, then as a partner, and eventually the ladders run out. It's an inflection point. We decided not to climb ladders anymore, and we said to ourselves, let's build ladders instead."

The creation of Eolas Capital Partner followed swiftly from there. The name comes from Celtic mythology, which—unlike the Greek tradition that speaks of Aeolus as the god of winds—attributes to Eolas the power of "knowledge, experience, and wisdom."

The team was assembled with Nerio Peitiado, who led wholesale banking at Santander; Javier Goñi, who spent 8 years leading Ledesma; Gabriel "the bird" Martino, former CEO of HSBC; lawyer Guillermo Lipera; PWC consultant partner and IDEA president Santiago Mignonè. They share a generation: all are over 60 and under 65 years old. "We're vintage adolescents," they laugh.

When asked what motivates them, they elaborate: "A communion of values and complementary knowledge from different experiences. In corporations, we went through deep strategic reflection processes and analogous situations, and we're eager to help and make projects viable." They encounter spectacular ideas from young entrepreneurs "that sometimes won't work, so guiding them toward the right path is very motivating."

For Eolas, they invested their own money in equal shares but are seeking partnerships with other funds. They assert that they differentiate themselves not only by offering capital to startups—something considered a commodity. "What's unique is the accompaniment. It's very difficult to hire on the market a CEO with thirty years of experience to help you rethink strategy or how to attack the market. That's our proposal. What matters is helping with implementation."

Another characteristic they offer is their extensive network of personal contacts, which facilitates access to diverse areas for those just starting out. "We try to go together and cross the first part of the river, where many drown. We are smart money because of the intelligence we bring. Entrepreneurs are usually very focused on their ideas but get stuck choosing a provider or developing the app, and we establish a very fruitful relationship—they're constantly asking us about their real needs. There's a combination of energy and respect. It's a dynamic and collaborative relationship."

The ex-CEOs meet weekly to analyze projects. They decide unanimously and define two partners as mentors for each project. They've already invested in two and are about to disburse capital in a third. One is Amar mascotas, which is evolving toward pet health insurance in an Argentina where the human birth rate is declining rapidly, with cities like Buenos Aires having more pets than children. The other is SAVE, dedicated to purposeful savings. It specializes in savings strategies tailored to each person or household for specific goals, like going to the World Cup.

**What do they look for when choosing?**

The idea, the quality of leadership, the team, thinking big with Latin America as the first step. And the capacity to execute and implement those good ideas. We get involved.

**How do they see the country's environment for projects to develop?**

Today the conditions are in place for businesses to develop, even though we're all waiting for consumption to pick up. From our experience in leading firms, those who did well are the ones who kept going regardless of the moment. Without overexposing themselves, without taking excessive risks, but those who truly built were always doing so regardless of context. We are not financial investors; we are knowledge investors.
