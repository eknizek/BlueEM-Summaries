# Stock Market, Mutual Funds and AFP Investments: Defensive Strategies Due to Elections

Financial markets started the year at a good pace. However, the momentum was first halted by the war between the United States and Iran. And now in the Peruvian market, uncertainty is growing about the outcome of the presidential elections.

The Lima Stock Exchange (BVL) registers a rise of 20.9% for the year (general index). But the advance slowed in April, with a return of only 0.14% (data accumulated as of April 20).

The evolution of equities and fixed income has an impact on investments in the stock market, mutual funds, and AFP (which manage pension and non-pension funds). The current situation makes it even more necessary to diversify the investment portfolio, said Luis Eduardo Falen, professor at the University of the Pacific.

"We are in a context of uncertainty at local and international levels. In stress horizons like the current one, equities tend to react faster and fixed income helps cushion the fall," he stated.

"It is recommended to have a diversified portfolio, not only at the asset level, but also geographically. With local instruments you can have exposure to global equities, U.S. equities, international fixed income and U.S. fixed income, which gives you an important layer of diversification," Falen added.

For his part, César Huiman, senior equity research analyst at Renta4 SAB, noted that "in an electoral context, the market is prioritizing greater capital preservation. Experience in previous electoral cycles has made investors much more tactical. When Pedro Castillo won, the market closed with an 8% decline for the year," he recalled.

Therefore, there is a recommendation for caution when investing. "In this strategy there are three clear moves: we are prioritizing more defensive or dollarized sectors, so as not to be so exposed to the local market; greater preference for liquidity; and a wait-and-see stance," Huiman stated.

Luis Eduardo Falen agreed with the recommendation to reorder investment portfolios, giving a bit more weight to 'defensive' stocks. "At the local level, stocks exposed to political fluctuations are stocks in the mining sector. So, looking ahead, you can diversify a portfolio with more stable sectors like electricity; the recommendation is to opt for more defensive stocks," Falen pointed out.

## Mutual Funds and AFP

In the case of mutual funds, in this second quarter electoral uncertainty could cause some investors to make a migration in their portfolios, estimated Paul Zevallos, professor at the Faculty of Economics at the University of Lima. "A movement from equity funds and variable income, towards more conservative funds, debt funds. A repositioning to avoid volatility," he added.

If a left-wing candidate wins the presidency, Paul Zevallos estimates that the scenario of 2021 could be repeated, when the patrimony of mutual funds was reduced by 29% and approximately S/ 13,000 million left this market.

"It was due to investor withdrawals, added to negative returns. That is the worst-case scenario that could happen," he projected.

On the other hand, if a pro-market president wins the elections, Zevallos estimated that the patrimony of mutual funds could grow this year around 11%, a rate similar to the average of the last 20 years.

In the case of pension funds in AFP, these closed the first quarter of the year in the black. The highest returns were recorded in fund 3, with advances between 4.15% and 8.21% depending on each AFP.

If a left-wing candidate wins Peru's presidency, this could affect the performance of equities and with it the pension funds in AFP, but it would be a temporary effect, as the market recovers later, according to analysts.

That is why they recommend investors to maintain the type of fund in the AFP chosen, according to their risk profile (funds 1, 2 or 3).

Jimmy Astocondor, finance professor at Pacífico Business School, estimated that —if the left wins Peru's presidency— the stock market fall would be temporary, since Congress will have a right-wing majority, which will limit any radical measures.

"That will be an important brake, because although a president may be left-leaning, they won't allow radical measures. It would be a short-term scare, and could be an investment opportunity, since portfolios would temporarily fall," Astocondor noted.
