# Startup launches AI-powered operating system for energy sector M&A

The regulation of distributed energy generation in Brazil has created an opportunity for practically anyone to build small solar plants and sell the generated energy, transforming energy assets into a new investment class. However, the rush to take advantage of incentives has also exposed governance gaps in a rapidly growing market, with hastily structured assets, disorganized documentation, and buying and selling processes still conducted manually.

This is the scenario in which Draives wants to position itself. The startup is developing what it calls an "operating system for energy M&As" in Brazil: a platform that organizes data, structures data rooms, automates due diligence stages, and begins to incorporate operational and financial data from assets to support both management and buying and selling transactions.

The demand was identified by founder André Figueiredo himself when he worked as commercial head at Élis Energia, a Patria Investimentos portfolio company in Distributed Generation through its Infrastructure Fund 4. After participating in the construction of 40 solar plants and taking the lead on asset acquisitions, he began to see up close a recurring bottleneck: sellers arrived with poorly structured assets, without documentary governance, and with regulatory, environmental, or land-related liabilities poorly mapped.

"As an asset buyer, I remember that when a disorganized asset arrived, often I wouldn't even look at it, because I knew it would be a lot of work. Especially because they are small assets that weren't worth the cost of due diligence and organization," explains the founder.

## Technological foundation

The origin of the problem dates back to the 2022 law that encouraged the construction of small distributed generation plants with capacity of up to 5MW. With a favorable regulatory window and subsidies, the segment experienced a kind of "gold rush," attracting major managers like Pátria, Brookfield, and even BlackRock, alongside independent investors such as doctors and farmers.

The result was an explosion in asset supply, but without the same level of sophistication seen in centralized generation projects. It was in this context that André identified the opportunity to build a technological layer to organize this market. The insight gained momentum in 2023 when he began testing generative AI to extract information from documents during due diligence. "It became clear that artificial intelligence could drastically reduce the cost and time of this process," he states.

Founded in January 2025, Draives officially went to market about a month ago, focusing on applying software and AI to the complete M&A flow in the electrical sector. Today, the startup operates with a team of seven people, six dedicated to technology. The exception is the founder, who comes from the energy market. Besides André Figueiredo, Draives has Ulysses Castro and Roberto Navarro as partners, acting as investors and strategic advisors to the business. Although they are not in day-to-day operations, the two bring complementary experience to the business: the first comes from the M&A market in the electrical sector, while the second has a background linked to centralized energy generation. According to the founder, the corporate composition sought to combine capital and sector knowledge to support the construction of the thesis.

## AI to organize data rooms and map red flags

The platform uses proprietary artificial intelligence to organize data rooms in up to 10 minutes, identifying documents, classifying files, and structuring folders in a market standard. From there, a second layer of AI performs pre-documentary due diligence and points out potential red flags in minutes — without replacing human analysis, but reducing initial operational work for investors and buyers.

According to the startup, 144 assets are already registered on the platform, totaling 425 megawatt-peak of capacity and approximately R$ 1.5 billion in market value within the ecosystem. But ambition goes beyond the data room. The company began connecting the platform to plant generation to bring real-time operational data and launched an Open Finance pilot to integrate asset bank accounts and monitor financial movements. The idea is to transform the platform into a kind of "asset oracle," bringing together regulatory, operational, financial, and documentary information in a single environment.

This also opens space for use even before a transaction. "There are generators using the platform with no immediate intention to sell. They just want to have the asset organized and use it in day-to-day management," says the founder.

The startup is operating bootstrapped and for now not seeking external funding. According to the founder, the plan is to use own revenues to scale and save fundraising for a moment when the company has more traction — and higher valuation. For 2025, Draives wants to multiply billing by 10 times.

Although the market for new small distributed generation plants tends to slow with the reduction in regulatory incentives, André sees the consolidation of existing assets as the next major movement and bets that this creates demand for digital infrastructure. "We understand that we have these smaller assets to consolidate and work on the tool and technology, but we want to aim for large assets, because we understand that this focused M&A operating system doesn't exist today. We want to be the partner of boutiques and banks," points out André.
