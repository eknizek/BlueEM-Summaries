# Why AI is a Danger to Digital Security

Much is said about the benefits of artificial intelligence for improving company efficiency, but there is another side of the technology that demands attention. A study by cybersecurity company Fortinet released this Wednesday (29) shows that AI is also making cyber attacks more sophisticated, increasing companies' exposure to cloud attacks and causing ransomware cases to skyrocket.

According to Frederico Tostes, Country Manager of Fortinet Brasil, the advancement of artificial intelligence reduces the barrier to entry for cybercriminals and increases the efficiency of attacks. According to him, AI-based tools allow creating more convincing phishing campaigns with a high level of personalization and real-time adaptation, in addition to accelerating the development of malicious code. "Artificial intelligence itself can be a major security hole," he states in an interview with Startups.

With the use of technology, criminals can automate offensives, test different approaches, and quickly adapt their strategies when an attempt fails — which accelerates the attack cycle. The executive also points out that by generating code from public databases, these solutions can incorporate vulnerabilities or malicious snippets without the user noticing, opening gaps right at the origin of applications.

This entire movement helps explain the explosion of records in the country: according to the Fortinet report, Brazil concentrated 753.8 billion cyber attack attempts in 2025 alone. Among them, 743 billion were denial-of-service (DDoS) attempts, capable of bringing down systems and critical infrastructure. During the same period, the country also registered 187.5 million malware distribution activities, a 535% increase compared to 2024.

## Attacks are Faster and Smarter Without "Values"

Another point of concern is that artificial intelligence has no critical sense or judgment about what is safe or not. According to the country manager, these tools operate based on probabilities, delivering responses based on data patterns — not on an analysis of values or consequences. "That is not a mind that thinks, it is mathematical calculation giving a response. That's why there is no ideal whatsoever," he summarizes.

The report released by Fortinet also points to a structural shift in cybercrime, which has moved from operating in isolation to functioning as an organized industry. According to the executive, attacks today are integrated, combining different techniques and stages in a coordinated manner. This level of organization puts companies at a disadvantage, since many still operate with fragmented security solutions, while criminals act in an increasingly unified manner.

At the end of the day, as the executive explains, it is a somewhat unequal race. On one side, criminals use artificial intelligence to attack at scale and in real-time; on the other, many companies still face integration issues between their security tools, which delay response to threats. "Because companies have several different solutions and different 'designs,' they end up becoming unstructured in a combined attack," warns Frederico.
