# Brazil's Supreme Court nears decision on foreign land ownership—historic debate

The Supreme Federal Court (STF) is one vote away from vetoing the acquisition of land by foreigners or Brazilian companies with foreign shareholding control, putting an end to a discussion that has lasted more than 50 years.

The Supreme Federal Court (STF) may soon decide on the purchase of land by foreigners, a topic that has generated debate in Congress for more than a decade. The Brazilian Rural Society (SRB) questions restrictions from the 1971 law, which limits the acquisition of rural properties by foreigners and companies with foreign capital. The trial, which stands 5-0 in favor of maintaining restrictions, was resumed in March and awaits a new date for conclusion. The discussion involves concerns about national sovereignty, especially in a tense geopolitical context. Advocates for relaxing restrictions argue it would attract investment and boost the economy, while opponents fear land speculation and predatory exploitation of resources. The issue has faced resistance across different governments, reflecting the complexity of the question in the Brazilian political and economic scenario.

Brasília — A controversial topic that faces historic debate in the National Congress for more than a decade and transcends governments, the purchase of land by foreigners may be close to a resolution in the Supreme Federal Court (STF). The subject divides opinions even within agribusiness, a sector that has already advocated for flexibility in the law to attract more international capital for investment in the sector.

The Supreme Court can at any moment resume and conclude the trial of a lawsuit filed by the Brazilian Rural Society (SRB) against restrictions imposed by the law on the subject, which dates back to 1971. The entity questions an article of the Law, which extends the current restrictions to the purchase of rural property by a foreigner or any Brazilian company composed of citizens or companies with a majority of foreign capital or that reside or are headquartered abroad.

After proceeding since 2015 in the Supreme Court, with even a vote from an already retired justice—former Justice Marco Aurélio Mello—the trial was resumed in March of this year and the score stands 5-0 to maintain the restrictions imposed by legislation on the purchase of land by foreigners. If one more justice votes in line with their colleagues in this direction, the SRB and permission for foreigners to buy land in Brazil without so many legal constraints will be defeated in Brasília.

The vote is suspended in plenary after Justice Alexandre de Moraes requested a review, but he has already returned it for judgment and asked Supreme Court President Edson Fachin to set a new date to resume the trial. For now, there is no date yet, but NeoFeed learned that the matter could be resumed soon. If this trend forms a majority—just one justice needs to vote in this direction to maintain the law's restrictions—the discussion should inevitably return to parliamentary debate, but it is unlikely that a law to guarantee greater purchase of land by foreigners will pass in an election year, according to a Congressional source.

At a time when geopolitical tensions are escalating amid wars around the world, those opposed to greater permission for the purchase or leasing of land by foreigners or even Brazilian companies controlled by foreign capital, such as soybean and corn producers from the Center-West, have been defending the discourse of national sovereignty more forcefully. In fact, sovereignty is a factor that has always blocked greater permission for these transactions in Congress, even for Brazilian companies controlled by multinationals or foreign capital. The military and environmentalists have always feared that an unlimited liberalization for land transactions in Brazil could compromise control of Brazilian borders.

"The pressure exists and is great on the Court, we admit it, but the whole world talks about national sovereignty today," says lawyer Eumar Novacki, who represents Abrafrutas, one of the entities participating in the trial and advocates for limiting land purchases by foreigners. "Brazil has a fundamental role in global food security. And this defense is very important to ensure the sustainability of agribusiness and obviously sovereignty," he adds.

For Novacki, removing restrictions from the law could even increase land values initially and benefit companies or rural producers who want to abandon their activities in the country. But it could also increase pressure to open new areas or "predatory" exploitation of Brazilian natural resources. He also believes there could be a "land speculation effect without precedent."

Novacki was Executive Secretary of the Ministry of Agriculture under Blairo Maggi, one of Brazil's largest agribusiness entrepreneurs, who at the time opposed the liberalization of land to foreign groups.

Foreigners can also participate

Those in favor of greater liberalization of land for foreigners or multinational companies are betting on more investment by large international groups in sectors such as paper and pulp, for example, provided there are limits to prevent the ownership of large territories in the same municipality or region by a foreign individual or company.

Better known as "Rural," the SRB argues that if the STF were to accept its lawsuit and consider that the law in effect for 55 years cannot equate foreigners to foreign companies with Brazilian tax numbers, the STF will increase land acquisition by Brazilian companies with foreign capital, "heating the market, generating investments, jobs, and economic and regional development."

"If unfounded, [the action in the Supreme Court] will maintain current restrictions, with the enforcement of legislation that limits the ownership of rural land in the country," the entity said in a note sent to NeoFeed.

The entity considers it important to have provisions in the law for specific restrictions, especially in border areas, "aiming at full protection of national sovereignty and ensuring the appropriate use of these lands." However, it understands that the national sovereignty debate in a more "risky and unstable" geopolitical environment does not weaken the thesis of foreign investments in Brazilian lands.

"In the case of foreign investment for land, it is essential that the requirements be objective for optimization of inspection and regulation by competent bodies. Therefore, with strengthened regulatory compliance, it is perceived that there is no relativization of compliance with requirements, generating greater legal certainty and, consequently, strengthening of national sovereignty," adds the entity in a note.

In addition to the lawsuit filed by the SRB, the STF also discusses another similar action, filed by the Union and Incra to annul an opinion from the Court of Justice General Inspection of the State of São Paulo that exempts notaries and registry officers from applying the law restricting land purchases by foreigners.

Back and forth

The Workers' Party governments of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva and former President Dilma Rousseff have always opposed greater liberalization of land by foreigners. In fact, an opinion from the Attorney General's Office of the Union (AGU) tightening limits for these transactions, which remains in effect today, dates from 2010, during Lula's second term.

Subsequently, with support especially from the rural caucus, during Michel Temer's government (MDB), the National Congress made several attempts to approve bills or include amendments in provisional measures and other proposals under discussion to try to expand permission mainly for Brazilian companies.
