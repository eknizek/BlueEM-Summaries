"Is the Land Ours?" Brazil's Supreme Court Nears Resolution of Historic Foreign Land Ownership Debate

06 April 2026 · 09:30 PM

# "Is the Land Ours?" STF is Close to Ending a Historic Debate

The Supreme Federal Court (STF) may soon decide on the purchase of land by foreigners, a topic that has generated debate in Congress for more than a decade. The Brazilian Rural Society (SRB) is challenging restrictions from a 1971 law that limits the acquisition of rural properties by foreigners and companies with foreign capital. The trial, which stands at 5-0 in favor of maintaining restrictions, was resumed in March and awaits a new date for conclusion. The discussion involves concerns about national sovereignty, especially in a tense geopolitical context. Advocates for loosening restrictions argue that it would attract investments and boost the economy, while opponents fear land speculation and predatory exploitation of resources. The issue has already faced resistance in different governments, reflecting the complexity of the question in the Brazilian political and economic landscape.

Brasília - A controversial topic that faces historic debate in the National Congress for more than a decade and transcends governments, the purchase of land by foreigners may be close to a resolution in the Supreme Federal Court (STF). The subject divides opinions even within agribusiness, a sector that has already advocated for flexibilization in the law to attract more international capital for investment in the sector.

The Supreme Court can resume and conclude at any moment the trial of an action brought by the Brazilian Rural Society (SRB) against restrictions imposed by legislation on the subject, which dates back to 1971. The entity questions an article of the Law, which extends current restrictions on the purchase of rural property by a foreigner or any Brazilian company composed of citizens or companies with a majority of foreign capital or that reside or have headquarters abroad.

After being processed since 2015 in the Supreme Court, with even a vote from a now-retired rapporteur—former Justice Marco Aurélio Mello—the trial was resumed in March of this year and the score stands at 5-0 to maintain the restrictions imposed by legislation on the purchase of land by foreigners. If one more justice votes in line with the others, the SRB and the permission for foreigners to buy land in Brazil without as many legal constraints will be defeated in Brasília.

The vote is suspended in plenary after Justice Alexandre de Moraes requested to review the case, but he has already returned it for trial and asked President of the Supreme Court Edson Fachin to set a new date to resume the trial. For now, there is no date yet, but NeoFeed learned that the matter could be resumed soon.

If this trend forms a majority—only one justice needs to vote in favor of maintaining the law's restrictions—the discussion should inevitably return to parliamentary debate, but it is unlikely that a law to guarantee greater land purchases by foreigners will pass in an election year, according to a source in Congress.

In a moment when geopolitical tensions are intensifying amid wars around the world, those opposed to greater permission for purchase or lease of land by foreigners or even Brazilian companies controlled by foreign capital, such as soybean and corn producers from the Midwest, have been defending the discourse of national sovereignty more forcefully. In fact, sovereignty is a factor that has always blocked greater permission for these transactions even by Brazilian companies controlled by multinationals or foreign capital in Congress. Military and environmental groups have always feared that unlimited liberalization for land transactions in Brazil could compromise control of Brazilian borders.

"There is pressure on the Court, and it is great, we admit it, but the whole world speaks today about national sovereignty," says lawyer Eumar Novacki, who represents Abrafrutas, one of the entities participating in the trial and defending limiting land purchases by foreigners. "Brazil plays a fundamental role in global food security. And this defense is very important to ensure the sustainability of agribusiness and of course national sovereignty," he adds.

For Novacki, the removal of restrictions in the law could even increase land values initially and benefit companies or rural producers who want to abandon the activity in the country. But it could also increase pressures for opening new areas or "predatory" exploitation of Brazilian natural resources. He also believes there could be an effect of "unprecedented land speculation."

Novacki was executive secretary of the Ministry of Agriculture under Blairo Maggi, one of the largest agribusiness entrepreneurs in Brazil, who at the time opposed the liberalization of land to foreign groups.

**Foreigners Can Also**

Meanwhile, defenders of greater liberalization of land to foreigners or multinational companies are betting on more investment by large international groups in sectors such as paper and pulp, as long as there are limits to prevent possession of large territories in the same municipality or region by an individual or company based abroad.

Better known as "Rural," the SRB argues that if the Supreme Court grants its judicial action and considers that the law in force for 55 years cannot equate foreigners to foreign companies with a Brazilian CNPJ, the STF will boost land acquisition by Brazilian companies with foreign capital, "heating up the market, generating investments, jobs and economic and regional development."

"If it is denied, it will maintain current restrictions, with the enforcement of legislation that limits the ownership of rural land in the country," the entity said in a note sent to NeoFeed. The entity sees it as important to have provisions in the law for specific restrictions, especially in border areas, "aiming at full protection of national sovereignty and ensuring the proper use of these lands."

However, it believes that the national sovereignty debate in a geopolitical environment that is "riskier and more unstable" does not weaken the thesis of foreign investments in Brazilian land. "In the case of foreign investment in land, it is essential that requirements be objective for optimization of inspection and regulation by competent bodies. Therefore, with strengthened regulatory compliance, it is perceived that there is no relativization of requirement compliance, generating greater legal certainty and, consequently, strengthening national sovereignty," the entity adds in a note.

Beyond the action brought by the SRB, the STF is also discussing another similar action brought by the Union and Incra to annul an opinion from the General Auditor of Justice of the State of São Paulo that exempts notaries and registry officials from applying the law that restricts land purchases by foreigners.

**Back and Forth**

The leftist governments of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva and former President Dilma Rousseff have always positioned themselves against greater liberalization of land by foreigners. And indeed, an opinion from the Attorney General's Office of the Union (AGU) tightening limits for these transactions, which remains in effect today, is from 2010, in Lula's second term.

Later, with support especially from the rural caucus, in the government of Michel Temer (MDB) the National Congress made several attempts to approve bills or include amendments in provisional measures and other proposals under discussion to try to expand permission mainly for Brazilian companies...
