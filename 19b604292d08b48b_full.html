# Colombian fashion brand Domenica prepares to conquer major fashion capitals with New York arrival

With its arrival in New York, the Colombian clothing brand Domenica is preparing to conquer the major fashion capitals of the world. The founders highlight that during the first quarter of 2026, the company registered a 47% increase in sales.

María Carolina Gutiérrez and María Paula Ramón, co-founders of Domenica.

With a proposal centered on "elevated comfort" and a global vision from its inception, the Colombian clothing brand Domenica takes a decisive step in its internationalization process with its arrival in New York, one of the most competitive fashion markets in the world.

Founded by María Carolina Gutiérrez (M.C.G), operations director, and María Paula Ramón (M.P.R), creative director, the company has consolidated sustained growth in Colombia, leveraged by the quality of its garments and a solid commitment to e-commerce.

Now, with its entry into the U.S. market, the brand seeks to position itself in major fashion capitals and connect with a global consumer without losing its local essence. In an interview with Portafolio, its founders explain how they have built this process, the challenges of competing abroad, and their expansion plans toward new cities.

**They have just announced their arrival in New York. What does this step represent for the brand?**

M.C.G.: It is a very important leap and, above all, a dream we have had for a long time. Domenica was born with a global vision. From the beginning, we understood that it was a brand that could compete in international markets, not just in Colombia.

M.P.R.: Even in our early years, when we were at university, many customers told us they thought the brand was foreign. That gave us clear signals that there was potential to reach other cities in the world.

**How do you define the essence of the brand?**

M.P.R.: Domenica means "Sunday" in Italian, and that summarizes our concept perfectly: garments as comfortable as the ones you wear on a Sunday, but versatile enough to wear any day of the week.

M.C.G.: We always wanted to build an aspirational brand with international references. From the beginning, we compared ourselves to global brands, not local ventures. That led us to be very demanding about quality and design.

**Precisely, quality has been one of your differentiators. How was that construction process?**

M.P.R.: It was work over several years. The first three or four years we dedicated almost exclusively to finding suppliers that met the standards we wanted.

M.C.G.: And it is a process that never stops. We constantly review, adjust, and listen to the customer. Our proposal is based on achieving that "perfect basic," a simple garment, but with a level of detail and quality that makes it unique.

Domenica brand carries out its production in Colombia.

**How has the brand performed in terms of growth and sales?**

M.C.G.: We have had very positive performance. In the first quarter of 2026, we are growing around 47% compared to the same period last year.

M.P.R.: Much of that growth comes from one of our flagship products, the "ovejero," which increased the average ticket and allowed us to improve margins. Additionally, the internationalization process has also boosted invoicing.

**How was the decision to expand to international markets made?**

M.C.G.: We understood that our proposal is timeless and can work in different geographies. Rather than depending on climate, we look for cities with high foot traffic and a culture of constant consumption.

M.P.R.: New York was the first major step, but it did not happen overnight. We spent several years traveling, understanding the market, and building relationships.

**How was the process of entry into the United States?**

M.P.R.: We started with a "merch" model for universities. We worked with Latino communities in institutions like Harvard, Columbia, and NYU, which allowed us to understand the market and create an initial customer base.

M.C.G.: Then we took the next step: start selling the brand directly. We started with small volumes, but the response was very positive. Today we are growing at a rate close to 64% monthly in that market.

**What challenges have you found in such a competitive market as New York?**

M.C.G.: The main challenge has been the level of demand, especially in the digital channel. There, e-commerce is very developed, with very efficient delivery times and return processes.

M.P.R.: That forced us to strengthen our platform and invest in technology to be up to the task. It has been important learning.

**What role does sustainability play in your proposal?**

M.P.R.: For us it is a comprehensive approach. Beyond materials, we talk about sustainability from the team, the processes, and the social impact. We are a team of young women who promote entrepreneurship and local production.

M.C.G.: We also work with sustainable packaging and seek to reduce environmental impact across the entire chain. It is a value that increasingly matters in markets like the United States.

**What comes next in your expansion roadmap?**

M.C.G.: Madrid is the next step. We will start with a business mission to test the market, just as we did in New York.

M.P.R.: In the medium term, we also see opportunities in Mexico City and other global capitals. Our goal is to consolidate ourselves in key fashion cities.

**How is your operation in Colombia composed today?**

M.C.G.: Currently we have a showroom in Bogotá and we are looking to open our first flagship store, with a more complete customer experience.

M.P.R.: However, the digital channel is the main driver. Today it represents about 70% of sales, while the remaining 30% comes from the physical location.
