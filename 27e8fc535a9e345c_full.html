# Brazil Has Surplus Clean Energy. They Want to Turn It into Bitcoin

Every day, various wind and solar plants across Brazil need to be shut down at certain moments to prevent excessive generation on the electrical grid that could destabilize the system and lead to blackouts. This cut in energy production, known as curtailment, is deterring investors from the renewables sector, but is beginning to be seen as an opportunity in another niche: that of bitcoiners.

Created by an entrepreneur with previous experience in cryptoassets and the financial market, Radius Mining is negotiating agreements with generators to transform into Bitcoin at least part of the energy that is currently being wasted. To debut in these operations, Radius raised R$ 28 million from its founders and in an initial fundraising round. One of the investors who entered the cap table was Leonardo Midea, former partner of Prime Energy, a energy trading and consulting company sold to Shell in 2023.

"In crypto mining, 95% of operational costs (opex) is energy. It's a highly energy-intensive sector. Why not combine them? The proposal fit very well with the know-how I already have from the electrical sector," Midea told Brazil Journal.

Radius's plan is to sign agreements with renewable energy companies to install Bitcoin mining equipment inside the area of their plants. The machines would not be connected to the grid, but directly to the generation, allowing them to "circumvent" curtailment – which occurs when the National Electrical System Operator (ONS) sees excess energy being injected into the network.

"Many generators are having 40%, 60% of their energy cut. At least a fraction of that would be delivered to us and billed normally for them," said Radius founder and CEO Flávio Hernandez. "We have three negotiations well advanced. What we already have, and is normally the most complex, is capital. Many people say they will do it, but because it's still something very new, capital doesn't appear."

Radius is close to a first agreement (proof of concept) involving 6 megawatts average in energy – enough to power a city of 24,000 people for a month. With other conversations underway, there is potential to reach 50 MW average. Under the business model, the company will import mining equipment, handle its operation and maintenance, and keep the Bitcoin generated. The formats of energy supply agreements with generators will be evaluated case by case.

This is because Bitcoin mining doesn't need to use only energy that would be wasted – in fact, returns are higher the longer the equipment is running. Radius is also evaluating partnerships with players who installed solar plants to supply energy "by subscription" and couldn't sell all their volume, accumulating stuck credits.

While pursuing its first agreement for proprietary mining, Radius has already signed a contract to provide operation and maintenance (O&M) services to AXIA Energia on a research and development (R&D) project for proprietary Bitcoin operations from a wind farm in Bahia. Besides AXIA, other renewable groups are studying the topic of cryptoassets, such as Renova Energia, Serena Energia, and Casa dos Ventos.

"Probably this year and next we will see the first projects. I'd say there are projects there for around 500 MW average (in energy demand), counting with ours," said Hernandez.

In 2025 alone, Brazil "threw away" approximately 20% of wind and solar energy due to generation cuts, according to a study by Volt Robotics. The consulting firm estimated the revenue loss for renewable energy companies from curtailment at R$ 6.5 billion.
