# Brazil has 'Surplus' Clean Energy. They Want to Transform It Into Bitcoin

Every day, various wind and solar plants across the country need to be shut down at certain moments to prevent excess generation on the electrical grid that could destabilize the system and lead to blackouts. This cut in energy production, known as curtailment, is deterring investors from the renewable sector, but is beginning to be seen as an opportunity in another niche: that of bitcoiners.

Created by an entrepreneur with previous experience in crypto assets and the financial market, Radius Mining is negotiating agreements with generators to transform into Bitcoin at least part of the energy that is currently being lost. To debut in these operations, Radius raised R$ 28 million from its founders and an initial funding round. One of the investors who joined the cap table was Leonardo Midea, former partner of Prime Energy, a trader and consulting firm sold to Shell in 2023.

"In crypto mining, 95% of operational costs (opex) is energy. It's a very energy-intensive sector. Why not combine them? The proposal aligned very well with the know-how I already have in the electricity sector," Midea told Brazil Journal.

Radius's plan is to sign agreements with renewable companies to install Bitcoin mining equipment within their power plants' premises. The machines would not be connected to the grid, but directly to generation, allowing them to "dodge" curtailment – which occurs when the National Electric System Operator (ONS) sees excess energy injected into the network.

"Many generators are having 40%, 60% of their energy cut. At least a fraction of that would be delivered to us and billed normally for them," said Radius founder and CEO Flávio Hernandez. "We have three negotiations quite advanced. What we already have, and is usually the most complex, is capital. Many people say they will do it, but because it's still something very new, capital doesn't appear."

Radius is close to a first agreement (proof of concept) involving 6 megawatts average in energy – enough to supply a city of 24,000 people for a month. With other conversations underway, there is potential to reach 50 MW average. Under the business model, the company will import mining equipment, handle its operation and maintenance, and keep the Bitcoin generated. The formats of energy supply agreements with generators will be evaluated case by case.

Partly because Bitcoin mining doesn't need to use only energy that would be wasted – in fact, the return is higher the longer the equipment runs.

Radius is also evaluating partnerships with players who installed solar plants to supply energy "by subscription" and couldn't sell their entire volume, accumulating stranded credits.

While seeking its first agreement for its own mining operation, Radius has already signed a contract to provide operation and maintenance (O&M) services to AXIA Energia, in a research and development (R&D) project for a proprietary Bitcoin operation from a wind farm in Bahia. Besides AXIA, other renewable groups are studying the topic of crypto assets, such as Renova Energia, Serena Energia, and Casa dos Ventos.

"We'll probably see the first projects this year and next. I'd say there are projects out there for about 500 MW average (in energy demand), including ours," said Hernandez.

In 2025 alone, Brazil "threw away" about 20% of wind and solar energy due to generation cuts, according to a study by Volt Robotics. The consulting firm estimated the revenue loss for renewable companies from curtailment at R$ 6.5 billion.
