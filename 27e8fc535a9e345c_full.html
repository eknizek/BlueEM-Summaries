# Brazil Has 'Surplus' of Clean Energy. They Want to Transform It into Bitcoin

Every day, various wind and solar plants across the country need to be shut down at certain moments to prevent excess generation on the electrical grid that could destabilize the system and lead to blackouts. This cut in energy production, known as curtailment, is deterring investors from the renewable sector, but is beginning to be seen as an opportunity in another niche: that of bitcoiners.

Created by an entrepreneur with previous experience in crypto assets and the financial market, Radius Mining is negotiating agreements with generators to transform into Bitcoin at least part of the energy that is currently being lost. To debut in these operations, Radius raised R$ 28 million from its founders and in an initial funding round. One of the investors who entered the cap table was Leonardo Midea, former partner of Prime Energy, a trading company and consultancy sold to Shell in 2023.

"In crypto mining, 95% of operational costs (opex) is energy. It's a highly energy-intensive sector. Why not combine them? The proposal aligned very well with the know-how I already have in the electrical sector," Midea told Brazil Journal.

Radius's plan is to sign agreements with renewable energy companies to install Bitcoin mining equipment inside their plant areas. The machines would not be connected to the grid, but directly to generation, allowing them to "circumvent" curtailment – which occurs when the National Electrical System Operator (ONS) sees excess energy injected into the network.

"Many generators are having 40%, 60% of their energy cut. At least a fraction of that would be delivered to us and billed normally to them," said Radius founder and CEO Flávio Hernandez. "We have three negotiations well advanced. What we already have, and is normally the most complex, is capital. Many people say they're going to do it, but because it's still something very new, the capital doesn't show up."

Radius is close to a first agreement (proof of concept) involving 6 average megawatts of energy – enough to supply a city of 24,000 people for a month. With other conversations underway, there is potential to reach 50 average MW. Under the business model, the company will import mining equipment, operate and maintain it, and keep the generated Bitcoin. The formats of energy supply agreements with generators will be evaluated case by case. Especially since Bitcoin mining doesn't need to use only energy that would be wasted – in fact, the return is greater the longer the equipment stays online.

Radius also evaluates partnerships with players who installed solar plants to supply energy "by subscription" and couldn't sell the entire volume, accumulating stranded credits. While seeking its first mining agreement, Radius has already signed a contract to provide operations and maintenance (O&M) services to AXIA Energia, in a research and development (P&D) project for a proprietary Bitcoin operation from a wind farm in Bahia.

In addition to AXIA, other renewable groups are studying the topic of crypto assets, such as Renova Energia, Serena Energia, and Casa dos Ventos. "Probably this year and next we'll see the first projects. I'd say there are projects out there for about 500 average MW (in energy demand), including ours," said Hernandez.

In 2025 alone, Brazil "threw away" about 20% of wind and solar energy due to generation cuts, according to a Volt Robotics study. The consultancy estimated R$ 6.5 billion in lost revenue for renewable companies due to curtailment.
