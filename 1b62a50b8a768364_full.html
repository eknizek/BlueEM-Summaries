# "Two Fewer Business Days and a General Strike," Finance Minister Caputo's Explanation for Economic Activity Decline

After the release of data by Indec, Economy Minister Luis Caputo explained on his social media the reasons why in February the Monthly Economic Activity Estimator (EMAE) registered "a negative variation of 2.6%" seasonally adjusted and 2.1% year-over-year.

"In 2026, the month had two fewer business days than in 2025, in addition to a general strike having been recorded. In relation to February 2025, 8 of the 15 sectors that make up the EMAE registered growth," he specified.

According to the official, "beyond the specific decline in February, the underlying trend remained in positive territory" and emphasized that the indicator "accumulates almost two years of uninterrupted expansion."

To find a larger year-over-year decline, one must go back to September 2024, when the index was at 2.4% — and until December of the previous year to match the loss against the previous month.

The relevance of the data released by the Institute of Statistics and Census relates to the fact that the indicator had come from two months with positive figures that fueled the idea that activity might have begun to take a turn. And, according to the new data revealed this Wednesday, the second month of the year appears to have redirected that course, while inflation, in contrast, stopped the deceleration process ten months ago.

From the report by the agency directed by Pedro Lines, it appears that eight of the 15 sectors that make up the EMAE registered increases in February, among which Fishing (14.8%) and Mining and quarrying (9.9%) stood out.

The distinctive phrases Caputo used to justify the retraction in activity are in line with other expressions he has used to explain various movements and changes in the economic scenario. Just recently, on April 1st, he gave a talk at the Rosario Stock Exchange, where he defended the Government's economic model and, with an outburst, lashed out at economists who ask him to devalue.

"We have to stop buying the story that to be competitive you have to devalue. I find it pathetic when people say that. Is that the proposal you have as economists? Seriously, is the solution to devalue? It makes me want to... kick all of you in the ass," he stated.

In February, amid tension with the textile sector over the high prices that industry maintains, he claimed he had never bought clothing in the country. "I never bought clothes in Argentina in my life because it was robbery," he said and completed his argument by saying that those who had "the possibility to travel or something" purchased clothing abroad.

In the same tone, in July of last year the official had responded with irony to those who thought the dollar was cheap. "Buy, don't miss out buddy. There's something very obvious: the dollar floats. If you have pesos and the exchange rate floats, and you know it's super cheap, buy," he said then before 700 executives at the business school of Universidad Austral.
