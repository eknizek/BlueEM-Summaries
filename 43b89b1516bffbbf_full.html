# UniversalizaSP Advances with Up to R$100 Billion in Investments and Auction Planned for This Year

The UniversalizaSP program for universal basic sanitation is entering a new phase this Monday, April 27, with the opening of public consultation by the São Paulo government. The program provides for sanitation concessions in 146 São Paulo municipalities not served by Sabesp. The expectation is that the auction will take place later this year, with investments potentially reaching R$100 billion until 2060.

The public consultation will run for 30 days and will be accompanied by five regional hearings, according to publication in the State Official Gazette. The following stages include the disclosure of the tender, scheduled for the third quarter, and the auction, expected for the fourth quarter, according to the secretary of Environment, Infrastructure and Logistics of the State of São Paulo, Natália Resende.

The project will be structured as a subsidized common concession. In this model, there is no fixed division between the State and private initiative, but the government can contribute resources when necessary to guarantee the viability of operations and fair rates. "The State is willing to subsidize sanitation in these municipalities to solve identified problems," Natália said in an interview with Estadão/Broadcast.

The modeling provides for the division of municipalities into blocks, following the logic of regionalization and criteria such as hydrographic basins and infrastructure sharing. The final configuration of the lots and auction rules will be defined after the public consultation. Among the already established guidelines is the possibility of the same operator winning more than one block, according to Natália.

According to her, the program combines the goal of universal access to water and sewage by 2033 with an axis of water security and resilience. "It is through structural projects that look at the entire chain, from production to network replacement, that we can transform the reality of sanitation," she said.

UniversalizaSP provides for approximately R$29 billion in financial movement until 2033 and up to R$100 billion until 2060, considering investments (capex) and operational expenses (opex) throughout the contracts. The expectation is that approximately 6 million people will benefit, including residents of rural areas. Among the planned works are the expansion of sanitation networks, renovation of pipes to reduce losses, and reinforcement of water collection and production systems. The model also includes social tariffs for low-income families and provides for the allocation of part of revenues to municipalities through Municipal Environmental Sanitation and Infrastructure Funds (FMSAIs).

Sabesp's Interest

Sabesp has already demonstrated appetite to participate in UniversalizaSP with an eye toward possible synergies. "It is quite natural for us to be interested in opportunities in São Paulo," said the company's CEO, Carlos Piani recently, citing potential operational and scale gains.

Even with macroeconomic challenges and recent postponements of other sanitation auctions, Natália assesses that there is room for competition in the dispute for lots, beyond Sabesp. "The sanitation market has natural attraction and São Paulo offers regulatory security," she said.

The secretary also said she does not see relevant risks of competition with other sector projects, such as the privatization of Copasa in Minas Gerais, stating that "they are different models that attract different competitors." She also assessed that the election calendar should not affect the progress of the process. "We developed contracts with legal security, predictability and regulatory stability," she said.
