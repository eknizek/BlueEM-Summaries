# 'Cheap Blouse Tax' Misses the Mark – It Should Be Called 'Chinese Incentive,' Says Riachuelo CEO

Major fashion retailers in the country are tired of the expression "cheap blouse tax." They consider it too lighthearted for something they view as harmful to the national industry. They are asking for a different term to be used—one that would give a true dimension to the problem.

"The best term would be 'Chinese incentive,'" says André Farber, CEO of Riachuelo. "This destroys a country. You're stimulating growth in other countries at the expense of yours. What's the point of that?" questions Fabio Faccio, CEO of Renner.

Riachuelo and Renner are two of the three largest companies in the sector in the country (the trio is completed by C&A). Together, the two employ approximately 350,000 people when considering the entire production chain.

In recent statements, President Lula said he considers the value of R$ 50 too low for taxation, giving renewed force to the debate over charging a tax on products purchased through e-commerce, preferably from Asian countries.

The law, created in August 2024, established a tax rate of 20% on international purchases of up to US$ 50 and 60% for amounts between US$ 50 and US$ 3,000. Combined with state ICMS of 17% to 20%, the tax burden reached approximately 40% to 50% of the product's value.

Brazilian retailers argue that this percentage is less than half of the 90% that the national industry pays. They see this as a senseless asymmetry.

"We pay double the tax and we're discussing whether those paying half should pay less still," says Farber. "The products made in Brazil would generally be much cheaper if everyone paid 45%. The big discussion is: why give tax benefits for companies to export to Brazil while companies operating here pay double the tax burden?" adds Faccio.

The 90% sum for national companies is the result of Import Tax, IPI, PIS, Cofins, and ICMS. It is the standard taxation for formal foreign trade operations. The cross-border model, used by platforms like Shein, Shopee, and AliExpress, appeared with different logic, with lower taxation and, according to retailers, at prices that national companies cannot match.

Through cross-border, merchandise crosses borders directly from the supplier to the end consumer, whether a physical or legal person.

For the executives, this is a political issue. The minister of the Institutional Relations Secretariat, José Guimarães, defended the revocation of the tax, classifying it as "one of the strongest elements of government wear." An AtlasIntel survey in partnership with Bloomberg indicates that 62% of Brazilians view the tax as a government error, while 30% consider it correct.

There is a communication issue, as perceived by the national industry's discourse—explaining how the country loses from this.

"If they exempt taxes from all items, we sell at the same price. Why for some and not others?" questions Renner's CEO. "It's a Brazilian state policy that will harm national employment. It's not protectionism, it's a major disadvantage for those operating here," adds Farber.

The Ministry of Finance resists reversal because it believes the tax contributes to meeting the fiscal target. It also corrects a distortion resulting from the importation of Chinese products sold below market prices. In 2025, the government collected R$ 5 billion from the import tax on international parcels.

For representatives of the national industry, backing down would also represent an incoherence for the government's policy that, in discourse, would be about encouraging the national industry.

"If it's a measure to gain more votes, the better way would be to make the economy grow and do things for those who invest in the country. If it's like this with the inequality, we'll close all companies in Brazil and open them in other countries," says Faccio. "We ask for justice. Industry and retail are enormous employers," concludes Farber.
