Brazil to begin testing higher biodiesel content in diesel by May

24 April 2026 · 08:00 PM

Brazil's Ministry of Mines and Energy (MME) expects tests to increase biodiesel's share in diesel to begin in May, said Marlon Arraes, Brazil's acting secretary of oil, gas, and biofuels, during an event promoted by the Association of Vegetable Oil Industries (ABIOVE) and the Brazilian Petroleum Institute (IBP) on Thursday (23) in São Paulo.

"The ministry is committed and expects the tests to begin in May. We want and need to get the tests started as quickly as possible. We are managing to have the schedule already started in May," he said.

The trials will test biodiesel blends of 20% and 25% to assess the feasibility of the increased levels established in the Future Fuel Law. Today, the level is 15%. According to the current understanding of the Brazilian government, an increase in the mandatory blend to 16% or 17%, for example, depends on the validation of tests with a 20% blend.

The biodiesel industry has advocated for simpler ways of conducting the tests, including trials with levels lower than 20%, but these proposals were not accepted by the ministry.

At the event, Arraes said that the tests "will be completed in the shortest possible time." According to the schedule set by the ministry, if the trials begin in May, they should be completed by February 2027. After that, the results will still have to be ratified by the ministry, and a decision on increasing the blend to 16% or more will need to be made later by the National Council for Energy Policy (CNPE).

In addition to the Mauá Institute of Technology (IMT), biodiesel producers are also beginning to negotiate with other laboratories to carry out the tests, said André Nassar, president of ABIOVE, in a press conference. The total cost of the trials should be R$8 million, and part of it should be financed by biodiesel companies. According to Nassar, the sector is working to ensure the participation of biodiesel producers in raising funds. It will also need to be defined which organization will monitor the tests.

The tests will verify the performance of engines using B20 or B25 diesel, as well as the fuel's physical-chemical characteristics, explained Arraes.

The increase in the blend is seen as an opportunity for Brazil to reduce its dependence on imported fossil diesel at a time of global supply shock caused by the war in the Middle East. In a presentation at the event, Pietro Mendes, director of the National Agency of Petroleum (ANP), said that a 1 percentage point increase in biodiesel in the blend allows Brazil to stop importing 700 million liters of diesel per year.

The sector expects that the tests will authorize increasing the blend, but should also lead to changes in biodiesel specifications, which could increase production costs. "Some measures will have to be taken. I don't see the test results negating the increase. I see a need to change some processes in the plants," said Nassar.
