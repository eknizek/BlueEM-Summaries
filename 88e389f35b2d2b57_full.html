# Debt Relief Package Could Become Electoral Tool to Boost Consumer Spending

At the center of the discussion about measures to combat indebtedness is a growing clash in the Lula government during this election year over the current level of the Selic rate maintained by the Central Bank—currently at 14.75%. The government believes that the Desenrola debt renegotiation program—the first measure announced by former Finance Minister Fernando Haddad during the transition period—worked, but was undermined by high interest rates sustained over a prolonged period.

While the Selic rose to 15%, the debts of families, informal workers, and small, medium, and large companies grew proportionally. According to its critics, it would have failed because since then there has been an increase of 9 million defaulters. The premise is true, but it also overlooks that the government has worked in recent years with a policy of stimulating consumption through increased credit to boost GDP. It was a counterpoint to the Central Bank's interest rate tightening to prevent economic slowdown. Credit policies and other government spending helped maintain economic activity, even with highly prohibitive interest rates. It was supposed to have slowed down much more. Lots of money was poured into the economy.

Now, the problem of indebtedness rears its head in a concerning way at the moment when the beginning of the Selic rate cuts coincided with the war in Iran. The Central Bank began dealing with an inflationary shock due to rising oil prices and became more constrained in cutting interest rates significantly. In a recent report, the Central Bank calls for caution in granting credit and acknowledges that the environment of high interest rates, combined with high indebtedness, maintains risks for micro, small, and medium enterprises. There are already signs of deterioration in the payment capacity of large companies.

Lula's package can and should remove companies and families from more expensive debts. However, it would be a disservice to turn it into an electoral weapon to stimulate consumption with an eye on the election. Then, the problem increases.
