# Between Waves and Curves: Bruno Garfinkel's Business Lessons From Porto

Bruno Garfinkel does not accept the classic label of heir. Son of Jayme Garfinkel, founder of Porto, he grew up within one of the largest groups in the sector, but says he transformed that starting point into fuel to pursue something of his own. Today, as president of the board of Porto, he divides his time between strategic decisions of a company with tens of thousands of employees and a routine that includes racing tracks and waves around the world. A Porsche Cup driver and surfer since adolescence, he uses sports as a laboratory for testing, error, and evolution. It was in this context that he received surfer Carlos Burle for an experience outside the ocean. In an interview with Bravamente, a program in partnership with NeoFeed, Garfinkel took Burle to the track at a stage of the Porsche Cup, reversing roles and showing how the world of speed also requires risk assessment, emotional control, and decision-making under pressure.

This logic of challenge runs through his trajectory. In motorsports, he speaks of "accelerating and braking" as a direct metaphor for management: knowing when to advance and, especially, when to hold back. In surfing, he finds an even more unpredictable environment — where there is no control, only adaptation. "It's in this type of scenario, where you don't control everything, that true evolution happens," Garfinkel states.

The same mindset appears in corporate decisions. Garfinkel avoids shortcuts that could compromise company culture, even when they involve significant financial opportunities. In a recent episode, he chose not to move forward with a project in the investment market after realizing the strategy relied on unfounded promises — a path that, for him, doesn't align with the trust relationship built by Porto.

More than numbers, the businessman has directed his attention toward behavior and culture. He argues that companies are, above all, platforms of values at scale. In Porto's case, this means impacting not only employees but a network involving brokers, service providers, and millions of customers.

But it is outside the office that he identifies his greatest challenge. Father of two teenagers, Garfinkel admits that fatherhood exposes vulnerabilities that don't appear in the corporate world. After years of adopting a more rigid stance, he now tries to recalibrate his role, with less demand and more presence.

At the center of everything is an unease that has nothing to do with performance or legacy: the fear of not deserving it. Garfinkel repeats that he doesn't want to be seen as someone who got there without effort. He wants to feel that every achievement carries intention, work, and consistency.
