# Belterra to Accelerate Agroforestry with R$ 75 Million Investment

Belterra Agroflorestas announced the raising of R$ 75 million and the entry of its first new partners in the startup that combines forest restoration and agriculture. The funding round was led by Bold.t and included participation from MOV and Rise. The three asset managers invest in businesses with positive socio-environmental impact. The German foundation Ecosia, which previously operated in philanthropy to promote tree planting, made its first equity investment and completes the group. The quartet acquired a stake in the holding company.

The Series A round culminates in a redesign of the company. Projects will be organized under different corporate entities, or special purpose companies (SPEs), under the parent company. "The major change we made in recent months was to organize our project portfolio by investor," says Valmir Ortega, founder and CEO of Belterra.

The model is similar to other startups attempting to turn the recovery of degraded areas into a business. For now, Belterra has only one established SPE. It brings together approximately 2,500 hectares where the company implements agroforestry systems (SAF) to produce cacao while simultaneously recovering devastated native vegetation. The business model is to generate revenue from the sale of agricultural production and carbon credits generated by tree growth. In this first SPE, Belterra has no partners. The company contributed R$ 30 million in already ongoing projects. The operation is financed by debt: R$ 100 million from the Climate Fund, administered by BNDES, and another R$ 20 million from the Amazon Biodiversity Fund (ABF), managed by Impact Earth.

"We expect to announce two others we are structuring soon," says the CEO. In terms of area, Ortega envisions larger SPEs than the initial one: one of 4,000 hectares and another that could reach 10,000 hectares (one hectare is roughly the size of a football field).

## Focus on Management

The newly announced investment will not go toward setting up these SPEs, but will be fundamental for Belterra to get them off the ground. Expansion depends partly on a structure provided by the holding to support projects spread across the country. This includes the administrative part shared by all subsidiary companies: technology and management systems, finance, and human resources. The latter point is not trivial, says the CEO. "We just hired a director of operations at a salary level closer to the reality of the agricultural world."

To execute the plan, Belterra will have to compete for talent in one of the most competitive and heated sectors of the Brazilian economy. The team will need to grow in several parts of the country. The company's headquarters is in Curitiba, Paraná. Pará and Bahia concentrate most of the projects, and the company also operates in Mato Grosso and Rondônia. In all these places, work begins with selling the idea to rural producers. The startup does not own the land it works on. Projects are developed on private properties. "The producer needs to see to believe," says Ortega. In certain regions, where production is already entering its second year, this part is becoming easier. He identified a sign: Belterra is beginning to circulate in producers' WhatsApp groups. Bahian farmers who heard reports from acquaintances or saw an SAF in operation are seeking out the company. But to open new areas, the company will have to continue this evangelization work.

## Larger and More Partners

The profile of partners will also have to change somewhat. Today, most are small producers. The strategy needs to be adjusted when dealing with tens of thousands of hectares. Ortega says the idea is to seek some larger properties to anchor Belterra's presence in new operating areas. "That way we can make larger investments in machinery and basic infrastructure, such as warehouses to store production and equipment."

This is another function of the holding: to provide all service and logistics support at the operational end. Cacao must go through drying and fermentation before being sold to the industry. "It doesn't make sense to have this on each property."

And the search for large farms does not mean ignoring family farmers. "Belterra will never work only with large properties," says Ortega. "We want to have a majority participation of small ones. We monitor income growth, women's engagement, and involvement of new generations."

The number of partners and geographic dispersion are just one element of the complexity of the type of business the startup is creating. Pedro Alves, from Rise's investment and sustainability area, looks at the issue from another angle. Covering vast territories and dealing with multiple producers requires governance, standardized processes, and on-the-ground execution, he says. "But it's not just a risk. It can also represent an important barrier to entry in a sector where scaling with quality is, in itself, a competitive advantage," says Alves. "Belterra built the business with this complexity in mind, not despite it."

## Ahead of the Competition

The company "is ahead of the others" attempting to explore this new segment, says Paulo Bellotti, partner at MOV. The recognition and technical capacity accumulated over five years put the company in a unique position to take advantage of "very, very large" opportunities, says the investor. "The buyer of carbon and other commodities wants integrity in their supply chain, and Belterra wants to connect these small farmers to large global markets fairly."

After five years leading the company, Ortega reports some lessons learned. One of the most important: projects can only begin when the capital structure is fully in place. "After planting a cacao tree, you have to spend until it becomes self-sufficient, which takes three or four years. During that time you need cash flow guarantee. Money in the account."

In the past, the company relied on partners' access to subsidized agricultural credit—but the money did not come. "An overdue Casas Bahia bill, an unpaid electricity bill... This prevents a producer from getting loans." From now on, SPEs will start operating when financing is secured. The plan is to design them according to the specific interests of potential partners or financiers. "We will do carbon credits, cacao, and other products. What changes is the partner's motivation. One might want cacao, another carbon. You structure the SPE to meet those specific demands."

## Big Tech

Ortega says he is talking with global technology companies. They are the largest buyers of carbon on the voluntary market and specifically seek credits that represent environmental impact.
