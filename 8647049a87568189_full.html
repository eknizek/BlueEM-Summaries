# With R$ 75 Million Investment, Belterra Will Accelerate Agroforestry

Belterra Agroflorestas announced the raise of R$ 75 million and the entry of the first new partners in the startup that combines forest restoration and agriculture. The investment was led by Bold.t and included participation from MOV and Rise. The three managers invest in businesses with positive social and environmental impact. German foundation Ecosia, which until then worked in philanthropy to promote tree planting, made its first equity deal and completes the group. The quartet acquired a stake in the holding company.

The Series A round culminates in the redesign of the company. The projects will be organized under different corporate entities, or special purpose vehicles (SPVs), under the parent company. "The major change we made in recent months was organizing the project portfolio by investor," says Valmir Ortega, founder and CEO of Belterra.

The model is similar to other startups trying to turn degraded area recovery into a business. For now, Belterra has a single SPV established. It brings together approximately 2,500 hectares where the company implements agroforestry systems (SAF) to produce cocoa while recovering devastated native vegetation. The business model is to generate revenue from the sale of agricultural production and carbon credits generated by tree growth.

In this first SPV, Belterra has no partners. The company invested R$ 30 million in ongoing projects. The operation is financed with debt: R$ 100 million from the Climate Fund, administered by BNDES, and another R$ 20 million from the Amazon Biodiversity Fund (ABF), managed by Impact Earth. "We expect to announce soon two others we are structuring," says the CEO.

In terms of area, Ortega envisions larger SPVs than the initial one: one of 4,000 hectares and another that could reach 10,000 (one hectare is roughly the size of a soccer field).

## Focus on Management

The newly announced investment will not go toward setting up these SPVs, but will be crucial for Belterra to get them operational. The expansion depends partly on a structure provided by the holding to support projects spread across the country. This includes the administrative part shared by all subsidiary companies: technology and management systems, finance and people.

The latter point is not trivial, says the CEO. "We just hired an operations director at a salary level closer to the reality of the agricultural world." To execute the plan, Belterra will have to compete for talent in one of the most competitive and heated sectors of the Brazilian economy. The team will need to grow in various parts of the country.

The company's headquarters is in Curitiba, Paraná. Pará and Bahia concentrate most of the projects, and the company also operates in Mato Grosso and Rondônia. In all these places, work begins with selling the idea to rural producers. The startup does not own the land it works on. Projects are developed on private properties.

"Producers need to see it to believe it," says Ortega. In certain regions, where production is already entering its second year, this part is becoming easier. He identified a sign: Belterra is beginning to circulate in farmer WhatsApp groups. Bahian farmers who heard reports from acquaintances or saw a functioning SAF are seeking out the company. But to open new areas, the company will have to continue this evangelization work.

## Larger and More Partners

The profile of partners will also have to change slightly. Today, most are small producers. The strategy needs adjustment when scaling to tens of thousands of hectares. Ortega says the idea is to pursue some larger properties to anchor Belterra's presence in new operating areas. "That way we can make larger investments in machinery and basic infrastructure, like warehouses to store production and equipment."

This is another function of the holding: providing support services and logistics. Cocoa must go through drying and fermentation before being sold to industry. "It doesn't make sense to have that on each property."

And the search for large farms does not mean abandoning family farmers. "Belterra will never work only with large properties," says Ortega. "We want a majority participation of small ones. We follow the increase in income, the engagement of women, the involvement of new generations."

The number of partners and geographic dispersion are just one element of the complexity of the kind of business the startup is creating.

Pedro Alves, from the investment and sustainability area at Rise, looks at the question from another angle. Covering vast territories and dealing with multiple producers requires governance, standardized processes and execution on the ground, he says. "But it's not just risk. It can also represent an important barrier to entry in a sector where scaling with quality is, in itself, a competitive advantage," says Alves. "Belterra built the business with this complexity in mind, not despite it."

## Ahead of the Competition

The company "is ahead of the others" trying to pioneer this new segment, says Paulo Bellotti, partner at MOV. The recognition and technical capacity accumulated over five years put the company in a unique position to seize "very, very large" opportunities, says the investor. "The buyer of carbon and other commodities wants integrity in their supply chain, and Belterra wants to connect these small farmers to major global markets fairly."

After five years heading the company, Ortega reports some lessons learned. One of the most important: projects can only begin when the capital structure is fully assembled. "After planting a cocoa tree, you have to spend on it until it becomes self-sufficient, which takes three, four years. During that time you need cash flow guarantee. Money in the account."

In the past, the company relied on partners' access to subsidized rural credit—but the money didn't come. "An overdue invoice from Casas Bahia, an unpaid electricity bill... This prevents a producer from getting loans." From now on, SPVs start working when financing is secured. The plan is to design them according to specific interests of potential partners or financiers. "We'll do carbon credits, cocoa and other products. What changes is the partner's motivation. One might want cocoa, another, carbon. You structure the SPV to meet those specific demands."
