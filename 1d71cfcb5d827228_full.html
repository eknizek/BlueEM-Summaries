# Will Banxico End Interest Rate Cuts? Here's What Omar Mejía Says

The Bank of Mexico is moving toward concluding its monetary calibration process. For Omar Mejía Castelazo, subgovernor of the central bank, the possibility of an additional adjustment in the reference rate should not be read as a change in signals, but rather as the orderly closure of a technical cycle. According to the official, making one last move would allow the bank to position itself with greater strength to face external risks, such as volatility stemming from conflicts in the Middle East.

"I would see it more as a closure of this calibration process that we have been carrying out. My opinion is that the evolution of these inflationary determinants confirms that carrying out one last rate cut would place us in an appropriate position to understand risks on both sides of the balance sheet." While participating in the "Norte Económico" podcast by Grupo Financiero Banorte, he emphasized that this "terminal level" of cuts does not imply lowering the guard. Making one final adjustment would not mean relaxing the stance; it would mean calibrating it better. "It would allow the restriction that is operating to continue supporting us in achieving convergence of inflation toward the target."

He stressed that monetary policy in Mexico has maintained a significant degree of restriction for more than three years, which allows the central bank to act prudently without compromising its primary mandate: price stability.

## The Inflationary Outlook Allows Flexibility: Rodríguez Ceja

In response to voices suggesting a premature start to easing after the recent 25 basis point cut, the subgovernor clarified that this is fine-tuning in response to shocks that have mostly proven to be transitory. "The 25 basis point cut should be understood as a fine adjustment, not as a relaxation of the stance, and this is particularly important in an environment of high global uncertainty."

Despite general inflation standing at 4.53% in the first half of April, above the 3.00% target, Mejía ruled out that the country faces an out-of-control inflationary process. The subgovernor explained that current inflation in Mexico is not a widespread phenomenon, but rather the result of specific shocks in relative prices, such as modifications to the IEPS (excise tax) and specific increases in agricultural products (tomatoes and potatoes).

According to his data, 64% of the consumption basket already presents inflation rates below their pre-pandemic historical average.

## Slack and Growth

The subgovernor's vision is supported by evidence of economic cooling. Following a dynamic close to 2025, first quarter 2026 indicators, such as IGAE and investment, point to a weak start to the year. This economic "slack," he explained, functions as a natural buffer for inflation, since lower demand prevents producers from fully passing on their costs to the final consumer for fear of losing market share.

Finally, Mejía recalled that even with the adjustments, Mexico's real interest rate remains considerably elevated in historical terms. He compared that in previous periods with similar inflation levels, the rate averaged 5.75%, while today it remains significantly higher, ensuring that the "screw" of monetary policy continues to tighten sufficiently to bring prices to the 3.00% target.
