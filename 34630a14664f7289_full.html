# Ibovespa Closes Down 1.65% on Return from Holiday, with Losses in Banks and Vale

The Ibovespa (IBOV) closed Wednesday (22nd) down 1.65%, at 192,889 points, contrary to American stock exchanges which rose following an extension of the ceasefire deadline between the United States and Iran. It was the largest daily decline for the Brazilian index since March 20th. The decline reflected a profit-taking movement following the return from Tiradentes' Day holiday. Losses hit mainly banking sector stocks, which were primarily responsible for the day's downturn.

**Banking stocks:**

- Banco do Brasil (BBAS3): -3.62%

- Santander (SANB11): -3.37%

- Bradesco (BBDC4): -2.95%

- Itaú (ITUB4): -2.89%

- Bradesco (BBDC3): -2.66%

Vale (VALE3) also fell, closing with losses of 1.70% following a BHP agreement with China for iron ore supply. The dollar closed stable at R$ 4.974.

President Donald Trump reversed course once again and extended the ceasefire with Iran hours before the deadline for the countries to reach an agreement. Vice President JD Vance was set to travel to Pakistan to resume talks with Iran, but Tehran representatives refused to participate, citing demands considered unreasonable by the United States. Trump blamed the collapse of negotiations on "seriously fragmented" leadership in Iran and stated that the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz would continue despite the suspension of new attacks.

"I have therefore determined that our Armed Forces continue the blockade and, in all other respects, remain ready and capable, and I will extend the ceasefire until their proposal is presented and discussions are concluded, one way or another," Trump wrote on social media.

On Monday (20th), Trump had said it would be "highly unlikely" that he would extend the ceasefire if no agreement was reached by then. Following the decision, American indices reacted positively and closed higher.

**U.S. markets:**

- Dow Jones: +0.69%

- S&P 500: +1.05%

- Nasdaq: +1.64%

"What we have is a situation that is technically 'calm and chaotic' at the same time," said Kenny Polcari of SlateStone Wealth to Bloomberg News. "Although the conflict with Iran is ongoing, my impression is that investors are already beginning to look beyond this and focus on what really matters. We've seen this movie before: headlines arrive, the market reacts, volatility spikes and then calms down and refocuses on fundamentals."
