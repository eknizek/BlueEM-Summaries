# Dollar Falls 4% Against Real in April to R$4.95, Lowest Level Since March 2024

The spot dollar closed Thursday's session in firm depreciation against the real, on a day of relief in global risk perception, even without evident signs of improvement in geopolitical tensions between the United States and Iran. Traders also mentioned some technical end-of-month adjustment, which may have increased the magnitude of the currency appreciation movement.

With today's closing, the American currency accumulated a depreciation of 4.38% against the real in the month of April, a period marked by adjustments in the risk embedded in prices amid expectations around a resolution of the Middle East conflict, with ceasefire throughout negotiations and back-and-forth between countries. Thus, the Brazilian currency had the fourth best performance among the 33 most liquid currencies this month, behind only the Russian ruble, the Israeli shekel, and the Hungarian forint.

At the end of today's negotiations, the spot dollar registered a depreciation of 0.99%, quoted at R$4.9518, the lowest level since March 7, 2024 (R$4.9336). At today's low, the dollar reached R$4.9513 and at the high, R$4.9990. The commercial euro fell 0.48%, to R$5.8114, while in April the European currency depreciated 2.91% against the real.

Near the end of Thursday's session, the DXY index, which measures the strength of the dollar against a basket of six developed market currencies, fell 0.89%, to 98,085 points.

Since the start of today's session, the dollar has shown depreciation against the real. Although without relief in geopolitical tensions, there was a drop in oil prices and a recovery in risk assets that benefited currencies in general. Another factor that may have contributed to this session's movement was the strengthening of the Japanese yen, which pulled the DXY down, the reference for the American currency.

The dollar plummeted against the yen since this morning, after Japan's Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama stated that the moment to take "decisive action" in the foreign exchange market was approaching. The Japanese currency has reached its lowest level since 1990 in recent days, which led the market to once again raise the possibility of currency intervention, as was seen at the end of last year.

"The dollar exceeded the 160 yen level and was being traded above previous highs recorded in April. Now we are within the currency intervention zone, but Japanese authorities may be reluctant to act too quickly, given the high currency volatility and speculative yen short positions, which are far less extreme than in 2024, when they last intervened," says strategist Francesco Pesole in a note.

A currency manager, speaking on condition of anonymity, said that since the Ptax formed today will be used in swap contracts that the Central Bank will allow to expire in May, a $1 billion purchase should have occurred today, so perhaps that limited the appreciation of the Brazilian currency in the morning. Additionally, the more conservative stance of the Central Bank in yesterday's decision may be helping to keep the real well anchored.

Strategists at BBVA bank say, in a note, that the combination of slow cuts, high "carry," and the diversification of Brazilian exports (including energy and food) supports a relatively constructive perspective for the real in the current environment of geopolitical volatility. "However, the combination of external uncertainty and the proximity of the electoral cycle suggests that positioning should remain more tactical, with potential for greater volatility as the political scenario evolves."

Senior manager Carlos Carranza, from M&G, says that the real continues to be a favored position in portfolios, supported by strong performance this year and robust monetary policy that continues to stand out among emerging markets. "The currency has benefited from a combination of credibility in monetary policy and resilient economic fundamentals, in addition to broader challenges faced by the dollar," he says.

According to Carranza, the currency's resilience relative to its peers reflects three main factors: "Brazil combines disciplined monetary policy, improvement in external accounts, and still solid growth, a relatively rare combination among emerging economies."

Looking ahead, the manager says that political developments, including presidential elections, may generate periods of volatility in the short term, as is typical in electoral cycles. "However, the strength of monetary policy tends to offset this political noise in the medium term. High 'carry,' high real interest rates, and improved external accounts are seen as important stabilizing factors.
