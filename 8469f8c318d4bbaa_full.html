# Basic Food Basket Price Rises 4.2% in March, Largest Monthly Increase Since 2024

In March, the set of 33 products considered essential for the diet of Mexican families reported a monthly increase of 4.20%, the most pronounced advance for a single month since May 2024.

According to the price survey conducted monthly by Excélsior, the cost to purchase the basic food basket, defined by the now-defunct National Council for the Evaluation of Social Development Policy (Coneval), reached 2,339.70 pesos on March 31. This figure not only represents immediate pressure on household budgets, but also raises the quarterly increase in essential foods to 2.51 percent. Meanwhile, the annual increase in basic necessity products stood at 7.1 percent.

## Reflection of Inflation

The rise in basic foods is not an isolated phenomenon. Octavio de la Torre, president of the Confederation of National Chambers of Commerce, Services and Tourism (Concanaco Servytur), noted that the behavior of the food basket is a mirror of current inflationary pressure. According to data from the National Institute of Statistics and Geography (Inegi), inflation accelerated in the first half of March to 4.02% annually.

This reading not only exceeds the 3.67% recorded in the same period of 2025, but marks the fifth consecutive acceleration. Additionally, it accumulated two fortnights above 4.00%, the upper limit of the tolerance range of the Bank of Mexico.

Alejandro Saldaña, chief economist at Ve por Más (BX+), explained that this behavior was due to the rebound in non-core inflation in recent readings. On this occasion, he highlighted the sharp increase in fruits and vegetables. For their part, energy costs accelerated from low levels, perhaps partly reflecting the first effects of increases in international oil prices.

## Chile and Lemon, A Luxury

The report highlights annual variations that have disrupted household budgets. The protagonist of the price increase was the Lenten chile, whose price per kilogram shot up to 33.90 pesos, representing an annual increase of 127.5% compared to the previous year. This was followed by lemon, with an increase of 118.5%, apple (44.7%), and beef steak (22.5%).

Faced with rising prices, consumption habits in Mexico are undergoing a forced transformation. The need to stretch spending has led families to reduce their consumption of beef and chicken, opting for purchases by piece or more economical substitutes. An analysis by the firm Kantar reveals the magnitude of this change: 46% of Mexicans have already modified their shopping habits to survive the high cost of living. 35% of consumers make direct cuts to their grocery list, while 29% seek alternatives in discount stores. Furthermore, strict financial planning has become the norm for 23% of the population, in an effort to maintain food security in an environment where prices show no mercy.
