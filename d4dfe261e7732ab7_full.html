# Brazil has 50 "ChatGPTs" with Brazilian accent. Will they survive the AI race?

The launch of ChatGPT at the end of 2022 sparked the hype around generative artificial intelligence. And the hefty checks captured by OpenAI, owner of the tool, and other Large Language Models (LLMs), the engines behind this technology. However, so far, the billion-dollar race for LLMs has concentrated in a few names. And in two geographies: the United States, with OpenAI itself, which has already raised over $200 billion, Anthropic, Google, and xAI. Plus China, with players like DeepSeek. But there are those willing to challenge the predictions of this race. A study by the Federal University of Goiás (UFG) identified more than 50 LLMs developed in Brazil, which raises the following question: is there actually room for "Brazilian ChatGPTs" on this map and will they survive the AI race?

"The US and China have resources on an incomparable scale," says Patricia Magalhães, co-founder and CEO of NeuralMind, to NeoFeed. "It's not about replacing these global models. But about creating a position that gives us some advantage and sovereignty. We cannot be 100% dependent on external AIs."

Founded in 2017, emerging from Unicamp's laboratories and well before the noise generated by ChatGPT, NeuralMind is an example of this David and Goliath dispute. The startup has raised R$ 20 million, which includes a R$ 1 million contribution from Fundepar and funding resources from organizations like Finep. But this substantial difference in resources doesn't prevent the company from being one of the local deep techs willing to prove that there is a viable path for Brazilian-made LLMs, whether created from scratch or from already pre-trained models. A thesis that resonates with other market voices.

For Anderson Soares, vice president of AI Brasil and coordinator of the Center of Excellence in Artificial Intelligence at UFG, it's difficult to go head-to-head with generalist LLMs like ChatGPT and Claude, from Anthropic. But there's a good shortcut in models specialized in niches and local particularities. "Brazil has unique problems and is a very propitious validation field for almost everything, in different sectors," says Soares. "Healthcare, for example, is a data machine that few countries have, as are the financial and legal markets, which are very advanced in digitalization."

For Soares, although Brazil has relevant size, diving into this level of detail here wouldn't be on the priority list of large global models. At least not at this moment, because the country is behind markets like North America, Europe, and Asia.

Gustavo Araújo, co-founder and Chief AI Officer of Distrito, reinforces this chorus. "You can't take a US healthcare model and do a copy and paste," he says. "And the fact of having local LLMs allows companies here not to be held hostage to the terms and conditions of one or two major providers."

In parallel, a movement championed by a player that dictates the rules in another essential link of the generative AI chain promises to shake up the LLM board. This could also favor Brazilian models. "There's a relevant change in Nvidia's strategy, which has been encouraging the construction of other models and pushing a decentralization in this arena," says Araújo. "After all, they don't want to have 60% of their revenue dependent on four or five players."

With an eye on this gap and with a portfolio of models, a proprietary search engine, and applications, NeuralMind is focused on sectors like healthcare, energy, and legal services. And already serves clients such as the Court of Accounts of the Union, the National Health Agency, and Hospital das Clínicas in Porto Alegre. In addition to a spin-off from its healthcare vertical, the company's plan is to position itself as a legal tech, which involves developing, in partnership with Escavador, Jurema, a family of models for legal tasks. Magalhães has the verdict that guides these strategies. "We're talking about extremely regulated sectors and critical infrastructure with little room for error," says NeuralMind's CEO. "So, depending on a foreign model is a real risk. You can have price variation in dollars, usage policy changes, and impacts from geopolitical instabilities, which are not unlikely."

**Local Accent**

This local accent is also reflected in the names of some LLMs and local deep techs. Among them, Sabiá, from Maritaca AI, one of the pioneers in the field, as well as Cabrita, Bode, Samba, and Amazônia IA. And, from this accent, Clarice.ai is one of the startups seeking to prove it can be a different creature. Founded by Felipe Iszlaji, trained in computational linguistics with a postdoctoral degree in creative computing and AI, the company was born in 2020, the centennial year of writer Clarice Lispector, who inspired its name. And it bets on models for writing, editing, reviewing, and humanizing texts in Portuguese. After creating two Small Language Models (SLMs), Clarice.ai is developing a third, more robust version, aiming to improve its accuracy by at least 30%. On one hand, this seeks to reinforce its differentiation. On the other, it shows how the startup has already made good progress with its proposal. "We are training and refining this model with our own data," says Iszlaji. "Every month, we have 1.5 million corrections that are validated by our users."

Clarice.ai has accumulated over 500,000 users since its creation – 80,000 active today in B2C, where it has two subscription plans. Already in B2B, a front the company is beginning to develop, there are around a hundred subscribers, including publishers and media groups like Itatiaia from Minas Gerais. NeoSpace also bets on proprietary data. But from another perspective and based on a concept called Data Language Model (DLM), which, unlike LLMs and SLMs, is not fed solely by language mechanisms, but by any data and document format. Based on this principle, the company provides platforms for companies like banks, insurers, and telecom operators to train their models from their own data. The idea is to create space for applications ranging from improving customer service to anticipating customer behavior. "We deliver the brain, the DNA, the newborn child. But he will study and learn with that company's data," says Bruno Pierobon, co-founder.
