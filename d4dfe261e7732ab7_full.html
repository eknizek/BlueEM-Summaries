# Brazil has 50 "ChatGPTs" with Brazilian accent. Will they survive the AI race?

The launch of ChatGPT at the end of 2022 kicked off the generative artificial intelligence hype. And the hefty checks captured by OpenAI, owner of the tool, and other Large Language Models (LLMs), the engines behind this technology. So far, however, the billion-dollar race for LLMs has concentrated on a few names. And in two geographies: the United States, with OpenAI itself, which has already raised over $200 billion, Anthropic, Google and xAI. Plus China, with players like DeepSeek. But there are those willing to challenge the predictions of this race. A study by the Federal University of Goiás (UFG) identified more than 50 LLMs developed in Brazil, which raises the following "prompt": is there actually room for "Brazilian ChatGPTs" on this map and will they survive the AI race?

"The US and China have resources on an incomparable scale," says Patricia Magalhães, co-founder and CEO of NeuralMind, to NeoFeed. "It's not about replacing these global models. But about creating a position that gives us some advantage and sovereignty. We cannot be 100% dependent on external AIs."

Founded in 2017, from Unicamp's laboratories and well before the buzz generated by ChatGPT, NeuralMind is an example of this David versus Goliath dispute. The startup has raised R$ 20 million, which includes a R$ 1 million contribution from Fundepar and funding resources from agencies like Finep. But this substantial difference in resources does not prevent the company from being one of the local deep techs willing to prove that there is a viable path for made-in-Brazil LLMs, whether created from scratch or from already pre-trained models. A thesis that resonates with other voices in the market.

For Anderson Soares, vice president of AI Brasil and coordinator of the Center of Excellence in Artificial Intelligence at UFG, it is difficult to compete head-to-head with generalist LLMs like ChatGPT and Claude, from Anthropic. But there is a good shortcut in models specialized in niches and local particularities. "Brazil has unique problems and is a very suitable validation field for almost everything, in different sectors," says Soares. "Health, for example, is a data machine that few countries have, as are the financial and legal markets, which are very advanced in digitalization."

According to Soares, although Brazil has relevant size, delving into this level of detail here would not be on the list of priorities for major global models. At least for now, due to the fact that the country is behind markets like North America, Europe and Asia.

Gustavo Araújo, co-founder and Chief AI Officer of Distrito, reinforces this chorus. "You can't take a health model from the US and copy and paste it," he says. "And the fact of having local LLMs allows companies here not to be hostages to the terms and conditions of one or two major providers."

In parallel, a movement championed by a player that sets the rules of the game in another essential link of the generative AI chain is promising to shake up the LLM board. Which could also favor Brazilian models. "There is a relevant change in Nvidia's strategy, which has been encouraging the construction of other models and driving decentralization in this arena," says Araújo. "After all, it doesn't want to have 60% of its revenue dependent on four or five players."

With an eye on this gap and with a portfolio of models, a proprietary search engine and applications, NeuralMind is focused on sectors such as health, energy and law. And already serves clients such as the Court of Accounts of the Union, the National Agency of Supplementary Health and the Hospital das Clínicas of Porto Alegre. In addition to a spin-off from its health vertical, the company's plan is to position itself as a legal tech, which involves developing, in partnership with Escavador, Jurema, a family of models for legal tasks.

Magalhães has the verdict that guides these strategies. "We're talking about extremely regulated sectors and critical infrastructures with little margin for error," says NeuralMind's CEO. "So depending on a foreign model is a real risk. You can have price variation in dollars, changes in usage policies and impacts from geopolitical instabilities, which are not unlikely."

Local accent

This local accent also translates into the names of some LLMs and local deep techs. Among them, Sabiá, by Maritaca AI, one of the pioneers in the field, as well as Cabrita, Bode, Samba and Amazônia IA. And from this accent, Clarice.ai is one of the startups seeking to prove it can be a different beast. Founded by Felipe Iszlaji, trained in computational linguistics and with a postdoctoral degree in creative computing and AI, the company was born in 2020, the centennial year of writer Clarice Lispector, who inspired its name. And it bets on models for writing, editing, reviewing and humanizing texts in Portuguese.

After creating two Small Language Models (SLMs), Clarice.ai is developing a third, more robust version, aiming to improve its accuracy by at least 30%. Which, on one hand, seeks to reinforce its differentiation. And on the other, shows how the startup has already made good progress with its proposal. "We are training and refining this model with our own data," says Iszlaji. "Every month, we have 1.5 million corrections that are validated by our users."

Clarice.ai has accumulated more than 500,000 users since its creation – 80,000 active today in B2C, where it has two subscription plans. In B2B, the front that the company is beginning to develop, there are about one hundred subscribers, including publishers and media groups like Itatiaia from Minas Gerais.

NeoSpace also bets on proprietary data. But from another angle and starting from a concept called Data Language Model (DLM), which, unlike LLMs and SLMs, is not fed solely by language mechanisms, but by any format of data and documents.

Based on this principle, the company provides platforms for companies such as banks, insurers and telecom operators to train their models using their own data. The idea is to open space for applications ranging from improving customer service to anticipating customer behavior. "We deliver the brain, the DNA, the newborn baby.
