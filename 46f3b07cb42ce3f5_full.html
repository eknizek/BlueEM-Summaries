# Colombia's Drone Market Takes Flight: Over 500 Certified Companies Projected in Coming Years

The country stands out for its regulation, which regulates and promotes the operation of unmanned aircraft systems.

A few years ago, drones were seen as recreational devices or tools for video and photography. Today, in Colombia, they have become a key tool for sectors such as agriculture, mining, security, and logistics, amid a rapidly growing ecosystem that is gaining prominence in the region.

But the country is not only advancing in the adoption of this type of tool, but is also positioning itself as one of the leaders in regulation and attractiveness for companies. According to Mario Fernando Rodríguez Montoya, coordinator of the Drones and Urban Air Mobility Group of Civil Aeronautics, "Colombia is positioned as a reference in the continent."

"At the continental level in the Americas, after the United States, Canada, and Brazil, Colombia is a reference in regulatory, administrative, and operational matters. We are setting the pace and the purpose of Civil Aeronautics is to continue doing so, always maintaining safety, both in the air and on the ground," Rodríguez said in an interview with Portafolio.

One of the factors that has enabled this progress is the regulatory framework. The country has the Colombian Aeronautical Regulation (RAC 100), which regulates the operation of unmanned aircraft systems and acts as an industry enabler. This development has allowed for the consolidation of a dynamic ecosystem. In fact, Colombia currently has more than 5,000 certified UAS (Unmanned Aerial System) pilots, more than 8,300 registered aircraft, and more than 137 companies in the certification process, with a projection exceeding 500 companies in the coming years.

"Colombia has one of the most structured and dynamic unmanned aviation ecosystems in the region. The differentiator lies in the articulation between regulation, institutional support, academia, and industry, generating trust and formalization of the sector," Rodríguez added.

## Regulation and Professionalization of the Sector

According to civil aeronautics, the use of drones in the country is governed by two main categories: open and specific. The first corresponds to low-risk operations with strict limitations, while the second involves greater complexity and requires certification, risk management, and flight authorizations. In this regard, the official clarifies that in Colombia, the term used is not "licenses" but "certifications."

To operate in the specific category, pilots must have a certificate of aptitude, and companies must have a certificate as a UAS operator. "Certification implies operating under operational safety standards, protecting the reputation of operators and promoting the professionalization of the sector," he explains.

In 2025, for example, 2,376 flight authorization requests were analyzed, exceeding by 644 those registered in 2024, which shows the growth of this industry. Additionally, regulation has evolved with the participation of multiple state entities. Among recent changes is Resolution 00998 of 2025, which introduces new requirements, such as the obligation to have at least two aircraft for the provision of commercial services.

"Although initially perceived as restrictive, in practice regulation formalizes, organizes, and projects the competitiveness of the sector," Rodríguez notes.

## Where and How Should Drones Be Used?

The use of drones in Colombia depends on the level of risk and the environment. In the open category, flight is permitted as long as it is not carried out in restricted or high-risk areas, such as airports, military installations, or mass events. For commercial operations or in complex environments, such as cities, it is mandatory to operate under the specific category, with prior authorization from Civil Aeronautics.

In urban areas, the rules are stricter: you cannot exceed 400 feet in height (123 meters), you must maintain visual contact with the aircraft, and you must respect minimum distances from people and infrastructure. "Urban environments are of high complexity, so they require higher levels of control and authorization. This responds to the risk implied by population density," the expert explains.

Conversely, rural areas offer greater flexibility and have become a key space for the development of new applications, such as agricultural monitoring or cargo transport via drones.

Today, the impact of drones extends to multiple sectors. In agriculture, they allow optimization of crops and cost reduction; in mining, they facilitate supervision of operations; and in security, they improve surveillance and monitoring. "Unmanned aircraft transform sectors such as cargo transport, security, infrastructure, agriculture, and mining, increasing operational efficiency and improving results," Rodríguez highlights.

At the territorial level, regions such as Antioquia, Cundinamarca, Bogotá, Meta, Valle del Cauca, and Santander concentrate the highest number of authorized operations, reflecting growing adoption in different productive sectors.

## Challenges and Opportunities

Despite the progress, the growth of the sector also presents challenges. One of them is ensuring compliance with regulations, especially in a context where more and more people are acquiring these devices for illegal purposes.
