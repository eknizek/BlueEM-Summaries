# The Blind Spot of Corporate AI

*By Fernando Wolff, CEO of Tech for Humans*

Artificial intelligence is already at the center of operations for many companies. It's in customer service, internal support, commercial journeys, and process automation. The debate is no longer whether AI should be adopted, but how. And that's precisely where the blind spot lies.

The technical study "Decision Architecture and Guardrails in Corporate AI Systems" identified that a significant portion of failures in enterprise systems don't occur through explicit attacks or obvious manipulation attempts. They arise in ambiguous scenarios, when the user simply deviates from the ideal anticipated flow.

This finding shifts the focus of the discussion. For months, the market concentrated energy on how to prevent prompt injections, jailbreaks, and direct security violations. These are important topics. But in practice, what puts production systems under the most pressure are common interactions: reformulated questions, requests partially disconnected from the initial context, mixed instructions.

When AI architecture doesn't clearly separate business scope, blocking criteria, and decision hierarchy, the system operates on unstable ground. The study points out that many models struggle to distinguish legitimate intention from potential deviation when the decision structure isn't well-defined. It's not just about blocking prohibited content, but about knowing how to interpret priority, context, and limits. This is the blind spot: believing that adding filters solves a problem that is, in fact, structural.

Companies tend to communicate that their AI is safe because it has layers of protection. But security, in probabilistic environments, isn't a layer—it's a design. It's the way the system was organized to make decisions. Without this organization, two equally problematic effects emerge: either the AI becomes excessively restrictive, harming user experience, or too permissive, creating reputational risk. In both cases, the impact is silent. Small inconsistencies, small unwarranted blocks, small misaligned responses. At scale, these small frictions become operational costs and brand damage.

Corporate artificial intelligence has already moved beyond the experimental phase. If it operates as part of the company's decision-making infrastructure, it needs to be treated as such. The real debate isn't which model to use. It's how to structure responsibility, limits, and predictability within the system. Ignoring this blind spot doesn't prevent it from existing. It only makes its consequences harder to control.
