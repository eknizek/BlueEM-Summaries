# Caputo's Golden Months Accelerate Dollar-Denominated Inflation in Argentina

Argentina is heading toward becoming a country with more dollars from agriculture, Vaca Muerta, and mining, but more expensive in competitive terms for many activities. Dollar-denominated inflation has reached 18% since the last elections. How does the dollarization of investment portfolios impact Caputo's plan?

Economy Minister Luis Caputo's bet to expand the horizon of the economic program was summed up in the claim that "the next 18 months will be the best of the last decades" in terms of lower inflation and growth. Trade balance data shows robust export increases (driven by agriculture and energy, boosted by the Middle East conflict) and modest imports determined by the decline or stagnation of industrial activity levels.

According to the Capital Foundation, this year's trade balance would reach $16 billion USD, which—even without knowing where global oil prices will land (currently around $100 per barrel, estimated to end at $85)—would mean $5 billion USD more than in 2025.

The "shower" of dollars that the Economy Minister is betting on would be fueled by funds from the IDB and World Bank ($3 billion USD), which in turn could serve as collateral for another loan from private banks. Despite the currency dance, and with just over two months until debt maturities of around $4.2 billion USD, the Economy Ministry has not disclosed how it will pay. The market has no doubt it will pay on time and in full.

It is clear that the Central Bank's dollar purchases are one of the fundamental pieces of the 18 golden months. This year, the Government has purchased $6.4 billion USD in a clearly oversupplied market. But what would the dollar's price be without official demand?

A wholesale dollar at 1,378 pesos, the official rate at 1,400 pesos, and the blue market at 1,415 pesos—all well below the 1,690-peso ceiling of the exchange rate band—explain the exceptional currency moment, whose counterpart is high dollar-denominated inflation with strong impact on some key activities.

For economist Marina Dal Poggetto, dollar-denominated inflation stands at 18% since the last elections as a consequence of the advance in the cost-of-living index and the decline in the dollar price. Thus, Argentina is heading toward a country with more dollars from agriculture, agroindustry, Vaca Muerta, and mining, and more expensive in competitive terms for many activities.

Since the "Dutch disease" of 1960, when Holland discovered oil and gas at sea and the guilder appreciated against the German mark, it has been known that these processes affect many other activities. A construction report from consultant Empiria by Hernán Lacunza highlights 15 months of stagnation and that costs, despite this, are at a maximum. It states that construction costs "rose 11% in the quarter and accumulated an increase of 109% since November 2023" and forecasts that this trend will continue in the second half.

Surely Minister Caputo's golden months have as their starting point a relatively lagging and calm dollar and an interest rate, as currently exists, negative against peso-measured inflation. Guaranteeing dollar gains on peso placements (known as "carry trade") is bearing fruit for the Government in "anchoring" a portion of exported product prices.

A key and decisive result regarding the forecast of the golden months—which is also a "classic"—refers to the quantity of dollars that Argentines buy for savings, hoarding, uncertainty, or distrust. The issue is so important that for months three economists (Ricardo Arriazu, Carlos Melconian, and Rodolfo Santángelo) have been highlighting the importance of the External Asset Formation concept for the future of the economic plan.

They consider that the dollarization of investment portfolios (companies, funds, individuals, savings under mattresses, safe deposit boxes, etc.) which today exceeds $2 billion USD per month and which before the last elections in one month exceeded $6 billion USD, is the determining result for the future of Caputo's plan.

The preview of the golden months shows an economy in transition with fiscal and external surpluses becoming expensive, with sectors that grow and others that do not quite fall. And, once again, depending on greater or lesser confidence regarding whether the path toward dollar refuge moves closer or further away for those who can save.
