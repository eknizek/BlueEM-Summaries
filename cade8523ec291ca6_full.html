Lula Government Sets Rules to Cap Interest Rates on Worker Payroll Loans

24 April 2026 · 07:39 PM

# Lula Government Sets Rules to Cap Interest Rates on Worker Payroll Loans

The Ministry of Labor and Employment (MTE) published a resolution on Friday (24th) aimed at preventing excessively high interest rates in the Worker Credit program, which offers payroll-deductible loans to professionals hired under the CLT (Labor Laws Consolidation).

Created in 2025 by the Lula government to expand workers' access to bank credit to replace the old private loan line, the interest rates charged on CLT Payroll Loans have become a problem for the modality and have been concerning the government. With population debt levels rising, the government is preparing a new debt renegotiation program, a sort of "Desenrola 2.0."

The regulation does not set a ceiling for rates but creates a monitoring method that limits interest. A calculation will be made based on average interest rates charged in the market, plus a weighted standard deviation. This average rate found will be used to compare rates and attempt to curb abusive interest.

The payroll loan is guaranteed by the FGTS (Severance Indemnity Fund for Employees). The installment amount is deducted directly from the worker's paycheck.

In a statement, Febraban (Brazilian Federation of Banks) says that transparent measures aimed at curbing abuses, which appears to be the case with the MTE's initiative, would be positive and contribute to "the proper functioning of the payroll credit market, for consumer protection, in line with the sustainability of the segment and its role in providing credit access to the population." The federation adds, however, that it is still analyzing the regulation more carefully.

The ABBC (Brazilian Banks Association) says it is concerned about the measures and claims to understand that this type of action "is not the most effective instrument for reducing credit costs or expanding population access to financial products."

"The ABBC, in line with its positions, expresses concern about any form of market intervention, notably regarding interest rate limitations through administrative frameworks or regulations," says the text sent to Folha.

The regulation also determines that financial institutions charging interest considered abusive may be punished with suspension of payroll operations for workers.

Central Bank data shows that CLT payroll loan interest rates range from 1.63% to 6.87% per month, according to the latest April measurement. The regulation, edited by the Management Committee for Payroll Credit Operations, also determines that the CET (Total Effective Cost) monthly on operations cannot exceed by more than one percentage point the value of the contracted interest rate.

Another rule concerns the limitation of extra charges. Creditor insurance also cannot be charged unless there is express authorization from the worker. The MTE says that insurance was being charged as embedded in contracts, a practice prohibited by the Consumer Protection Code.

Institutions are authorized to charge only remunerative interest, penalties and late payment interest, applicable taxes, and creditor insurance if authorized. According to the norm, rates that exceed the sum of the weighted market average with its standard deviation, calculated based on operations from the previous quarter, may be considered abusive.

To reach this calculation, MTE technicians will monitor contracts made through Dataprev systems (federal government technology company), allowing the government to identify distortions and require adjustments. If institutions do not reduce rates considered excessive, they may be suspended and required to revise recent contracts.

The MTE's strategy avoids setting an interest rate ceiling, as occurs with INSS (National Social Security Institute) payroll loans and which banks opposed. The fear is that, in addition to accusations of market interference, the ministry would have to deal with credit denial to workers with lower income. With the change, the government bets on a dynamic control model in dialogue with the financial sector.

Since its launch in March 2025, the CLT payroll loan has already lent R$ 131 billion to over 9 million workers. Part of this amount, R$ 91 billion, is from new contracts. The remainder, R$ 30 billion, consists of contracts migrated from the old modality to the new one. The average interest rate is around 3.66% per month, while the average CET reaches 4.48%, according to the MTE.

Despite the expansion of the model, interest rates remain the main challenge, especially in a scenario of rising population debt. A Datafolha survey shows that two out of three Brazilians say they have financial debts, such as loans. The percentage represents 67% of respondents. Of the total, one in five Brazilians (21%) say they have overdue financial debts.

The interest rates on payroll loans can vary by 100% between one institution and another, according to Central Bank data.
