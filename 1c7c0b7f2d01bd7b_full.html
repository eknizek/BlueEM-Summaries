# Mexico's Economy Contracts 0.8% During First Quarter of 2026

Mexico's economy began 2026 with clear signs of weakening. Gross Domestic Product (GDP) registered a quarterly contraction of 0.8% between January and March, a decline larger than anticipated by market consensus and confirming a widespread deterioration in activity. This marks the first decline since late 2024 and the most pronounced contraction for a first quarter since 2020, according to the timely estimate released by the National Institute of Statistics and Geography (INEGI).

The figure surprised analysts, who expected a more moderate decline of between 0.5% and 0.6%, according to Reuters and Bloomberg surveys. The magnitude of the decline reveals a deeper slowdown in internal dynamism and questions the strength of the recovery observed at the end of the previous year.

## Widespread Decline Across Productive Sectors

The GDP contraction was neither isolated nor sectoral—it affected the three major components of the economy. Primary activities, which include agriculture, fishing, and livestock, led the decline with a 1.4% drop, reflecting structural difficulties in the agricultural sector. Secondary activities followed, such as manufacturing, construction, and mining, with a contraction of 1.1%. Even more significant was the performance of tertiary activities, linked to services and commerce, which fell 0.6%. This segment had functioned as a buffer in 2025, so its weakening suggests a broader cooling of internal demand.

In year-over-year terms, growth was practically nonexistent. GDP advanced only between 0.1% and 0.2%, well below market expectations, which reinforces the reading of an economy with limited momentum at the start of the year.

## A Deterioration That Is Not Cyclical

Economist Andrés Abadía from Pantheon Macroeconomics warns that the contraction "reflects widespread deterioration in agriculture, industry, and services," and emphasizes that this is the largest quarterly decline since late 2024. In his analysis, the decline "almost completely reverses the growth of the previous quarter." Abadía insists that the data should not be interpreted as an isolated phenomenon.

"Lower consumption, lower capital investment, and still-restrictive financial conditions continue to affect activity," he explained. Added to this are specific problems in sectors such as agriculture and mining, which have accentuated productive weakness. This diagnosis points to a more complex economic environment where deceleration responds to both internal factors and less favorable external conditions.

## Moderate Expectations for the Rest of the Year

Despite the negative start, some factors could offer relief in the coming months. Among them stands out the temporary boost that could be generated by the FIFA World Cup 2026, especially in sectors such as tourism and consumption. Additionally, construction could benefit if the United States economy shows resilience, given the close integration between the two countries. However, these elements are considered transitory and not necessarily sufficient to reverse the trend.

Pantheon Macroeconomics maintains its growth forecast for Mexico at 1.2% for 2026, although it acknowledges that risks are skewed to the downside. The first quarter balance leaves a clear conclusion: Mexico's economy enters 2026 with weak momentum and signals of deeper deceleration than anticipated. Final confirmation of this data will arrive on May 22, when INEGI publishes definitive GDP figures with complete information. In a context of lower consumption, contained investment, and restrictive financial conditions, the challenge for economic policy will be to reactivate growth without compromising stability. For now, the data suggests that the room for maneuver is limited.
