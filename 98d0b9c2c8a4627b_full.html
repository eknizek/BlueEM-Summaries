# The Up-and-Down of the Government's Agenda in Congress's Cauldron

Less than a week after the Senate imposed on President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) one of his biggest political defeats, the government assesses what remains of its priority agenda with the best chances of approval among deputies and senators. As elections approach, the voting window shrinks.

Among parliamentarians and political scientists heard by NeoFeed, the immediate assessment is that the government can advance with relative ease on matters being analyzed in the Chamber of Deputies, but will face even greater difficulty approving its initiatives in the Senate. There are some bills—under analysis by deputies—and provisional measures, more removed from polarization and political disputes contaminated by the pre-election scenario, that may survive current turbulence: ending the 6x1 work schedule; the package of provisional measures (MPs) to contain fuel prices due to the Iran war; the Desenrola 2.0 program launched on Monday, May 4; and the critical minerals bill, whose report was presented today.

"Some things escape this dispute. The basic rates of taxes from the tax reform, for example. These are state agendas that stay out of this fight. I believe these things move forward," analyzes political scientist and professor Leonardo Barreto. "The government can pass themes it finds significant, but what will pass is beyond its control."

For Barreto, so-called "state agendas" remain more immune to the effects of the political storm imposed by Senate President Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP), who orchestrated the rejection in the plenary of Lula's nominee to the Supreme Court (STF), Attorney General of the Union (AGU) Jorge Messias. Since 1894, no nominee to the Supreme Court had been rejected by senators.

Still, these proposals may pass because they depend more on coordination among political leaders and economic sectors than on the Palácio do Planalto. The prime example is the Constitutional Amendment (PEC) or bill proposals that reduce work hours in Brazil and eliminate the 6x1 schedule (six working days and one day off).

The business sector is conceptually opposed to ending the current work schedule and would even prefer this discussion be postponed until after elections, preferably starting in 2027. However, with the installation of the special committee and the political will of Chamber President Hugo Motta (Republicanos-PB) to accelerate the matter, a more favorable environment is created for faster processing.

The count of sessions needed to end the committee has already begun. And the rapporteur of the PEC on the new work schedule, Deputy Leo Prates (Republicanos-BA), is open to discussing with business sectors possible points that could be included in his opinion. A source following negotiations says that business parliamentary fronts now accept discussing which points of the labor reform proposed and approved by former President Michel Temer's (MDB) government could be incorporated into the final report of the PEC ending the 6x1 schedule.

To NeoFeed, Prates said that President Motta is comfortable regarding the PEC's submission for voting, and the forecast is that the proposal will go to the plenary around May 28.

When asked in recent days whether the PEC will ultimately be identified with the right or left, they respond: "the PEC will have Hugo Motta's face."

"In the Chamber, the PEC will pass in May. No political turbulence will hamper processing of the 6x1 end in the special committee. We are confident," Deputy Rubens Pereira Jr (PT-MA), vice-leader of the government in the Chamber and one of government parliamentarians most actively supporting the PEC, told NeoFeed.

Even if it reaches the Senate later this year, though surrounded by doubts, ending the 6x1 schedule still has more viable path toward possible approval. Motta's interlocutors have said that Alcolumbre's dispute with the government and quarrels with the STF are isolated and in theory there is favorable context for the proposal: parliamentarians will hardly risk voting against such a popular theme in an election year.

**Stalled Agendas**

On another front, bills like Redata, a tax incentive program to expand datacenter investments in the country, which was already shrouded in uncertainty and stalled in the Senate, face lower probability of passing, at least for now.

According to a source following negotiations on the subject, there is still uncertainty about the size of tax benefits the Ministry of Finance would accept granting companies. Redata is an issue of government interest, which even issued an MP and sent a bill to Congress. The MP, however, expired without congresspeople voting in time, and the bill stalled.

Another proposal on the subject, authored by current Minister José Guimarães (Institutional Relations), who was the government's leader in the Chamber, was even approved by deputies in plenary but is stuck precisely in the Senate, awaiting Alcolumbre and allies.

"Redata, if it's the way the government wants, remains compromised and is unlikely to move forward. [The AI regulation bill] also won't pass anytime soon," analyzes political consultant João Henrique Hummel, creator of several parliamentary fronts in the productive sector.

The AI bill, with Aguinaldo Ribeiro (PP-PB) as rapporteur—who also reported the tax reform in the Chamber—moves with difficulty, and there's even a possibility the deputy will include Redata points in his final opinion.

A senior member of the economic team admits "much uncertainty" at this moment regarding government initiatives in the legislature but says there is still hope that some bills can be approved this year.

"Everything that provides political advantages for President Lula doesn't pass in the Senate right now. In the Chamber, only what interests Hugo Motta passes," Hummel adds.

For the consultant, more than in the first three years of Lula's current mandate, the government will need to intensify negotiations project by project with the tools at its disposal, such as unlocking 17 appointments to executive positions in regulatory agencies.
