# Colgas leads energy sector with accelerated payments to SME suppliers

In Colombia, the lack of liquidity continues to be a problem that limits the growth of SMEs, despite the Fair Payment Terms Law established in 2022, which set a maximum of 45 calendar days—with some exceptions—for companies to meet their commercial obligations. However, in practice, payment times continue to extend to 60 and even 90 days, shifting the financial burden to smaller suppliers.

According to Fedesarrollo's 2025 evaluation, the Fair Payment Terms Law was known by only 51% of the 515 companies surveyed, while 56.8% believed that payment times had not improved with its implementation. Additionally, before the law, 56.6% of 267 companies indicated that the greatest negative impact of delays was on their cash flow.

Amid this lag that leaves SMEs bearing the burden of financing large companies with their own working capital, some companies have begun adjusting their processes to reduce payment terms, in an effort to relieve financial pressure on SMEs and boost the productive chain.

Colgas is an example of how to promote business development for smaller companies. The company went beyond what the law establishes and decided to pay its SME suppliers in 7 days.

"This initiative stems from a deep conviction: if we want to foster the entrepreneurial spirit, we must also do so through the way we relate to those who make our operations possible. By accelerating payments, we seek to reduce dependence on short-term financing, provide greater operational capacity to our suppliers, and build fairer, more sustainable, and long-term commercial relationships," explained Luis Felipe Ocampo, legal and corporate affairs manager at Colgas.

During 2025, Colgas' 7-day payment benefited 1,419 suppliers, 100% of which are SMEs. Furthermore, during that period, 24,337 invoices were processed, showing that this is not a one-time measure but a scheme with scale and recurrence within operations.

To achieve 7-day payments, the company had to adjust its invoice reception platform, expedite SME supplier identification, implement management indicators to monitor bottlenecks, and provide continuous monitoring at each stage of the process. In other words, it was not just a matter of willingness but redesigning operations to make timely payment truly possible.

"Experience demonstrates that paying faster is not just a good administrative practice; it is a decision that strengthens supplier liquidity, improves supply chain sustainability, and materializes the purpose of fostering the entrepreneurial spirit through commercial relationships. That's why we believe this should not be an isolated effort: we hope more companies in Colombia will join more agile payment models, because when a large company pays better, it not only strengthens its suppliers but helps strengthen the entire business fabric of the country," explained Luis Felipe Ocampo.

This change in payment terms has allowed suppliers to directly improve cash flow and reduce the need to resort to external credit to cover basic obligations such as payroll, purchase of inputs, taxes, or operational expenses. In other words, they can operate with greater peace of mind and less financial pressure.

Additionally, timely payments strengthen business sustainability, facilitate planning, and increase capacity for investment and growth.

Colgas' initiative shows that, with determination and commitment, large companies can do their part to improve their suppliers' cash flow, reduce their dependence on debt, and contribute to a more balanced business ecosystem, to the benefit of the country's development.
