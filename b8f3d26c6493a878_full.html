SMU President Pilar Dañobeitía: Shareholders Committed to Continue Investing

25 April 2026 · 05:31 PM

# Pilar Dañobeitía, SMU President: "Our Shareholders are Convinced They Want to Continue Investing"

Pilar Dañobeitía has known Álvaro Saieh for decades and is one of the businessman's most trusted confidants. She herself emphasizes that she has "great admiration and respect for him as a great businessman and for the great contribution he has made to this country," she says. "It has been quite a trajectory and I owe him a lot, not only in the professional sense," she acknowledges.

With the votes of this group, this professional is installed as president of supermarket company SMU, which has just announced a three-year plan requiring an investment of approximately 370 billion pesos. This plan, which she details in this interview with DF MAS, involves opening 60 stores: 25 Unimarc, 8 Super 10, 5 Alvi, and 22 Maxi Ahorro Perú, in addition to the opening of a new distribution center in Santiago and two others in southern Chile—in Los Ángeles and Puerto Montt. According to the company, fulfilling this entire plan will result in hiring around 2,000 employees.

Dañobeitía projects that after a 2025 she calls one of "consolidation," combined with the store openings announced, 2026 should show better performance. "It has to be better," she says, agreeing to answer questions circulating publicly regarding the difficulties Saieh has faced, of a financial and debt service nature, with Dañobeitía ruling out that they have affected the supermarket company's plans.

**In the 2025 balance sheet, profits increased 30%, but there was a sales decline, and a significant part of that good result was due to extraordinary factors, such as local asset sales. Was it a good year?**

It was a very important consolidation year for SMU in terms of formats. We had four formats: Unimarc; Alvi, a wholesale format; Mayorista 10, which was a hybrid; and Súper 10, an economical supermarket positioned as Chile's cheapest. What we did was finish transforming the Mayorista 10 format stores and adapted them to clear wholesale and economical supermarket formats. So 2025, being this consolidation year, the wholesale formats show flatter performance. However, we're already seeing the results. On the Unimarc side, it was a spectacular year—all the stores we opened performed almost 25% better than planned in sales.

SMU is almost purely a food business, so it has very stable risk because food is a necessity item and is quite stable across cycles. It's not a company affected by price increases or decreases. It's a company with a stock that has very noble behavior because it has very stable returns. And as we continue with the growth we have projected for the next three years, we will increase the execution of that growth by 40%.

**If the asset and Lo Aguirre distribution center sales hadn't occurred, given that sales per square meter and sales in general are declining, how would the performance have been?**

Growth has been quite healthy. The matter of extraordinary asset sales also has a strategic purpose. SMU is a company that doesn't invest in properties but rather has locations with long-term contracts. And in cases of opportunity, when there's a location, a very strategic and available piece of land to buy, we do it, but then we look for an interested buyer and sell it. These very specific asset sales will continue.

**Adding and subtracting?**

We're very happy. This is a company, an industry whose margins are quite modest. So you have to be very concerned with seeking efficiencies. Last year, for example, with the increase in electricity rates and the minimum wage hike and all those effects, we managed to offset all those spending increases through operational efficiency. We applied for free client tariffs.

**Is Peru a market where you want to remain?**

In particular, we're growing in northern Peru, in Piura. We have locations in Lima, but we're focusing on northern Peru because it's an area with higher safety levels. We have seven locations we'll open this year in northern Peru.

**How will you finance this plan?**

The entire organic growth plan is financed 100% by the company's operational results. SMU's operational cash flow is very healthy. It doesn't require major debt increases. It has been able to self-finance not only capex but the complete operation, dividends, and operations, and also handle its debt maturity profile and interests.

**You also announced a new share buyback plan, and the rationale given was that the current price doesn't reflect the company's value. Why doesn't the market believe in SMU?**

Our conviction as a board and as shareholders is that the stock price does not reflect the company's intrinsic value. It's not just us saying it—analysts say it too. Those who follow the company have an average target price of 183 pesos per share. Therefore, we believe it's necessary to strengthen and give signals in that direction. If the market didn't believe in SMU, the AFPs wouldn't have increased their stake in the company less than a year ago, and they did so strongly, increasing their participation by more than 10%. We hope that, as happened the last time we gave this signal and issued this program, this will have an effect that reaffirms the value we believe the company has.

**Do you think there's a distrust effect from Álvaro Saieh's financial difficulties?**

That's a matter on which it doesn't correspond to me to give an opinion. All I can say regarding the relationship or influence it might have is that we have tremendously solid corporate governance. Our corporate governance is very strong.

**The interpretation is that there might be a contagion effect from his financial difficulties or perhaps some reluctance or fear of being in a company where he's the controlling shareholder. Don't you see it that way?**

No. And not just us. Our risk rating agencies just reaffirmed our classification. So I think there are enough concrete facts ratifying that the company is sufficiently profitable, that it has its plan and that it also has intrinsic value that isn't being reflected.

**Is Álvaro Saieh committed to this plan and to SMU?**

We gather that our shareholders are convinced they want to continue investing. Yesterday (Thursday) we had the shareholders' meeting; there were representatives from the controlling shareholder and the AFP. If he's convinced, of course he is. He's committed.

**I ask because it's a public fact that Álvaro Saieh faces a difficult situation. The obvious question is: Is selling SMU part of his strategy?**

On that matter, it doesn't correspond to me to give an opinion.

**It's not an opinion. You're close to the owner, you know his plans, you have his votes. Therefore, it's absolutely relevant to ask if selling SMU is part of that strategy as a way to address those various financial situations, etc.**

I repeat: it doesn't correspond to me to participate in those decisions. I'm not at that decision-making table because those are decisions that belong to the controlling shareholder and the team that CorpGroup has in charge of these matters. I'm not part of that team. I'm president of SMU. In my role it doesn't correspond to me to give an opinion, and I don't have that information as a peer.
