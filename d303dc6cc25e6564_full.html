# Global Economy: War and Inflation

The above is a triggering factor for the increase in the cost of almost all basic consumer goods worldwide that, when added to the low growth the global economy has experienced, could revive one of the worst fears of economists and put central banks around the world in a dilemma.

Since the end of February of this year, when the armed conflict between the United States and Israel against Iran began, the uncertainty that already persisted in the global economic outlook grew even further. At the most critical moment of the conflict between both countries, the price of oil rose 12.23% in just one day, and its effects have quickly spread to all sectors.

Although its price is currently controlled at around 90 dollars per barrel, concerns persist about the effects this could have in the future. According to the latest figures from the Bureau of Labor Statistics, in March of this year the U.S. economy experienced a sharp increase in the annual inflation rate, recorded at 3.3%, up from 2.4% in February.

As expected, this increase is mainly linked to the rise in energy prices, as gasoline increased 18.9% and diesel 44.2% on an annual basis. These figures set records in the institution's registrations, which noted in its report that gasoline had not seen an increase of this magnitude in its monthly rate index since the first record was published in 1967.

Moreover, this inflation increase does not come alone, as even in the strongest economies there is fear that in addition to rising prices, economic activity could begin to decelerate. This effect, known as "stagflation," is not new; it became popular in the 1970s when a similar event occurred involving the two nations at the center of the current conflict.

Based on problems that originated in Iran during 1973 and 1979, the reduction in supply by Middle Eastern countries that were part of the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC) caused a sudden increase in crude oil prices and, in turn, an increase in the cost of basic goods in both developed and developing countries.

This triggered a chain effect that directly impacted industrial production worldwide and, of course, economic growth. The largest economies—and at the same time, most dependent on Middle Eastern oil—were the most affected: the United States, United Kingdom, Germany, Italy, and Japan suffered the effects of the combination of double-digit inflation and low economic activity growth.

Although five decades have passed since then, the dependence on oil for the functioning of a large part of the economy, especially the industrial sector, has not changed significantly and continues to create vulnerability to external shocks.

Bringing this issue to our country and again comparing the situation with that of 1973, in that decade Mexico went from being a net importer to an exporter of oil, so while the world's largest economies were affected, the Mexican economy benefited. However, this status did not last long for our economy, which has led the government to currently seek to reduce dependence on fuel imports, which would be an effective measure to avoid external shocks in the medium and long term.

On the other hand, the dilemma faced by central banks is far from simple. According to a survey on the expectations of private sector economic specialists conducted by Banco de México during March, inflation showed an increase from February, rising from 4 to 4.21%. Meanwhile, expectations regarding economic growth remained at a level close to 1.50 percent.

This dilemma will continue to resonate in the coming months, because while our central bank's primary objective is not to use its tools to favor economic growth, it must pay attention to these factors when making its next decisions. Finally, despite achieving a truce between the nations involved in the war, with a ceasefire and reopenings (and new closures) of the Strait of Hormuz, the parallels with what happened in the 1970s remain present. Let us hope that this time, the outcome will be different.
