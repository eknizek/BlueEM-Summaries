Arequipa Projects 20% Tourism Growth in 2026: Which Sectors Does It Drive and How Does It Aim to Be a Southern Hub?

# Arequipa Projects 20% Tourism Growth in 2026: Which Sectors Does It Drive and How Does It Aim to Be a Southern Hub?

Arequipa is consolidating itself as one of the most important tourist destinations in southern Peru, having closed 2025 as the year in which it solidified its post-pandemic recovery. During that period, the region received approximately 300,000 visitors, a figure that practically doubles pre-pandemic levels (between 150,000 and 170,000 tourists) and marks favorable progress compared to 2024. Of the total, 60% were foreign and 40% were domestic, reflecting the weight of the international market in the sector.

With this performance, what is projected for 2026? The flow of visitors allowed tourism to generate an estimated economic impact of S/360 million in 2025, not counting airfare. This result responds to an average spending of between S/1,000 and S/1,200 per tourist for stays of up to three days, which drives sectors such as hospitality, gastronomy, transportation, and commerce. "This tourism moves our economy because it helps from the microentrepreneur to the large businessman," affirmed Carlos Fernández, president of the Arequipa Chamber of Commerce and Industry.

## 2026 Projections: Tourism, Visits, and Economic Impact

Looking ahead to 2026, projections point to maintaining the dynamism of the tourism sector in Arequipa. The Arequipa Chamber of Commerce and Industry estimates growth close to 20% in visitor flow, which would allow reaching between 350,000 and 360,000 tourists, maintaining the historical composition of 60% foreign and 40% domestic.

In economic terms, this greater flow would translate into a relevant increase in sector revenues. For 2026, tourism is projected to generate approximately S/500 million, consolidating its role as one of the main drivers of the regional economy. In that context, the average ticket per tourist, currently between S/1,000 and S/1,200, could rise to S/1,500.

In this context, strengthening infrastructure emerges as the main challenge to sustain tourism growth, especially in air and road connectivity. "If we improve the airport and access routes, we can turn Arequipa into a tourism hub for the southern part of the country," said Carlos Fernández, proposing a vision that seeks to position the city as a connection hub with destinations such as Colca, Lake Titicaca, Cusco, and Madre de Dios.

## Most Visited Places and Sectors Driven by Tourism

Regarding tourist attractions, the Santa Catalina Monastery and the Colca Valley remain the most visited destinations in Arequipa, both with approximately 290,000 visitors in 2025, in line with the total tourist flow in the region. To these are added the historic center, Yanahuara, the stone route in Yura, and circuits such as Sachaca, Sabandía, and Huasacache, which integrate the most demanded routes, generally on short itineraries before transfers to Colca.

This movement directly impacts the tourism value chain, where hospitality and gastronomy each concentrate around 30% of revenues, while the other 30% is distributed among transportation, shopping, and additional services. Among the most benefited sectors stand out accommodations, restaurants, and the trade in crafts and textiles.

## Tourist Profile: Predominance of Foreign Visitors

The profile of tourists visiting Arequipa maintains a clear predominance of foreign visitors, representing approximately 60% of the total, compared to 40% of domestic tourists. According to Carlos Fernández, the main source markets come from the United States, Europe, and Asia, to which is added regional flow, especially from Chile, with entries via land through Arica. However, domestic tourism has gained greater participation in recent years.

"The domestic tourist is traveling much more than before, especially young people with families," indicated Fernández, evidencing a change in consumption habits after the pandemic. This greater dynamism responds to greater willingness to spend on leisure and experiences.

## Growth of Corporate and Premium Tourism

Corporate tourism in Arequipa shows sustained evolution, driven primarily by business events and the mining sector. According to Fernández, activities such as Perumín and conventions linked to mining and construction attract international visitors and executives, generating occupancy peaks in hotels and restaurants. "The mining issue brings together companies from all over the world and during that week the hotels and restaurants fill up," said the executive.

In that context, greater dynamism is also observed in the premium segment, associated with more demanding demand for lodging, gastronomy, and transportation services. This visitor profile—mainly executives and foreign tourists—drives a higher value-added offering in the region. "Demand has increased for premium hotels, restaurants, and transportation," indicated Fernández.

## Challenges Facing the Tourism Sector in Arequipa

Tourism growth in Arequipa faces a series of structural challenges that could limit its expansion in the coming years, especially in terms of connectivity and infrastructure. The local airport lacks the capacity and equipment needed to sustain a greater flow of flights, a situation that worsens during the rainy season, when cancellations occur that directly affect economic activity. Adding to this is congestion on the Pan-American Highway, the main access route to the region. "We are not prepared," warned Carlos Fernández, noting that these limitations have been addressed in dialogue forums with the Ministry of Transportation, Corpac, and the road concessionaire.
