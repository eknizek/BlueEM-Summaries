# Market Sees Opportunity for Soft Recovery

The Brazilian capital market has gone through a cycle of extremes over the last five years. A period of abundant stock offerings (IPOs), driven by low interest rates, was followed by a rise in the Selic rate, putting pressure on company balance sheets, foreign investors leaving the stock exchange, and no IPOs for more than four years. Now, the market may be at an inflection point, driven by the start of an interest rate cutting cycle and strong interest from foreign capital. If war allows it.

The U.S. confrontation in the Middle East has pushed the oil barrel past $100, which discourages economic growth, is inflationary and compromises interest rate cuts — for Brazil, however, it also helps the balance of payments. But a recent comment by President Donald Trump about the proximity of an agreement was enough for the dollar to hit its lowest point against the real in more than two years and for the Ibovespa, the benchmark of the Brazilian stock exchange, to hit a new all-time high.

The country's fiscal situation and the electoral race even took a back seat, forgotten by investors. "The level of activity between investors and companies is visibly higher than last year," says Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

It is not yet local fundamentals that have moved the Ibovespa needle: a flood of foreign capital entered the Brazilian stock exchange to diversify risk in light of noisy political impulses in major economies. More than R$ 67 billion from foreign investors has already entered the exchange — but local institutional investors withdrew half of that.

The external flow led to a rise in securities, after a period of compression, causing local managers to take some profits to guarantee returns on applications and rotate portfolio securities, as new capital raises with local investors have not yet resumed. One of the country's largest managers, Luis Stuhlberger, also realized part of his Brazilian stock position in early of the year, but later ended up buying more. His view on the Brazilian economy hasn't changed much in recent months, but the flow prevailed.

"I'm not going to get in front of this emerging markets allocation train," Stuhlberger told Pipeline. Verde's letter for March highlighted the increase in local stock position and fund gains from the allocation.

The dystopia of indicators has kept bankers reasonably optimistic about a resumption of activity in the market, while businesspeople remain more cautious, trying to renegotiate their still-expensive debts and planning modest investment cycles. Stock offering volumes are still modest, but the queue has formed again. Before the Iran war, which ended up affecting asset pricing worldwide, many companies were anticipating the disclosure of fourth-quarter results to prepare for this window.

"The point is that interest rates fall and the stock market rises, it's hydraulic," says André Moor, head of investment banking at Bradesco BBI, who continues to see an inflection point. Although amid market concerns that the cycle could be altered, Copom cut the Selic by 0.25 percentage points in March. In the Focus Bulletin, the forecast is that the Selic, currently at 14.75%, will be at 12.5% by December. It is still a high enough rate to keep local investors in fixed income, but for companies draining their cash generation in debt service, it begins to make some difference.

"We are at an inflection point, but it will be a little less pronounced than expected. It will be more gradual and over time because interest rates will take time to fall," says Cristiano Guimarães, head of investment banking at Itaú BBA. He emphasizes that in April through the 15th, almost R$ 15 billion entered, with the war cooling down giving body to foreign flow again. "But the Brazilian investor is still very relevant in offerings, so the migration of capital to the stock exchange won't happen suddenly. A gradual warming presupposes a return of IPOs from larger companies, with more liquidity and with potential sensitivity to value," adds Guimarães.

Precisely because of the tight financial situation of many companies in the current high-interest scenario, a stock market drained for new offerings over a long period and banks more cautious in lending, the movement in recent years of expansion of the private credit market was relevant — now resulting in a series of restructurings.

"We've had very high interest rates for five years. The reflection is appearing now in companies and we should remain at high rates for another two years," says businessman Rubens Menin. "For better or worse, Brazil is a state of law, the environment works and we have strategic advantages in various sectors, but interest rates are killing our future."

A renowned lawyer involved in several current restructurings agrees that new processes should continue to happen, but the renegotiation climate can improve. "With the start of the interest rate cut, the conversation can be healthier with the creditor because the company's operational situation tends to improve," he says.

The foreigners help, but what defines the turnaround is the Selic.
