# Market Sees Chance for Soft Recovery

The Brazilian capital market has gone through a cycle of extremes over the last five years. A period of abundant stock offerings (IPOs), driven by low interest rates, was followed by the rise of the Selic rate, pressuring company balance sheets, foreign investors leaving the stock exchange, and no IPOs for more than four years. Now, the market may be at an inflection point, driven by the beginning of an interest rate cut cycle and strong interest from foreign capital. If war allows it.

The confrontation between the United States and the Middle East has pushed the oil barrel past US$ 100, which discourages economic growth, is inflationary, and compromises interest rate reductions - for Brazil, however, it also helps the balance of payments. But a recent statement by President Donald Trump about the proximity of an agreement was enough for the dollar to hit its minimum against the real in more than two years and for the Ibovespa, Brazil's stock market benchmark, to hit a new all-time high.

The country's fiscal situation and the electoral race were even left in the background, forgotten by investors. "The level of activity between investors and companies is visibly higher than last year," says Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

It is not yet the local fundamentals that have moved the Ibovespa's needle: a flood of foreign capital entered the Brazilian stock exchange to diversify risk amid noisy political impulses in major economies. Over R$ 67 billion from foreign investors has already entered the exchange - but local institutional investors withdrew half of that.

The external flow led to the rise of securities, after times of compression, causing local managers to realize some profits to guarantee returns on investments and rotate portfolio papers, as new fundraising with local investors has not yet resumed. One of the country's largest managers, Luis Stuhlberger, also realized part of his position in the Brazilian stock market at the beginning of the year, but then ended up buying more. His view on the Brazilian economy hasn't changed much in recent months, but the flow imposed itself. "I'm not going to get in front of this allocation train for emerging markets," Stuhlberger told Pipeline. Verde's letter for March highlights the increase in position in the local stock market and the fund's gains with the allocation.

The dystopian nature of the indicators has kept bankers reasonably optimistic about a resumption of market activity, while entrepreneurs remain more cautious, trying to renegotiate their still-expensive debts and planning modest investment cycles. Stock offering volumes are still modest, but the queue has formed again. Before the Iran war, which ended up affecting asset pricing worldwide, many companies had been anticipating the disclosure of fourth-quarter balance sheets to prepare for this window.

"The point is that interest rates fall and the stock market rises, it's hydraulic," says André Moor, head of Bradesco BBI's investment banking, who continues to see an inflection point. Although amid market concerns that the cycle could be altered, Copom cut the Selic by 0.25 percentage points in March. In the Focus Bulletin, the forecast is that the Selic, currently at 14.75%, will be at 12.5% by December. It is still a rate high enough to keep the local investor in fixed income, but for companies that are draining their cash generation in debt service, it begins to make some difference.

"We are at an inflection point, but it will be a little less pronounced than expected. It will be smoother and over time because interest rates will take longer to fall," says Cristiano Guimarães, head of investment banking at Itaú BBA. He notes that in April up to the 15th, almost R$ 15 billion entered, with the easing of war again supporting foreign flows. "But the Brazilian investor is still very relevant in offerings, so capital migration to the stock market won't happen suddenly. A gradual warming presupposes a return of IPOs from larger companies, with more liquidity and with potential sensitivity to value," adds Guimarães.

Precisely because of the tight financial situation of many companies in the current high interest rate scenario, due to a stock market drained of new offerings for a long period, and more cautious banks in lending, the movement in recent years of expansion of the private credit market has been relevant - resulting now in a series of restructurings.

"We've had very high interest rates for five years. The reflection of it is showing up now in companies and we should remain at a high rate level for two more years," says businessman Rubens Menin. "For better or worse, Brazil is a rule of law, the environment works, and we have strategic advantages in various sectors, but interest rates are killing our future."

A renowned lawyer involved in several current restructurings agrees that new processes should continue to happen, but the renegotiation climate could improve. "With the beginning of the interest rate decline, the conversation can become more healthy with the creditor because the company's operational situation tends to improve," he says.

The foreigners help, but what defines the turnaround is the Selic rate.
