# Market Sees Chance for Soft Recovery

The Brazilian capital market has gone through a cycle of extremes over the past five years. A period of abundant stock offerings (IPOs), driven by low interest rates, was followed by the rise of the Selic rate, pressuring company balance sheets, foreign investors fleeing the stock exchange, and no IPOs in over four years. Now, the market may be at an inflection point, driven by the beginning of an interest rate cutting cycle and strong interest from foreign capital. If the war allows it.

The U.S. confrontation in the Middle East drove the oil barrel to pass $100, which discourages economic growth, is inflationary, and compromises interest rate reductions – for Brazil, however, it also helps the balance of payments. But it took just a recent speech by President Donald Trump about the proximity of an agreement for the dollar to hit its lowest point against the real in over two years and for the Ibovespa, Brazil's stock market benchmark, to hit a new historical high.

The country's fiscal situation and the electoral race even took a back seat, forgotten by investors. "The level of activity between investors and companies is visibly higher than last year," says Antonio Coutinho, head of investment banking at Citi Brasil.

It is not yet the local fundamentals that moved the Ibovespa needle: a flood of foreign capital entered the Brazilian stock exchange to diversify risk in the face of noisy political impulses in large economies. Over R$ 67 billion from foreigners have already entered the stock exchange – but local institutional investors withdrew half of that.

The external flow led to the rise of securities after a period of compression, causing local managers to realize some profit to guarantee returns on their investments and rotate portfolio securities, as new fundraising with local investors has not yet resumed. One of the country's largest managers, Luis Stuhlberger, also realized part of his position in the Brazilian stock market at the beginning of the year, but later ended up buying more. His view of the Brazilian economy hasn't changed much in recent months, but the flow imposed itself. "I'm not going to get in front of this emerging markets allocation train," Stuhlberger told Pipeline. Verde's letter for March highlights the increase in position in the local stock market and the fund's gains from the allocation.

The dystopia of indicators has kept bankers reasonably optimistic about a recovery in market activity, while businesspeople remain more cautious, trying to renegotiate their still expensive debts and planning modest investment cycles. Stock offering volumes are still modest, but the queue has reformed. Before the Iran war, which ended up affecting asset pricing worldwide, many companies had been anticipating the disclosure of their fourth-quarter balance sheets to prepare for this window. "The point is that interest rates fall and the stock market rises, it's hydraulic," says André Moor, head of Bradesco BBI investment banking, who continues to see an inflection point.

Even though the market feared the cycle could be altered, Copom cut the Selic by 0.25 percentage points in March. In the Focus Bulletin, the forecast is that the Selic, currently at 14.75%, will be at 12.5% by December. It's still a high enough rate to keep the local investor in fixed income, but for companies that are draining their cash generation in debt service, it begins to make some difference.

"We are at an inflection point, but it will be a little less pronounced than expected. It will be softer and over time because interest rates will take time to fall," says Cristiano Guimarães, head of investment banking at Itaú BBA. He notes that in April through the 15th, almost R$ 15 billion entered, with the easing of war giving new body to foreign flow. "But the Brazilian investor is still very relevant in offerings, so capital migration to the stock market won't happen suddenly. Gradual heating presupposes a return of IPOs from larger companies, with more liquidity and with potential sensitivity to value," adds Guimarães.

Precisely because of the tight financial situation of many companies in the current high interest rate scenario, a stock market drained for new offerings over a long period, and more cautious banks in lending, the movement in recent years of private credit market expansion was relevant – resulting now in a series of restructurings. "We've had very high interest rates for five years. Its reflection is appearing now in companies and we should remain at elevated rate levels for another two years," says businessman Rubens Menin. "For better or worse, Brazil is a rule of law state, the environment works and we have strategic advantages in various sectors, but interest rates are killing our future."

A renowned lawyer involved in several current restructurings agrees that new processes should continue to happen, but the renegotiation climate can improve. "With the beginning of interest rate cuts, the conversation can become healthier with the creditor because the company's operational situation tends to improve," he says.

The foreigners help, but the Selic defines the turnaround.
