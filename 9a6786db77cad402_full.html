Rebound Coming?: Private Sector Reports 1.5% Jump in Mass Consumption in March

23 April 2026 · 10:25 PM

# Rebound Coming?: Private Sector Reports 1.5% Jump in Mass Consumption in March

After negative economic activity data in February, with an interannual decline of 2.1% according to INDEC, some March figures are beginning to show improvement. The most significant development concerns mass consumption, which according to NielsenIQ measurements grew 1.5% last month compared to the same month in 2025.

NielsenIQ tracks consumption across supermarket chains, mini-markets, kiosks, and pharmacies, among other similar formats. The 1.5% figure in March exceeds the 0.8% and 0.6% recorded in January and February, respectively. However, it falls far short of March's year-ago numbers, when it had advanced 4.1% compared to March 2024.

In the measurement, food and beverage categories show the most notable increases, with rises of 2.2% and 2.4%, respectively. The standout segment is candy, with a jump of 7.7%. In contrast, the Personal Care and Cleaning sector continues showing negative numbers, contracting 2.3%, with declines of 1.7% for Cosmetics and Toiletries and 3.8% for Home and Laundry Cleaning.

Meanwhile, a report from the Argentine Chamber of Commerce reflects data contradictory to Nielsen's, showing a consumption decline of 1.3% in March. However, this is a broader measurement covering various sectors. They note increases in Clothing of 0.6% and Transportation of 0.7%. Meanwhile, Recreation and Culture fell 2.9% and Housing, Rentals and Services fell 0.8%.

The Private Consumption Index developed by Universidad de Palermo rose 0.7% in March compared to February, but compared to March last year it fell 2.6% and accumulated a 2% decline in the first three months of 2026.

March also shows some positive data in durable goods. The most notable case is motorcycles, whose sales jumped 54.8% in interannual measurement. As for cars, March's increase in vehicle registrations was 1.2% compared to the same month last year.

## Two-Speed Economy

Despite these encouraging figures, the economy continues moving at two speeds. The Monthly Economic Activity Estimator (EMAE) measured by INDEC fell 2.1% interannual in February, the worst result in 17 months. Another concerning figure comes from Torcuato Di Tella University's measurement. In April, the Consumer Confidence Index (ICC) stood at 39.64 points, implying a 5.68% decline compared to the previous month. Compared to March last year, the plunge is even deeper, reaching 10.12%.

The UTDT report also emphasizes that the current level is 16.33% below the peak reached in January 2025, though it remains 11.35% above the floor recorded in January 2024 when the impact of Javier Milei's administration's initial measures was strongly felt.

Private analysts agree on highlighting the duality with which the economy operates. From BBVA, they point out that "economic activity shows signs of recovery, driven by exporting sectors and some recomposition of consumption, though with marked heterogeneity. The labor market continues weak, with informal employment growth and scarce dynamism in registered employment, while the external front remains a potential vulnerability factor."

From the Fundación Mediterránea, Marcos Cohen Arazi notes that February's EMAE stumble "is not just an isolated figure: it reflects the underlying difficulties the economy faces in sustaining growth. Without changes that improve competitiveness and the functioning of productive sectors, the most probable scenario is that activity continues moving in a logic of advances and setbacks, still far from a firm expansive cycle, at least in the short term."

Economic activity in this first quarter is marked by a sharp divide between the sectors that grow most—energy, mining, agriculture, and fishing—and industry, construction, and commerce, which aren't recovering and are destroying jobs. This cocktail impacts consumption, amid inflation that has been rising in recent months, reaching 3.4% in March.

Among the positive news the Government can show is the recomposition of reserves thanks to Central Bank purchases, which so far this year have brought in almost $6.7 billion, while the dollar retreats 6%. The Government overflows with optimism, to the point that Minister Luis Caputo said the best eighteen months in decades are coming for the Argentine economy. More cautiously, the ACM consulting firm projects growth around 3.1%. It warns that "it will be key to monitor whether the recovery expands toward sectors linked to the domestic market: the stability of the exchange rate regime and the evolution of the real exchange rate will continue to be relevant for traded goods performance, while for the rest of the economy, the trajectory of real rates and credit availability will be determining factors in recomposing real incomes and demand."
