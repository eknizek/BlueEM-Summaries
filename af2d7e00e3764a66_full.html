# From Doctor to Entrepreneur: Sami Founder Sought Innovation as a Remedy

The healthcare sector stands out as one of the main bets of the startup ecosystem to reveal the next Brazilian unicorn. Among the companies most watched by the market is Sami Saúde, a healthtech that seeks to expand and democratize access to healthcare in the country — and which was born from the concerns of a doctor facing the limitations of the system itself.

This is the story of Vitor Asseituno, president of Sami, who identified an important gap in patient care while still in medical school: the lack of a broader vision of the healthcare journey. "Medicine today is much more than just the body. Many people cannot buy medications because they are too expensive, or they don't have surgery because their plan doesn't cover it. Practicing medicine is much more than thinking: patient, disease, medication. But everything else, doctors often ignore. In Medical School, I asked questions about this," he says.

This concern was the starting point for his entrepreneurial journey, which began during university with the creation of a junior company and culminated in the founding of Sami in 2018. The startup's proposal is precisely to attack these structural failures by offering a more integrated, accessible, and patient-centered model.

Vitor was this week's guest on the MVP podcast with Emerging Giants, a KPMG initiative in partnership with Startups. During the conversation, he shared lessons from his journey and the challenges of innovating in a highly sensitive sector.

In his view, healthcare is one of the most complex environments to implement changes. "If you make a mistake, the patient can die. If a doctor offers you innovative surgery, that's not always well received," he says, highlighting the low margin for error as a natural barrier to innovation.

Despite this, the potential is enormous — and goes beyond Brazil. According to the entrepreneur, there is room for global models, especially given a structural problem that has not yet been solved. "There is data from the World Health Organization saying that half the world's people don't have access to basic healthcare services when they need it. When we talk about technology, Sami's model can be global, exportable. There are regulatory differences, but the players are very similar," he says.

With an aging population and rising costs, the trend is that consumers will seek more complete solutions oriented toward experience. "People want to live longer, but that costs more. They will seek companies that solve the problem comprehensively," he says.

For Vitor, Sami has already validated its fundamentals and is now entering a new phase: scaling. "We already have the fundamentals in place, now it's about scaling. The point is to take what worked in the laboratory to millions of people in different markets. That's the challenge right now."

The podcast episodes air biweekly on Startups and KPMG networks, bringing interviews with entrepreneurs who are shaping the future of innovation in Brazil.
