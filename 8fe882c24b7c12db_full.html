How R$ 32.7 Billion "Disappears" from Brazilian Roads Every Year

# How R$ 32.7 Billion "Disappears" from Brazilian Roads Every Year

Almost all products that arrive at supermarkets, pharmacies, construction supply stores, factories, and Brazilian dinner tables have previously passed through a truck. Road freight transport is the country's main logistics mode, responsible for more than 60% of national production flow, in a market that moves R$ 818.6 billion per year. The problem is that almost half of that money circulates outside the law. And the cost of this – to the State, to companies in the sector, and to truck drivers themselves – is much greater than imagined.

An unprecedented study commissioned by Ampef (Association of Electronic Freight Payment Administrators) and conducted by GO Associados revealed that informality represents 43% of the road freight transport market in the country, generating an estimated tax collection loss of R$ 32.7 billion per year, including uncollected pension contributions (R$ 11.5 billion), state taxes (R$ 10.2 billion), federal taxes (R$ 7.6 billion), and municipal taxes (R$ 3.4 billion).

To understand what this loss represents, a simple exercise suffices: recovering the entire federal road network – approximately 50,000 kilometers of roads – would require an investment of R$ 11.5 billion per year.

"The market knew about the problem; it's visible, everyone knows it," says Raphael Rodrigues, executive president of Ampef. "But we didn't have the data showing the gravity of the situation. That's why we sought it out."

The carta-frete (freight voucher) is prohibited by law. Yet it persists for decades as the main payment mechanism for medium and long-distance freights. The mechanism works like this: the shipper/transporter, the cargo owner, issues to the autonomous truck driver a voucher without real legal value, linked to gas stations previously chosen by the shipper themselves.

"The truck driver is forced to exchange this at the gas stations stipulated by the shipper," says Rodrigues. "When he arrives at the pump, he pays a markup on the voucher value, generally around 10%."

According to data from the National Energy Balance cited in the study, freight transport consumes 34.6 billion liters of diesel per year. A significant portion of this volume is obligatorily directed to specific networks with inflated prices, also distorting competition among gas stations.

But the losses go beyond fuel. Without formal registration, there is no pension contribution collection. The truck driver ends his trip without social protection, without a way to prove income, and without access to credit.

The study estimated that, adding below-minimum payment to the markup charged at gas stations, the truck driver operating informally receives 34% less than what he would earn in the formal market. Those operating legally therefore pay twice: they collect taxes that competitors ignore and still need to compete with cheaper freight – not because the rival is more efficient, but because he simply doesn't follow the rules.

"When the competitor has a gigantic advantage by paying much less, he ends up attracting others to informality," says Rodrigues.

If the numbers are so striking, why does the carta-frete survive? Rodrigues points to a structural problem that feeds the cycle: the payment deadlines imposed by large shippers on transport companies.

"We're talking about deadlines that can extend up to 180 days," says the executive. "This creates a cash flow need that the transport company can't meet any other way."

In other words: without cash, the transport company resorts to informal payments, like the carta-frete.

Some recent initiatives are beginning to change this scenario. Provisional Measure 1343, issued in 2026, represented an important advance by reformulating the CIOT (Transport Operation Identification Code), a document that registers all data from each operation involving autonomous transporters, and toughening penalties for those who fail to respect the minimum freight rate, with fines between R$ 1 million and R$ 10 million.

For Ampef, the most important step is missing. The CIOT registers the operation but doesn't confirm whether payment was made, nor whether it was for the correct amount. This link between document and money is what the association wants to make mandatory.

What the association defends is that IPEFs (Electronic Freight Payment Institutions), regulated by the Central Bank and approved by ANTT, become the mandatory link between the transport document and actual payment.

"The only way for a freight payment to be 100% legal and respecting the minimum rate is to have traceability of the entire process," says Rodrigues.

Ampef has already sent official letters to ANTT and is working with legislators on an amendment that would turn this model into legislation, with a defined implementation deadline.

In this context, tax reform emerges as an opportunity to address the high informality in road freight transport. In the new model, when an operation is not formalized, the next link in the chain loses the right to tax credit, creating an economic disincentive for irregularity.

Still, this mechanism alone is not sufficient to solve the serious problem in the sector. Rodrigues emphasizes that tax reform tends to significantly reduce informality, provided there is effective integration between electronic transport documents and payment methods. For this, it is essential to ensure clear and verifiable recording of freight payments, linking the use of tax credits to complete proof of operations, with consistent documentation verified against amounts paid.

It is also necessary to ensure integration between these systems and promote coordinated action between tax authorities and regulatory bodies, with alignment of records, subcontracting rules, and control mechanisms.

In this model, taxes are collected automatically at the moment of freight payment, reducing dependence on later declarations. With financial flows properly recorded, space for tax evasion diminishes, allowing up to R$ 32.7 billion per year currently lost to return to public coffers.
