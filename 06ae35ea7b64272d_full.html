# Agrishow: 'Interest rates border on extortion and producers are losing land to banks', says secretary

RIBEIRÃO PRETO AND SÃO PAULO - São Paulo's Secretary of Agriculture and Food Supply, Geraldo Melo Filho, stated on Monday, April 27, that Brazilian rural producers face interest rates that "border on extortion" and that the country is moving toward a "land reform" imposed by debt, with producers losing land to banks and creditors. In a speech at the 31st edition of Agrishow, Melo Filho criticized the federal government's agricultural policy.

"The sector operates with minimal, if not negative, margins, and poor results are driven by interest rates that border on extortion of our producers. (...) Our countryside is heading toward a true land reform, except it will be imposed by indebtedness, with indebted producers losing land to banks and creditors," said the secretary.

Melo Filho attacked the credit announcement made by the federal government the day before, during the fair's official opening. "Yesterday (Sunday, 26), the government announced a kind of phantom credit here, it was announced as if it existed, almost nobody believes it exists and even those who do believe can't see or touch this money. Another disappointment for producers with another promise with no defined interest rates, no timeline to start, with no direction focused on the pain of those on the ground who need to produce," he said.

The secretary stated that the Harvest Plan "has lost its relevance and producers have been left to their own devices." According to him, rural insurance resources, already insufficient, "disappear from the budget" in the first budget cut, and credit, when it comes, charges more than 20% interest rates.

"We are living through a very particular moment in Brazilian agriculture. On one hand, we have record production, abundant harvests, large export volumes, job creation and the certainty that this sector continues to sustain this country's economy. But, unlike a few years ago when we had national leadership that actually cared about our sector and our people, what we have now is a reality full of empty promises and in practice this lack of action makes a large part of activities unfeasible with interest rates that are unpayable for those who need to seek credit to produce and sustain their families," said Melo Filho.

The secretary said the countryside continues producing "because what sustains Brazilian agriculture is the competence of rural producers, even with the system weighing on them." "The more it tightens, the producer goes out there, plants again and breaks production records, but that has its limits," he said.

Melo Filho highlighted records of default and judicial recovery in rural areas. "In addition to harvest records, we now have new records, which are records of default and judicial recovery in the countryside," he said.

According to the secretary, the federal government "doesn't want to reform the countryside, it wants to confiscate the effort of those who produce." "They are trading ideological land reform for the financial expulsion of the producer from their own land. The producer has come to live with a State that in practice became his hidden partner, except it's a partner that doesn't share the risk but demands results, that appears in success and disappears in crisis," he said.
