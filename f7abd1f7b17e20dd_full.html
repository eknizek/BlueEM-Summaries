# X, Alphabet's Moonshot Factory

X, or Moonshot Factory, has a single objective: to create financially viable solutions to problems that seem straight out of the best science fiction movies and books. X has no relation to Elon Musk's social network of the same name and belongs to Alphabet, also owner of Google. The company operates as a cutting-edge technology laboratory for Alphabet, a giant valued at over $3.5 trillion. The division was created 16 years ago to "develop radical innovation efficiently" – the so-called moonshot, in free translation.

The most well-known project to emerge from there recently is Waymo, which operates autonomous vehicles on the streets of California and Texas. But it is also from X that projects like Wing (for delivery drones) and Google Brain emerged (responsible for the study that revolutionized AI in the past decade and is the basis of rival OpenAI's ChatGPT).

Every year, about 200 projects are evaluated, but only two are selected to move forward. "A 'moonshot' has three basic components: an enormous problem in the world to solve, a product or service that seems like science fiction, and lastly, a way for us to reduce its risk," says scientist Astro Teller, the "captain" of the Moonshot Factory.

Teller is one of the main speakers at Brazil at Silicon Valley, a technology and innovation event organized by Brazilian students from American universities Stanford and Berkeley in the United States. The event takes place between April 6-8 in Sunnyvale, in the heart of Silicon Valley, and brings together the main names in Brazilian technology entrepreneurship.

Teller specialized in cutting-edge innovation in Silicon Valley. He has a degree in computer science from Stanford University, where he is a master's in symbolic computation. His doctorate is in artificial intelligence from Carnegie Mellon University. He has been at Alphabet since 2010, when he began leading X.

Despite hitting the moon with Waymo and Google Brain, Teller has a defeat on his resume. In 2013, Google Glass, augmented reality glasses, became one of the biggest failures in the world of technology, being discontinued by the company shortly after. Today, smart glasses are resurging with more successful initiatives from other companies, like Meta and Apple, which suggests the project may have been launched "too early," the CEO assesses, more than a decade later.

Similarly, X worked on nuclear fusion projects using alternative concepts created by physics in the 1980s. The company's team bet that, if the bet paid off, the benefit would generate simpler and cheaper engineering in the sector. Four and a half years and over a thousand experiments later, the project was terminated for not delivering expected results.

The lesson, says Teller, is not to insist when there are no different results. "If we are exploring an innovative idea and we convince ourselves that it is unlikely to become a profitable business, we end it. No matter how good the science behind it is," he states categorically. "I don't believe a company has to choose between purpose and profit. There is relevant overlap between both."

Currently, X has dedicated itself to clever contemporary issues. In the energy area, the Tapestry project seeks to make the electrical grid more efficient by treating it as an "electron market," with real-time information management. In biotechnology, A-Life bets on creating "virtual cells" to accelerate the production of food, medicine, and materials. Matera targets the circular economy, using AI to identify and reuse waste with greater added value. Anori drastically reduces the time of urban planning and zoning processes.

Teller states that Brazil has become an ally in the company's projects. Rio de Janeiro became the company's main operational base in the country, in a partnership with the city government to transform the city into a pole of advanced technologies. The strategy involves testing solutions on a real scale that can later be replicated in other markets. Among ongoing projects are Matera, in partnership with Comlurb, and Tapestry, in collaboration with Light and Axia. "We are using Rio as a means to see how we can help the world," or at least the country as a whole, since some of these companies have national reach.

Ironically, in the invention factory of one of the world's leading technology companies, artificial intelligence is a thing of the past. Although X participated in the birth of contemporary AI, today the company's focus is on using the technology to solve concrete problems, like energy and recycling – not creating an artificial superintelligence with superhuman capabilities, as with AGI, general artificial intelligence, the subject of the recent race between OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google itself.

"When people get into this 'wow, wow, AI,' either they're not very good at explaining the benefits they're trying to offer – or worse, they have no benefit at all and use AI as a smoke screen," says Teller. "Computers are levers for our minds and are already infinitely more intelligent than us, but in a different way."
