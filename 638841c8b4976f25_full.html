Average worker salary contracts in Latin America while CEO pay soars: Oxfam

# Average worker salary contracts in Latin America while CEO pay soars: Oxfam

The average remuneration of chief executive officers worldwide increased 20 times faster than that of workers in 2025, while gaps continue to widen in Latin America, according to a report by the NGO Oxfam.

The Oxfam report, published on International Workers' Day on May 1st, states that chief executive officers of the world's largest companies received an 11% salary increase in real terms last year. The average CEO pocketed $8.4 million in salary and bonuses last year, compared to the $7.6 million earned in 2024. An average worker would take 490 years to earn the same amount, according to Oxfam calculations.

In contrast, the real salary of the average worker increased only 0.5%, the report says.

Although this trend is global, "its effects are especially harsh in Latin America and the Caribbean," where real wages fell 5.1% in just the last year.

"In Latin America and the Caribbean we are seeing wealth concentrate at a speed that has nothing to do with economic progress or the reality of working people," said Gloria García-Parra, regional director of Oxfam in Latin America and the Caribbean. "While a minority accumulates fortunes equivalent to entire countries, millions of people see their wages and living conditions deteriorate."

According to García-Parra, "this disconnect between wealth and well-being is a direct threat to people's rights and democracy in the region."

According to data compiled by Oxfam, companies such as Blackstone, Broadcom, and Goldman Sachs reported paying their CEO more than $100 million in 2025. The report also notes that the ten best-paid CEOs earned collectively over $1 billion.

## 108 Days Without Pay

Oxfam states that the growing gap between CEO remuneration and average worker wages is part of a long-term trend in which executives and shareholders are capturing an increasingly larger share of the global economic "pie."

It is estimated that real wages for workers have fallen 12% since 2019. In practice, according to Oxfam's calculation, these workers have worked 108 days without pay between 2019 and 2025 (31 days free in 2025 alone).

On the other hand, CEO remuneration has skyrocketed from an average of $5.5 million in 2019 to $8.4 million in 2025, a 54% increase in real terms.

In Latin America and the Caribbean, real wages for workers contracted 35.1%, translating to more than three months of unpaid work in 2025 in real terms. In countries like Chile, the cumulative loss reaches 18.5%, equivalent to 47.4 days of unpaid work. In Uruguay it reaches 13.4%, equivalent to more than a full month of unpaid work.

The report was prepared based on analysis of data from 1,500 companies in 33 countries with the highest salaries that have available information on their CEOs' remuneration in 2025. The gender wage gap among employees of these 1,500 companies averages 16%. This means that these women workers, in practice, work for free starting November 4th each year.

## Stock Payments

The report also notes that compensation for executives through stock participation is becoming increasingly prominent. Worldwide, approximately 1,000 billionaires whose investment portfolios were identified collectively received $79 billion in dividends in 2025. This is equivalent to about $2,500 per second. The average billionaire earned more in dividends in less than two hours than the average worker earned in salary in an entire year.

According to Oxfam, some of the largest global payments in 2025 were allocated to Bernard Arnault, owner of luxury brand LVMH. Bernard Arnault pocketed approximately $3.8 billion. Meanwhile, Amancio Ortega, owner of Inditex (Zara), received approximately $3.7 billion.

Billionaires' wealth has reached historic highs in 2026, according to Oxfam. In just 12 months, they gained $4 trillion, bringing their wealth to $1.5 trillion more than that of the 4.1 billion poorest people. Additionally, it is estimated that there are 400 more billionaires compared to last year. Notably, 45 of these new billionaires have amassed their fortunes in artificial intelligence.
