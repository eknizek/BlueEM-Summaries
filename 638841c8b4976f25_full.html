# Average Worker Wages Shrink in Latin America, but CEO Pay Keeps Rising: Oxfam

The average remuneration of chief executives worldwide increased 20 times faster than that of workers in 2025, while gaps continue to widen in Latin America, according to a report by the NGO Oxfam.

The Oxfam report, published on International Workers' Day on May 1st, states that chief executives of the world's largest companies received a salary increase of 11% in real terms last year. The average chief executive pocketed US$8.4 million in salary and bonuses last year, compared to the US$7.6 million earned in 2024. An average worker would take 490 years to earn the same amount, Oxfam calculates.

In contrast, the real salary of an average worker only increased by 0.5%, the report says.

Although this trend is global, "its effects are especially harsh in Latin America and the Caribbean," where real wages fell by 5.1% in just the last year.

"In Latin America and the Caribbean we are seeing how wealth concentrates at a speed that has nothing to do with economic progress or the reality of working people," said Gloria García-Parra, regional director of Oxfam in Latin America and the Caribbean, in a statement. "While a minority accumulates fortunes equivalent to entire countries, millions of people see their wages and living conditions deteriorate."

According to García-Parra, "this disconnect between wealth and well-being is a direct threat to people's rights and democracy in the region."

According to data compiled by Oxfam, companies such as Blackstone, Broadcom, and Goldman Sachs have reported paying their chief executive more than US$100 million in 2025. It also explains that the ten highest-paid executives earned collectively more than US$1 billion.

## 108 Days Without Pay

Oxfam says that the growing gap between CEO remuneration and average worker pay is part of a long-term trend in which executives and shareholders are taking an increasingly larger share of the global economic "pie."

Real wages for workers are estimated to have fallen 12% since 2019. In practice, according to Oxfam's calculation, these people have worked 108 days without pay between 2019 and 2025 (31 days free just last year).

On the other hand, CEO remuneration has skyrocketed from an average of US$5.5 million in 2019 to US$8.4 million in 2025, an increase of 54% in real terms.

In Latin America and the Caribbean, real wages for workers contracted by 35.1%, which translates to more than three months of unpaid work in 2025 in real terms. In countries like Chile, the accumulated loss reaches 18.5%, equivalent to 47.4 days of unpaid work. And in Uruguay it reaches 13.4%, equivalent to more than a full month of unpaid work.

The report was prepared based on analysis of data from 1,500 companies across 33 countries with the highest salaries, which have available information on CEO remuneration in 2025. The gender wage gap among employees at these 1,500 companies is, on average, 16%. This means that these working women, in practice, work for free starting November 4th of each year.

## Stock Payments

The report also notes that remuneration for executives through stock holdings is becoming increasingly prominent. Globally, around 1,000 billionaires whose investment portfolios were identified collectively received US$79 billion in dividends in 2025. This equates to about US$2,500 per second. The average billionaire earned more in dividends in less than two hours than what the average worker earned in salary in an entire year.

According to Oxfam, some of the largest global payments in 2025 were allocated to Bernard Arnault, owner of luxury brand LVMH. Bernard Arnault pocketed about US$3.8 billion. While Amancio Ortega, owner of Inditex (Zara), received about US$3.7 billion.

The wealth of billionaires reached historic highs in 2026, according to Oxfam. In just 12 months, they gained US$4 trillion, raising their wealth to US$1.5 trillion more than that of the 4.1 billion poorest people. Additionally, it is estimated that there are 400 more billionaires compared to last year.

As a particular note, it explains that 45 of these new billionaires have amassed their fortunes in artificial intelligence.
