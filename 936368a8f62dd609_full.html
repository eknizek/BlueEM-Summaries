# Now with Patria, Solis Prepares for a New Leap in FIDCs

Solis Investimentos is restructuring its operations to double in size by 2028. With approximately R$ 30 billion under management, the firm specializing in Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) has reorganized its structure, created five credit verticals, and redesigned processes and leadership to achieve this goal. According to the firm, this movement was designed before the arrival of Patria Investimentos, a partnership established in November 2025, which acquired 51% of Solis in a transaction that provides for the integration of 100% of the operation within three years. But this association could accelerate the trajectory.

"With Patria, we have a new front for originating quality assets, giving us greater access to the universe of its portfolio companies, and we also expand the spectrum of investors, particularly international ones. This could accelerate us reaching our goal," says Ricardo Binelli, also co-managing director of Solis.

The reorganization translates into dividing Solis into five fronts: public payroll loans, private payroll loans, multi-cedent and multi-debtor, real estate, and structured assets in general, a category that brings together corporate debt operations and other more specific assets. The segmentation was a direct response to the increasing weight of each type of collateral within the platform, better separating the areas. Instead of a single analyst alternating between assets with quite different dynamics, Solis decided to build dedicated structures by vertical, with specific professionals for origination, structuring, credit, monitoring, and risk management.

In the firm's view, the new structure deepens knowledge about each segment and improves analytical capacity precisely when the market demands more sophistication. The FIDC industry has gained visibility in recent years, both among investors and borrowers, and has more than doubled in size since 2021. Today it has over R$ 700 billion under management. And in Solis's calculations, it could reach R$ 1 trillion by 2028. For this reason, FIDCs have increasingly been viewed as an alternative to traditional bank credit. This movement expanded interest in the product and brought more players to the sector. For Solis, this means that future growth will depend less on being a generalist and more on deepening understanding of each niche.

"Competition tends to narrow spreads and bring unusual operations to the market. I think our specialization is what will allow us to maintain the level of our deliverables even in this environment," says Ricardo Binelli, also co-managing director of Solis.

More than an organizational chart adjustment, the change is treated internally as the basis for sustaining a leap in scale without losing the capacity for origination, analysis, and monitoring. "We know that the model that enabled us to grow to this point is not what's needed to grow at the scale we want," says Delano Macêdo, co-managing director of Solis.

This combination of internal reorganization and new corporate structure also requires team reinforcement. Macêdo says Solis has hired at least eight people since November and has nine other open positions, including senior positions, directorates, management roles, and operational functions. The consulting firms Futuro Consultoria and Mataxis Advisory, which helped redesign the organizational structure, continue working with the firm through June to support implementation of the changes.

## The Market Grew

At the same time that Solis gained momentum with Patria as a partner, the credit environment is under pressure, with high interest rates, families more indebted, small and medium-sized enterprises facing greater financial difficulty, and signs of deterioration appearing in different portfolios. For Solis, this means that the ambition to double in size by 2028 must coexist with a kind of silent crisis in the credit market, which forces the firm to closely monitor asset quality and recalibrate risk appetite much more frequently.

According to Macêdo, the firm's reaction is not to indiscriminately exit sectors or simply shut off the tap. The movement has been more surgical. The firm maintains a constant schedule of committees and reviews to identify where deterioration is concentrating — by segment, region, borrower profile, or portfolio type — and from there, adjust terms, exposure, concentration, and guarantee packages. In some cases, this means slowing the pace of origination. In others, raising the level of protection required to continue doing business.

This filter has already produced clearer choices within Solis. Credit for individuals without guarantees, for example, has been off the firm's radar for at least two or three years, except in operations like payroll loans or structures with some additional protection. In the small and medium-sized enterprise segment, the logic is similar: the firm avoids clean operations and prefers transactions anchored in receivables, invoices, real estate, or financial applications. The understanding is that in a higher-stress environment, growth does not come from indiscriminately assuming more risk, but from better selecting operations and reinforcing protective measures.

Even in a more selective credit market, Solis maintains that there is still much room to advance. And it understands that in a still-fragmented sector, expanding presence would already be sufficient to consolidate its position. "There are currently about 300 FIDC managers, and we are one of the largest with about 6% of the market. Growing with and above this market in the coming years will be fundamental to maintaining our relevance," says Macêdo.
