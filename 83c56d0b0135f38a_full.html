# Cetrel capitalizes on Ambipar crisis, positioning as Brazil's second-largest environmental services firm

Since its founding 48 years ago, Cetrel has become Brazil's second-largest environmental services company, providing services ranging from water supply to industrial waste management and emergency response. Now, the company controlled by Grupo Solví (which operates some of the country's largest landfills) is preparing for a new growth cycle — largely turbocharged by the crisis of Ambipar, its main competitor.

Cetrel was originally created to serve companies in the Camaçari petrochemical hub with waste management. In addition to being clients, these companies were business shareholders. In 2017, the business was sold to Braskem, which transferred it to Grupo Solví two years ago for R$ 284 million. Today, it is already the second player in the three markets in which it operates, generating approximately R$ 1 billion in revenue last year and serving more than one thousand clients. Ambipar — the sector leader — generates around R$ 2 billion in the same verticals. The question is how long this revenue will be sustained.

Since Ambipar entered judicial recovery, some of its clients have been seeking alternatives for when contracts expire. A natural beneficiary of this movement is Cetrel — since below it, the market is extremely fragmented and regional.

"Governance and safety issues are very relevant in our sector, and situations like this rarely pass through the compliance of large companies," Cetrel CEO Ciro Gouveia told Brazil Journal. "Our sector is also very capital-intensive in working capital, so a major crisis at a company naturally leaves clients concerned."

According to him, Cetrel's growth over the coming years is not based on this — although the crisis of its largest competitor is certainly an important push. Most of Cetrel's revenue today comes from water and wastewater and circular economy verticals (waste management), each accounting for 40% of the top line. The remaining 20% comes from emergency response, which despite being the smallest, is also the fastest growing.

Part of this growth has come from M&A. In February, Cetrel acquired CTA, a company focused on training and simulations, and intends to make other acquisitions. The CEO said Cetrel has several ongoing conversations and is analyzing M&As in the three verticals it operates. In water and wastewater, for example, the company has very high concentration in Bahia, and one of the plans is to grow in other markets. In emergency response, there are few potential targets, the CEO says.

"Few companies remain, and very small ones, because Ambipar ended up buying everyone," he said. "But if we find specific opportunities to accelerate our growth, we will look at them."

Cetrel has R$ 120 million in cash for acquisitions and operates with a "very healthy" capital structure, according to Gouveia, with leverage of only 0.7x EBITDA. M&As should help Cetrel fulfill an ambitious expansion plan. The company wants to more than double its revenue over the next five years, reaching R$ 2 billion, an average growth of 20% to 25% per year.

The company benefits from being part of Grupo Solví, which is controlled by businessman Carlos Villa, who owns 62% of the capital. Australian Macquarie Group, one of the world's largest asset managers, entered the cap table in 2019 when it purchased 38% of the shares. In addition to controlling Cetrel, Solví also operates in the landfill market, where it competes with Orizon, and in biomethane production. The group as a whole generates close to R$ 4 billion in revenue.
