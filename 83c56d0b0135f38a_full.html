# Amid Ambipar Crisis, Cetrel Emerges as Clear Winner in Brazilian Environmental Services

Since its founding 48 years ago, Cetrel has become Brazil's second-largest environmental services company, providing services ranging from water supply to industrial waste management and emergency response. Now, the company controlled by Grupo Solví (which operates some of the country's largest landfills) is preparing for a new growth cycle — largely turbocharged by the crisis of Ambipar, its main competitor.

Cetrel was originally born to serve companies in the Camaçari petrochemical hub in managing their waste. In addition to being clients, the companies were also shareholders of the business. In 2017, the business was sold to Braskem, which transferred it to the Solví group two years ago for R$ 284 million. Today, it is already the second player in the three markets in which it operates, generating around R$ 1 billion in revenue last year and serving more than a thousand clients. Ambipar — the segment leader — generates around R$ 2 billion in the same verticals. The question is how long this revenue will be sustained.

Since Ambipar entered judicial recovery, some of its clients have been seeking alternatives for when their contracts end. And a natural beneficiary of this movement is Cetrel — since, below it, the market is extremely fragmented and regional.

"Governance and safety issues are very relevant in our sector, and situations like this rarely pass through the compliance of large companies," Cetrel CEO Ciro Gouveia told Brazil Journal. "Our sector is also very intensive in working capital, so a major crisis in the company naturally leaves clients worried."

According to him, Cetrel's growth for the coming years is not based on this — although the crisis of its largest competitor is certainly an important push. Most of Cetrel's revenue today comes from the water and effluent verticals and circular economy (waste management), each accounting for 40% of the top line. The remaining 20% comes from emergency response, which despite being the smallest is also the fastest growing.

Part of this growth has come from M&As. In February, Cetrel acquired CTA, a company focused on training and simulations, and plans to make other acquisitions. The CEO said Cetrel has several conversations underway and is analyzing M&As in the three verticals it operates. In water and effluent, for example, the company has a very large concentration in Bahia, and one of the plans is to grow in other markets. In emergency response, there are few potential targets, the CEO says.

"Very few companies remain, and very small ones, because Ambipar ended up buying everyone," he said. "But if we find specific opportunities to accelerate our growth, we will look."

Cetrel has R$ 120 million in cash for acquisitions and operates with a "very healthy" capital structure, Gouveia said, with leverage of only 0.7x EBITDA. M&As should help Cetrel fulfill an ambitious expansion plan. The company wants to more than double its revenue in the next five years, reaching R$ 2 billion, an average growth of 20% to 25% per year.

The company benefits from being part of Grupo Solví, which is controlled by businessman Carlos Villa, who owns 62% of the capital. Australian Macquarie Group, one of the world's largest asset managers, entered the cap table in 2019 when it bought 38% of the shares. In addition to controlling Cetrel, Solví also operates in the landfill market, where it competes with Orizon, and in biomethane production. The group as a whole generates close to R$ 4 billion in revenue.
