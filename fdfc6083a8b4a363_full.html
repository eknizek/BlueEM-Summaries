# Economic Shock of Middle East War Expected to Overshadow IMF and World Bank Meetings

High-level financial authorities from around the world will meet in Washington this week under the shadow of the Middle East war, which has caused the third major shock to the global economy following the Covid-19 pandemic and Russia's large-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022.

Representatives of the IMF (International Monetary Fund) and the World Bank said last week that they would lower their global growth forecasts and raise their inflation projections as a result of the war, warning that emerging markets and developing countries will be hardest hit by higher energy prices and supply disruptions.

Before the war with Iran erupted on February 28, both institutions expected to raise their growth forecasts given the resilience of the global economy—even after the major tariffs imposed by U.S. President Donald Trump starting last year.

But the war has triggered a series of shocks that will slow progress in recovering growth and fighting inflation.

The World Bank's base estimate now projects growth in emerging markets and developing economies at 3.65% in 2026, down from 4% in October, but predicts that figure could fall to 2.6% if the war lasts longer. Inflation in these countries is now forecast to reach 4.9% in 2026, up from the previous estimate of 3%, and could spike to as high as 6.7% in the worst-case scenario.

The IMF warned last week that about 45 million people could also face acute food insecurity if the war persists and continues to disrupt shipments of fertilizers needed now.

The IMF and World Bank are racing to respond to the latest crisis and support vulnerable countries at a time when public debt levels have reached records and budgets are tight. The IMF said it expects demand for $20 billion to $50 billion in short-term emergency support for low-income countries and energy importers.

The World Bank said it could mobilize about $25 billion through crisis response instruments in the short term, and up to $70 billion in six months, as needed.

But economists are urging governments to use only targeted and temporary measures to relieve the impact of higher prices for their citizens, as broader measures could fuel inflation.

"Leadership matters, and we have overcome crises in the past," said World Bank President Ajay Banga, praising work on fiscal and monetary controls that helped economies weather previous storms. "But this is a shock to the system."

Countries now face a difficult balance between managing inflation while keeping an eye on growth and the long-term challenge of creating enough jobs for the 1.2 billion people who will reach working age in developing countries by 2035.

The IMF and World Bank also face a very different global scenario, with heightened tensions between the United States and China, the world's largest economies, and the G20, a group of the 20 largest economies hampered in its ability to coordinate a response. The United States currently holds the rotating presidency of the G20, which also includes Russia and China, but has excluded another member—South Africa—from participation, complicating the group's ability to coordinate on this crisis.

"You're trying to operate by consensus when there is no consensus in the world now on anything," said Josh Lipsky, president of international economics at the Atlantic Council.

Lipsky said that statements from the IMF, World Bank and other multilateral lenders about their readiness to support countries hard hit by the war were clearly intended to reassure markets.

"It's a signal to private creditors. This is not the time to flee from countries that are in troubled waters. They will have support from multilateral development banks and international financial institutions. This won't be like Covid. This is something we can manage."

Mary Svenstrup, a former senior U.S. Treasury official now at the Center for Global Development, said many emerging markets and developing economies entered the crisis in worse shape than a few years ago, with smaller reserves, greater debt vulnerabilities and lower reserves.

"We need this crisis to be a catalyst for IMF stakeholders to really rethink how the Fund supports vulnerable countries, with the recognition that we will see more global shocks," she said. "We cannot ask them to sacrifice growth and development for the sake of rebuilding reserves."

Svenstrup said countries should pursue more ambitious reforms if they receive new resources. "There probably needs to be more support from international financial institutions, but it needs to be accessible and in the context of reform programs and, potentially, broader debt relief," she said.
