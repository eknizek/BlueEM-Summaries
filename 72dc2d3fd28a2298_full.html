# Raízen Signals Capital Injection in Debt Restructuring but Resists Creditor Demands

Raízen, a joint venture between Cosan and Shell, sent an alternative proposal to creditors while trying to finalize the terms of a R$ 65 billion (US$ 13 billion) debt restructuring, according to people familiar with the matter.

In the proposal presented Saturday night, Raízen informed creditors that it is negotiating to raise between R$ 2.5 billion and R$ 5 billion in new capital, these people said. While this new detail should please debt holders, who had proposed that current shareholders inject R$ 8 billion, the company rejected other changes requested by creditors, including relinquishing control of the board.

The capital included in Raízen's new proposal would come on top of the R$ 4 billion in financing that Shell and billionaire Rubens Ometto have already committed to injecting into the bioenergy company, said the sources, who asked not to be identified. It was unclear where the new money would come from. Cosan, the conglomerate founded by Ometto that shares control of Raízen with Shell, is not injecting resources into the struggling company.

Raízen is resisting creditors' demands that shareholders relinquish the majority of board seats or that executives be held accountable for possible liabilities that may materialize in the future, the people said. The company did, however, agree to a request to create a creditor committee to more closely monitor governance, according to one of the sources.

Ometto still wants to remain as president of Raízen's board, although the company is aware this will be a point of tension with debt holders, the people said. Bank creditors and bond holders separately requested in their proposals that Ometto be removed, Bloomberg previously reported.

Raízen, Cosan and Ometto refused to comment. Shell did not respond to a request for comment by publication time.

The company reiterated its proposal that creditors receive a 70% stake in a possible debt-to-equity conversion, the sources said. The company's new offer does not include the suggestion from bank creditors that 30% of the proceeds from the sale of assets in Argentina be used to reduce debt, according to one source.

Raízen has been negotiating with creditors to reach an agreement and avoid the need to resort to judicial recovery, after filing for extrajudicial restructuring in March. The parties face a legal deadline of June 6 to reach an out-of-court agreement with sufficient support from bond holders and bank creditors.

Previously Brazil's biofuel production leader, Raízen was affected by high interest rates, heavy investments that have not yet generated returns, and operational challenges in its sugar and ethanol divisions, leading to a series of below-expected results. These problems eroded its cash flow and caused its debt to surge. As negotiations between shareholders for a rescue dragged on, bonds fell to levels considered high-risk. When it hired advisors to optimize its capital structure, rating agencies downgraded the company from investment grade to deep "junk" levels, further accelerating the fall in its securities.
