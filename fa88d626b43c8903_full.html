# Aegea Restates 2024 Balance Sheet with R$5 Billion Writedown After Credit Loss and Investment Review

Sanitation company Aegea disclosed a restatement of 2024 data over the weekend of April 10-11 that brought an accounting writedown of R$5 billion, alongside the company's financial results for the previous year. Pressured by the financial market after successive delays, the company sent the information 11 days after the end of the balance sheet season and without the usual fourth-quarter breakdown.

Aegea, whose shareholders include Equipav, GIC (Singapore's sovereign wealth fund), and Itaúsa, published the results at 11:59 p.m. on the CVM (Securities and Exchange Commission) website. The company posted net income of R$856 million in 2025, a 31% decline compared to the previous year. In the same period, net revenue rose 21% to R$18.3 billion, and investments (capex) jumped 35% to R$7.3 billion.

Net debt increased 37% to R$47 billion, while leverage rose to 4.51 times Ebitda (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization). The figures reflect the "pro forma" basis of the so-called Aegea ecosystem—showing the performance of the group of controlled and affiliated companies, including the Rio Grande do Sul-based Corsan and Águas do Rio, which operates water and sewage services in Rio de Janeiro.

Hours after disclosing the balance sheet, in the early morning hours, Aegea announced an adjustment to 2024 reports. In this restatement, the company's total equity took a hit of R$5 billion, falling from R$11.4 billion in the previously reported statement to R$6.3 billion in the updated version.

According to the document, the adjustment to equity—which is the company's book value, or everything it owns minus everything it owes—does not represent a cash loss but rather a "cleanup" in value. One reason was the revaluation of securities and investments it held in affiliated companies, such as Águas do Rio. In practice, the adjustment means Aegea concluded that part of the previously disclosed value exceeded what the assets were actually worth.

The adjustments, according to the company, also relate to: recognition of water and sewage revenue, construction revenue from PPPs (public-private partnerships), provisions for expected credit losses on accounts receivable, and capitalization of interest related to concessions. Aegea stated that balances from renegotiations with customers with installments overdue for more than 30 days were entirely written off.

Beyond equity, net income in the period also fell by R$593 million, as did accounts receivable (R$643 million) and other line items. In a statement, Aegea said the restatement of financial statements resulted from improvements in accounting criteria. "These revisions aim at greater alignment between reported results and the company's cash generation. There is no impact on cash, liquidity, analysis of new projects, or compliance with financial covenants," it said.

Additionally, Aegea said it remains focused on executing its investment plan and expanding sanitation services across the country. The delay in balance sheet disclosure and the prospect of revaluation in 2024 data had already prompted rating agencies to downgrade the company's credit rating. According to S&P, the scenario evidenced "fragility in the company's internal controls, potentially exposing it to additional risks." Fitch considered the delay evidence of deterioration in the quality and transparency of Aegea's financial information.

The restatement comes at a sensitive moment. The sanitation company plans to list on the stock exchange through an initial public offering (IPO) between the end of this year and 2027. Last month, Itaúsa—one of Aegea's shareholders—said it expected a market valuation greater than R$40 billion. Beyond the IPO, the company is among those interested in the privatization of Copasa, a Minas Gerais state-owned sanitation utility. The process is expected to involve approximately R$10 billion.
