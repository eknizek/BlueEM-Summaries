# Industry Fell 2.7% in First Quarter But Industrialists Already Speak of Recovery in March

Industrial activity completed the first quarter of the year with a negative balance, accumulating a decline of 2.7% compared to the same period last year, according to a report presented by the Research Center of the Argentine Industrial Union. However, March data sparked a glimmer of hope in the manufacturing sector.

According to UIA estimates, based on electricity consumption and consultations among different industries, the third month of the year registered year-on-year growth of 3.6% and a recovery of 5% compared to February.

Despite the optimism generated by March figures, the technical report warns that this growth responds, to a large extent, to a "low comparison base." Industry still treads through difficult terrain: both at the aggregate and sectoral levels, performance remains around 10% below the levels recorded during 2022 and 2023.

The start of 2025 (and its projection to 2026) shows uneven recovery. Although the March rebound is significant, the accumulated first two months already dragged negative momentum that conditioned the quarterly result.

## Sectoral Overview

The scenario within factories is one of profound heterogeneity. The crisis hit especially hard on 14 of the 16 sectors that make up the index.

Among the most affected sectors are textile product manufacturing, leading declines with a collapse of 33.2%, closely followed by machinery and equipment (29.4%) and automotive vehicles (24.6%), the latter affected by a considerable drop in exports. Mass consumption also suffered the impact, with declines in food and beverages (6.9%) and in the leather and footwear industry.

On the opposite side, petroleum refining stands out as one of the big winners, with a jump of 20%, bolstered by the dynamism of Vaca Muerta. The chemicals and chemical products sector (3.7%) also posted positive results, thanks to the performance of the pharmaceutical and cleaning industries, and sectors related to oleaginous grain milling following crop recovery.

Government officials have been highlighting that activity levels will begin an upward path. Economy Ministry officials, including Economy Minister Luis Caputo, emphasized this. They pointed out that "industry is undergoing a necessary process of adaptation to a framework of greater competition and openness."

The Government understands that the March reactivation is the first indication that "the ordering of macroeconomic variables is beginning to impact real production." However, they acknowledge that the decline in public works conditioned key sectors such as steel and construction inputs, but they trust that the export boost from energy and agriculture will help balance the scales in coming months.

The major challenge for the second quarter will be consolidating the March increase and preventing the deceleration observed in sectors like automotive from deepening, in a context where internal demand is still seeking its equilibrium point against competition from imported products.
