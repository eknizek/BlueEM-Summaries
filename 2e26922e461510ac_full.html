# Industrial output fell 2.7% in Q1 but manufacturers already discussing March recovery

Industrial activity completed the first quarter of the year with a negative balance, accumulating a decline of 2.7% compared to the same period last year, according to a report presented by the Research Center of the Argentine Industrial Union (UIA). However, March data sparked a glimmer of hope in the manufacturing sector.

According to UIA estimates based on electricity consumption and consultations among different industries, the third month of the year registered year-over-year growth of 3.6% and a recovery of 5% compared to February.

Despite the optimism generated by March figures, the technical report warns that this growth responds largely to a "low comparison base." Industry still treads through murky waters: both at the aggregate and sectoral levels, performance remains around 10% below levels recorded during 2022 and 2023.

The beginning of 2025 (and its projection to 2026) shows uneven recovery. While the March rebound is significant, the accumulated results from the first two months already carried negative momentum that conditioned the quarterly outcome.

## Sectoral breakdown

The scenario inside factories is one of profound heterogeneity. The crisis hit especially hard on 14 of the 16 sectors that make up the index.

Among the hardest-hit sectors are textile product manufacturing, which leads declines with a collapse of 33.2%, followed closely by machinery and equipment (29.4%) and motor vehicles (24.6%), the latter affected by a considerable drop in exports. Mass consumption also suffered impact, with declines in food and beverages (6.9%) and the leather and footwear industry.

On the other side, petroleum refining appears as one of the big winners, with a jump of 20%, bolstered by the dynamism of Vaca Muerta. Positive data were also registered in the chemical substances and products sector (3.7%), thanks to performance in the pharmaceutical and cleaning industries, and those sectors linked to oleaginous grain milling following harvest recovery.

From the Government's environment, they have been highlighting that activity levels will begin an upward trajectory. Economy officials emphasized this, particularly Economy Minister Luis Caputo. They pointed out that "industry is undergoing a necessary adaptation process to a scheme of greater competition and openness."

The Government understands that March's reactivation is the first indication that "the ordering of macroeconomic variables is beginning to impact real production." Nevertheless, they acknowledge that the decline in public works conditioned key sectors like steel and construction inputs, but trust that the export boost from energy and agriculture will help balance the scales in coming months.

The major challenge for the second quarter will be consolidating March's gains and preventing the deceleration observed in sectors like automobiles from deepening, in a context where domestic demand still seeks its equilibrium point against competition from imported products.
