# The Economy at Stake: These Are the Proposals of the Parties Heading into the Second Round

Amid electoral uncertainty, economic debate returns to center stage. That is why, from Videnza Instituto, we evaluate the main economic proposals of Fuerza Popular, Juntos por el Perú, and Renovación Popular.

## Fuerza Popular: Continuity with Caution

Fuerza Popular presents the most explicit approach to macroeconomic discipline. It proposes maintaining the autonomy of the Central Reserve Bank (BCR) and the continuity of its current leadership, as well as a return to fiscal rules and spending consolidation. However, these proposals conflict with the estimated annual increase of 13.5% in public spending—according to its own calculations derived from its government plan—and with the recent behavior of its congressional bloc.

Regarding economic growth and employment, the emphasis is on reducing administrative barriers, digitizing the State, and promoting private investment through public-private partnerships and works-for-taxes schemes. Additionally, it proposes a universal minimum pension and increased and indexed benefits from the National Pensions Office (ONP), which administers the National Pension System. However, it does not propose a comprehensive pension reform that addresses system sustainability, formality incentives, and coordination between regimes.

Regarding Petro-Perú, it proposes restructuring aimed at efficiency, including the sale of non-strategic assets and the separation of the Northern Pipeline. Although it supports private investment and major mining projects, there is concern about its congressional bloc's stance on initiatives favoring informal and illegal mining.

Overall, the balance shows mostly positive signals, with relevant alerts regarding pension sustainability and informal mining management.

## Juntos por el Perú: Breaking Equilibriums

Juntos por el Perú's proposals represent a significant break with the existing economic framework. In monetary policy, they propose modifying the BCR's leadership and using international reserves to finance social spending. On the fiscal front, they propose progressive tax reform, taxes on large fortunes, and raising tax pressure to 25% of GDP—ten points higher than current levels—alongside greater social spending and public investment, without presenting any credible fiscal transition plan.

Regarding economic activity, they promote a constituent assembly that would drive an entrepreneurial State with direct participation in sectors deemed strategic. They propose renegotiating natural resource concession contracts and direct intervention in mining and hydrocarbons, including the nationalization of Camisea gas and the preservation of Petro-Perú as a non-sellable state company.

Particularly concerning is their favorable stance toward informal and illegal mining, presented as part of the popular economy without clear environmental, labor, or tax requirements. This approach weakens institutions and favors the expansion of illicit economies.

Regarding employment and wages, they defend a more active role for the State without detailing productivity policies or sustainable formalization, and they prioritize wage increases disconnected from economic growth. On pensions, they bet on redistributive increases without clear design or sustainability evaluation.
