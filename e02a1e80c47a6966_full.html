# The Economy in Play: These Are the Economic Proposals of the Parties Heading Toward the Runoff

Amid electoral uncertainty, economic debate returns to center stage. For this reason, from Videnza Instituto we evaluate the main economic proposals of Fuerza Popular, Juntos por el Perú, and Renovación Popular.

## Fuerza Popular: Continuity with Caution

Fuerza Popular presents the most explicit approach to macroeconomic discipline. It proposes maintaining the autonomy of the Central Reserve Bank (BCR) and the continuity of its current leadership, as well as returning to fiscal rules and consolidating spending. However, these proposals come into tension with the estimated annual increase of 13.5% in public spending—according to its own government plan calculations—and with the recent behavior of its congressional caucus.

Regarding economic growth and employment, the emphasis is on reducing administrative barriers, digitizing the state, and promoting private investment through public-private partnerships and works-for-taxes initiatives. Additionally, it proposes a minimum universal pension and the increase and indexing of the National Pension System (ONP). However, it does not propose comprehensive pension reform that addresses system sustainability, incentives for formality, and coordination between regimes.

Regarding Petro-Perú, it proposes a restructuring oriented toward efficiency, including the sale of non-strategic assets and the separation of the Northern Pipeline. Although it supports private investment and major mining projects, the position of its caucus concerning initiatives that favor informal and illegal mining is concerning. Overall, the balance shows mostly positive signals, with relevant alerts regarding pension sustainability and management of informal mining.

## Juntos por el Perú: Breaking Equilibriums

The proposals of Juntos por el Perú represent a significant break with the current economic framework. In monetary policy, they propose modifying the BCR's leadership and using international reserves to finance social spending. On the fiscal front, they propose a progressive tax reform, taxes on large fortunes, and raising tax pressure to 25% of GDP—ten points above current levels—along with greater social spending and public investment, without presenting any credible fiscal transition path.

Regarding economic activity, they promote a constitutional assembly that drives a state entrepreneur model with direct participation in so-called strategic sectors. They propose renegotiating natural resource concession contracts and direct intervention in mining and hydrocarbons, including the nationalization of Camisea gas and the preservation of Petro-Perú as a non-sellable state company. Particularly concerning is their favorable stance toward informal and illegal mining, presented as part of the popular economy without clear environmental, labor, or tax requirements. This approach weakens institutions and favors the expansion of illicit economies.

Regarding employment and wages, they defend a more active state role without detailing productivity policies or sustainable formalization, and prioritize wage increases disconnected from economic growth. On pensions, they bet on redistributive increases without a clear design or sustainability evaluation.

## Renovación Popular: Macro Order with Contradictions

Renovación Popular declares its commitment to macroeconomic stability and central bank autonomy. However, its fiscal proposals present inconsistencies: it proposes reducing the state's size and decreasing the VAT without detailing compensatory measures or a clear consolidation path. To this is added the fact that the cost of its plan would imply an estimated increase of 25.7% in public spending.

In growth and employment, it bets on less regulation and greater private participation, but lacks an explicit strategy for formal employment and productivity. It also does not present a structured pension reform proposal. In mining, it adopts a permissive stance toward informality, treating it primarily as an administrative problem and omitting its fiscal, environmental, and security impacts. In the case of Petro-Perú, it proposes its privatization and withdrawal of public financing, although it is concerning that its candidate has offered to hand over the company to its workers.

Finally, there is concern about the party's relationship with the legal framework and contracts signed by the state, in light of its track record in managing the Lima Metropolitan Municipality.

## Balance

The Peruvian economy maintains solid fundamentals, but its sustainability depends on the decisions being made now. The analysis of the proposals reveals substantive differences between the three alternatives evaluated. Fuerza Popular and Renovación Popular, with their own nuances and contradictions, fall within the macroeconomic stability framework that has sustained growth over recent decades. Juntos por el Perú represents a break of a different nature, which in many ways contemplates the proposals of the Pedro Castillo government. It is worth remembering that that experiment triggered the largest capital flight in the country's recent history: 7% of GDP in the third quarter of 2021, according to the BCR. Our own history demonstrates that this type of transition generates costs that do not fall on those who promote them, but on those who have the least. That asymmetry deserves to be at the center of the debate.
