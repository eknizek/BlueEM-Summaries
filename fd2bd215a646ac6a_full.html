# Caputo and the IMF: Behind-the-Scenes of Meeting with Georgieva and How Guarantees from Multilateral Banks Were Secured

Minister Luis Caputo is leaving Washington with good feelings. As a veteran official of two Argentine governments, he is experienced in the corridors of the IMF, though this time he says the Assembly he just finished has been "the best" he has ever participated in.

"First we closed the agreement with them (the second Fund review), then with the World Bank for financing, then came the agreement with the IDB, and in some way we showed the market and the people again that when we say something we fulfill it, we don't say it for nothing," Caputo explained to Argentine journalists, including Clarín, Friday night after his meeting with the head of the organization, Kristalina Georgieva, which lasted an hour late into the evening when all other officials had already left.

"It was a pleasant meeting because there is a relationship of trust," Caputo said, adding that Georgieva "is superimpressed with the achievements" of the Argentine government.

When the Fund's executive board approves the second review (possibly in mid-May), it will allow a disbursement of US$1 billion. The agreement with the World Bank establishes that the organization will provide guarantees of US$2 billion for loans with private banks that could double that amount. A similar agreement was signed with the IDB for US$550 million.

"We were saying we weren't going to the market because we were analyzing cheaper investment alternatives. Some said we were missing the opportunity, that we were lying. No, we weren't lying at all, and this is the proof. The thing is that closing these agreements takes time, it's not something that happens in two minutes," the minister added, accompanied by his deputy José Luis Daza, Central Bank chief Santiago Bausili, and his deputy Vladimir Werning.

The meeting with Georgieva took place at the end of the Assembly. In a yellow jacket, he stood out as always in a sea of dark suits from the Argentine delegation and the vast majority of attendees at the crowded meeting of ministers from around the world in Washington.

Caputo recounted that with her they discussed "a bit of everything": international issues, the war in the Gulf. "Those things you don't say publicly, in confidence," he explained.

The minister also said they discussed Venezuela, which made news during the Assembly because countries voted that the government of Delcy Rodríguez rejoin the IMF, since Nicolás Maduro's had been suspended. "We talked quite a bit about Venezuela. I think it's very good news, because after all it's a country in the region and we want it to do well. Venezuela is in everyone's interest. So, honestly, we're happy they achieved that. We voted in favor," the minister said.

But obviously the conversation focused on Argentina. "We explained the growth strategy to her, the financial one; we explained in detail what the steps were. She's aware of everything anyway, but above all it was a very pleasant meeting because the relationship is very direct and very good. There is a relationship of trust that never existed between Argentina and the Fund. So that facilitates everything a lot; she is superimpressed with the achievements. She trusted from day one. She told me: 'You said you were going to do something impossible and you made it possible,' and from that day on she always had great confidence, so she congratulated us for everything we were doing and for the results."

Caputo assured that they did not discuss inflation in the conversation, which in March climbed to 3.4%, the highest in a month. The Fund estimates anyway that "it will decelerate in the coming months," according to what the official handling negotiations with the country, Luis Cubeddu, told Clarín at a press conference.

## Debt Payment Secured

The minister recounted that the Fund was aware of the strategy of obtaining guarantees from multilateral organizations to obtain loans from private banks. "We explained to them that we're not losing an opportunity by not going to the market. On the contrary, we're refinancing debt. Our function is to refinance it at the lowest possible rate," he said.

"And in turn, that helps the country risk to fall and so in the future we can go out (to the market) at a more reasonable rate. They were 100% in agreement with the strategy," and he said they told them what numbers they ultimately expected to obtain: US$4 billion locally, US$4 billion in loans with multilateral guarantees plus US$2 billion from asset sales, that is, privatizations. "We already have the financing for the next 3 payments, that is, 18 months," the minister explained.

## Tax Reform

Caputo affirmed that there were no promises that the tax reform would be approved in Argentina this year. "We didn't talk about that at all. But we never promised them on that issue," he assured.

"They always understood that on tax matters, we are going to do it as the fiscal surplus allows us. Because the reality is that what we never want is to incur any deficit. And while, of course, when you lower taxes, it then favors the economy and in the medium term returns you with higher collections, in the short term you inevitably have an impact of lower collections. And we absolutely don't want to expose Argentines to a situation where, in some way, we could lose the fiscal anchor. So, we're going to continue lowering taxes as we manage to increase the level of surplus," he said.

Georgieva emphasized "the issue of countries not increasing their deficits" in the context of the war in the Middle East, Caputo said, and assured that at the Assembly she put Argentina as an "example of the country that best weathered the war." "Because Argentina, in terms of its currency and the stock market, was the country that did best," since the conflict began, the minister assured.

"And Argentina has always been a country that experienced the opposite. Each time there was a shock, it was the one that suffered the most, regardless of what the shock was. So she really puts us as an example, telling other countries: try to control the only thing you can control. And that's what you can do at fiscal levels. Don't fall into the error of trying to make fiscal shocks right now, because those shocks are only going to increase debt levels, increase interest rates, and then it could end up being a bigger problem than the conflict itself. So she says: 'Be careful with these strategies that can end up being very problematic.'"
