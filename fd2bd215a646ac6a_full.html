Finance Minister Caputo's IMF meeting: securing multilateral bank guarantees and confirming 18-month debt payment capacity

18 April 2026 · 02:52 PM

# Caputo and the IMF: Behind-the-scenes of the meeting with Georgieva and how millions in multilateral bank guarantees were secured

Finance Minister Luis Caputo is leaving Washington with positive impressions. As an official of two Argentine governments, he is a veteran of the IMF corridors, though this time he says the Assembly that just concluded was "the best" one he has participated in.

"First we closed the agreement with them (the Fund's second review), then with the World Bank for financing, then came the agreement with the IDB, and in some way we showed the market and the people again that when we say something we fulfill it, we don't say it for nothing," Caputo explained to Argentine journalists, including Clarín, Friday night after his meeting with the organization's head, Kristalina Georgieva, which lasted an hour after everyone had gone home.

"It was a pleasant meeting because there is a relationship of trust," said Caputo, adding that Georgieva "is super impressed with the achievements" of the Argentine government.

When the Fund's executive board approves the second review (possibly mid-May), it will allow a disbursement of US$1 billion. The agreement with the World Bank establishes that the organization will provide guarantees of US$2 billion for loans with private banks that could double that amount. With the IDB, a similar one was signed for US$550 million.

"We were saying we weren't going to the market because we were analyzing cheaper investment alternatives. Some said we were missing the opportunity, that we were lying. No, we weren't lying about anything, and this is the proof. What happens is that closing these agreements takes time, it's not something that takes two minutes," the minister added, accompanied by his deputy José Luis Daza, Central Bank head Santiago Bausili and his deputy Vladimir Werning.

The meeting with Georgieva took place at the end of the Assembly. Wearing a yellow jacket, Caputo stood out as always in the sea of dark suits of the Argentine delegation and the vast majority of attendees at the crowded meeting of world ministers in Washington.

Caputo said that with her they talked "a bit of everything": international issues, the war in the Gulf. "Those things one doesn't say publicly, in confidence," he explained.

The minister also said they talked about Venezuela, which made news during the Assembly because countries voted that Delcy Rodríguez's government rejoin the IMF, since Nicolás Maduro's had been suspended.

"We talked quite a bit about Venezuela. I think it's very good news, because after all it's a country in the region and we want it to do well. Venezuela is in everyone's interest. So, the truth is we're happy they managed to achieve that. We voted in favor," said the minister.

But obviously the conversation centered on Argentina.

"We explained the growth strategy, the financial one; we explained in detail what the steps were. She's aware of everything anyway, but above all it was a very pleasant meeting because the relationship is very direct and very good. There is a relationship of trust that never existed between Argentina and the Fund. So that makes everything much easier; she is super impressed with the achievements. She trusted from day one. She told me: 'You said you were going to do something impossible and you made it possible,' and from that day she always had a lot of confidence, so she congratulated us for everything we were doing and for the results."

Caputo assured that in the conversation they did not talk about inflation, which in March rose to 3.4%, the highest in a month. The Fund nonetheless estimates that "it will decelerate in the coming months," according to Luis Cubeddu, the official handling negotiations with the country, who told Clarín at a press conference.

## Debt Payment Assured

The minister said that the Fund was aware of the strategy of obtaining guarantees from multilateral organizations to secure loans from private banks.

"We explained that we are not missing an opportunity by not going to the market. On the contrary, we are refinancing debt. Our function is to refinance it at the lowest possible rate," he said.

"And at the same time that facilitates the country risk to fall and so in the future we can go out (to the market) at a more reasonable rate. They were 100% in agreement with the strategy," and he said they told them what numbers they ultimately expected to obtain: US$4 billion locally, US$4 billion in loans with multilateral guarantees plus US$2 billion from asset sales, that is, privatizations.

"We already have the financing for the next three payments, that is, 18 months," explained the minister.

## Tax Reform

Caputo stated that there were no promises that tax reform would be approved in Argentina this year.

"We didn't talk about that at all. But we never promised them on that issue," he assured.

"They always understood that tax matters, we are going to do it as the fiscal surplus allows us to. Because the reality is that what we never want is to incur any deficit. And while, of course, when you lower taxes, it later favors the economy and in the medium term returns to you with greater revenue, in the short term inevitably you have an impact of lower revenue. And we don't want to expose Argentines to a situation where, in some way, we could lose the fiscal anchor. So, we are going to continue lowering taxes to the extent that we manage to increase the level of surplus," he said.

Georgieva emphasized "a lot the issue of not increasing countries' deficits" in the context of the war in the Middle East, said Caputo, and assured that at the Assembly she held Argentina up as "an example of the country that best weathered the war."

"Because Argentina, in terms of its currency and stock market, was the country that fared best," since the conflict began, assured the minister.

"And Argentina has always been a country that experienced the opposite. Every time there was a shock, it was the one that suffered the most, regardless of what the shock was. So she actually holds us up as an example when she tells countries: try to control the only thing you can control. And that's what you can do at fiscal levels. Don't fall into the error of trying to make fiscal shocks at this moment, because those shocks will only do one thing: increase debt levels, increase interest rates, and then it can end up being a bigger problem than the conflict itself. So she says: 'Be careful with these strategies that can end up being very problematic.'"
