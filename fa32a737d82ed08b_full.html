Real estate fund index hits record high amid Selic rate cut projections

19 April 2026 · 06:00 PM

# Real Estate Fund Index Hits Record High Amid Selic Rate Cut Projections

Even with elevated interest rates, real estate funds have regained momentum. The IFIX, the main sector index on B3, renewed its historical record on Friday's trading session (17th) by reaching 3,930 points. Over the past 12 months, the gain stands at 18%.

According to analysts, this movement could extend if the Selic continues to fall. The index brings together the most traded FIIs (Real Estate Investment Funds). It rose 21.1% between January and December of last year, despite the Selic remaining at its highest level in nearly two decades throughout 2025. The first Selic cut came in March of this year, when Copom reduced the rate from 15% to 14.75% per year. According to the latest Focus Bulletin, the expectation is that interest rates will end 2026 at 12.5% per year. In this scenario, analysts see room for continued improvement in FIIs, although possibly at a more moderate pace.

FIIs are funds that invest in the real estate market. In practice, they function as a way to invest in this market without directly purchasing a property. Shares are traded on the Exchange, and investor returns can come from both the appreciation of these shares and periodic distributions paid by the fund. These distributions come from rental income on properties in the portfolio or interest paid on securities acquired by the fund, the so-called brick funds and paper funds.

For Julya Wellisch, director of Anbima (Brazilian Association of Financial and Capital Market Entities), one of the main advantages of the product is democratizing access to this market, allowing investors to apply in shopping centers, logistics warehouses, or corporate spaces with a much lower entry ticket than required to purchase a property directly.

Larissa Gatti and Fausto Menezes, analysts at Itaú BBA, highlight the same logic. For them, the main attractions for individual investors are the lower entry value, professional management, diversification, and liquidity, although FII trading is less intense than equity trading.

An important characteristic of these funds is the distribution of returns. By law, FIIs must distribute to shareholders at least 95% of profits calculated on a cash basis each semester. In practice, however, many funds make monthly payments, which helps explain the product's appeal among investors seeking recurring income.

Investments in real estate funds are exempt from income tax if they meet certain criteria. Income paid by funds remains exempt from income tax for individuals, as long as the fund complies with some rules, such as having at least 50 shareholders, being traded on the Exchange, and not being overly concentrated in a single investor. The profit obtained from selling shares, however, remains taxed at 20%.

For the beginning investor, the first distinction to understand is between brick funds and paper funds. So-called brick funds invest in physical properties, such as shopping centers, warehouses, and offices. In them, revenue typically comes from rent and appreciation of assets. Paper funds, meanwhile, invest in securities linked to the real estate sector, mainly CRIs (Real Estate Receivables Certificates).

Menezes summarizes the difference directly: "In a brick fund, the investor is exposed to the property; in a paper fund, it's to debt." In paper funds, resources are mainly applied to CRIs, debt securities linked to the real estate sector. According to Itaú BBA analysts, these securities can have guarantees such as property pledged as collateral, future revenues like rent tied to debt payment, and a reserve of resources to cover delays or defaults.

This difference also changes the risk profile. In brick funds, factors such as vacancy, default, location, and property quality weigh in. In paper funds, the focus falls more on credit quality, operation guarantees, and the indexation of securities. In simplified terms, there is no safer type in any scenario: it all depends on the risk the investor accepts and the quality of the portfolio.

The Selic's trajectory helps explain the sector's recent behavior. Generally, paper funds tend to benefit more from high interest rates, especially when carrying securities tied to CDI (Interbank Certificate of Deposit) or IPCA, the index that measures inflation in Brazil. Brick funds, meanwhile, typically gain traction when the market begins to see interest rate cuts, since a lower cost of capital can improve asset pricing, stimulate real estate activity, and increase appetite for risk investments.

In Wellisch's assessment, this is what happened in 2025. According to her, the different fund strategies reacted differently to the macroeconomic scenario, but still-elevated dividend yield helped maintain demand for FIIs even with high interest rates.
