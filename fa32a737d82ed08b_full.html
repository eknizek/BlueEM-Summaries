# Real Estate Investment Fund Index Hits New Record Amid Selic Rate Cut Expectations

Even amid high interest rates, real estate investment funds have regained momentum. The IFIX, the sector's main index on B3, set a new historical record on Friday's session (17th) by reaching 3,930 points. Over the past 12 months, the increase is 18%.

According to analysts, this movement could extend further if the Selic continues to decline.

The index comprises the most traded Real Estate Investment Funds (FIIs). It rose 21.1% between January and December of last year, despite the Selic remaining at its highest level in nearly two decades throughout 2025. The first Selic cut came in March of this year, when the Central Bank's Monetary Policy Committee reduced the rate from 15% to 14.75% per year. According to the latest Focus Bulletin, the outlook is for interest rates to end 2026 at 12.5% per year. In this scenario, analysts see room for continued improvement in FIIs, though possibly at a more moderate pace.

FIIs are funds that invest in the real estate market. In practice, they function as a way to invest in this market without purchasing a property directly. Shares are traded on the stock exchange, and investor returns can come from both appreciation of these shares and distributions made periodically by the fund. These distributions come from rental income on properties in the fund's portfolio or interest paid on securities acquired by the fund, known as brick funds and paper funds.

For Julya Wellisch, director of Anbima (Brazilian Association of Financial and Capital Market Entities), one of the main advantages of the product is democratizing access to this market, allowing investors to apply in shopping centers, logistics warehouses, or corporate spaces with a much smaller initial investment than required to purchase a property directly.

Larissa Gatti and Fausto Menezes, analysts at Itaú BBA, highlight the same logic. For them, the main attractions for individuals are the lower entry value, professional management, diversification, and liquidity, although FII trading is less intense than stock trading.

An important characteristic of these funds is dividend distribution. By law, FIIs must distribute at least 95% of accrued profits to shareholders, on a cash basis, twice per year. In practice, however, many funds make monthly payments, which helps explain the product's appeal among investors seeking recurring income.

Investment income from real estate funds is exempt from income tax if they meet certain criteria. The distributions paid by the funds remain income tax-exempt for individuals, as long as the fund complies with some rules, such as having at least 50 shareholders, being traded on the stock exchange, and not being too concentrated in a single investor. Profits obtained from selling shares remain taxed at 20%.

For beginning investors, the first distinction to understand is between brick funds and paper funds. So-called brick funds invest in physical properties, such as shopping centers, warehouses, and offices. In these, revenue typically comes from rent and appreciation of assets. Paper funds, meanwhile, invest in securities linked to the real estate sector, mainly CRIs (Real Estate Receivables Certificates).

Menezes summarizes the difference directly: "In a brick fund, the investor is exposed to the property; in a paper fund, to debt."

In paper funds, resources are invested mainly in CRIs, debt securities linked to the real estate sector. According to Itaú BBA analysts, these securities can have guarantees such as a property pledged as collateral, future revenues such as rents linked to debt repayment, and a reserve of funds to cover delays or defaults.

This difference also changes the risk profile. In brick funds, factors such as vacancy, default, location, and property quality carry weight. In paper funds, the focus falls more on credit quality, operation guarantees, and the indexation of securities. In simplified terms, there is no safer type in any scenario: it all depends on the risk the investor accepts and portfolio quality.

The trajectory of the Selic helps explain the sector's recent behavior. Generally, paper funds tend to benefit more from high interest rates, especially when carrying securities tied to the CDI (Interbank Deposit Certificate) or the IPCA, the index measuring inflation in Brazil. Brick funds, meanwhile, typically gain traction when the market begins to foresee interest rate declines, since lower capital costs can improve asset pricing, stimulate real estate activity, and increase appetite for risk investments.

According to Wellisch's assessment, this is what happened in 2025. Different fund strategies reacted differently to the macroeconomic scenario, but still-elevated dividend yields helped maintain demand for FIIs even with high interest rates.
