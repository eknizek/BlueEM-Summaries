# Adapting to Any Market Demand: BMW's Strategy

Within the global automotive industry, the BMW Group plant in San Luis Potosí, which celebrates its 10th anniversary in July, stands not only as a center of high manufacturing efficiency, but as a bastion of adaptability in the face of the political and technological uncertainty that reigns in the world today. Klaus von Moltke, new president and CEO of the plant, is emphatic in assuring that the strategy for the coming years focuses on radical flexibility and a firm stance regarding the upcoming T-MEC review, constant technological changes, and what he considers the rival to overcome: China.

"The investment we made some years ago, we made independent of the current situation, because we knew that Mexico is a great opportunity to grow. Today, we are improving technology at the San Luis plant to be very flexible with market behaviors, accelerate and decelerate production, preserve jobs, and be ready if internal combustion vehicles, electric vehicles, or in the short term even hydrogen vehicles are required. We are ready to adapt to any scenario without affecting the customer or the worker," he states.

One of the central pillars for the growth of the Bavarian firm in Mexico is the resolution of asymmetries within the framework of the Treaty between Mexico, the United States and Canada (T-MEC). Von Moltke pointed out that the industry is aligned in the country to correct distortions, especially regarding Section 232, which currently imposes tariffs on Mexico at a disadvantage compared to other regions.

"The fact of imposing 25% (tariff), but on other countries, including Europe, you impose 15%, automatically puts at a disadvantage who should be your best commercial partner. You cannot put your best ally in a worse situation than other countries," the executive declared.

For the CEO, understanding with the United States must evolve toward a solid regional bloc: "If you want to differentiate yourself, you start thinking in terms of blocs... and you have your best ally which is Mexico."

## The Chinese Monster

The expansion of Chinese brands, driven by state subsidies and dominance of the battery supply chain, represents an unprecedented challenge. However, von Moltke analyzes the phenomenon from the saturation of the Asian market and BMW's strategic response through innovation.

"The domestic market (Chinese) is completely saturated. Rather, they are damaging each other... the only way for this to be successful is to expand on a global scale," he explained.

Facing this pressure, BMW is betting on its New Class (Neue Klasse), a concept that redefines the vehicle as a digital object. "We have a response with the new class that we believe is the product and a very clear strategy to compete against that," he added confidently.

In the face of a vision that many consider "ideologized" regarding total electrification, BMW defends "technological neutrality." The executive warned that consumers cannot be forced to adopt a technology if the infrastructure and energy costs are not adequate.

"We cannot make a total transformation, but you cannot force people... you are practically forcing them to pay expensive energy and in many countries with an energy source that is not green," he questioned.

Under this logic, the San Luis Potosí plant is preparing for a hybrid future where synthetic fuels and hydrogen play a crucial role. "Think openly and give an opportunity to other technologies as well, because in the combustion part... dieselgate was history and passed... combustion has value, because we can also use it with eFuels or green fuel," von Moltke highlighted.

The BMW leader in San Luis Potosí reiterated that real competitiveness resides in the independence of materials and technologies. "If we depend on other regions to manufacture, we are not competitive. And that is where I see Mexico offers many opportunities... access to suppliers that we can localize is an advantage," he said, reaffirming that the Mexican plant is the key piece for navigating the geopolitical storms of the sector.

## The Neue Klasse

– Investment of 800 million euros to expand operations over 80,000 m²

– 500 million euros allocated to the High-Voltage Battery Module Production Center, initiated in May 2024

– Production of the first Neue Klasse vehicle in 2027: BMW iX3, based on the BMW Vision Neue Klasse X

– San Luis Potosí will be the first premium automotive plant in Mexico to produce electric vehicles and high-voltage batteries

– SLP is one of four plants selected worldwide to produce Neue Klasse, along with Debrecen, Munich, and Shenyang
