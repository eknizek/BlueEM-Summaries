Hospital Felix Bulnes Concessionaire Renews Attack Against Ministry of Public Works, Claims Six Years of Non-Compliant Operations

27 April 2026 · 11:38 PM

# Hospital Felix Bulnes Concessionaire Renews Attack Against Ministry of Public Works, Claims Six Years of Non-Compliant Operations

In May 2024, the concession company operating Hospital Félix Bulnes—linked to French fund manager Meridian—initiated a battle against the Ministry of Public Works (MOP), in which it requested compensation for a sum that could reach up to UF 2,409,452, equivalent to approximately US$ 107 million. According to the firm, the hospital "has operated in a completely different manner than agreed under the concession contract," affecting the concessioned services both in volume and in additional requirements.

Specifically, in a discrepancy filed that year before the Technical Concessions Panel, the company argued that since 2020—the period when it assumed operation of the facility—there have been a series of situations that altered the conditions originally agreed in the contract. Faced with this scenario, the Technical Panel proposed a mechanism to recompose part of those payments. However, the formula did not satisfy the concessionaire, which decided to escalate the case to the Arbitration Commission to demand compensation; a process that is still ongoing.

Almost two years after that initial conflict, the concessionaire opened a new front against the ministry led by Martín Arrau. This time, the company filed a new discrepancy requesting compensation of UF 351,431, equivalent to approximately US$ 16 million, on the same basis as its previous complaint: that the reported issues from 2024 persisted between April of that year and December 2025.

"Therefore, this discrepancy is materially limited by the damages and negative effects that have been caused to the concessionaire for the period in question," according to the document presented by the firm.

"These transgressions have implied a scenario of structural overload, not transitory, that has permanently affected the operation of the concession with operational requirements outside the Concession Contract. This situation prevents the fulfillment of the principles of efficient self-financing and economic equilibrium established in the regulatory framework of the Concessions Law," the company argued.

## The Details

The company linked to the French fund maintained that, since the beginning of the concession's operational phase, and due to causes attributable to the State, the MOP, and the Ministry of Health, the hospital has operated under conditions different from those provided in the contract, affecting—as indicated in 2024—both the volume of concessioned services and the level of requirement needed to provide them.

One of the main points the concessionaire raised is the permanent installation of beds and hospitalization equipment in areas not contemplated for that use. Specifically, it asserted that 111 devices have been permanently installed in the hospital and used as hospital beds for patient care in clinical support areas. According to the firm, this has sustainably increased demand for concessioned services, without this increased use being contemplated in the contract or in the payment mechanism.

Another point the concessionaire questioned is the imposition of additional requirements in the provision of six concessioned services, which—according to its claim—has forced it to operate under more burdensome conditions than originally agreed. In the firm's view, this has meant assuming unforeseen charges and additional costs outside the contractual framework. To this it adds the operation of services not contemplated in the contract, such as the so-called "Pre-Discharge Unit," which—it claimed—is not regulated in the concession and yet has begun to operate, generating actual costs without recognition in the payment scheme associated with occupied bed days.

## Loss of Economic Equilibrium

This type of requirement, the concessionaire affirmed, has directly affected the economic structure of the contract, by imposing permanent costs derived from services that exceed the original design of the concession. In this regard, the firm argued that there was a breakdown of the economic equilibrium of the contract, since the actual operation of the hospital since the beginning of operations has significantly departed from the originally agreed conditions.

"These transgressions have implied a scenario of structural overload, not transitory, that has permanently affected the operation of the concession with operational requirements outside the Concession Contract. This situation prevents the fulfillment of the principles of efficient self-financing and economic equilibrium established in the regulatory framework of the Concessions Law," the company concluded.
