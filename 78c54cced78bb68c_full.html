# How Colombia's Credit Rating Downgrade Impacts Households in the Country

Colombia's sovereign credit rating downgrade to BB- by S&P Global Ratings has generated reactions in the business sector, which warned of direct effects on households, access to credit, investment, and fiscal sustainability. Natalia Gutiérrez, president of Acolgen and the Business Council, and the National Association of Colombian Entrepreneurs (ANDI) agreed on the economic implications stemming from the decision.

## Effects on Financing and Cost of Capital

Gutiérrez stated that the downgrade "does have an impact, and more than we imagined," explaining that the move from BB to BB- "deepens the speculative grade condition" and raises the perception of risk for international investors. According to her analysis, this translates into an increase in the cost of capital throughout the economy. She indicated that "financing becomes more expensive and more limited," implying higher costs for the State and more demanding conditions for businesses.

Households also face higher interest rates and reduced credit availability in an environment that pressures variables such as the exchange rate. These conditions occur alongside S&P's decision to adjust Ecopetrol's rating to BB-, in line with the sovereign rating, due to its relationship with the State and its role in generating fiscal revenue.

## Impact on Investment, Productive Sectors, and Competitiveness

Acolgen's president maintained that a lower rating reduces the country's competitiveness against other investment destinations. "Global capital is allocated according to risk. With a lower rating, Colombia loses competitiveness against other destinations and interest in long-term projects decreases," she noted. She added that the impact is cross-sectoral, affecting energy, infrastructure, industry, agriculture, and technology, as all depend on financing and investor confidence.

In particular, she warned that in capital-intensive activities, the probability of project delays or cancellations increases. "This means fewer works, fewer jobs, lower growth, and risks to the availability of essential services," she indicated. She also noted that an "adverse circle" could be generated in which lower investment reduces productivity and limits economic growth.

ANDI stated that S&P's decision "is not an isolated fact" and reflects the management of public finances in recent years. The business group noted that the growth of the national budget by nearly 50% in nominal terms has been accompanied by an increase in debt.

## Fiscal Pressure, Public Debt, and Effects on Households

According to ANDI, public debt increased from just over 800 trillion pesos at the beginning of the current administration to approximately 1.3 trillion pesos currently. The group indicated that this increase occurs in a context where Colombia faces higher interest rates in international comparison. It also warned that approximately three of every 100 pesos of the budget are allocated to paying interest, which reduces the margin for other public investments.

According to the statement, this fiscal burden will be transferred to future administrations and citizens. Gutiérrez also pointed out effects on daily life, indicating that "credit is more expensive, job opportunities are reduced, projects stop being executed, and income growth slows down." She added that lower investment in infrastructure and energy could translate into limitations in services and pressure on tariffs.

For his part, ANDI President Bruce Mac Master called on economic authorities to avoid decisions that would deepen the fiscal imbalance in the remaining months of the government. "Few months remain before this government ends, and we must ask economic authorities not to deepen the immense problem we already have," he stated. The leader added that the situation of public finances "went from being simply a concern for economists to becoming a long-term problem" whose effects will fall on the population, especially on the most vulnerable sectors.

The business sector's reactions come after S&P linked the rating downgrade to factors such as fiscal weaknesses, increased indebtedness, and limitations in economic flexibility—elements that affect the investment environment and the performance of strategic companies for the country.
