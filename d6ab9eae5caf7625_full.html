# Scotiabank Projects Inflation in Peru at 3.2% by End of 2026

According to Scotiabank's Weekly Report, the inflation estimate for Peru has been raised from the previous 2.2% to 3.2% by the end of 2026 following the start of the war in the Middle East and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

"Looking at the longer term, we believe there would be a deceleration in inflation throughout 2026, probably beginning during May and June, but culminating above the upper limit of the target range (3%). We have raised our inflation estimate that we had before the start of the armed conflict between the US, Israel and Iran and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, from 2.2% to 3.2% by the end of 2026," Scotiabank stated in its report.

"Our assumption is that international crude oil prices will remain around US$100 per barrel between March and May, and then begin to decelerate to reach an annual average of US$80 per barrel in 2026," it added.

Regarding April's inflation, it would be slightly above the historical average of the last 20 years (0.2%). With this, they expect there would be no significant changes in annual inflation and it would remain close to the 3.8% recorded in March.

Meanwhile, underlying inflation would increase by approximately 0.1% monthly, maintaining the annual rate at levels close to those recorded in March (3.7%).

"In detail, we are seeing a correction in chicken prices, after they increased over the last four months, but we continue to see increases in potatoes, sugar and some vegetables and fruits," they explained in their report.

On the energy side, there would be a significant correction in taxi fares; however, pressure on fuel prices such as gasoline, gasohol and diesel would continue, keeping inflation in the transportation sector on the rise.

In the recent Central Reserve Bank (BCR) macroeconomic expectations report, published on April 10, the initial impact of the Middle East conflict on local price expectations was observed. First, 12-month inflation expectations have increased from 2.1% to 2.5%, levels not seen in almost two years (since June 2024). On the other hand, inflation expectations for 2026 have also increased from a range of 2.0% and 2.2% to 2.3% and 2.8%. These expectations include economic analysts, financial and non-financial companies.

In this context, Scotiabank maintains the current 4.25% reference rate as the base scenario (prior to the conflict we expected a cut of 25 basis points to 4.0%). However, there is a risk that new increases could occur during the second half of the year if inflation expectations, currently at 2.5%, continue to show upward persistence and rise above the BCR's target range (1%-3%).
