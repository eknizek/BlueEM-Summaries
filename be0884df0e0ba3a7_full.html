# Mexican Investor Manages Through Mobile Teams: WeTrade

Mexican investors use their mobile devices to conduct trading. The architecture of Mexico's financial market is undergoing a structural metamorphosis driven by technological adoption and a new generation of operators. What was previously reserved for the upper echelons of financial institutions is now managed from mobile devices.

For Borja Gómez, WeTrade's director for Latin America, this transition is not coincidental, but rather reflects a user who demands autonomy and sophisticated tools to face global volatility. With a career spanning over a decade in wealth management and financial advisory, Gómez identifies that democratic access to assets such as forex, indices, and commodities is reconfiguring the profile of the national investor.

The executive maintains that the market is no longer exclusive to large capitals, as "today we have users who open their first account from their mobile phone, with accessible amounts, and within a matter of weeks are operating with a clarity that previously took years to develop."

The current economic environment, characterized by adjustments in interest rates and fluctuating inflation data, has forced the local operator to abandon passive strategies. Rather than stepping back from risk, the Mexican investor is learning to capitalize on turbulence. Gómez observes that traders operating on his platform "are not fleeing the market when there is turbulence, they are learning to read it," integrating macroeconomic variables into their daily decision-making.

This analytical capacity has resulted in real portfolio diversification that transcends academic theory. Users are building strategies that link currencies, digital assets, and energy products in a single ecosystem. In this regard, WeTrade's director emphasizes that the context demands an agile response, noting that "the user doesn't have to go to five different places to build a diversified strategy; they have everything in one place, with institutional execution technology."

## The Future of Social Trading

Going forward, the evolution of Mexico's financial ecosystem will be marked by three pillars: the fall of barriers to entry, community learning, and individual professionalization. The social trading trend is shaping up as the industry standard, allowing the experience of some to serve as a guide for others.

Borja Gómez asserts that "the Latin American investor learns in community, shares what works for him, and follows those who inspire his confidence," a dynamic that is already a reality in daily operations. The executive highlights that Mexico possesses ideal conditions—economic size, technological adoption, and entrepreneurial culture—to lead the financial transformation of the region.

The transition from amateur operator to methodical one is the great milestone of this era, as "we are seeing people who started trading in their spare time develop serious methodologies, real discipline, and consistent results."
