# Rise in Agricultural and Road Machinery Imports Concerns Brazilian Sector

Brazilian grain harvests have entered an upward trajectory over the last decade, breaking records repeatedly and experiencing few downturns due to climate. However, this growth rate is not reflected in agricultural machinery sales in the country. In the first three months of this year, domestic retail sales totaled just 9,800 units, 13.1% below the January-March 2025 level.

The industry views this slowdown with concern, but what worries the sector most at the moment is the pace of imports. In 2025, 11,000 foreign agricultural machines entered Brazil, 17% more than in 2024. At the start of this year, foreign purchases continue to accelerate, with numbers indicating a 48.4% increase in imports from January to March compared to the same period in 2025. China and India are the main suppliers.

In the first quarter of this year, imports of agricultural machinery from China surged 192%. Throughout 2025, compared to 2024, they had already risen 86%.

"Our concern is with the advance of these imports without support from domestic production, without supply chains and without backing from research and development in the country," says Igor Calvet, president of Anfavea (National Association of Automobile Manufacturers).

Beyond the pressure from external market imports of agricultural and road machinery affecting the industry, Brazilian monetary policy, particularly high interest rates, has made it difficult for Brazilian producers and companies that purchase road machinery to invest. One reflection of macroeconomic policy is that 38% of those who purchased road machinery last year used their own capital, above the 33% in 2024. That is, companies that still have capital resources are the ones able to drive the road machinery market, according to Calvet.

The Anfavea president says there is a concentration of imports in two countries: China and India. Together they accounted for 85% of imports, particularly compressors and loader buckets. The entity is not against imports, but conditions must be equal, according to Calvet. When three important variables in the competitiveness of producing a low-power tractor are considered—labor, steel, and scale—Brazil is 22% less competitive than the Chinese and 21% less than the Indians.

"And all of this [this competitiveness] is beyond our companies," the president affirms.

Growing imports and lower competitiveness combine with the great volatility of the world, brought by the pandemic and wars such as those in Ukraine and Iran. All this reflects in oil prices, fertilizers, and translates into costs for producers. These already have reduced income from falling commodity prices and credit difficulties due to interest rates.

"It's a difficult year with many uncertainties, and, gradually, will also influence the year 2027," Calvet says.

The industry projects a 5.6% drop in domestic sales of agricultural and road machinery and an 11.2% contraction in exports this year. Agribusiness and the industry are extremely linked, and any competitiveness strategy for agriculture involves the industry, and any industry strategy involves agriculture, he says.

"You can't make a program focused only on agricultural competitiveness without involving the industry."

For Jorge Görgen, vice-president of Anfavea, market impacts, such as the Iran war, make producers more reluctant to invest. There is greater difficulty in predicting tomorrow. Already Alexandre Bernardes de Miranda, also vice-president of the entity, states that the moment is almost a perfect storm, including war.

Anfavea consolidated the sector numbers for 2025. 86,800 agricultural and road machines were sold, and the estimate for this year is 82,000 commercial units in retail. Exports should fall to 20,800 units, below the 23,400 in 2025.

Road machinery sales fell 0.4% last year to 37,000 units. Agricultural machines dropped to 49,800, 3.6% less. In the tractor sector, small-scale units registered higher sales, but large-scale units declined. The commercialization of harvesters fell to just 3,300 units in retail, 22.1% less than in 2024 and 51% less compared to 2023.
