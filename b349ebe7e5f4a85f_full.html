# Colombian Steel Sector Starts 2026 Stronger but Faces Cost and Energy Headwinds

The first quarter of the year showed positive performance for Diaco, driven by a better balance between supply and demand in the market. This behavior was accompanied by a reduction in imports and a rebound in construction activity, especially in social interest housing (VIS), reflected in higher project initiations. According to Mauro de Castro, country director of the company, these factors generate positive expectations about the dynamics toward 2026.

However, this favorable environment coexists with relevant pressures on the cost structure. On one hand, the increase in the minimum wage impacts both the industry directly and contractors, which are labor-intensive. On the other, the unavailability of natural gas and the need to resort to imported gas, with higher prices. He also highlighted the implementation of safeguards by the Government as a necessary response to the global oversupply of steel.

## How have you been doing? What was the first quarter like?

We had a very good first quarter. A more balanced supply and demand scenario allowed us to have better results, with fewer imports. The start of the year was positive and hopefully it continues that way.

Is this balance also explained by fewer imports?

It's a combination. On one hand, there was higher demand, although concentrated in some areas, especially due to project initiations, mainly VIS. On the other hand, we saw fewer imports and lower supply in the country, which allowed for better balance and greater business utilization.

What is causing the decline in imports?

I couldn't point to a specific cause. In coils we had relevant imports, but in rebar they did fall, as did supply from domestic producers. Andi data shows a better balance between supply and demand. In addition, Dane figures show higher initiations toward the end of last year, which energizes the market.

Has the war in Iran had an impact?

We did not see relevant impacts on the business in Colombia. What we did observe was the use of alternative fuels due to the unavailability of natural gas, some with increases, possibly associated with the international price of oil, although I don't see a direct correlation. In general, we don't anticipate relevant impacts.

What about energy costs?

The increase in the minimum wage affected us, both directly and to labor-intensive contractors, which is reflected in costs. Additionally, the unavailability of gas and the need to import more expensive gas represent a relevant impact, especially for the steel sector.

Regarding tariffs, is the Government's measure sufficient?

The measure is already in practice. In a context of global steel oversupply, countries with less protection receive more subsidized materials, which creates unfair competition. Other countries are also taking measures, such as Peru with anti-dumping actions. The intelligent tariff safeguard seeks to protect the metallurgical sector, including aluminum. It has not implied price increases, but rather seeks to prevent the entry of products under conditions of unfair competition.

When would effects be seen?

Since the measure was published for consultation in October, the market anticipated possible changes, which reduced import appetite. However, the time between announcement and implementation allowed inventory to enter. Going forward, it is likely that materials from countries like China or Russia will decrease, at least under the previous price conditions.

Does the measure reduce the sector's concern?

Rather than solving everything, it blocks a threat. It ensures there isn't such strong unfair pressure. It has a one-year term, during which impacts, benefits, and possible adjustments should be evaluated. It's a mechanism that should remain active to protect competitiveness and support reindustrialization.

## Let's talk about Diaco's announcement of DAP, Environmental Product Declaration. What does it mean and what does it imply for the company?

The Environmental Product Declaration (DAP) is a transparency exercise that allows measuring and making traceable the environmental impact throughout the product's life cycle. For the company, it is part of a growth strategy based on sustainability and circular economy. This certification evaluates multiple impacts—such as water, air, soil, and energy consumption—and delivers verifiable information that allows comparing products according to their environmental footprint. Having it doesn't necessarily imply better performance, but the ability to demonstrate it with audited data. Its usefulness lies in that builders can make informed decisions, prioritizing materials with lower emissions, in a sector that is highly intensive in CO2.

Who grants it and what is its scope?

It is granted by an international body that verifies and audits production processes. In markets like Europe it is mandatory, and in Colombia it is projected that by 2030 it will be a standard for construction materials. DAP allows measuring embedded carbon in projects, that is, emissions associated with materials during their life cycle. This makes it easier to compare options and advance toward more sustainable buildings. The expectation is that by 2035, all materials will have this type of declaration, which would allow generalized comparison in the sector. In this context, Diaco positions itself as a pioneer in making transparent the environmental impact of its products.
