# Steel industry starts year with improved balance, but faces cost and energy pressure

Mauro de Castro, country director of Diaco, explains that tariffs on imports are halting the threat to the industry. The industry closes ranks with a 35% tariff on steel and supports the Government decree. Diaco says there is greater balance between supply and demand.

The first quarter of the year showed positive performance for Diaco, driven by better balance between supply and demand in the market. This behavior was accompanied by a reduction in imports and a rebound in construction activity, especially in social interest housing (VIS), reflected in higher construction starts. According to Mauro de Castro, country director of the company, these factors generate positive expectations about the dynamics toward 2026.

However, this favorable environment coexists with relevant pressures on the cost structure. On one hand, the minimum wage increase impacts both the industry directly and contractors, which are labor-intensive. On the other, the unavailability of natural gas and the need to resort to imported gas with higher prices. He also highlighted the implementation of safeguards by the Government as a necessary response to global steel oversupply.

**How have things gone? How was the first quarter?**

We had a very good first quarter. A more balanced supply and demand scenario allowed us to have better results, with fewer imports. The start of the year was positive and hopefully it continues that way.

**Is this balance also explained by fewer imports?**

It's a combination. On one hand, there was higher demand, although specific in some areas, especially due to construction starts, mainly VIS. On the other hand, we saw fewer imports and lower supply in the country, which allowed for better balance and greater business utilization.

**What caused the drop in imports?**

I couldn't point to a specific cause. In coils we had relevant imports, but in rods they did fall, as did the supply from domestic producers. The Andi data shows better balance between supply and demand. Additionally, Dane figures show higher construction starts toward the end of last year, which energizes the market.

**Has the war in Iran had an impact?**

We didn't see relevant impacts on business in Colombia. What we did observe was the use of alternative fuels due to natural gas unavailability, some with increases, possibly associated with international oil prices, although I don't see a direct correlation. In general, we don't anticipate relevant impacts.

**What about energy costs?**

The minimum wage increase affected us, both directly and contractors that are labor-intensive, which is reflected in costs. Additionally, gas unavailability and the need to import more expensive gas represent a relevant impact, especially for the steel sector.

**On tariffs, is the Government measure sufficient?**

The measure is already in practice. In a context of global steel oversupply, countries with less protection receive more subsidized materials, generating unfair competition. Other countries are also taking measures, like Peru with anti-dumping actions. The intelligent tariff safeguard seeks to protect the metallurgical sector, including aluminum. It hasn't implied price increases, but rather seeks to halt the entry of products under unfair competition conditions.

**When would effects be seen?**

Since the measure was published for consultation in October, the market anticipated possible changes, which reduced import appetite. However, the time between announcement and application allowed inventory entry. Going forward, it's likely that materials from countries like China or Russia will decrease, at least under previous price conditions.

**Does the measure reduce sector concerns?**

Rather than solving everything, it blocks a threat. It guarantees there won't be such strong unfair pressure. It has a one-year term, during which impacts, benefits, and possible adjustments must be evaluated. It's a mechanism that should remain active to protect competitiveness and support reindustrialization.

**Let's talk about the DAP announcement, Environmental Product Declaration. What does it mean and what does it imply for the company?**

The Environmental Product Declaration (DAP) is a transparency exercise that allows measuring and making traceable the environmental impact throughout the product's life cycle. For the company, it's part of a growth strategy based on sustainability and circular economy. This certification evaluates multiple impacts—such as water, air, soil, and energy consumption—and provides verifiable information allowing product comparison based on environmental footprint. Having it doesn't necessarily imply better performance, but rather the ability to demonstrate it with audited data. Its usefulness lies in that builders can make informed decisions, prioritizing materials with lower emissions, in a sector that is highly CO2 intensive.

**Who grants it and what is its scope?**

It's granted by an international body that verifies and audits production processes. In markets like Europe it's mandatory, and in Colombia it's projected to be a standard for construction materials by 2030. The DAP allows measuring carbon embedded in projects, that is, emissions associated with materials during their life cycle. This facilitates comparing options and advancing toward more sustainable buildings. The expectation is that by 2035, all materials will have this type of declaration, allowing generalized comparison in the sector. In this context, Diaco positions itself as a pioneer in making transparent the environmental impact of its products.
