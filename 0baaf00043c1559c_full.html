# Kalshi and Polymarket Face New Rival in Hottest Cryptocurrency Exchange

Hyperliquid, the decentralized cryptocurrency exchange that has emerged as one of the most active trading venues for digital assets, is proposing to add prediction markets to its platform, a direct challenge to Kalshi and Polymarket as the rapidly growing sector attracts new competitors.

The proposal, known as HIP-4 and currently in public testing phase, would allow traders to bet on real-world outcomes on a platform that has increasingly attracted Wall Street's attention due to the speed and ambition of its product expansion.

Hyperliquid traditionally focuses on perpetual futures, contracts with no expiration date that involve large amounts of leverage and, therefore, risk. For prediction markets, the contract design would be simpler. A market on whether US inflation in July could exceed 3.5%, for example, would generate two tokens, one for each outcome. Traders buy or sell either one, and the winning token is settled at a fixed value when the answer is known. Unlike Hyperliquid's perpetual products, the proposed contracts would not include leverage, reducing the risk of forced liquidations that frequently occur in cryptocurrency markets.

What makes Hyperliquid a credible threat, at least on paper, is not the contract structure but the platform underneath. What began as a niche for crypto derivatives has quickly become one of DeFi's most ambitious markets, adding contracts linked to oil, gold, silver, and US equities, attracting billions in trading volume and creating and shipping new products at a speed traditional centers cannot match.

In March, Hyperliquid handled $219 billion in total volume, according to data site Hydromancer.

"Sophisticated traders will be able to leverage portfolio margin and discover ways to generate alpha from these two different market types," said Sunny Shi, investor at crypto fund Syncracy Capital. "A way that you wouldn't see as much on Polymarket or Kalshi, where today most of it is just betting. It's like one-sided betting."

Some important details remain to be resolved, such as how Hyperliquid would decide which events meet requirements for contracts, what governance process would approve new markets, or when HIP-4 would move from testing to full launch. A Hyperliquid representative declined to comment.

The platform's growing popularity was evident during the Iran crisis, when oil-linked contracts recorded over $1 billion in volume in a single day, providing one of the first readings on how traders were pricing geopolitical risk while traditional commodity markets were closed. An equities-linked contract tracking the Nasdaq 100 has attracted over $60 million in open interest.

The platform has attracted backing from firms like Paradigm and Pantera Capital, and an $888 million fund trading on Nasdaq now owns its native token.

Polymarket and Kalshi already offer binary outcome contracts, and prediction markets are increasingly accessible through conventional brokerages like Robinhood, Interactive Brokers, and Coinbase. What Hyperliquid proposes is distinct in at least two ways.

First, prediction markets would be native to a platform where traders already trade cryptocurrencies, commodities, and equity-linked contracts, giving the product instant distribution rather than requiring it to build an audience from scratch. Second, the contracts would sit within the same trading system as those other products, meaning a single user could manage event bets, Bitcoin positions, and oil exposure within one account.

Competition moves in both directions. While Hyperliquid enters prediction markets, Polymarket and Kalshi have been exploring perpetual futures and other derivatives, products that would bring them closer to the multi-asset trading Hyperliquid already offers.

A Polymarket spokesperson did not respond to requests for comment. A Kalshi representative did not comment on the competitive landscape.

Overlap between user bases is already visible. Analysis by chain researcher Fleck shows that approximately 3.3% of Polymarket users are also active on Hyperliquid, but those overlapping traders represent around 12% of Polymarket's total volume, suggesting the most active speculators already have a foot in both worlds.

The timing of Hyperliquid's entry is notable. The prediction markets sector has spent the last two years working toward regulatory legitimacy: Kalshi obtained CFTC approval, Polymarket returned to the US, Washington showed favor toward the sector, and venture capital flowed to platforms building within the regulatory framework. Hyperliquid is going against the current: an offshore decentralized exchange that restricts US user access is proposing to offer event-like contracts at a time when much of the sector is betting the future is in regulated markets.

For its supporters, that freedom is key: faster product development and user access to markets that US platforms do not serve. Industry participants expect early adoption to be gradual and activity to concentrate outside the US, in markets that have lacked access to conventional prediction platforms, such as cricket betting in countries like India.

"Historically, prediction markets have been very focused on sports, geopolitical events and elections, and most activity has come through sports," says Rajiv Patel-O'Connor, partner at Framework Ventures.
