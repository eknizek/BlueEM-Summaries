# Energy Transition Plan Includes Actions to Expand Batteries and Biofuels Through 2029

The National Energy Transition Plan (PNTE) from the Lula government (PT) foresees measures to be implemented by 2029 so that the country expands the use of renewable sources and advances in reducing greenhouse gas emissions in the coming decades. The document, submitted for public consultation on Wednesday (29), brings together more than one hundred actions. Among them are incentives for energy storage—such as the battery auction promised by the government—increased use of biofuels, expansion of electrification in transportation, and promotion of carbon capture technologies.

The plan does not establish mandatory targets, but defines guidelines to guide public policies and investments in the sector. According to projections, the country could reach 2055 with up to 81% of its energy matrix composed of renewable sources and up to 99% of clean electricity generation, in addition to reducing petroleum dependence. Today, 51% of Brazil's energy matrix comes from renewable sources. In the electricity system, 91%.

According to the document, the Brazilian energy transition should seek to "reduce emissions from the energy sector, but also meet the large expansion of energy demand with security, reliability, and resilience to the effects of climate change, as well as combat inequalities in access to energy services."

The plan still specifically cites the need to reduce dependence on fossil fuels, an agenda defended by President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva during COP30, the UN climate conference.

"Decarbonization efforts must be associated with guaranteeing energy security and energy justice. For this, the PNTE seeks to integrate existing public policies and regulatory and financing instruments, promoting a framework that organizes the energy transition process in a coordinated manner and directs the efforts of different government areas," the text concludes.

The projections also point to reducing petroleum dependence from 36% to up to 6%—with an increase of up to 3.8% per year in GDP (Gross Domestic Product) growth.

However, despite this direction, as Folha reported, the wind and solar generation sectors project a reduction of R$ 40 billion in investments for this year, especially in light of system saturation. This occurs mainly due to curtailment, a procedure that cuts electricity production when the grid approaches collapse due to overload risk. This process already causes Brazil to waste the equivalent of one Belo Monte power plant and happens because there is an excess of energy caused by renewables.

Generation from wind and solar sources varies during the day, depending on sun and wind. Thus, at certain times, when production from these sources is high, curtailment is carried out to prevent system overload. One of the PNTE's guidelines, expanding storage systems such as batteries, is identified as one of the main solutions for this bottleneck, as it allows excess energy to be stored and used during periods of lower production. The Lula government promised to hold a battery auction to address this bottleneck, which has not yet occurred. At the end of 2025, the Ministry of Mines and Energy opened the public consultation for the tender notice.

The plan also advocates for increased use of biofuels. Currently, Brazilian fuel guidelines mandate a mixture of 30% ethanol in gasoline and 15% biodiesel in diesel. The agribusiness sector is one of the main advocates for the government to increase these percentages—which could be on the agenda in May, in a meeting of the CNPE (National Council of Energy Policy).

The PNTE also mentions carbon capture technologies. The procedure, still little explored on a large scale, consists of trapping greenhouse gases in air pockets underground, for example, instead of releasing them into the atmosphere.
