European Commission Proposes Excluding Leather from Deforestation Law

04 May 2026 · 07:45 PM

# European Commission Proposes Excluding Leather from Deforestation Law

The European Commission proposed excluding leather from the list of products subject to the EU's anti-deforestation law (EUDR) while simultaneously including instant coffee and palm oil derivatives within the legislation's scope. The decision was announced on Monday (4th), and the European Parliament or Council can still reject the changes. The proposal will receive public contributions until June 1st.

Jessika Roswall, commissioner for Environment, Water Resilience and Competitive Circular Economy, stated that the alterations represent a simplification that should reduce annual compliance costs for companies by approximately 25%. "Our efforts are entirely focused on facilitating implementation in the most efficient manner possible. Now, we all need to work toward a successful entry into force of the law by the end of 2026, keeping in mind its important objective of reducing deforestation globally," she declared.

The EUDR (European Union Deforestation Regulation) requires companies in various sectors, such as meat and soy, to prove the absence of deforestation in their supply chains to access the European market. The Commission maintained the prediction that the law will enter into force starting December 30th, with staggered deadlines.

Socio-environmental organizations criticize the exclusion of leather and claim the change limits the law's scope. For Andrea Carta, legal strategist at European Greenpeace, the alteration is unacceptable and opens a loophole to please fashion brands. "The leather supply chain continues to be a factor in forest destruction just as much as it was when it was included in the law," he said.

Daniela Ikawa from the NGO Climate Rights International states that Brazilian exporters will continue to be impacted by European laws. "Even if the European Commission's proposal to remove leather from the Deforestation Regulation is approved, other EU laws already establish that companies must trace where cattle comes from and prove there is no connection to slave labor, other human rights abuses, or deforestation," she states.

Products outside the EUDR's scope will still be monitored by the European Union's forced labor regulation, which should enter into force in December 2027. Climate Rights International says companies will still need to link their supply chains to specific locations where cattle was raised and present reliable proof that products are deforestation-free. "In the case of cattle chains in Brazil, this continues to be particularly challenging, since animals frequently pass through several farms before slaughter, and many companies still do not have the capacity to identify or monitor indirect suppliers," the NGO states.
