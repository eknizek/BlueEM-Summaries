# Mercosur-EU Agreement Reshapes Wine Market and Benefits High-End Imports

The trade agreement between Mercosur and the European Union, which entered its implementation phase on May 1st, is already reorganizing the Brazilian wine market even before its financial effects fully materialize. Brazilian importers are reviewing their portfolios and accelerating negotiations, while European producers are stepping up their offensive in the country, according to Felipe Galtaroça, CEO of Ideal.BI consulting firm, which specializes in the sector, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

According to the executive, the impact will occur on different fronts and at different paces.

For sparkling wines valued at US$8 per liter or higher, equivalent to US$6 per 750ml bottle, which currently reach Brazilian consumers for approximately R$150 (US$30), the import tariff was immediately zeroed. For still wines (those without carbonation), the reduction will be gradual over eight years, but strategic market repositioning is already underway. The sector ended 2025 moving R$21.1 billion, a 9% increase over 2024, according to data from the consulting firm itself.

Galtaroça's macroeconomic assessment points to a favorable scenario for those bringing European wine to Brazil. "The combination of tax relief and real appreciation creates an optimistic scenario for Brazilian importers," he said.

According to him, this context allows importers to rebuild margins historically squeezed by exchange rates and inflation, while simultaneously offering more competitive prices to consumers, which tends to boost consumption especially of higher value-added items.

The caveat lies at the base of the market. "The entry-level wine segment [costing up to R$50] remains pressured by loss of purchasing power and high interest rates, which restrict access to credit," said Galtaroça. In other words, the virtuous effect of tariff reduction does not reach the cheaper shelf uniformly.

The segment receiving immediate tax relief is numerically small but relevant in value. Sparkling wines above US$8 per liter represent approximately 6% of total import revenue in Brazil, and the category has high concentration: Italy, France, and Spain account for more than 80% of sales.

It is in this premium segment that tariff opening translates into immediate price movement, with advantages for Champagnes and products such as Cavas and Crémants, which can gain direct competitive margin over competitors from the Old and New Worlds.

Galtaroça also evaluates the agreement's effect on Brazilian producers, which he divides into three layers.

The first is sparkling wines, a segment in which the national sector secured a 12-year window in negotiations before the tariff on similar European entry-level products falls to zero. "Protection for national producers proved highly assertive in one of their main segments: sparkling wines, in which they hold 83% market share," he said.

The second layer is table wines, produced with American and hybrid grapes, which account for more than half the volume sold in the country. For this category, the agreement's impact tends to be null, according to Galtaroça, due to the product's sensory characteristics, marked by high residual sugar, and its cultural connection with Brazilian consumers.

The third layer is the most delicate and counterintuitive. In fine wines, national production accounts for only one in every ten bottles sold in the country. "In this scenario, price pressure intensifies direct competition mainly with Chilean and Argentine wines," said Galtaroça.

The assessment is that Brazilian fine wine producers, although affected by the agreement, feel the squeeze mainly in competition with South American neighbors, who currently dominate the segment and compete for the same price space.

The portfolio reorganization already underway among importers suggests that this competitive reshuffling should solidify well before the end of the eight-year cycle planned for total tariff reduction, the executive said.
