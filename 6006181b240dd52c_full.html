# Movement launched in response to insolvency cartel allegations, rejects system stigmatization

Amid the debate over allegations of an "insolvency cartel," the Colombian Federation of Conciliation Centers (Fedecentros) and Peaceful Alternatives for Conflict Management (APG) launched the #LetUsProtectTheSecondChance movement to defend the legitimacy of the mechanism.

The initiative emerges in response to statements from Asobancaria, which have called into question the functioning of the insolvency system for non-commercial natural persons, generating concern among sector operators.

"The debate needs data and best practices, not generalizations," the organizations stated, warning that the use of unsupported claims can erode confidence in a mechanism that is key to protecting the rights of those in financial crisis. In this sense, the movement seeks to convene public and private actors to a technical discussion based on evidence, rather than narratives that, according to its promoters, affect access to justice for people facing financial hardship.

## Official figures show low incidence of sanctions

One of the movement's main arguments is supported by figures from the Ministry of Justice and Law, through the SICAAC system, which registers 156 authorized centers to process insolvency cases in the country. Of these, all present normal functioning without current sanctions, except for a single center that has been sanctioned, representing only 0.64% of the sector total.

In disciplinary matters, the balance is also reduced. Out of a total of 18,728 cases processed in 2025, only three sanctioned lawyers, two mentioned centers, and three debtor cases were identified, equivalent to 0.016% of the annual total. According to the movement, these figures show that irregular cases are marginal compared to the system's operational volume, which questions the narrative of a structural or widespread problem.

## Growth of the system and social role of insolvency

The movement also highlights the sustained growth of the insolvency mechanism in Colombia, which has grown from 422 requests in 2016 to 18,728 in 2025, multiplying by 44 in less than a decade. This increase reflects the consolidation of the system as a tool for natural persons to reorganize their debts within the legal framework, avoiding scenarios of financial exclusion or extreme over-indebtedness.

"Behind every number is a person and their family who chose the legal path to fulfill their obligations," the organizations stated, emphasizing the social impact of the mechanism. In this context, they warned that stigmatizing the system could drive good-faith debtors away, affecting their access to legal solutions and weakening the social function of insolvency.

## Legal debate on the use of the term "cartel"

Another key point of the statement is legal precision regarding the use of the term "cartel," which, according to the organizations, does not have an explicit definition in Colombian law. They explained that its use is typically associated with the crime of conspiracy to commit crimes, contemplated in Article 340 of the Penal Code, which requires demonstrating the existence of a structured criminal organization with permanence and criminal purposes.

In this regard, they warned that to date, there are no final convictions confirming the existence of such a structure in the insolvency system, making the use of the term technically imprecise. "The use of the term 'insolvency cartel' without support from final convictions constitutes an anticipated legal qualification," they stated, warning about the reputational impact of such claims.

## Recognition of bad practices and call for regulation

Despite their defense of the system, Fedecentros and APG acknowledged the existence of bad practices that must be sanctioned, both by advisors who promote non-payment of obligations and by centers that lower standards to attract cases. "We are not defending those who act wrongly; we clearly point them out," affirmed María Mercedes García Perdomo, director of APG and president of Fedecentros, emphasizing that these behaviors affect the system's credibility.

However, she insisted that these cases do not justify generalized stigmatization. "We cannot accept the establishment of discourse that an 'insolvency cartel' exists when there is only one sanctioned center in the entire country," she stated.
