# Institutionality, Electoral Process, and Economy

Peru faces a crisis of moral authority in its political class, a crisis of leadership in its business sector, and a crisis of representativeness in its population. All of this indicates the poor institutionality in which we live. Without it, we cannot expect an efficient State, sustainable macroeconomics, and accountability processes that limit excesses in the public sector. Without it, the very result of the electoral process could result in an approach to suicide.

Our public sector is seriously ill. Attempting to solve problems of poverty, education, health, transportation, justice, and security while keeping it as it is, is a chimera, a deception. Only a comprehensive reengineering of the State will begin to lead us toward sensible public policies. For this, well-directed structural reforms could bring us closer to a solution. This is the first major step toward change.

On the other hand, until today our economic policy has been applied without considering the particularity of the structural problems surrounding us. We want to achieve fiscal sustainability without addressing the urgency of finding solutions to the informality issue; we want to expand the scope of public investment without reversing the world of inefficiency and corruption behind it; we want to decentralize without management capacity. Thus, our solutions have limited and fragile reach. Our economic policy needs to be shored up.

In another area, it is clear that our private sector is what actually finances the public sector. The minimum it should have under these circumstances is an adequate accountability mechanism in its favor. Since it does not have it, excesses are evident in the public sphere: laws are drafted that protect the same political class, budgets are violated, legal loopholes are created to justify any folly, institutions are mortgaged through their dependence on a congressional election of origin. Hence the urgency of creating a new accountability mechanism in favor of the private sector. An important point that even the private sector itself does not properly value today.

The three elements outlined constitute limitations that are reflected even in the potential result of an electoral process. As we estimated in an article recently published by this newspaper, during the last five years the economic cost caused by our political class is no less than US$120 billion. This could multiply several times if the next five-year term falls into the hands of ignorance, resentment, and the demagoguery of an extremist group. The generation of uncertainty, the demonization of private investment, the desire to generate more corruption by expanding the scope of the State, the possibility of stripping independence from our Central Bank emerges again as a potential result of the current electoral process. What a shame.

How much will our GDP grow this year? In the current scenario we could stop growing at one extreme or, on the other, begin growth above 4% for the next five years. Everything will depend on who obtains the final victory. An economy subject to poorly designed and executed electoral processes, to a legal framework that fragments the vote, that makes it obligatory and is accompanied by entities run by individuals of questionable reputation, clearly reflects the lack of institutionality and national governance. All this reflects poor State quality, economic policy without vision, and the absence of adequate accountability mechanisms in favor of the private sector. Let it be understood clearly: the electoral process itself and its results are a consequence of the highly deficient institutionality upon which we make decisions.
