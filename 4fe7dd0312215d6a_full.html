# EWZ: BlackRock's Brazilian ETF Attracts Largest Weekly Inflow in Over 15 Years

Nearly $1 billion in new resources flowed into BlackRock's exchange-traded fund (ETF) tracking Brazilian stocks last week — the largest volume in over 15 years — as investors positioned themselves ahead of a decisive year for the country's market. The iShares MSCI Brazil ETF (EWZ), with assets of $11.9 billion, recorded more than $966 million in inflows in the week ended April 17, its largest weekly flow since January 2011. The fund has already attracted over $3 billion in new resources in the year-to-date, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

Bets on falling interest rates and expectations of market-friendly policies following the presidential election in October have attracted investors to Brazilian stocks this year, even amid greater market volatility driven by conflict in the Middle East. High real interest rates and exposure to oil have also boosted assets in recent months.

"Brazil has become, in a way, a safe haven in this environment of greater risk," said Ola El-Shawarby, manager at VanEck. "The country is a commodity exporter and rich in energy, the Central Bank has initiated an easing cycle that should continue, and there have also been promising signals in the political scenario" ahead of the October presidential elections.

Recent polls showing senator Flávio Bolsonaro maintaining momentum in the race against President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva have sparked optimism about a market-friendly government. The 80-year-old president has seen a 10-point advantage disappear since December as persistent inflation and stagnant economic growth weigh on his approval ratings.

Resource inflows also occurred as hopes for a de-escalation of the Middle East war boosted risky assets globally last week, before a deadlock between Iran and the U.S. over the weekend. An MSCI index of emerging market stocks has accumulated gains of nearly 15% in April, after a double-digit drop in the previous month.

"We went through a stress test in March and, even in the midst of all this volatility, the flow continued," said Frederico Sampaio, co-head for Latin America at Templeton Global Investments. "That doesn't mean we can't have new noise or something happening again. We have to wait to have more conviction and more certainty."

Sampaio said he remains cautiously optimistic about the outlook, noting that the situation in the Middle East and its potential impact on Brazil's interest rate trajectory are key factors. With the spike in oil during the conflict, traders have reduced bets on a more intense easing cycle and now see the central bank cutting the Selic by around 225 basis points in total, below the 300 basis points projected at the end of February. Monetary authorities made the first cut, of 25 basis points, in March.

Foreign interest in Brazilian stocks tends to be more "selective," and further gains depend on corporate results and sector selection, rather than a continuation of broad-based gains, according to Axel Christensen, chief strategist at BlackRock for Latin America.

In other markets, inflows into emerging market ETFs listed in the U.S. — which invest in developing nations or specific countries — totaled $3.29 billion in the week ended April 17, compared to gains of $1.15 billion in the previous week, according to Bloomberg data. Year-to-date, inflows total $40.6 billion.
