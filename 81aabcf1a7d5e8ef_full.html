# Four Direct Routes from Lima Canceled Due to TUUA Transfer Fee, Says AETAI

As of March 29, four international route cancellations from Jorge Chávez International Airport (AIJCH) came into effect: Lima–Orlando, Lima–Curaçao, Lima–Florianópolis, and Lima–Tucumán, all operated by Latam Airlines Perú. These cancellations add to the Lima–Havana route, effective since March 7, and precede the anticipated Lima–Cancún cancellation (SKY Airline Perú) beginning Wednesday, March 31.

Two additional routes that were scheduled to launch in the first quarter of the year—Lima–Guatemala and Lima–Belo Horizonte—have also been canceled. The cause of all these cancellations: the implementation of the Unified Airport Use Fee (TUUA) for international transfers, in effect since December 7, 2025.

The cumulative tally since Lima Airport Partners (LAP) began charging this fee stands at eight international routes canceled at Jorge Chávez. For the Association of International Air Transport Companies of Peru (AETAI), this outcome confirms what the industry warned before the fee took effect: penalizing connecting passengers destroys the hub's logic.

AETAI explained that the charge of $11.86 per segment, equivalent to nearly $25 round-trip, makes Jorge Chávez the most expensive airport in the region for transfer passengers. This additional fee has no equivalent in Bogotá, Panama City, Mexico City, Santiago, or Buenos Aires—cities competing with Lima for air competitiveness in Latin America.

According to industry projections, if the international transfer TUUA remains in place, annual growth of international passengers in Lima will be limited to between 0% and 3% through 2041. Without the fee, the projection is 9%. The cumulative difference by 2041 equals 29.5 million passengers per segment.

Additionally, airlines receiving new aircraft are assigning those planes to competitive markets. If Lima loses this investment window, it will not recover it in the short term.

It should be noted that the Ministry of Transportation (MTC) confirmed that Addendum 9 to the Concession Contract—expected to be signed with LAP in May—only contemplates the elimination of the national transfer TUUA. The international fee, which is generating route cancellations and traffic redirection, will remain outside the agreement. The MTC has conditioned any decision on it to the results of an econometric study being conducted by the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), with a schedule extending through May.

The problem also has an unresolved legal dimension. Proturismo filed an amparo lawsuit against Ositran, MTC, and LAP requesting the annulment of the Sixth Addendum to the concession contract, signed in 2013 between the MTC and LAP, which originated the transfer TUUA without any impact analysis, and Ositran's resolution setting the fee amounts. However, weeks ago the Ninth Constitutional Court of Lima declared the lawsuit inadmissible, choosing to rule on procedural form rather than the substance of the matter.
