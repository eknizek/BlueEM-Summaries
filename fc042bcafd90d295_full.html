Major Generator Advocates for Battery Mandate in New Brazilian Solar Parks to Prevent Grid Curtailment

03 May 2026 · 06:00 PM

# Brazil Needs to Install Batteries with Solar Panels, Says Atlas

Atlas Renewable Energy, one of the country's largest energy generators, argues that Brazil should require new solar units in the country to be built already coupled with large battery systems. The excess energy generated during certain periods of the day is the biggest bottleneck for the renewable energy sector in the country, which deals daily with cuts defined by the ONS (National Electric System Operator). Atlas itself is one of the companies that decided to suspend investments in the country until the situation is resolved. The company, controlled by a fund linked to BlackRock giant, made no final investment decisions in Brazil last year and does not foresee any in 2026.

According to Atlas's commercial vice president, Luís Ballester, the federal government should follow Mexico's example and determine a minimum required share of batteries for these installations. In March of last year, the Mexican government established that all solar and wind plants need battery systems equivalent to 30% of their installed capacity, with a minimum discharge autonomy of three hours—the measure should add 574 MW (megawatts) of batteries by 2028.

For him, however, this requirement should be accompanied by other factors. The first is the regulation of these systems, known in the industry as BESS. The topic has been on the Aneel (National Electric Energy Agency) agenda since 2023, but has been stalled since August of last year amid requests for director reviews at the regulatory body. The main discussions at Aneel concern how taxation and remuneration of battery-owning companies will be handled. Additionally, there are questions about whether they will be considered an extension of renewable units or a separate asset capable of being dispatched by the ONS itself.

The second factor is taxation. According to Ballester, the taxes paid in Brazil for imports currently correspond to 70% of the original price of these systems, which come mainly from China, the country that leads manufacturing of this technology. "It is an extremely high tax burden. We have a good national industry, but it has limited production and competitiveness," says Ballester.

Reducing taxes on storage systems is seen as essential by the entire renewable energy sector—including the domestic battery industry, which depends on importing equipment, also subject to taxation. Within the solar and wind industry, there is an understanding that today, with the high tax burden and without specific remuneration for these systems, battery adoption does not compensate for lost revenue from energy cuts.

The current situation of energy cuts has caused the suspension of investments close to R$ 38.8 billion between 2025 and 2026, mainly in northern Minas Gerais and the Northeast, where most of the country's large solar and wind plants are located. According to Ballester, Atlas has between 20% and 25% of its energy cut, which, according to the report, generated losses of US$ 45 million last year. The company does not reveal its annual revenue.

Beyond the damage generated by the inability to sell cut energy, companies in the sector also suffer from the need to buy energy from other agents to sell to their customers. This happens because these companies have continuous supply contracts with large industries, for example—so when there is no own generation, it is necessary to contract energy from another generator to then pass it on to the customer.

As a way to alleviate losses, the sector intensified its influence in Brasília last year to guarantee reimbursement of part of the cuts. In November, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva signed a law approved by Congress that guarantees compensation for those cuts generated by the lack of transmission lines—estimated at approximately R$ 4 billion by Abrace, the association representing large energy consumers. In this case, cuts generated by excess energy were left out of the reimbursement. But, five months after the law's approval, the government has still not published the decree that regulates how the reimbursement will be made. "Moreover, the law also brought no future forecast, so it is far from being minimally good," says Ballester.
