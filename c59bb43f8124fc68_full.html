# Tamborine Raises $100K from Koinz for Payment Infrastructure

For those in the payment processing market who know traditional players, explaining what Tamborine does is easy: "We are the Vision of the 21st century," summarizes Marcio Silva, innovation chief at the startup. For those less familiar with the sector, simply know that the fintech is reinventing payment infrastructure from scratch, replacing legacy architecture and creating a lighter, more agile model designed to work in the cloud.

Founded by a technical team, Tamborine has just raised $100,000 from Koinz Capital to leverage go-to-market and develop its business area. To do this, the founders will count on the help of a board with heavyweight names: Fabricio Coutinho (former Itaú BBA and ex-CEO of TopPerformance), Jerson Prochnow (founder of FISCOSoft, acquired by Thomson Reuters), Regina Botter (former Visa and Webmotors), Cleber Ferraz (former Visa), Fabian (former Cora), and Takamura (former Revolut and ex-Mastercard).

The fintech's partners are Marcio Silva, an engineer with 35 years of IT project management experience; Marcio Teixeira (CTO), a doctorate from ITA with patents in Cryptography and Digital Security; and João Guimarães (CEO), who came from the financial market and is a former member of BMG Bank's Board of Directors.

After several years developing the technological foundation, they understood it was time to expand Tamborine's presence in the market as a processor and BIN Sponsor, which aims to make card issuance and processing infrastructure in the country more efficient.

Henrique Valicente, Acquisition Manager at Koinz Capital, explains that the fintech acts as a technological layer for issuers and ecosystem partners, reducing operational complexity and accelerating the launch of new products. "When we evaluated Tamborine, we saw a product with disruption potential in a traditional market, with superior efficiency and lower cost," affirms the investor.

The expectation is to use this capital to validate traction, gain scale, and prepare the company for a next round on more solid grounds, with a more consolidated valuation. "Any check that comes in now is to generate traction, business, and revenue," says Marcio.

## How Tamborine Works

The startup's thesis is to attack a structural market problem: dependence on legacy systems that, according to the founders, have been adapted over decades, with new layers added to respond to regulatory and technological changes. The result, they say, are heavy, expensive, and inflexible infrastructures. "These are software that have been adapting since the 90s. It becomes that tower of blocks where if you remove the one at the bottom, everything falls," says Marcio Silva.

Tamborine's proposal is to replace this model with proprietary architecture, built from scratch and cloud-native, which allows simpler integrations, lower operational costs, and more agility for issuers to launch products.

In practice, this means reducing barriers that, according to the company, still mark the traditional market. For example, lengthy integration processes, requirements for high volumes to operate, and difficulties migrating between processors. "These others have a migration process where you need to change your entire system to adapt to the new processor. At Tamborine, we can plug into the issuer much more easily," says Marcio.

One example used by the founders to illustrate the difference is in adapting old systems to the cloud. "It's like taking a truck and putting it on top of a Beetle to drive and saying you're now transporting with a Beetle," compares Marcio. "Some took systems designed for mainframes and put simulators of those environments in the cloud. That's heavy, adds cost, and creates complexity."

The criticism aligns with the startup's central proposal: not modernizing legacy systems, but replacing them. According to the founders, inspiration for creating the company came from observing movements like Nubank's, which developed its own infrastructure to reduce dependence on traditional processors. "We understood that there was room to redesign this layer from scratch," points out Marcio.

## Not Reverse Engineering

The founders emphasize that, despite comparisons with old systems, Tamborine was not built from adaptations or reverse engineering. The distinction is relevant because, according to the partners, the startup's proposal is not to modernize an existing system, but to rethink infrastructure with a different architecture. Instead of porting technologies designed for mainframe environments to the cloud, the company states it started from native cloud computing, modularity, and security premises.

According to João, this approach also allowed them to incorporate encryption mechanisms and protection against vulnerabilities from the base. "One of the advantages of building from scratch is not carrying inherited limitations. You can design security as part of the architecture, not as a layer added later," says the CEO.

The founders affirm this choice also appeared in the homologation process with card networks. According to the company, Tamborine has already been homologated by three card networks and received positive feedback on technical practices and integration. "We had cases where we sent test results and they asked if they were real, because nobody usually operated that way," says João.

With part of the team coming from research in cryptography and digital security, the startup says it has incorporated its own protection and defense mechanisms in the platform architecture. "Simplicity ends up leaving fewer openings for something to go wrong. We have fewer weak points because the system is simpler," summarizes Marcio.
