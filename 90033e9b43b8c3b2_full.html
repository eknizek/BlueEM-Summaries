# 2026 Tariffs Increase Pressure on Colombian Companies in Increasingly Volatile Global Environment

Colombian companies face mounting competitive pressure due to global tariff volatility, according to KPMG. In its latest analysis, the firm warns that the 2026 commercial environment requires constant adjustments.

KPMG Economics' 2026 Trade Outlook report indicates that global trade is experiencing a phase of continuous disruptions, comparable to a "hydra" effect. Each tariff adjustment generates new complexities that demand immediate business response. Although a new wave of trade tensions has been recorded since April 2025, global flows have shown resilience. However, this adaptive capacity implies accelerated changes. Companies are reconfiguring operations to respond to more aggressive trade policies.

In this context, tariff pressure intensifies. The effective U.S. tariff rate is estimated to reach approximately 13% in 2026, quadrupling the levels observed at the beginning of 2025. The impact is not limited to costs. KPMG identifies that uncertainty is affecting business investment first. Companies are postponing key decisions despite demand remaining relatively stable.

This is compounded by faster retaliation dynamics between trading partners, which elevates operational risks. Trade countermeasures increase regulatory burdens and tighten rules of origin in global markets. The third front identified is structural change toward operational resilience, which redefines supply chains. Companies are abandoning the just-in-time model to prioritize diversified sourcing. This shift involves adopting strategies like nearshoring and higher inventory levels, with direct effects on costs and planning. Operational efficiency yields to stability in a more uncertain environment.

For Colombia, the impact of this volatility is especially sensitive due to its dependence on imported inputs and external markets. Tariff changes affect prices, competitiveness, and business supply continuity in the country. Additionally, the global environment intensifies competition to attract investment, as companies seek stability. Investors prioritize countries with clear rules and predictable commercial frameworks for operations.

Recent tensions in the region demonstrate the speed of these episodes, as in the case of the dispute between Colombia and Ecuador. Trade conflicts force companies to review contracts, logistics routes, and international exposure. In this scenario, tariff policy acquires a strategic role for regional competitiveness, especially in Latin America.

The analysis warns that discussion should not focus solely on raising or lowering tariffs, but on their design. "Focused and temporary protection can support strategic industries, but generalized increases can raise input costs and weaken export competitiveness."

In line with this, the recommendation points to reducing barriers on goods critical to production. Despite the risks, the context also opens opportunities derived from tensions between major global economies. "Trade frictions between the United States, China, and the European Union are generating disruptions, but also creating opportunities."

From a business perspective, the response must be more structured and anticipatory regarding growing commercial risk. Companies must evaluate exposure, critical suppliers, and vulnerable margins in real time. Additionally, scenario planning becomes a central element in corporate strategic decision-making. Preparing for tariff and regulatory changes will be decisive for sustaining operations in global markets.

Strengthening operational resilience appears as a cross-cutting axis in this business adaptation process. Supply diversification and flexible logistics gain relevance in the face of volatility.

In this new environment, Colombia has the possibility of positioning itself as an attractive destination within reconfigured global chains. "Latin America, and particularly Colombia, can position itself as a reliable alternative within reconfigured supply chains if it manages to offer predictable trade policy, efficient customs, and greater regulatory certainty."

According to the analysis, competition between countries is redefined around structural factors rather than trade barriers. "In a more protectionist world, countries will compete less through tariff walls and more through productivity, resilience, and integration into higher-value global markets."
