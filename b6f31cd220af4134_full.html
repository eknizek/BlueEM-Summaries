# Monashees Advances With Its 11th Fund Targeting R$1 Billion

The venture capital manager Monashees is close to completing the first closing of its eleventh fund, which aims to raise US$200 million (approximately R$1 billion), half of its previous fund, according to NeoFeed's reporting. Monashees XI is expected to raise between US$80 million and US$100 million at this stage and already counts on some anchor investors who participated in the previous fund. Among them are the IFC (International Finance Corporation), the private sector arm of the World Bank, and BID Lab, the innovation and venture capital arm of the Inter-American Development Bank Group (BID).

According to documents published by these institutions, the IFC will invest US$11.2 million in Monashees' new fund – the investment has already been approved by the investment committee and now awaits final approval, which should happen in May. BID Lab, in turn, will contribute US$6.5 million.

"No manager likes to raise a new vehicle smaller than the previous one," says a venture capital market investor. "But there is much global instability, wars, and investors are being selective with high interest rates." However, a source close to Monashees says the decision to reduce the fund size was intentional and strategic to maximize returns for LPs (limited partners, as shareholders are called).

"Even smaller, it's a size that allows us to lead rounds and is ideal for opportunities over the next three years," the source states. Monashees' last fundraising took place at the end of 2021, when it raised US$700 million in two funds: Monashees X, focused on early stage, and Expansion II, for growth, but which invests only in portfolio startups. The values of each vehicle were not disclosed at the time, but NeoFeed found that Monashees X raised approximately US$400 million and Expansion II raised US$300 million.

The manager still has capital to invest in at least three more startups from that vintage before beginning to deploy capital from the new fund. Now, Monashees will focus on early stage. The investment thesis will not change compared to other funds, but the intention is to strengthen its bet on AI, a topic on the agenda of all venture capital investors. According to a BID Lab document that approved the investment, Monashees XI "will invest in up to 30 technology-based startups that address market inefficiencies through technological solutions with scalability potential and regional impact."

It continues by adding that the "fund will follow a sector-agnostic approach, with emphasis on fintech, edtech, healthtech, retailtech, Software as a Service (SaaS) and emerging technologies, including Artificial Intelligence (AI) and climate technology." Despite being smaller, the fund should not reduce its check size. The target is to lead or co-lead investments in rounds ranging from seed to Series B.

"Today, because of AI, startups have a lower need for capital to scale," says a source close to Monashees. With offices in São Paulo and Mexico City, Monashees has just opened a branch in San Francisco, United States, to be closer to the cradle of American innovation and closely monitor artificial intelligence transformations. According to a source close to Monashees, the new fund should strengthen a thesis that was already gaining ground in the manager's strategy: Latinos who are entrepreneuring in the United States.

Currently, Monashees already has startups with this profile, such as Moises, Tractian, Slope, Simetrik, and Vortexa. "They noticed a greater movement of Latinos to Silicon Valley due to the AI trend. And with that they assembled a team to support and invest in these cases," says this source.

The study "Brazil Tech Diaspora in The USA" by Endeavor, published exclusively by NeoFeed, mapped 200 Brazilians in the American tech ecosystem, of which more than 140 were entrepreneurs, and showed how AI, abundant capital, and global ambition were driving this new diaspora.

## Pioneer of Venture Capital

Founded in 2005 by Eric Acher and Fabio Igel, Monashees is one of the pioneers of the Brazilian venture capital market. Throughout its trajectory, it has invested in 150 companies (including 12 unicorns) in Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, and Mexico, through 12 funds (10 of them early stage and two growth). Approximately 90 of them are still active. The portfolio includes companies such as Rappi, Wellhub (formerly Gympass), Creditas, Loggi, Ualá, and ID Wall.

The 99 app, Brazil's first unicorn, was part of Monashees' portfolio, as was Nubank. Méliuz and Enjoei, the first two startups to go public in Brazil in the wave of 2020 and 2021, were also investments by the venture capital manager. However, as is inherent to venture capital assets, whose risk is quite high, especially in early stage, the manager also has investments that did not work out. The noisiest of these investments was in social commerce startup Facily, which raised over US$500 million and was valued at US$1.1 billion. In 2023, Monashees wrote off this bet, according to NeoFeed's reporting.

The last batch of investments was also at the height of the venture capital market, in which valuations were inflated, which has led to a correction in valuations for many startups. "The last vintages of most venture capital managers suffered a lot. There is a valuation correction and there is a shortage of exits," says an investor. "They are going through the 'J curve'. We'll know if it was good or not further down the line."

The "J Curve" is a theoretical concept in which a venture capital investment has a cycle similar to the letter J. It has an initial downward event and then recovers and accelerates, surpassing the initial starting point.

## Fundraising Returns to Market

Monashees is the latest manager to advance in its fundraising, a positive sign for the venture capital market, which experienced what became known as "the startup winter" after 2022. On one hand, with market correction, it became good to invest, and those who had money, called dry powder, had the opportunity to make good deals. On the other, with high interest rates and disappointment with the 2020 and 2021 vintage, many investors began to view venture capital with suspicion. In short, it became much harder to raise money for new funds. There are more than 40 venture capital managers in the Brazilian market raising resources, according to NeoFeed's reporting.
