# Selic Rate Cut Surprised No One, But Perhaps It Should Have

The Selic rate cut to 14.5% surprised no one. But it well could have. A Martian economist interested in Brazilian monetary policy might have difficulty understanding this reduction.

Until the war with Iran, the inflation scenario was mild, almost boring. But everything suddenly changed. Market expectations for 2026 inflation, which had been falling since July 2025, jumped from 3.9% to 4.9%. The forecast for 2027 also rose, albeit less, with the implicit assumption that the conflict will be short—something no one can guarantee because we're talking about Trump.

We could explain to the Martian that in Brazil interest rates fall when inflation expectations rise because there is a time lag in the effect of high interest rates. And that interest rates were already excessively stratospheric. But it would also be worthwhile to discuss the proverbial inefficiency of monetary policy.

Decree No. 3,088 of June 21, 1999, which established the inflation targeting regime, established that "it is the responsibility of the Central Bank of Brazil to execute the necessary policies to comply with the targets." Policies, in the plural. In practice over these years, the monetary authority is content to set the Selic rate, after intense solitary reflection.

There are several factors that undermine the ability of interest rates to tame inflation, starting, of course, with complicit fiscal policy. But it's not just that. Credit expansion, for example, also weighs. The total loans of the National Financial System exceeded 7 trillion reais in March, with growth of 9.6% in 12 months. Credit for families (4.5 trillion reais) grew 10.9%, bringing income commitment to 29.7%, a record in the historical series. Loan concessions rose 8.9% in the 12 months through March 2026, above nominal GDP growth, estimated at 7.4%.

Driven by the government, federal banks have been leading the expansion of credit portfolios, in the opposite direction of the containment intended by high interest rates. Over the past two years, through December 2025, the credit portfolios of Caixa Econômica and Banco do Brasil grew, respectively, 23% and 26% (compared, for example, to 8.3% growth in Bradesco's credit operations). The objective, clearly, is to counteract the effect of monetary policy, thereby adding fuel to the interest rate fire.

The most difficult thing, however, will be making the Martian understand that the Central Bank itself stimulated credit expansion by reducing the compulsory deposit requirement for savings accounts, as it did in October 2025, or when, more recently, it failed to take advantage of a possible contraction in banks' lending capacity by allowing the use of compulsory deposits to recapitalize the FGC.

Oddities. Definitely, Brazil is not for Martians.
