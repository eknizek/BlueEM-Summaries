# Brazil Will Continue to Bear Fuel Price Impact from War

The average price of diesel in Brazil stopped rising from the week ending March 28 through the week ending April 11, according to the most recent public data from the National Petroleum Agency (ANP). Gasoline prices remained virtually stable. At the national average, it is worth noting that the diesel increase caused by the war was nonetheless the largest since the ANP began publishing weekly price statistics. Still, the magnitude of the increase was half what was seen in the United States. Upward pressures remain.

The risk of fuel shortages has disappeared from the news cycle (and from reality?), but it is a problem in many places around the planet, from Asia to Europe, with more immediate problems regarding fuel for aircraft. We have forgotten about the subject because so-called public opinion has turned its attention to family debt and the BBB finals, for example.

What happened? To use a euphemism, there was moderation from Petrobras, which says it is not losing money. The reduction in taxes must have had some effect. Pressure from the federal government on distributors may have intimidated these companies. Importantly, the American reference prices for Brazil also calmed down. The journalist hears various explanations, but has no means of independently verifying what occurred — there is a lack of public data.

Will this relative calm last? As has been said in a tedious but inevitable way, it depends on what becomes of this disaster that Donald Trump created in Iran. Or of this "dirty" ceasefire, with American blockade, an act of economic warfare, or with Iranian shots against merchant ships. On Wednesday (22), they fired at the Euphoria, the Epaminondas, and the Francesca. Yes, these are the names of the vessels, a drop of the picturesque in this sea of horrors.

Furthermore, the calm depends on the pace of reopening the Strait of Hormuz and what will be possible to produce in oil and gas, given the destruction from bombings. Despite appearances, the cooler war reporting and the animation of those trading big money in the United States, this crisis will go far, even as it cools down.

Here, it is necessary to know whether Petrobras will withstand the pressure of prices and whether the federal government will extend subsidies — it is unlikely that Lula 3 would risk dealing with this before attempting Lula 4's election bid.

From the week ending February 21 (the one before the war began) through the week ending April 11, the latest public data available, the national average price of diesel increased 24.3%. In seven-week periods, this was the largest increase since 2004, the beginning of the regulatory agency's data series. Only in September 2023 was there a similar jump. Part of the taxes on fuel returned to being charged; Russia complicated the sale of its cheap diesel.

It is worth noting: the return of tax collection. It will happen again, perhaps in 2027. Reminder: the price of American diesel as a reference for Brazil increased 55% in the same period. Gasoline prices increased less, at the national average, 7.4%. In the American reference market, the increase was 49.9%.

In summary, someone is paying this difference in price variations — Petrobras, other oil exporters, the federal treasury and those of states that adhered to the subsidy. The so-called gap between the international reference price and the domestic price has certainly decreased significantly, but it is still relevant. The bill from the war is far from being closed, and it will cost someone here.
