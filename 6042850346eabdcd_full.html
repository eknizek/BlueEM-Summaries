# Central Bank Slowed Reserve Purchases but Economy Ministry Secured US$850 Million for Debt Repayment

In parallel, the Central Bank added its fourth day of more limited reserve purchases, while the Ministry of Economy managed to secure around US$850 million in the local market for upcoming debt payments.

Friday and the first days of this week showed higher-than-usual volumes in the foreign exchange market, which seemed to indicate that this demand for foreign currency was explained by some "closing" of carry trade operations. In other words: funds that took advantage of the peso rate with a calm exchange rate and returned to the dollar to collect profits in hard currency.

It was one of the explanations found by the EcoGo consulting firm for the currency movements of recent days: "After the sharp compression in the Treasury curve in both fixed rates and those indexed to the CER that has been operating since mid-February with a declining dollar, incentives aligned to close positions," they indicated.

The fact that the Central Bank, during those same days, had less buying participation in the market led analysts to think that this high volume of operations was mainly from the private sector and that the BCRA did not want to add pressure to the exchange rate value.

By comparison: in the last four trading sessions, the monetary authority acquired US$263 million. In the four business days before that, purchases had totaled US$665 million.

The Ministry of Economy, meanwhile, accelerated its objective of adding dollars to its account, with debt maturities arriving in the coming months. First will be the International Monetary Fund, with a payment expected for next week, and in July with private bondholders.

Between Tuesday and this Wednesday, the Treasury Department awarded almost US$850 million in two bonds: Bonar 2027 and Bonar 2028. These are public Treasury securities denominated in dollars and paid in hard currency.

This week the auction was expanded to seek more foreign currency in the local market. Previously, the Economy Ministry had planned an initial round with limits of US$250 million for each bond on the first day of placement and another US$100 million more as a cap the following day. This week it raised the ceiling of the first auction to US$350 million. As a result, it collected US$848 million.

The bond that failed to reach the maximum quota is Bonar 2028, which matures after Javier Milei's term ends. It also has a higher interest rate. While the 2027 bond was issued at 5.1% annual in dollars, the 2028 bond was issued at 8.7% annual.

"The decision to expand the amount to be awarded proved successful and came in a context where the Treasury faces a payment to the International Monetary Fund of around US$796 million next week," noted Portfolio Personal Inversiones (PPI). "Although it should be taken into account that the Government expects to unblock the disbursement scheduled for May, which is approximately US$1.045 billion," they concluded.

For its part, the Outlier consulting firm interpreted that the rate at which the dollar bond came out "is slightly above the rate of the previous auction," which would show that "although the amount to be issued was completed, demand may be declining."
