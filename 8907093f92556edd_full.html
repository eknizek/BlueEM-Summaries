# Facing credit scarcity, Argentine SMEs diversify their financing sources: what they are and how to access them

In the Argentine context where access to financing continues to represent a challenge for the private sector in general, SMEs are exploring credit alternatives beyond the traditional lines offered by banks. Driven by the need to expand their sources of resources and supported by the facilities that technology provides today, an increasing number of small and medium-sized enterprises are turning to instruments offered by non-financial entities regulated by the Central Bank (BCRA) that intermediate with the capital market, such as ALyC (Settlement and Compensation Agents) and fintech platforms.

At the same time, the use of guarantees granted by Reciprocal Guarantee Societies (SGR) is gaining ground, which allow companies to access better conditions in terms of interest rates and terms. The Central Bank regulates non-financial entities that offer SMEs access to financing.

In this regard, statistics account for this situation. In the first two months of this year, financing obtained by SMEs through the local capital market grew 47% in real terms compared to the same period in 2025, according to official data from the National Securities Commission (CNV). Between March 2025 and February 2026, the increase had been 13% compared to March 2024-February 2025. Both figures show that small and medium-sized enterprises are increasingly turning to the capital market each year to discount promissory notes and post-dated checks (direct and guaranteed), among other instruments they use.

Regarding the search for SGR guarantees, which function as a complement to credit in general (whether from banks or other sources), the figures also show an increase. According to the latest data from the Argentine Chamber of Guarantee Societies and Funds (Casfog), the number of SMEs assisted with guarantees rose to 62,116 between January and September 2025, compared to 41,000 in the same period of 2024. Of the assisted firms, 77% are micro-enterprises, 18% are small enterprises, and 5% are medium-sized enterprises. By sector, 31% of SMEs that turned to an SGR belong to commerce, 24% to services, and 22% to industry.

"Although banks continue to be a natural source to which SMEs turn to obtain credit, financing options from ALyC and other non-financial but BCRA-regulated entities have grown in recent times, broadening the menu of financial instruments from which companies can choose," says Irene Alfiz, Strategic Planning Director of LUC, one of the digital platforms that connects SMEs with credit providers.

Among those tools that are now more within reach thanks to digital innovation are SGR guarantees, which are key to reducing SMEs' funding costs and enhancing the virtuous effects of credit. According to the same CNV data, in February last year post-dated guaranteed checks were discounted in the capital market at an average rate of 37.6%. A substantially lower figure compared to other alternatives such as overdrafts (averaging 54.9%).

In this same vein, Alfiz highlights that "one of the advantages offered by platforms like LUC, in addition to connecting SMEs with the available credit supply that entities have, is that it streamlines the procedures that a company must carry out to access funds." And concludes: "The documentation burden is done only once, from the profile that the company has on the platform. And each firm can carry out transactions and interact with different credit providers to find the option that best suits their needs."

## Financing for micro-enterprises: IFC of the World Bank joins with CMF Bank

The International Finance Corporation (IFC), a member of the World Bank Group, announced in recent days a new investment in CMF Bank S.A. with the objective of expanding financing to micro, small and medium-sized enterprises (MiPyMEs) in Argentina.

"This initiative will contribute to closing the financing gaps faced by MiPyMEs, which play a central role in economic growth and job generation in the country," it was reported. The IFC's $25 million financing will allow CMF Bank to expand its credit portfolio to MiPyMEs, strengthening the availability of medium-term financing in a market characterized by limited access to credit.

"The operation will improve the bank's ability to provide sustainable financial services, supporting private investment, productivity and employment," it was noted in a statement. According to the IFC, access to financing remains restricted in Argentina, where credit to the private sector represents only 12 percent of GDP, one of the lowest levels in the region.
