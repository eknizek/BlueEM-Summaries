The "Super Peso" Under Pressure: Argentina's Government Faces Trade-off Between Economic Reactivation and Currency Control

26 April 2026 · 11:30 PM

# The "Super Peso" Under Pressure: The Government Between Reactivating the Economy or Containing the Dollar

After several weeks of decline, the dollar "revived" in the last five trading sessions, still in a context of greater supply of foreign currency in the financial account and agricultural liquidation that picked up pace. On Friday, the retail exchange rate closed at $1,420 in banks, while the wholesale rate closed near $1,400, representing a jump of $35 in recent sessions. There are two factors that can explain this incipient dollar increase, even in a context where the Argentine peso has accumulated a real appreciation of more than 12% so far in 2026.

On one hand, the persistence of Central Bank purchases that collected US$745 million in the last week, bringing the month's accumulated stock to over US$2.3 billion. This allowed gross reserves to close above US$46 billion again, a weekly increase of nearly US$400 million. "Without debt payments, we understand that the limited increase in reserves is a correlate of the reduction in reserve requirements and the fall in gold (-2.9%)," indicated consultancy LCG. "Agricultural supply as the record corn harvest begins to be liquidated, corporate and provincial placements (Chubut) added to renewed dynamism in dollar loans are currently nourishing the supply in the foreign exchange market," they added.

According to estimates from Quantum Finanzas consultancy, with retail inflation of 9.5% in the first quarter, real appreciation against the dollar was 12.2%. Argentina is not an isolated case, although its currency appreciation is the highest in the region: in the same period, the Brazilian real appreciated 7.3% in real terms, the Colombian peso 5.5%, and the Mexican peso 2.0%. In April, the global dollar depreciated much more against Latin American currencies than against the Argentine peso: 4.3% against the Brazilian real, 3.4% against the Mexican peso, and only 0.4% against the Argentine peso.

The "super peso" in turn allowed the Central Bank to deepen its purchases in the foreign exchange market, a mechanism that in a way puts a floor under that appreciation. In the local market, currency calm was also tied to lower dollarization appetite, guaranteed in part by the attractiveness provided by high interest rates in pesos. However, these days there is doubt in the City about whether the "making interest rates" strategy will continue to be profitable in the coming weeks.

"During the week the exchange rate woke up and even more supports our fears of increases," commented Outlier consultancy, which days earlier had recommended its clients "close the carry." "We still don't believe exchange rate increases are large enough to justify returning to the carry, so for all profiles, we continue recommending staying in other assets and mainly dollarized. Around $1,400 we can see greater government defense, so we'll be attentive in the coming sessions," they anticipated.

Juan Manuel Franco from Grupo SBS explained: "In the midst of peak harvest season, the streak should continue, supported also by hydrocarbon exports, with an energy balance that set a record in March, without the full effect of oil prices following the war in the Middle East." At the same time, Franco warned: "In any case, some points to monitor in the foreign exchange market come from the peso market, with negative rates in real terms."

"We believe this level of real rate could have been sought to attempt some reactivation in the more lagging sectors of the real economy, but the potential impact on the exchange rate will need to be monitored, even in a context where flows seem to be playing in Argentina's favor," added the economist.

The question analysts are asking is whether the interest rate cut will be passed on to credit costs, so as to boost activity, or will again turn investors to demand currency hedging. "To the extent that dollar supply diminishes, positions in pesos will be reduced and will rotate to the exchange rate, anticipating price increases or will demand a higher return to stay in pesos and real interest rates will rise," forecasted IEB, while explaining that currently there is no increase in demand for real money, and that demand is conditioned by the slowdown in activity.
