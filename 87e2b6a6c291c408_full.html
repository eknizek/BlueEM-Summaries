# Peru Secures $37.5 Million from Green Climate Fund for Environmental Strategy

Peru, within the framework of the Natural Heritage of Peru Initiative (PdP), consolidated its environmental strategy against climate change by securing $37.5 million from the Green Climate Fund, the main international financial mechanism dedicated to this cause. This achievement has been driven by the Ministry of Environment (MINAM), the National Service of Natural Protected Areas by the State (Sernanp), and its allies.

The financing is part of a total package of $74.5 million aimed at strengthening the management of 25 natural protected areas and 8 buffer zones in the Amazon, directly benefiting more than 33,000 people, mainly from indigenous peoples.

The announcement was made during the 2026 Meeting of Heads of Natural Protected Areas, which brings together those responsible for the country's 78 protected natural areas (ANP), highlighting the role of this financing in closing historical gaps in the sustainability of the conservation system.

"Today Peru becomes an example for the international community of what can be achieved after years of dialogue and joint construction. This financing from the Green Climate Fund is the result of more than seven years of sustained work between the State, international cooperation, and communities," affirmed Vice Minister of Strategic Development of Natural Resources, Carmen Inés Vegas Guerrero.

The project "Natural Heritage of Peru – Amazon and Climate" will allow intervention in 15.8 million hectares, almost 18% of the national Amazon, prevent the emission of 1.8 million tons of CO₂, and strengthen the climate resilience of ecosystems and communities.

"Peru is demonstrating that conservation is not a cost, but a strategic investment for development," stated Sernanp executive president José Carlos Nieto Navarrete. He added that the main challenge is managing external threats to protected areas and integrating local populations: "We have decided to conserve with the people, converting these spaces into engines of local development and climate resilience."

The model incorporates financial sustainability mechanisms over 25 years, such as sustainable tourism, ecosystem compensation, and MERESE, positioning protected natural areas as platforms for generating economic and social value.

"What Peru is achieving reflects institutional maturity that is uncommon in the region, with a model where the State, communities, and cooperation work in an articulated manner from the central level to the territory, positioning the country as a benchmark that others are already beginning to follow," said Roberto Troya, senior vice president and director for Latin America and the Caribbean of WWF.

From the territory, indigenous organizations highlighted the project's approach. "This achievement marks a fundamental change: indigenous peoples are not beneficiaries, we are partners in implementation. When financing reaches the territory, it translates into real results: better-prepared communities, forest protection, and improved living conditions," affirmed Oseas Barbarán Sánchez, president of the Confederation of Amazonian Nationalities of Peru (CONAP).

During the event, Esteban Morales Cama, representative of the Inter-ethnic Association for the Development of the Peruvian Rainforest (AIDESEP); Kurt Holle, WWF Peru country representative; Antón Willems Delanoy, executive director of Profonanpe, as well as directors and officials from Sernanp also participated.
