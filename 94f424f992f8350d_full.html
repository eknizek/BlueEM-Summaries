# Catalan Exclusivity: Importer Focuses on Boosting Luxury Spanish Wine in Brazil

A shipment of 600 bottles of wine may not seem like much to enthusiasts more passionate about the beverage. But it is a symbol of the exclusivity of Spanish labels now arriving in Brazil with a focus on more strongly promoting the country's luxury wines. With the arrival of these six hundred bottles from the winery Clos de l'Obac, from Priorato, and another similar package with labels from Bodegas Mestres, from Penedès, the importer Tanyno is trying to strengthen the presence of premium Catalan wines in Brazil.

"We built a portfolio focused on small beverage producers that seem special to us, that escape the ordinary. Even with few bottles, we want to expand knowledge about wines from this region," explained Guillaume Verger, founder of the importer, during an event in São Paulo.

The presence of producers in masterclasses about their wines in Rio and São Paulo helps illustrate two complementary narratives within Catalonia, as well as the pursuit of exclusivity. On one hand, Priorato, a mountainous region about 150 kilometers from Barcelona, which has established itself as one of Spain's major poles of red wines. On the other, Sant Sadurní d'Anoia, in Penedès, recognized as a capital of Cava and symbol of Spanish sparkling wines.

Clos de l'Obac closely works this idea of limited production of labels seeking international value. It produces only 50,000 bottles per year, with small batch distribution to 35 countries. "The charm of wine is small production. We don't make wines to get high scores, but to be exceptional and exclusive," said Guillem Pastrana, manager of the winery, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

Although viticulture in the region has historical roots, it was from the late 20th century onward that producers like Clos de l'Obac began to lead a renewal movement, raising quality standards and projecting the region onto the international stage. Founded by Carles Pastrana, the winery became one of the icons of this renaissance. According to Pastrana, this small production meant his family took time to see financial returns on the project. "For the first ten years, we didn't make money from our wine. But now there is recognition, and the higher price means we get a return," he said. In addition to the winery, his family owns a hotel in the Spanish region. Clos de l'Obac wines arrive in Brazil at prices between R$ 420 and R$ 1,200, according to Tanyno.

These are values similar to those of Bodegas Mestres sparkling wines, which are arriving in Brazil for the first time also with batches of just hundreds of bottles. "Our total production ranges between 120,000 and 140,000 bottles per year. Wines are sold especially in Spain, and only 30% of production is exported to 30 countries, always with a focus on gastronomy and harmonization in starred restaurants," said David Aura, director of the winery. Mestres maintains a focus on beverages of high complexity in the style of great Champagnes. Long before this style gained popularity, the house was a pioneer in producing brut nature style sparkling wines, which are drier. It also stood out for betting on long aging periods on the lees, a practice today associated with the production of high-quality cavas.

At a time when European producers are intensifying their approach to the Brazilian market to compensate for the global decline in beverage consumption, the bet on premium Catalan wines reinforces the focus on higher-priced labels.
