# Catalan Exclusivity: Importer Focuses on Driving Luxury Spanish Wine into Brazil

A shipment of 600 bottles of wine may not seem like much to wine enthusiasts, but it symbolizes the exclusivity of Spanish labels now arriving in Brazil with a focus on projecting the country's luxury wines more strongly. With the arrival of six hundred bottles from the Clos de l'Obac winery from Priorato, and another similar package with labels from Bodegas Mestres from Penedès, the importer Tanyno is attempting to reinforce the presence of premium Catalan wines in Brazil.

"We built a portfolio focused on small beverage producers that seem special to us, that stand out from the ordinary. Even with few bottles, we want to expand knowledge about wines from this region," explained Guillaume Verger, founder of the importer, during an event in São Paulo.

The presence of producers in masterclasses about their wines in Rio and São Paulo helps illustrate two complementary narratives within Catalonia, as well as the pursuit of exclusivity. On one hand, Priorato, a mountainous region about 150 kilometers from Barcelona, which has established itself as one of Spain's major red wine hubs. On the other, Sant Sadurní d'Anoia, in Penedès, recognized as a capital of Cava and symbol of Spanish sparkling wines.

Clos de l'Obac works closely with this idea of limited production of labels that seek international appreciation. It produces only 50,000 bottles per year, with small batches distributed to 35 countries. "The beauty of wine is small production. We don't make wines for high ratings, but to be exceptional and exclusive," said Guillem Pastrana, the winery's manager, in an interview with Bloomberg Línea.

Although viticulture in the region has historical roots, it was from the late 20th century onwards that producers like Clos de l'Obac began leading a renewal movement, raising quality standards and projecting the region onto the international stage. Founded by Carles Pastrana, the winery became one of the icons of this renaissance. According to Pastrana, this small production meant his family took a long time to see financial returns on the project. "For the first ten years, we didn't make money from our wine. But now there is recognition, and the higher price means we get a return," he said. In addition to the winery, his family owns a hotel in the Spanish region.

Clos de l'Obac wines arrive in Brazil with prices between R$ 420 and R$ 1,200, according to Tanyno. These are values similar to those of Bodegas Mestres sparkling wines, which are arriving in Brazil for the first time also with batches of just a few hundred bottles.

"Our total production ranges between 120,000 and 140,000 bottles per year. Wines are sold especially in Spain, and only 30% of production is exported to 30 countries, always with a focus on gastronomy and pairing in starred restaurants," said David Aura, director of the winery. Mestres maintains a focus on high-complexity beverages in the style of great Champagnes. Long before this style gained popularity, the house was a pioneer in producing brut nature-style sparkling wines, which are drier. It also stood out for investing in long aging periods on the lees, a practice today associated with high-quality cava production.

At a time when European producers are intensifying their approach to the Brazilian market to compensate for the global decline in beverage consumption, the bet on premium Catalan wines reinforces the focus on higher-priced labels.
