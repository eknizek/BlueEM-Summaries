# "Confidence Is Decisive": Interest in Buying Agricultural Land Cooled in March Due to Political and Economic Uncertainty

The activity index prepared by the Argentine Chamber of Rural Real Estate (CAIR) showed a decline in the third month of the year and fell more than 8 points below the 2025 average. For the entity, the decline is "multicausal," with the government's direction imposing itself as a fundamental factor, especially in livestock business.

The buying and selling of farmland declined during the third month of the year. Operations for buying and selling, inquiries, and the supply of farmland experienced a slight decrease during March as a consequence of increased investor uncertainty due to "internal political circumstances and doubts about the economic direction."

This was indicated by the Argentine Chamber of Rural Real Estate (CAIR), which added to the aspects that determined a decline in sector activity the external context, where the war in the Middle East and the increase in prices of basic inputs for the agricultural sector impacted "decision-making."

In this way, the index prepared by the business entity, which measures the level of real estate activity taking into account supply, inquiries, and buying and selling operations of farmland, fell 1.73 points in March to 40.82 points, a value that is 8.41 points below the 2025 average.

In a conversation with Clarín Rural, the president of CAIR, Lucas Palma, indicated that this decline has a "multicausal" explanation, in which the current political and economic situation plays a fundamental role in this regard. "In this last month the Government had some stumbling blocks and this is reflected. Any political movement impacts investments," said Palma.

Regarding agricultural farmland, the executive emphasized that there is a contraction in supply that "impacts sales, because they are hard to find and few operations are reported." But on the livestock side, there is another reason for this cooling: the need for economic direction to continue, taking into account that these are long-term investments requiring stability in policies.

"Greater political clarity is expected in this regard and that this political course continues, because any cloud that appears makes them think about the policies applied in the last 20 years. Livestock farming is very promising and the business is good, but investment—especially from outside the sector—needs more clarity that this government will continue," Palma stated.

According to the CAIR president, the policy toward the sector applied in recent decades, which he considered "a systematic attack on livestock production, causing the herd to decline," not only impacted productive activity but also the value of land. "Farmland is worth between half and one-third less than what it sells for in neighboring countries," Palma argued.

Today, a hectare of livestock farmland in the Salado Basin is valued at US$4,000, in Corrientes between US$1,500 and US$2,500, and in more marginal areas between US$500 and US$1,200. Meanwhile, agricultural farmland in the core zone reaches prices of between US$18,000 and US$23,000 per hectare.

He also indicated that there are policies and reforms that must be carried out for investments to flow more quickly, such as changes to the "tax barrier" on the sale of breeding stock, which is taxed at 35%, the elimination of export taxes, a review of the Forest Law, and the regulation of foreign land purchases.

Although he emphasized that "farmland continues to be sold," he indicated that the level of activity in farmland buying and selling "will move at the pace of politics. Confidence is decisive, because these are long-term businesses."
