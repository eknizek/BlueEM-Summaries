# US imposes 50% tariffs on copper manufactures: impact on Peru's exports

The President of the United States, Donald Trump, announced the imposition of tariffs of up to 50% on imports of steel, aluminum, and copper, a measure that took effect today. According to business groups such as the Lima Chamber of Commerce (CCL), Peruvian exports with the highest added value would be the most affected.

According to the business group, the presidential proclamation updates the tariff treatment of these products, expanding the scope of goods subject to higher levies. In several cases, it is also established that the tariff applies to the total value of the imported product, which increases its cost of entry to that market.

In this context, while raw materials—such as copper minerals, cathodes, and scrap—would not be affected and would maintain their access conditions, products with higher added value would be, such as wires, bars, profiles, sheets, bands, foils, and accessories.

## Impact on exports

Gabriel Arrieta, head of Economic Studies and Commercial Intelligence at CIEN-ADEX, recalls that the United States has been applying tariffs to Peruvian exports of steel, copper, and aluminum since 2018, and in July of last year, 50% was set for the red metal. The difference between that time and what took effect this Monday is the change in the tax base.

"The [previous] tariff would only impact the value of the metal content that each product had, which was identified within the lists published by the American government. For example, if I wanted to export a copper bar, I would have to calculate how much of the copper bar is copper. To that, I apply the tariff. Now what is changing is that the tax base will no longer be the content, but rather the entire product," he detailed.

With this outlook, for some products "the effect would not be so significant," such as copper bars and plates, which are precisely the main products we export to the United States.

"It takes practically 99% or 99.5% copper to be able to produce that type of product. So, if we were already facing a 50% tariff on this metal tax base, we're talking about almost 99.9% of the value. Then, with the application of the tax base, we don't see that there is an effect," he mentioned.

Even so, he argued that there is no data to quantify the impact on decisions by Peruvian companies at this time. It is worth noting that in 2025, the value of exports to the United States of products at risk with this measure amounted to US$418.5 million in the case of copper products, US$30.5 million in steel, and US$25.5 million in aluminum.

From the business side, David Lavandero, commercial manager of Celsa Electrical Conductors, argues that the United States provision responds to a broader trend of industrial protection and relocation of productive capacities in developed markets, which has been reconfiguring global trade flows. In that sense, such decisions will have indirect effects on price dynamics and product availability at the global level, which also opens opportunities for regional actors.

"In this context, adjustments are generated that create spaces for regional actors with industrial capacity, like Celsa, to strengthen their positioning in markets where relative competitiveness becomes more relevant," he told this newspaper.

In Celsa's case, he mentions, the direct impact would be limited, given the limited weight of the United States in its client portfolio and the sporadic nature of its shipments to that market.

## Government actions

The situation has motivated actions by the Government, as reported by the Ministry of Foreign Trade and Tourism (Mincetur). The main strategy is to maintain diplomatic dialogue with United States authorities.
