# United States Imposes 50% Tariffs on Copper Manufactures: Impact on Peru's Exports

U.S. President Donald Trump announced the imposition of tariffs of up to 50% on imports of steel, aluminum, and copper, a measure that took effect today. According to business organizations such as the Lima Chamber of Commerce (CCL), Peruvian exports with higher added value would be the most affected.

According to the business group, the presidential proclamation updates the tariff treatment of these products, broadening the scope of goods subject to higher duties. In several cases, it is also established that the tariff applies to the total value of the imported product, which increases its cost of entry to that market.

In this context, while raw materials—such as copper minerals, cathodes, and scrap—would not be affected and would maintain their access conditions, products with higher added value would be impacted, such as wires, bars, profiles, sheets, bands, leaves, and accessories.

## Impact on Exports

Gabriel Arrieta, head of Economic Studies and Commercial Intelligence at CIEN-ADEX, recalls that the United States has been applying tariffs to Peru's exports of steel, copper, and aluminum since 2018, and in July of last year, 50% was set for the red metal. The difference between that time and what took effect this Monday is the change in the tax base.

"The [previous] tariff was only going to impact the value of the metal content that each product had, which was identified within the lists published by the American government. For example, if I wanted to export a copper bar, I would have to calculate how much of the copper bar is copper. I apply the tariff to that. Now what is changing is that the tax base is no longer going to be the content, but rather the entire product," he detailed.

With this scenario, for some products "the effect would not be so significant," such as copper bars and plates, which are precisely the main products we export to the United States.

"It takes practically 99% or 99.5% copper to produce that type of product. So, if we were already facing 50% on this metal tax base, we're talking about almost 99.9% of the value. So, with the application of the tax base, we don't see what the effect would be," he mentioned.

Still, he maintained that there is no data to quantify the impact on decisions by Peruvian companies for now.

It should be noted that in 2025, the value of exports to the United States of products at risk with this measure amounted to $418.5 million in the case of copper products, $30.5 million in steel, and $25.5 million in aluminum.

From the companies' perspective, David Lavandero, commercial manager of Celsa Electrical Conductors, argues that the United States' decision responds to a broader trend of industrial protection and relocation of productive capacities in developed markets, which has been reconfiguring global trade flows.

In that sense, these decisions will have indirect effects on the dynamics of prices and the availability of products globally, which also opens opportunities for regional actors.

"In this context, adjustments are generated that open spaces for regional actors with industrial capacity, like Celsa, to strengthen their positioning in markets where relative competitiveness becomes more relevant," he said to this newspaper.

In Celsa's case, he mentions, the direct impact would be limited, given the limited weight of the United States in its client portfolio and the specific nature of its shipments to that market.

## Government Actions

The situation has motivated actions by the Government, according to information from the Ministry of Foreign Trade and Tourism (Mincetur). The main strategy is to maintain diplomatic dialogue with U.S. authorities.
