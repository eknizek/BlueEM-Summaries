# Federal government launches oversight of transport and delivery applications

The federal government has begun inspecting transport and delivery platforms after the deadline for compliance with price transparency rules. This move follows the Palácio do Planalto's decision to postpone discussion of a bill regulating app-based work in Congress.

The National Consumer Secretariat (Senacon), linked to the Ministry of Justice, has begun verifying whether applications clearly inform users how the amounts paid are divided among the company, workers, and establishments—a requirement set out in a recent regulation.

The regulatory offensive comes after discussion of a bill regulating app-based work was suspended in the Chamber of Deputies at the government's request. The requirement to describe prices was one of the points covered in the text that was being discussed in the House.

As reported by Valor, the Palácio do Planalto made the decision to postpone the vote on the text due to difficulty in building consensus, even within the government itself, and chose to concentrate efforts on agendas considered more politically viable, such as the bill addressing the end of the 6x1 work schedule, pointed out as a priority for 2026.

In the Chamber, the "burial" of the bill, which was reported by deputy Augusto Coutinho (Republicanos-PE), has been attributed to the minister of the Secretary-General of the Presidency, Guilherme Boulos, who made proposals considered "tragic" by the platforms with possible impact on prices.

Within the government, the assessment is that any increase in costs for app-based rides and delivery services could have effects on the popularity of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT), a risk in an election year.

Following the suspension of discussion in the Legislature, Senacon's inspection seeks to ensure that platforms present, in each transaction, a detailed table with the total price and distribution of values, including the amount retained by the application, the transfer to drivers or delivery workers, and in the case of delivery, the amount allocated to establishments.

Non-compliance can be classified as a violation of the Consumer Protection Code, with penalties ranging from fines to suspension of activities.
