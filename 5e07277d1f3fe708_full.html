# Food basket skyrockets… Mexicans need 4,940 pesos monthly

The most recent data from the National Institute of Statistics and Geography (INEGI) reveal that in March 2026, the minimum cost to cover basic needs in Mexico continues to rise, driven mainly by the increase in food prices. The impact is direct on lower-income households, which dedicate a large portion of their spending to food.

According to the official report, a person needs at least 2,571 pesos monthly in urban areas and 1,940 pesos in rural areas solely to cover basic food and avoid extreme poverty. However, when other essential expenses are incorporated such as transportation, health, education, and housing, the threshold rises considerably. In that scenario, the cost amounts to 4,940 pesos monthly in urban areas and 3,553 pesos in rural areas, figures that reflect the true cost of living in the country.

These metrics correspond to the so-called Poverty Lines, considered the main indicator to measure whether a person can cover their fundamental needs.

## Tomatoes through the roof

Among all the products that pushed up the cost of the basic basket, one stands out disproportionately: tomatoes, whose price skyrocketed 126.3% annually. This increase is not only significant but determining. According to the data, tomatoes explain 41.1% of the increase in the rural basket and 28.8% in the urban basket, becoming the main inflationary factor within food. The phenomenon evidences the high dependence on certain products within the Mexican diet and how variations in their price can structurally impact the cost of living.

## A "taco" on the street hits the wallet

In cities, the increase is not limited to basic products. The cost of food and beverages consumed outside the home—such as meals at food stalls, restaurants, or cafeterias—recorded an increase of 7.1% annually. This category represents more than a quarter of the increase in the urban basket, reflecting a change in consumption habits and the growing importance of spending on food services within the family budget.

## Inflation versus food: a concerning gap

General inflation in March 2026 stood at 4.6% annually, but food prices have far exceeded this level. In rural areas, the food basket increased 7.9% annually, while in urban areas the increase was 8.1%, that is, more than three percentage points above general inflation. This implies that basic foods are becoming more expensive at a considerably faster rate than other goods and services, generating disproportionate pressure on the most vulnerable sectors.

## Other expenses: transportation, education, and services

The increase in cost of living is not limited to food. When considering the non-food basket, other factors emerge that pressure family spending. In rural areas, public transportation and personal care stand out as key elements in the increase. In cities, the impact expands to areas such as education, culture, and recreation, reflecting a more complex spending structure.

Combined, the total cost of the basket—food and non-food—grew 6.1% annually in rural areas and 5.6% in urban areas, both levels above general inflation.

Since July 2025, the National Institute of Statistics and Geography has assumed responsibility for calculating and publishing the Poverty Lines, a function that previously corresponded to the National Council for Evaluation of Social Development Policy. This change seeks to strengthen transparency and periodicity in measuring poverty in Mexico, offering monthly data that allows more precise monitoring of the evolution of the cost of living.
