# Democracy under pressure: Peru's electoral failures expose Latin America's democratic challenges

Everything that could go wrong in Peru's elections went wrong. The delay in ballot delivery to various polling stations triggered a series of errors by the electoral authority, culminating in the dramatic resignation of the head of the body, with the electoral cycle still incomplete. More than two weeks after voting, over 4% of votes remain uncounted due to contestations, while the candidates vying for second place in the June runoff — left-wing radical Roberto Sánchez and right-wing populist Rafael López Aliaga — are separated by approximately 24,000 votes. Final results may not be ready until mid-May, when all contested ballots have been reviewed, a delay that adds fuel to an already explosive political moment. Allegations of fraud, denunciations of manipulation, and calls to annul the vote are accumulating. Regardless of the outcome, the losing side will almost certainly complain of fraud, increasing the risk of social unrest.

Even the few certainties are bleak: Keiko Fujimori advanced to the runoff as the favorite with just 17% of votes; 23 presidential candidates (yes, 23!) each obtained less than 1%; and geographic division highlights a country deeply divided between two irreconcilable visions. This is remarkable even by the standards of a nation that has gone through three presidents since October. If Peru needed a definitive break with years of institutional instability, this election brought the opposite.

However extreme this confusion may seem, it is not exclusively a Peruvian case. Across Latin America, elections are under pressure due to administrative failures, hyperpolarized politics, cynical disputes for power, and violent interference from organized crime. Peru is less an isolated case and more a warning: questioning elections, fairly or not, inevitably erodes legitimacy.

Consider some examples. In Honduras, technical failures last year also delayed results for weeks in a tight race, triggering accusations of irregularities. The outgoing government tried to pressure for a recount after rejecting results, even when its own party candidate finished a distant third. Guatemala's 2023 election was marked by judicial interference aimed at blocking candidates and altering results, even after Bernardo Arévalo's surprising victory. Brazil's 2022 election ended with President Jair Bolsonaro's refusal to recognize defeat after repeatedly questioning the integrity of the electoral system, a farce that, months later, culminated in the riot in Brasília on January 8, 2023. And in the most flagrant case, Venezuela's regime didn't even bother to pretend and simply stole the 2024 election outright.

Viewed this way, Latin America's electoral problems point to a trend that should concern voters, policymakers, and investors. Politicians must understand that flouting rules unscrupulously for personal gain is a dangerous path that ultimately corrodes the system of which all are part. And as these populist tactics clearly are here to stay, the priority should be to strengthen institutional safeguards that protect electoral processes from such abuse.

In this context, it is unsurprising that confidence is declining, both in the region and globally. On average, only 34% of Latin Americans trust electoral authorities, according to Latinobarómetro, a number that Benjamin Gedan, director of the Latin America program at the Stimson Center in Washington, considers "alarmingly low." "Widespread and chronic skepticism about electoral management makes it easy for a losing candidate to cause trouble," he told me. "This is a reminder of the importance of reliable election observation."

These precedents offer a warning worth heeding before two of the region's largest and most contested elections: Colombia's next month and Brazil's in October, where approximately 200 million people combined are registered to vote. Colombian President Gustavo Petro recently suggested he could condition his recognition of results after repeatedly attacking the country's electoral authorities, even speculating there is a "100% risk of electoral fraud." Does that sound familiar? Yes, despite their ideological differences, figures ranging from Bolsonaro to Donald Trump have used the same cynical playbook.

Violence against security forces is also increasing ahead of voting, where insecurity is one of the campaign's main issues. In Brazil, the powerful Supreme Court Justice Alexandre de Moraes ordered an investigation against pre-candidate Flávio Bolsonaro for allegedly defaming his rival, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva. The court is also keeping an eye on a second pre-candidate for posting satirical videos of ministers amid growing public disapproval of their performance. These actions represent an overzealous interpretation of the court's role, if not outright interference in the electoral process, risking backfire.

Meanwhile, in Mexico, where the governing Morena party still makes baseless allegations that its leader Andrés Manuel López Obrador was deprived of the presidency in 2006, Congress has just approved a reform to reduce the electoral budget, a measure that may compromise the electoral body's independence. "Populist governments erode institutional checks and minority rights. What is at stake is not so much democracy's core, but its liberal appearances," says Andrés Malamud, political scientist at the Institut.
