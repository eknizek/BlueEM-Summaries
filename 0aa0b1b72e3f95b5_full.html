# Aegea Reports Results, but IPO Postponed

Aegea will not conduct its initial public offering this year, as much of the market had been expecting. The revelation was made today by CEO Radamés Casseb in a teleconference to present 2025 results. Aegea had already delayed the release of its fourth quarter earnings for several days, generating nervousness in the market while the numbers underwent revisions. The data was released on the deadline agreed with creditors, but failed to move the company's bonds today – partly because they showed a larger-than-expected increase in leverage.

In the teleconference, Aegea's CEO argued that the company still needs to improve market communication and transparency before listing shares on the stock exchange. "In the journey toward maturity for the IPO, projected for sometime during 2027, the company will continue to evolve its processes," said Casseb.

Aegea's 2025 results showed consolidated leverage rising to 3.78x, from 2.96x at the end of 2024. Aegea bonds were already trading at around 80% of face value last week due to concerns that the delay in releasing results could trigger debt maturities. The discount continued today.

Aegea stated that the adjustments "have no cash impact and do not result in breach of financial covenants." The company said there was a revision in accounting criteria for revenue recognition and calculation methodologies for default rates. There were also adjustments in interest capitalization for concession payments that increased financial expense.

After the adjustments, Aegea now expects to return to a process of gradual leverage reduction, said CFO André Pires. "We have no expectation that this number, over the course of 2026, especially toward the end of 2026, will rise again."

Still, Aegea continues studying participation in the Copasa auction, scheduled for this year. To bid on the privatization without pressuring debt levels, if necessary the company could seek capitalization from shareholders, said Casseb. Aegea's shareholders are Equipav (52%), GIC (35%), and Itaúsa (13%).

"For several years now, the shareholders have been discussing the basis on which future capitalizations will occur, primarily looking at transformational projects, preparing ourselves for major auctions," said Radamés.

In some sell-side sectors, the perception is that Aegea remains a strong candidate to buy Copasa, but the latest results showed a "more fragile" position, increasing the chances of rivals such as Sabesp and Equatorial. Aegea's shareholders could also forgo dividends to strengthen the balance sheet if necessary, said the CFO during the teleconference, in which analysts repeated concerns about the leverage level.

"The company and shareholders will never put the deleveraging trajectory at risk because of dividends," said Pires.
