# Mexico Makes Policy Shift on Fracking

In a change of position, the Government of President Claudia Sheinbaum opened the door to a topic that former President Andrés Manuel López Obrador promised not to authorize: the exploitation of gas through hydraulic fracturing or fracking.

Although the President's approach is that this exploitation be done under a new framework that prioritizes reducing environmental impact, in practice it represents a change in vision that contrasts with the political rejection that predominated in the previous administration.

Through a presentation, federal authorities warned about the risks of depending on gas imports.

## Pemex's Alert

Pemex director Víctor Rodríguez Padilla stated that the country finds itself in a "critical moment" to take advantage of its vast natural gas resources, particularly unconventional ones, given sustained growth in demand.

- Mexico currently imports 75 percent of the gas it consumes, of which 80 percent comes from Texas and the rest from California.

- According to the official, the country is exposed to changes in international prices and faces risk from climate failures or extreme events, and even international conflicts could interrupt supply.

- For Pemex, there is dependence on decisions of foreign governments and companies, uncertainty in guaranteeing energy supply, and limitations on industrial and regional development.

## What López Obrador Had Said

Former President Andrés Manuel López Obrador promised during his administration not to authorize its use. He halted new oil rounds that included unconventionals and favored exploitation of conventional deposits. The Tabascan maintained the prohibition in discourse but without reforming the law, reducing investment in unconventional gas exploration and maintaining dependence on natural gas imports from the U.S.

## What Does Sheinbaum Propose Now?

- Gas is trapped in rocks and does not flow naturally, so it can be extracted by drilling and injecting water and sand to fracture the rock.

- Mexico has greater potential in unconventional gas than conventional gas, although its exploitation is more complex and requires greater investment.

- A scientific committee, with academics from UNAM and IPN, will evaluate whether exploitation through hydraulic fracturing is viable using less aggressive technologies and biodegradable materials.

- According to the proposal, the intention is to use non-potable water and recycle it up to 80 percent, concentrating wells to reduce soil impact and with closed systems for waste management.
