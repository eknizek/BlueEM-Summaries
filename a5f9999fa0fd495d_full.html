# Parmessano Restaurant Chain Moderates Menu Price Increases Despite Substantial Cost Inflation

The Medellín restaurant chain accelerates its expansion in Bogotá with openings in shopping centers.

Pedro Restrepo, co-founder of Parmessano. Photo: Courtesy/Parmessano.

Amid a context of persistent inflation that has significantly raised food and labor costs, Parmessano decided to contain the impact on consumers. According to co-founder Pedro Restrepo, while costs have increased approximately 30%, prices to the public have only been adjusted between 2.5% and 3%.

The chain's strategy has been based on the search for internal efficiencies and process optimization to avoid passing increases on to the end customer. The objective, Restrepo explains, is to preserve a value proposition based on accessible prices, high quality, and an experience that makes the restaurant a place of frequent visits.

In parallel, Parmessano is advancing its expansion in Bogotá, a city that has become its main growth front. After opening its first location at Atlantis in 2023, the brand has added new openings in high-traffic areas such as Nuestro Bogotá, Atrio, Unicentro, and Parque de la 93, consolidating its presence in the capital.

**How long has the chain been in the market and how was Parmessano born?**

The family has a tradition in cooking and service going back to our great-grandparents. We have always been linked to the world of gastronomy. In the 1980s, my parents, when they were dating, along with my grandmother, started this journey with a small establishment called Todo Fresa, which was the first family brand to grow within the food service market.

Many years later, when my brother Camilo and I were in university, we decided to open a restaurant. That was in 2010, and that's when Parmessano was born. That's why today you see both brands in some spaces: they are family brands with different origins, but with the same purpose and philosophy.

Since 2010 we structured the business and grew in Medellín. In 2023 we arrived in Bogotá with our first location at Atlantis. Today, in 2026, we have 17 restaurants in Medellín, seven Todo Fresa cafes, and four locations in Bogotá. Soon we will open four more: Nuestro Bogotá opens next week; Atrio in May; and between July and August we will open Unicentro and the Parque de la 93 location.

**How many locations do you expect to have in Bogotá?**

We are very happy in Bogotá. People have received us very well and we feel a lot of affection for our proposal. Being a family business, we don't have external pressure to grow quickly. We like to keep close control of the business, consolidate teams and solid foundations before expanding. We dream of being in many places in Bogotá, but without rushing. We grow as we feel comfortable, find appropriate spaces, and the team adapts. The idea is to expand the brand in a conscious and coherent way with our values.

**How many jobs does Parmessano generate today?**

Currently we are around 870 direct jobs and we estimate closing the year above one thousand. It's a great responsibility, but also a very satisfying effort.

**How have you faced the increase in raw material and supply costs?**

Yes, we have felt it. We have made significant efforts to not pass those increases on to our guests, as we call our customers. Part of our philosophy is managing comfortable prices while raising service, quality, and experience standards. Raw materials and labor have increased significantly, but we have sought internal efficiencies to not pass those costs on to consumers in the same proportion. It's constant work.

**Any particular supply with a notable increase?**

In general, everything has gone up. It's a widespread phenomenon that affects practically the entire productive chain.

**Do you foresee this intensifying in the coming months?**

Probably yes. Not only in prices, but in macroeconomic impact. These are realities of the country and, from what we do, we remain firm working for this industry and for Parmessano.

**Have you had to raise prices for consumers?**

Very little. We've made adjustments between 2.5% and 3%, while costs have risen about 30% on average. We seek internal efficiencies to maintain our philosophy of prices and protect the consumer.

**Why did you decide to expand the Atlantis location?**

Atlantis was our first restaurant in Bogotá, in 2023. It was a small space and fell short of the experience we wanted to offer. There were long lines and some discomfort. That's why we took the second floor, doubled capacity, and added 56 new seats, with careful décor, special lighting, dedicated restrooms, and a larger bar. Today we can offer the experience we dreamed of for our guests.

**How would you define Parmessano's concept?**

We are in the casual premium category.

**Are you thinking of reaching other cities?**

Yes, later on. On the roadmap is the Coast, particularly Cartagena, toward the end of 2027 or beginning of 2028, without rushing. The priority today is consolidating the business structure.

**How do you see the consumer in the current context?**

The country's circumstances change consumption trends. Ultimately, people stick with brands that generate value for them and where they feel good. Our bet is to care for the experience, the bond with the guest, and also the price.

**The gastronomic proposal has an Italian focus**

Yes, it's artisanal Italian cuisine.

**What's new on the menu?**

We have the Conservation Menu, a project we developed since 2018 that links gastronomy with the conservation of Colombian wildlife in a threatened state.
