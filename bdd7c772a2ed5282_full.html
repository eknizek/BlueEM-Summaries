# Oil prices expected to rise as U.S.-Iran peace negotiations collapse

Oil market experts have warned that the collapse of peace negotiations between the United States and Iran, along with Donald Trump's plan for a naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, would raise oil prices and worsen the global energy crisis.

Amrita Sen, founder and market intelligence director at Energy Aspects, said she expects oil markets to open sharply higher on Sunday (12) if the U.S. proceeds with the proposed blockade of the strait, which would prevent the flow of Iranian oil to markets.

"Until now, the U.S. has allowed Iranian crude oil and derivative exports, and even relaxed sanctions so more buyers could import these cargoes, since the U.S. was very focused on keeping oil prices low. But if there is a genuine blockade, that's an additional 1.5 million to 1.7 million barrels per day [b/d] of oil exports that would be halted—on top of the over 10 million b/d already interrupted."

Trump's announcement about the blockade of the Strait of Hormuz appeared to be an effort to pressure the Iranian regime, which has maintained its ability to send oil to major markets like China during the conflict. The reopening of the strait was a sticking point in negotiations between the U.S. and Iran to transform a two-week ceasefire, which began last Tuesday (7), into a more lasting peace. Negotiations ended without agreement on Saturday (11).

About one-fifth of the global supply of oil and liquefied natural gas transits through the Strait of Hormuz, which is one of the world's most important energy chokepoints.

Analysts said the U.S. strategy of blockading the strait does not yet amount to a return to active combat. But they pointed to an escalation that would increase concerns about a worsening shortage of essential petroleum derivatives such as aviation fuel and diesel.

"Escalation tends to generate more escalation," said Kevin Book, head of research at ClearView Energy Partners. "Blocking Iranian tankers could raise prices and worsen shortages."

The plan also suggested that Trump was willing to risk a prolonged supply disruption despite rising gasoline and diesel prices in the U.S.

"This shows that President Trump is willing to risk a prolonged disruption on the eve of the summer travel season to preserve the zero uranium enrichment position [in Iran]," said Helima Croft, global head of commodities strategy at RBC Capital Markets.

The U.S. president has managed to keep oil prices relatively stable by "consistently signaling that he is ending the conflict," she said. "A blockade could actually change market sentiment about the duration and cause a convergence between the physical and paper markets."

The oil market closed lower on Friday (10), with Brent recording its largest weekly drop since August 2022, due to optimism that peace negotiations between the U.S. and Iran could lead to an agreement. Brent futures closed down nearly 1% at $95.20 per barrel, while West Texas Intermediate, the American benchmark, fell 1.3% to close at $96.57 per barrel.

Jorge León, analyst at Rystad Energy, said he expects oil prices to return above $110 per barrel when negotiations resume on Sunday evening. "What matters is that the chances of a lasting ceasefire have plummeted," he said.

Bob McNally, founder of Rapidan Energy Group and former White House energy advisor, said the big question now is whether Iran and its allies will retaliate against the region's critical energy infrastructure.
