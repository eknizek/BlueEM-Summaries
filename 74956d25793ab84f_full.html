# We Need to Discuss Board Member Compensation

They say a camel is a horse designed by a committee. Yet that is precisely what practically every large organization in the world has: a board of directors. The role of this body is to expand the capacity to perceive risks and see paths that the isolated shareholder cannot visualize — making decisions that maximize economic value over many years, not just the best number in the next quarter.

The ideal board member is not an "executive seeking a chair as a career plan." It is someone who has already incorporated investor mentality, capable of saying "no" when everyone prefers to say "yes." IBGC courses or international schools offer fundamentals, but they do not substitute for four elements: biography, character, soft skills, and — the most forgotten — incentive structure. It is this fourth element that makes the first three financially credible.

When fixed compensation enters the account every month regardless of value created, the dominant incentive becomes another: preserve the chair. The focus shifts to physical presence and protocol compliance. Reopening uncomfortable theses about business model or transparency becomes a "cost without return." With little personal exposure to stock value, the board member has less reason to insist on difficult corrections.

Variable bonuses work poorly for board members, whose role is to decide in the short term with long-term impact. Stock options create payoff asymmetry — one loses little when the stock falls, gains a lot when it rises — potentially encouraging high-risk bets instead of maximizing risk-adjusted value. Practice in developed markets has converged on Restricted Stock Units as the preferred vehicle. RSUs are promises of future stock delivery, without an exercise price. The board member receives lots periodically, blocked from sale for a minimum period of five years — longer than executive director programs. Three advantages: it exposes the board member to the same risk as the long-term shareholder; simplicity facilitates regulator scrutiny; and large-scale studies show that the greater the fraction of compensation in stock, the lower the risk of misaligned behaviors.

Introducing RSUs does not mean increasing total compensation — it is about changing the composition, replacing part of the cash with stocks with lock-in periods. In the U.S., two-thirds to three-quarters of the typical S&P 500 package consist of stocks or RSUs. In Europe, more than 90% of the 150 largest companies use stock plans. In Brazil, only one-fifth to one-quarter of companies compensate board members with shares — with signs of decline between 2024 and 2025, according to Spencer Stuart's Brazil Board Index. For independent board members, equity is a true desert.

The consequences appear in Brazilian corporate episodes. The Americanas case exposed supervisory failures that allowed significant accounting distortions. The sequence of recent judicial recoveries shows boards that did not reorient strategies in time. These are symptoms of misaligned incentives: relatively lower asset exposure than fixed compensation reduces the propensity to question risky decisions.

Resistance responds to the interest of controllers in more docile boards. For RSUs to work without generating capture, safeguards are necessary: expanded disclosure, remuneration committee with independent majority, caps on annual stock value, minimum five-year vesting without cliff, and clawback clauses. When the board member has their assets tied to the same trajectory as other shareholders, "what is best for the company?" and "what is best for me?" will converge to the same answer.

Brazil does not need to reinvent the wheel — it needs to adapt consolidated practices to its context, and it has sufficient repertoire of avoidable crises to justify the urgency.
