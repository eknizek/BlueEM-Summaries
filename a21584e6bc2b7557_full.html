Why the 1% ETF Milestone Matters Significantly for Brazilian Fund Markets

07 April 2026 · 09:50 AM

# Why the 1% of ETFs Matters, and Very Much So

Finally, we've reached the long-awaited 1%. It may seem like a small number, but it took 22 years for ETFs to reach R$ 100 billion in assets under management, equivalent to just 1% of the Brazilian fund industry, which today totals approximately R$ 10 trillion.

It all began in 2004, with the launch of PIBB11, Brazil's first ETF, supported by BNDES. The offering was a success and raised R$ 600 million, driven by the downside protection offered by the bank. Despite the promising start, the following years fell short of expectations.

In the early years, some local and international players entered the market, most notably BlackRock and its BOVA11, which began replicating the Ibovespa with enormous success. Still, the first decade was slow for the industry, which by 2013 had only seven managers, 15 funds, and approximately R$ 2.8 billion in AUM, equivalent to less than 0.2% of the industry. Additionally, ETFs were concentrated in local stocks and dominated by institutional investors.

In the following decade, through 2023, we saw gradual evolution: the number of managers and available products increased, but the growth of assets under management was unremarkable, with AUM of R$ 47 billion and 0.7% market share. However, there was important progress in diversifying investment theses, with the emergence of ETFs in fixed income, real estate, commodities, gold, and cryptocurrencies. Gradually, the market ceased to be a niche segment, gained popularity, and became more sophisticated. By the end of 2023, the sector had 15 managers and approximately 85 funds.

But to be precise, the real inflection point came in the last two years. To paraphrase Ernest Hemingway, "change happens slowly, and then suddenly." It took 20 years for the ETF market to reach approximately R$ 50 billion in AUM, and only two more years to more than double that to R$ 108 billion. The number of funds kept pace, reaching 187 vehicles. But then, what changed?

Drawing a parallel with the United States, where ETFs gained traction after the 2008 crisis, we see a direct relationship with the migration to the fee-based model. Simple, liquid, inexpensive, and tax-efficient structures drove adoption. Today, the American industry manages $13.5 trillion in ETFs, equivalent to approximately 37% of the fund market.

The good news is that we're beginning to see similar catalysts in Brazil. It's as if the last decade had prepared the ground for this expansion, with the advisory model gaining representation compared to the traditional dominant model, in which investment agendas were controlled by generalist bank managers. Then, an even more favorable cycle began three years ago, when we started observing the traditional advisory model coexisting with the fee-based model, the latter also driven by the growth of the consultant and wealth manager market.

This scenario combines with the macroeconomic environment of recent years, which has posed challenges for active fund managers, especially in equities and multimarket funds, who have faced greater difficulty generating alpha relative to benchmarks. This scenario has led investors to reassess the cost-benefit relationship. The result? Important space has opened for the growth of passive solutions. With simple, liquid, diversified, and low-cost structures, ETFs have finally broken through the 1% barrier of the industry and, by all indications, will not remain restricted to a niche.

Another component, if adjusted, could unlock even more growth: tax parity. The reasoning is simple. If an ETF fully replicates a real estate investment fund, why not have the same tax treatment on dividends and capital gains? If it distributes equity income, why not follow the same stock market rules? In a country where income is prominent, this asymmetry limits ETF reach, particularly among individual investors. Correcting this point could significantly accelerate the development of the Brazilian capital markets.

When simple, efficient structures aligned with investors gain scale, what's at stake is not a product, but the evolution of the entire industry. And if regulatory and tax adjustments happen, that 1% could transform into a trillion-real industry in just a few years.

*Gustavo Pires is a partner at XP Inc.*
