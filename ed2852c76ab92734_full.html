Self-Employed Workers Will Not Be Required to Contribute to Retirement: Does Pension System Reform Lose Steam?

18 April 2026 · 12:05 AM

# Self-Employed Workers Will Not Be Required to Contribute to Retirement: How Does Pension System Reform Lose Steam?

Peru's pension system reform loses one of its pillars. The Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) published this morning Supreme Decree No. 061-2026-EF, which modifies the Law on Modernization of the Pension System and formalizes Congress's decision to exclude self-employed workers from mandatory pension contributions.

The measure, approved in September of last year, materializes in article 23, which establishes that citizens who perform independent work or who are over 55 years old at the time the Law No. 32123 enters into force, provided they do not join the dependent labor market, may voluntarily affiliate with the National Pension System or the Private Pension System.

The change is significant. In a country where informality exceeds 70% and pension coverage is concentrated among formal workers, the mandatory contribution requirement for self-employed workers was one of the mechanisms to expand the system's reach.

## What Is at Stake?

For Martín Valencia, head of economic studies at the Peruvian Institute of Economics (IPE), the decision weakens incentives to build a culture of pension savings. "This measure goes against what is required to advance a culture of pension savings. From the IPE, we believe this is a step backward," he told El Comercio.

The problem, he explains, also responds to behavioral factors. In Peru, young adults, especially between ages 25 and 30, tend to postpone retirement savings by perceiving it as a distant need. Faced with this, pension systems typically incorporate mandatory mechanisms that correct this bias. Without that tool, he warns, public policy loses the capacity to induce long-term savings.

According to IPE figures, by 2021, 96% of self-employed or non-workers either were not affiliated (79%) or did not contribute in the past year (17%). In other words, nine out of ten of the total are informal workers.

However, the impact is not limited to individual incentives. For Luis Miguel Castilla, executive director of Videnza Instituto and former Minister of Economy and Finance, the decision also reflects persistent institutional weakness: the difficulty in sustaining pension reforms over time. During his tenure at the MEF in 2013, the mandatory requirement for self-employed worker contributions was approved gradually; however, Congress postponed its implementation and subsequently repealed it. In that vein, he described the current provision as a new step backward.

In fact, according to Sergio Espinosa, head of the Superintendency of Banking, Insurance, and AFP (SBS), the institution he leads published a flexible standard for the entry of more actors into the fund management market until February, when no requests were registered. "I would say the main reason was the eighth withdrawal, because we had expressions of interest from some local and foreign groups," he said in an interview with El Comercio (19/02/2026).

Noelia Bernal, professor and researcher at the University of the Pacific, warns that the measure eliminates one of the few tools aimed at including workers outside the formal market. "The policy of making contributions mandatory for self-employed workers was a good policy to expand their social protection; moreover, it was gradual. But what did they do? They repealed it, and this decree only materializes that decision. This, in terms of social protection, only sends the message: 'You will figure out how to protect yourself during your old age. I, as the State, am making no modifications to my pension system to include you,'" she stated.
