Brazil's farm debt crisis intensifies as restructurings rise 58%

High borrowing costs and supply shocks from Middle East war hit farmers

Brazil's farm-sector debt crisis shows no sign of easing. At the end of the first quarter of 2026, 539 companies in the agribusiness sector were under court-supervised reorganization, up 58% year over year and 9.3% from the fourth quarter of 2025, according to data from the RGF Monitor, compiled by the consultancy RGF & Associados.

The figures confirm the upward trend analysts anticipated. At least in the short term, there are no signs of a turnaround, as interest rates remain elevated, sector margins are under pressure, and access to credit is constrained, specialists told Valor. They added that a new aggravating factor has emerged: the war in the Middle East is further raising costs by driving up the prices of key inputs such as fertilizers and diesel.

The impact of the conflict is already cited as one of the reasons for new court-supervised reorganization filings by agribusiness companies. According to Neot, a data monitoring platform focused on insolvency, the effect should become more visible in the second-quarter results.

"The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has already been cited as a justification for reorganization filings. It is one reason [companies in the sector] were unable to meet their financial obligations," said Julio Moretti.

The strait, blocked by the war, is one of the world's main routes for transporting oil and fertilizers.

Rodrigo Gallegos, a partner at RGF, said the effects of the Middle East conflict are likely to appear not only in filings but also in reorganization cases approved later this year.

"When the war in Ukraine began, farmers quickly started citing it in their reorganization filings. Within one or two quarters, we were already seeing that justification in approved cases that year. Farmers point to the unexpected surge in fertilizer prices," Gallegos said.

Regarding the nearly 60% increase in the number of restructurings in the sector, the RGF specialist said the main drivers were the still-elevated benchmark interest rate (Selic) and high levels of indebtedness.

"The cost of financing the crop remains the key factor," he said.

Despite differences in governance standards and company size, Gallegos noted that many farmers delay negotiations over financial liabilities, waiting for improved field results in the next harvest. This has contributed to a "snowball effect" of defaults for many. At the same time, banks' risk perception has increased, prompting them to demand more collateral for lending.

Soybean farming remains the hardest-hit segment. Of the 539 companies under court protection at the end of the first quarter, 243 were engaged in soybean production. Beef cattle farmers (89 cases) and sugarcane growers (49) followed, according to the RGF Monitor.

Grupo Diamante do Tocantins was among those granted court protection on March 25 in the first quarter. The company operates in the production, processing, and trading of grains, particularly rice, soybeans, and beans. In a statement, the group said it sought protection from creditors due to difficulties stemming from weather-related disruptions. Total liabilities are estimated at around R$500 million. Its main creditors include Banco do Brasil, Caixa Econômica Federal, and Fertilizantes Tocantins.

"The government is trying to find solutions for agribusiness, but as long as interest rates remain at current levels and companies as a whole struggle to access credit, court-supervised reorganizations will continue," Gallegos said.
