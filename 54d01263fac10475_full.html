# Oil surpasses US$126: what it means for Latin America, the dollar, and investors

Oil continues to be the main concern for investors. Brent crude surpassed US$126 per barrel following the deterioration of negotiations in the Middle East and the persistence of restrictions in the Strait of Hormuz, a critical point for global energy trade.

Although prices have since retreated to US$114, the market has abandoned expectations of rapid normalization and has begun to incorporate a more prolonged supply shock. The change does not respond solely to geopolitics. It responds to an actual disruption in crude flows. Analysts at ING, Warren Patterson and Ewa Manthey, point out that "the oil market has moved from excess optimism to the reality of the supply disruption we are seeing in the Persian Gulf," with prices at their highest since the beginning of the conflict. This adjustment changes the macroeconomic outlook. Oil ceases to be just another input and becomes a determinant of inflation, interest rates, and investments.

## Rising inflation and growth under pressure in Latin America

The impact is transmitted rapidly. Oil increases transportation, fertilizers, food, and industrial input costs, ultimately filtering into consumer prices. This channel is already being quantified. A Goldman Sachs report estimates that "a 10% shock in oil prices raises inflation in Latin America by approximately 30 basis points," reflecting widespread transmission beyond fuels alone.

This effect is being incorporated into forecasts. The bank projects regional inflation of 8% in 2026, with upward revisions in most countries, with Argentina being the hardest hit. The bank's analysts also warn that "risks to consumer prices are expanding beyond fuels," which introduces greater inflation persistence.

Growth is also adjusting. The region would grow 1.9% in 2026, slightly below expectations, partly due to higher energy costs and tightening of global financial conditions. The shock is not uniform. The report emphasizes that "the impact on growth depends on whether a country is a net oil exporter or importer." This difference defines the regional response: while producers like Brazil, Colombia, or Ecuador receive higher crude revenues, import-dependent economies like Chile, Peru, or Mexico face deteriorating terms of trade. This imbalance introduces a macro divergence within Latin America, where the same shock generates benefits in some countries and pressure in others.

On a global level, oil acts as a broader channel. Ruben Dalfovo, analyst at SaxoBank, describes how "when crude rises due to a serious supply shock, it doesn't stay within the energy sector, it moves toward freight, food, inflation, and finally stock valuations," broadening the impact's reach. Therefore, the diagnosis also points to an environment that limits monetary policy. The persistence of inflationary pressures, reinforced by shocks like the energy one, reduces the scope for central banks to cut rates. This week the U.S. Federal Reserve warned about the risks of more resilient and less transitory inflation.

## The dollar and currencies: resilience with differentiated effects

The behavior of the dollar adds another layer of complexity. In theory, the rise in oil should strengthen it by reinforcing expectations of higher U.S. interest rates. However, the reaction has been more contained. Francesco Pesole, analyst at ING, said this week that "in theory this should be a favorable environment for the dollar, but the USD has found only limited support so far," partly due to the resilience of U.S. stocks and technical flow factors.

In Latin America, the dynamic has been different. The weakening of the dollar over the past year has acted as support for local currencies. Ernesto Revilla, chief economist at Citi, points out that "the dollar has weakened significantly since April last year, losing close to 10%," and adds that "when the dollar is weak, Latin American currencies are strong." This support is complemented by the commodity cycle. Revilla indicates that "prices of major commodities serve as support for local currencies against the dollar," which explains the region's recent resilience.

In the current year, currencies like those of Brazil, Argentina, and Colombia have appreciated, with the real rising nearly 10%, although currencies like the Peruvian and Chilean peso have taken a hit due to their status as import-dependent economies. Overall, the balance is fragile. A spike in global risk could reverse the flow toward safe-haven assets. In that case, the dollar would appreciate again, generating pressure on emerging market currencies. Citi anticipates a moderate movement, with a possible appreciation of between 3% and 4% toward the end of 2026.

## Investment strategy: the end of the disinflation manual

The biggest change occurs in how to invest. Oil at elevated levels alters the framework that has dominated markets in recent years. Charu Chanana, Chief Investment Strategist at Saxo Bank, warns that "a surprisingly large number of portfolios are still built on the assumption that inflation continues to fall and central banks can cut rates," a scenario that loses consistency when energy keeps inflationary pressure alive.

This mismatch reveals a structural problem. Chanana argues that "what appeared diversified in a disinflation regime can become highly correlated in an inflationary shock," since long-term bonds and growth stocks can fall simultaneously. Oil introduces a new filter. A company's ability to pass through costs, sustain margins, and maintain demand becomes determinant. Dalfovo summarizes it by noting that "oil has become a test of corporate quality."

In this context, Saxo Bank reorganizes its strategy accordingly.
