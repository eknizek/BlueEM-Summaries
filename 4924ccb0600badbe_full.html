# Spirit Airlines: What Did the Iran War Have to Do With the Airline's Operational Shutdown and Bankruptcy?

Thousands of passengers in Colombia and Latin America are affected by the cancellation of routes, while financial rescue is ruled out.

The international air transport landscape has suffered a severe blow following confirmation that Spirit Airlines ceased operations yesterday. The company, a pioneer in the low-cost model and famous for its yellow aircraft, formally declared bankruptcy, becoming the first major casualty in the aviation sector due to the armed conflict involving Iran.

## The Trigger: Fuel and Global Instability

Spirit's bankruptcy is not an isolated event, but rather the consequence of a perfect storm in energy markets. Since the conflict in the Middle East began on February 28th, the price of aviation fuel has doubled in just two months. This unsustainable increase ultimately destroyed the company's recovery plans, which it had agreed with creditors on February 24th, when it still expected to emerge from its financial crisis by summer.

Despite transporting 28 million passengers between February 2025 and January 2026, according to figures from the Department of Transportation, the airline's financial muscle could not withstand the pressure of war.

The collapse of the ninth-largest airline in the United States puts at risk almost 7,500 direct jobs that the company had at the end of last year.

## No State Rescue but Guaranteed Refunds

The possibility of government intervention was flatly ruled out. U.S. Secretary of Transportation Sean Duffy stated that he does not believe the government should intervene to rescue low-cost carriers, which had requested aid of $2.5 billion due to rising input costs. However, the official sent a reassuring message to affected travelers, stating that "funds have been reserved" and that users with valid tickets will receive full refunds of their money.

## Solidarity in the Airline Sector

In response to Spirit's operational shutdown, other airlines have activated contingency plans to mitigate the impact on travelers and crews stranded at various airports. Companies such as American Airlines, United Airlines, Southwest and Avelo, as well as former interested parties in acquiring the company, Frontier Airlines and JetBlue Airways, announced immediate measures.

The sector's support plan includes:

- Enhanced flight programs on routes shared with Spirit

- Preferential fares for affected passengers

- Timely assistance at air terminals

- Plans to repatriate and hire Spirit Airlines crews

- Increased frequencies from Fort Lauderdale, Florida, led by JetBlue

With 34 years of history since its founding in 1992, Spirit Airlines leaves a significant gap in the low-cost market that transformed how millions of people across the continent travel. The future of its operations now rests in the hands of bankruptcy legal proceedings, while the sector attempts to adjust to a reality marked by international volatility.
