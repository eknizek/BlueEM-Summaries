Small Businesses, Men and Commerce: Faces of Job Destruction in Chile

15 April 2026 · 11:59 PM

# Small Businesses, Men and Commerce: Faces of Job Destruction in Chile

The latest employment report from the National Institute of Statistics (INE) brought discouraging news. While the December 2025-February 2026 quarter closed with an annual decrease in the unemployment rate, from 8.4% to 8.3%, and the creation of 94,753 jobs, a deeper look at the data reveals a different picture. Over the past year, there has been a destruction of 53,129 formal salaried jobs in the private sector. However, that is not the most complex part.

According to a report by the Economic Context Observatory at Diego Portales University (OCEC-UDP), the figures show a collapse of 154,833 formal salaried jobs in the micro, small, and medium-sized enterprise (MiPYME) segment compared to the same period the previous year, equivalent to a 5.7% annual contraction. "The collapse is so large that such a year-over-year drop had not been observed since the November 2020-January 2021 quarter," noted the text prepared by OCEC director Juan Bravo. Additionally, it is the eighth consecutive month registering an annual decline in this type of employment in the MiPYME segment, which, according to the analysis, reveals that "this is not a one-time phenomenon, but rather is becoming persistent, with a clear trend toward deepening the destruction of this type of employment. These facts show the serious difficulties this business segment is experiencing in generating employment."

## Those Affected by the Phenomenon

Bravo's report identified that of the annual destruction of 154,833 jobs, 120,130 corresponded to men, and in this group, 76.4% were aged 34 or younger. Looking at the situation by nationality, data showed that 129,523 formal salaried jobs of Chileans were destroyed in MiPYMEs in the past year, and 25,309 of foreigners. While the absolute number was higher among Chileans, in terms of annual percentage variation, the decline was greater among foreigners (-6.7% vs. -5.5%). Breaking down the picture by educational level, the scenario is also concerning. The bulk of the collapse concentrated on people without complete higher education. In the past year, 74,154 jobs were destroyed among those with complete secondary education in MiPYMEs and 50,868 among those with lower educational levels. Now, in which sectors is this phenomenon occurring? Commerce and construction led the annual destruction of this type of employment. In fact, these two sectors explained 59% of the annual collapse in formal salaried employment in MiPYMEs.

## The Causes

Regarding the causes behind this phenomenon, Bravo's report stated that the reduced capacity for growth in productive activity has impacted the dynamism of job creation. "In particular, over the past two months the economy has exhibited a contraction in economic activity, reflected in a decline in Imacec of 0.5% in January 2026 and 0.3% in February 2026. Given the close relationship between economic growth and formal salaried job generation in the private sector, this is certainly a relevant factor in explaining its dynamics," he stated.

However, it is not the only factor behind what has been observed. Thus, it is suggested that increases in hourly wage costs misaligned with labor productivity have played a role in the capacity to generate formal salaried employment in the private sector, especially in smaller companies. The report also mentioned that a milestone that may help explain why the annual decline in formal salaried employment in MiPYMEs began in the May-July 2025 quarter is that in April 2025, the minimum wage subsidy for this type of entity ended. That benefit was in effect from May 2022 until April 2025. "Thus, while wage costs had been misaligned for some time with the evolution of labor productivity, once the minimum wage subsidy for smaller businesses expires, they truly faced the full impact of wage cost increases, no longer having that help to cushion the rise," the analysis stated.
