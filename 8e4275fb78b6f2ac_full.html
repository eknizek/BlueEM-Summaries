Uber expands its operations to all 32 departments and 1,100 municipalities of Colombia

The mobility platform expands its national coverage under a model that integrates taxis, private vehicles, and motorcycles.

The technology company Uber confirmed that its application will be available in all 32 departments of the country, moving from operating in 22 cities to having a presence in more than 1,100 municipalities. This massive expansion seeks to bring the digital transport ecosystem to areas where mobility supply has traditionally been limited or insufficient, allowing millions of citizens to access transportation options at the touch of a button.

Under this new phase of operation, the platform will enable three specific services to adapt to the diverse geographic and economic needs of the country: Uber Taxi, Uber Economy, and Uber Moto. The strategy not only targets users seeking transportation but positions itself as a source of income for those wishing to register as private vehicle owners or taxi drivers. This national deployment transfers the technological model that already functioned in major capitals to rural areas and intermediate cities in Colombia.

One of the pillars supporting this growth is the implementation of a protection protocol that includes more than 40 safety functions and tools. This standard will be available uniformly to all actors in the ecosystem, including users, taxi drivers, and vehicle owners.

Ángela Mendoza, General Manager of Uber for the Andean region, highlighted the relevance of this movement for regional development: "Uber is no longer an urban solution, but a national platform. Being available in more than 1,100 municipalities means contributing to the growth of local economies, generating new income opportunities for Colombians, and expanding access to technology."

The expansion also responds to a change in the perception of private transportation services in the country. According to Mendoza, the objective is to bring the experience of major capitals to every corner of the territory. The platform seeks to close gaps that exist in municipalities where access to technology was scarce, allowing the safety and traceability of trips to stop being an exclusive privilege of large cities. This safety standard is the factor the company considers decisive for gaining trust in new markets.

The announcement of the expansion was supported by a study conducted by the firm Ipsos, which analyzed Colombians' mobility habits and their opinions about applications. In cities where the platform already has a consolidated presence, data reveals deep integration into daily life. Seventy-one percent of respondents use these applications daily and 78 percent consider them the safest mobility option, surpassed only by private vehicles. Additionally, 88 percent of participants in these areas see applications as a reliable alternative, while 8 out of 10 assure that their mobility experience has improved thanks to technology.

On the other hand, the outlook in cities where Uber will begin operating formally in 2026 reflects an unmet need. The Ipsos study indicates that 52 percent of respondents find it difficult to get around in their current environment and 6 out of 10 express dissatisfaction with available public transportation. Expectations for the application's arrival are high: 9 out of 10 people believe their quality of life would improve with this service and 88 percent believe Uber would fill critical gaps in terms of wait times, quality, and safety.

The arrival in 1,100 municipalities presents a scenario of local economic reactivation through the generation of additional income. In a context where mobility remains a challenge, 88 percent of Colombians surveyed by Ipsos identify applications as an opportunity for substantial improvement.
