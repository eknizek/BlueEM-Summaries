Inflation forecast for April: Stepping down but still pushed by March's spike

02 May 2026 · 09:03 PM

# How the Coming Inflation Looks: On a Lower Step, But Still Pushed by March's Spike

After 10 consecutive months with inflation that wouldn't decline, finally in April the general prices of the economy would have decelerated, for the first time since May 2025. The decline in inflation would continue until mid-year, anchored in the stability of the peso against the dollar and the loss of purchasing power of wages. However, a phenomenon that caused problems decades ago in Argentina remains current: indexation. Both energy tariffs and transportation rates are linked to past inflation, which pushes inflation forward. For example, the price movements in March (3.4% above the previous month) have an impact in May.

Both the Equilibra consultancy, headed by Martín Rapetti, and C&T Economic Advisors, run by Camilo Tiscornia, estimated that April inflation would have been 2.4% monthly, one percentage point below March. For its part, a few days before the month ended, EcoGo, run by Marina Dal Poggetto, projected that inflation would close April at 2.5%. Econviews, by Miguel Kiguel, observed 2.6%, and for Daniel Artana, chief economist at Fiel, in April "the first quarter trend would break: inflation would be closer to 2.5% than to 3%."

During the past month, one of the categories that most boosted the Consumer Price Index (IPC) was fuels. Although there was a price freeze in April, which expires on May 15, the impact of the Middle East war was distributed throughout all weeks of March with daily increases, which impact by the methodology of the National Institute of Statistics and Censuses (Indec) in the following month. Thus, Equilibra calculated that gasoline moved an average of 9.1% and EcoGo that fuel prices climbed 10.4%, for an inflation of regulated prices that would have reached 4.7% (compared to the previous 5.1%).

Oil continues to rise and closed the week around $110 per barrel. In April, the average of daily quotes was $102 and the export parity was $99. However, in the local market it traded at $90 in Neuquén and $86 in Chubut, according to data from former Energy Secretary Daniel Montamat—head of Montamat & Associates—and sector executives. That is, new adjustments or an extension of the price freeze will be needed.

Already in May, for statistical purposes the effect of fuel on inflation almost disappears, but the effect of gas tariffs at the national level and electricity and public transportation in the Buenos Aires Metropolitan Area (AMBA) continues. Bus fares in the City and Buenos Aires province have a 5.4% increase this month, 2 percentage points more than March inflation, to maintain real value and have the governments of Jorge Macri and Axel Kicillof increasingly pay fewer subsidies.

Meanwhile, electricity and gas bills are more determined by the sale prices of generation, production, and import costs, but two of their components are the transportation and distribution of energy, which are updated monthly through contracts that run until 2030. For this reason, past inflation permanently affects its future evolution. As it falls and approaches the "0 point something" promised by President Javier Milei for August, its impact will be increasingly smaller.
