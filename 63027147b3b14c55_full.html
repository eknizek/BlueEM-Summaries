# Break the piggy bank: Celebrating Children's Day costs Mexican families up to 5,000 pesos

Families will invest up to 5,000 pesos in Children's Day, a celebration that ranges from school gatherings to purchasing trending toys or a special outing. According to data from the National Alliance of Small Merchants (Anpec), a household with two children can spend between 2,000 and 5,000 pesos to celebrate April 30th.

At school alone, for the gathering, a contribution is requested that ranges between 100 and 200 pesos per child, an amount that covers food, cake, beverages, and piñata, depending on the institution. Added to this is the frequent request for costumes, generating an additional expense of between 300 and 500 pesos per child.

"My daughter goes to daycare, they've asked us for crazy hair, crazy socks, and a educational gift. I've spent 500 pesos on that—these are my first experiences with this," said Viridiana Hernández, a mother.

For her part, Luz Padilla, also a mother, mentioned that at her daughter's primary school they requested cooperation for lunch, which was 110 pesos. However, she usually gives her daughter a gift for April 30th, so she invested around 1,000 pesos in a surprise gift.

Entertainment options also represent a significant expense. According to Anpec, a family outing to the cinema can reach 1,600 pesos, while visits to interactive museums or amusement parks average 2,000 pesos in spending. A visit to a shopping mall, including food consumption and entertainment in video game arcades, can exceed 2,000 pesos. In higher-cost recreational activities, such as water parks, spending can rise to 4,250 pesos, considering admission, food, and transportation.

"This panorama reflects how a date with a noble purpose has been transformed into a significant economic burden for many families, especially in a context where purchasing power continues to be below market prices," said Cuauhtémoc Rivera, president of Anpec.

## Watch out for piracy

The Children's Day season is usually a sales peak for toy stores; however, Rivera warned about the increase in piracy of items that are novel for children.

"Smuggling and piracy represent a growing problem in the national and children's markets. Many families, facing tight purchasing power, are forced to purchase cheaper, contraband, pirated toys of questionable quality and unsafe, which can imply health risks for minors. Additionally, these toys last less, proving the fact that cheap always comes out expensive."

He emphasized that a counterfeit toy costs 40% less than what its legal version costs in stores, while its profit margin is high.
