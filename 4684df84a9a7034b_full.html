Market Alert on Small Caps: Is It Worth Investing Now?

03 May 2026 · 01:37 PM

# Market Alert on Small Caps: Is It Worth Investing Now?

The current elevated level of the Selic rate remains the main obstacle preventing mid and small cap stocks from showing recovery and reducing the significant performance gap with the Ibovespa. Experts interviewed by Broadcast disagree on whether it is time to bet on this segment, given that the basic interest rate entered a downward trajectory in March.

According to Werner Roger, CIO and founding partner of Trígono Capital, it is not the moment to bet on small caps. "It is the moment to wait," he states. "Interest rates are what is hurting locally. It is the fall of small caps. The foreign investor who invests in the stock market is not the same one who invests in interest rates."

Daniel Utsch, manager at Nero Capital, agrees and points out that it is still not the moment to set up maximum allocation in small caps. However, he states that if the investor is aggressive, perhaps a small allocation makes sense. Utsch recalls that the last peak of optimism for mid and small cap stocks was between June and July 2021, and since then, stocks in the sector have lost much in value.

"We will only see improvement in mid and small caps if we have a drop in interest rates, fiscal improvement, and possibly a market-friendly electoral scenario. This entire package could lead to a bull cycle. These stocks are very tied to the domestic scenario," explains Utsch, recalling that the Selic between 10% and 12% could already bring some attractiveness to these stocks.

Victor Bueno, stock analyst at Nord Investimentos, shares the opinion that high interest rates lock in small cap performance. He warns that it is necessary to stay away from highly leveraged companies and companies that don't depend as much on low interest rates. "It's good to avoid companies that depend on economic indicators."

Despite this, Bueno disagrees with Roger and says that for aggressive profile investors, it is a good moment to increase purchases of mid and small caps.

A metric widely used by experts to demonstrate how mid and small cap stocks are penalized in moments of high interest rates is the comparison of the Ibovespa with the B3 Small Caps Index over the last 60 months (5 years), as it was in 2021 that the Selic was near its historical floor. In March 2021, the Selic was at 2.75% per year, while in the same month this year it was at 14.75% per year. During this period, the Ibovespa showed an advance of 62.2%, while the index of small and mid-size companies fell 14.7%.

The consensus among experts is on the investor profile issue. Small and mid-size companies typically show strong volatility over long periods, and you need a more aggressive profile to withstand the "jolts."

## Small Caps by Business Sector

Among the investment options, the main divergence among experts is in the construction sector. For Bueno, it is important to focus on low and high-income builders and not invest in middle-income builders. Utsch, meanwhile, sees potential in builders targeting the middle-income population.

"In construction, shares of companies focused on high and low-income segments have shown good performance lately, so it would not be the moment to enter. Already the shares of middle-income developers have barely risen at all. These are companies like Eztec, Tecnisa, Even, and Mitre," argues the Nero Capital manager.

Still in construction, Werner highlights that the cycle of interest rate reduction could help builders and developers like Cyrela and Lavvi. The expert also highlights Embraer as a good investment alternative.

In other sectors, Bueno says that agribusiness sector companies are good alternatives at the moment, as are some retail companies. "In retail, you need to be very selective. Companies that have stood out as alternatives are from the sports segment, such as Vulcabras. Vivara has also stood out," recalls the Nord Investimentos analyst.

## Caution Even for the Ibovespa

Even the Ibovespa, which unlike small caps is less sensitive to domestic cycles and can better ride the international scenario, may present a downward trend in the short term due to the Middle East conflict, according to Gabriel Mollo, analyst at Daycoval Corretora. Mollo says that "the war in the Middle East greatly affected the interest rate scenario, with strong migration to fixed income."

"The Brazilian stock market only didn't fall due to the strong participation of oil in the index," he states. The conflict ended up closing the Strait of Hormuz, through which 20% of the world's traded oil transits, raising the commodity quote above the $100 level.

"It is necessary to seek companies with solid fundamentals, such as Taesa, for example. An opportunity, if the war drags on, would be the insurance sector," says Mollo.
