# End of alliance at ANP concerns market

Nobody understood anything. Throughout last year, two directors of the National Petroleum Agency (ANP) – Artur Watt and Pietro Mendes – worked in tandem to make major advances in combating fraud and tax evasion in the fuel market, constituting a majority in votes that frequently ended 3×2. Last Friday, however, this partnership appears to have come to an end. Watt – the agency's director-general – joined his previous voting antagonists, Daniel Maia and Fernando Moura, to approve a change to ANP's bylaws, altering rules about the conduct of proceedings in the agency, the powers of its directors, and the publicity of its administrative meetings. In the same meeting, which lasted nearly ten hours, the new trio also aligned to place regulatory changes in the LPG market on the agency's agenda, changes that concern companies in the sector.

Although it seems a merely bureaucratic matter, ANP's new bylaws affect even provisions behind a legal dispute between the agency and the Refit group, owned by businessman Ricardo Magro, accused of habitual tax evasion by ANP and tax authorities. The new rules and the split in the directorate are being monitored with concern by companies regulated by ANP, at a time when the agency is addressing key issues ranging from combating informality in the fuel market to current diesel subsidies, including the LPG market.

The bylaw reform was led by Watt, a career prosecutor from the Attorney General's Office (AGU) who served as general counsel at ANP for 12 years, reached his current position in August after also serving as legal advisor to PPSA (Pre-Sal Petróleo S.A.) and briefly at the Ministry of Mines and Energy. Directors Pietro Mendes and Symone Araújo, who opposed the bylaw amendments, argue that they will give more power to Watt himself to the detriment of collegial decisions, in addition to reducing agency transparency. Among other changes, the rules end the live streaming of ANP's directorate meetings that discuss administrative matters, such as the internal bylaws themselves and new hires. Any deliberation on further bylaw changes can only be placed on the agenda in these meetings by the collegiate body's leader.

"There is a concentration of decision-making power in the figure of the director-general," Pietro said during analysis of the process.

Watt's agenda with the changes is unclear, but businesspeople following the sector fear they could be a harbinger of setbacks in recent achievements in fuel market hygiene. For example, on a matter connected to the agency's dispute with Refit, the new bylaws eliminated the so-called "reference director" at the agency. The reference director figure, created by ANP in 2024, is at the center of Refit's questioning of the participation of two directors – Pietro and Symone – in directorate deliberations about the closure of the Manguinhos refinery. Refit alleged, in appeals to ANP itself and in court, that Pietro and Symone could not vote on the Manguinhos refinery closure because they were the "reference directors" for the inspection theme. In the lawsuit filed by Refit, ANP responded that inspection and closure of Manguinhos were decisions of the technical area. But the 11th Panel of the Federal Regional Court (TRF-1) partially accepted Refit's arguments and determined that the agency hold a new vote on the directors' disqualification. The market fears that the new vote will end 3×2, with the new axis formed by Watt prevailing.

"This judicial decision signals unequivocally, in my view, that this is not the most appropriate moment for eliminating the reference director," Symone said when reading her vote.

Watt told Brazil Journal that an opinion from ANP's legal office indicated there would be no legal risks in eliminating this figure, and that the decision was "celebrated internally" by the agency's technical staff. He said there was a diagnosis that the reference director rule "wasn't working."

"This doesn't exist in other agencies. It was a unique Brazilian thing and created a lot of divided loyalties among directors," Watt said.

On other sections of the new bylaws, Pietro and Symone also criticized the decision to concentrate in the director-general the reporting of removals and appointments to commissioned positions and the definition of their hiring form – whether by direct appointment or selective process. Under the regulation, the director-general will still have the power to approve travel and secondments of staff to other bodies or leave, and may assume for himself the reporting of processes he considers urgent (though subject to directorate majority approval).

"We did extensive research on all these changes, and we are bringing ANP's bylaws closer to how other regulatory bodies function," Watt said.

On administrative meetings, he said these "are internal deliberations" and there is no legal obligation to display them in video. Watt also said that decisions on regulatory matters will continue to be broadcast normally, as in other agencies.

Beyond disagreements over the new bylaws, directors opposed to ANP changes questioned the timing of the discussion itself. Pietro cited several new attributions given by the Government to the agency, such as regulating diesel subsidies created after the Iran war, and said that "institutional priority should be focused on ensuring supply and efficient implementation of these emergency measures."

The public disagreement at ANP attracted market attention given that Watt has always positioned himself against disqualifying his two colleagues in all of Refit's appeals. Meanwhile, the other directors, Daniel Maia and Fernando Moura, who now voted with Watt for the governance reform, have always held positions more favorable to Refit's demands. In an ANP meeting in December, Maia even pointed out problems in Manguinhos' inspection that, according to him, should have been reported to other directors. The position was criticized at the time by Watt and Pietro.

Of the current ANP collegiate members, Watt and Pietro were sworn in this year under the Lula administration, with terms until 2029 and 2028, respectively. Daniel Maia and Fernando Moura began their terms under Jair Bolsonaro's administration in 2022, with terms ending in October and November of this year. Symone joined the directorate in 2020, was reappointed in 2022, and will end her term in 2027.
