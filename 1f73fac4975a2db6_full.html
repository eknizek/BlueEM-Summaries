# End of 'partnership' at ANP concerns market over fuel fraud oversight

No one understands anything. Throughout last year, two directors of the National Petroleum Agency (ANP) – Artur Watt and Pietro Mendes – worked in tandem to make major advances in combating fraud and tax evasion in the fuel market, forming a majority in votes that frequently ended 3×2. Last Friday, however, that partnership appears to have come to an end. Watt – the agency's director-general – aligned himself with his previous voting opponents, Daniel Maia and Fernando Moura, to approve a change to ANP's bylaws, altering rules on the conduct of proceedings within the agency, the powers of its directors, and the publicity of its administrative meetings. At the same meeting, which lasted nearly ten hours, the new trio also aligned to add to the agency's agenda regulatory changes in the LPG market that concern companies in the sector.

Although it seems merely a bureaucratic matter, ANP's new bylaws affect even devices behind a legal dispute between the agency and the Refit group, belonging to businessman Ricardo Magro, accused of persistent tax evasion by ANP and tax authorities. The new rules and the rift in the directorate are being followed with concern by companies regulated by ANP, at a moment when the agency is focused on key issues ranging from combating informality in the fuel market to current diesel subsidies, including the LPG market.

The bylaws reformulation was led by Watt, a career prosecutor from the Attorney General's Office (AGU) who served as general prosecutor at ANP for 12 years, took his current position in August after also serving as legal advisor to PPSA (Pre-Sal Petróleo S.A.) and briefly at the Ministry of Mines and Energy. Directors Pietro Mendes and Symone Araújo, who opposed the bylaw changes, argue that they will give more power to Watt himself at the expense of collegiate decisions, in addition to reducing agency transparency. Among other changes, the rules end the online transmission of ANP directorate meetings that discuss administrative matters, such as the bylaws themselves and new hires. Any deliberation on new bylaw changes can only be added to these meetings' agenda by the collegiate leader.

"There is a concentration of decision-making power in the figure of the director-general," Pietro said during the process analysis. Watt's agenda with the changes is unclear, but business leaders following the sector fear they could be a harbinger of a setback in recent achievements in fuel market hygiene. For example, on a matter connected to the agency's dispute with Refit, the new bylaws eliminated the so-called "reference director" at the agency. The reference director figure, created by ANP in 2024, is at the center of Refit's questioning of two directors' – Pietro and Symone – participation in directorate deliberations on the closure of the Manguinhos refinery. Refit argued, in appeals to ANP itself and in court, that Pietro and Symone could not vote on the Manguinhos refinery closure because they were the "reference directors" for the inspection theme. In the case filed by Refit, ANP responded that the inspection and closure of Manguinhos were decisions by the technical area. But the 11th Panel of the Federal Regional Court (TRF-1) partially accepted Refit's arguments and determined that the agency conduct a new vote on the directors' disqualification. The market fears the new vote will end 3×2, with the new axis formed by Watt prevailing.

"This judicial decision signals unequivocally, in my view, that this is not the most appropriate moment for eliminating the reference director," Symone said when reading her vote. Watt told Brazil Journal that an opinion from ANP's prosecution office indicated there would be no legal risks in eliminating this figure, and that the decision was "celebrated internally" by the agency's technical staff. He said there was a diagnosis that the reference director rule "wasn't working."

"This doesn't exist in other agencies. It was a unique thing that generated a lot of divided attention among directors," Watt said. On other sections of the new bylaws, Pietro and Symone also criticized the decision to concentrate in the director-general the reporting of removals and appointments for commissioned positions and the definition of their hiring method – whether by direct appointment or competitive selection. Under the regulation, the director-general will still have power to approve travel and the assignment of civil servants to other agencies or leaves of absence, and may assume for himself the reporting of processes he considers urgent (though subject to directorate majority approval).

"We did very extensive research on all these changes, and we are bringing ANP's bylaws closer to how other regulatory bodies function," Watt said. On administrative meetings, he said they "are internal deliberations" and there is no legal obligation to display them in video. Watt also said that decisions on regulatory matters will continue to be broadcast normally, as in other agencies. Beyond disagreements over the new bylaws, directors opposed to ANP changes questioned the very timing of the discussion. Pietro cited several new assignments given by the Government to the agency, such as regulating diesel subsidies created after the Iran war, and said that "institutional priority should be directed at ensuring supply and efficient implementation of these emergency measures."

The public disagreement at ANP attracted market attention given that Watt always positioned himself against barring his two colleagues from deliberations in all of Refit's appeals. The other directors, Daniel Maia and Fernando Moura, who now voted with Watt for the governance reformulation, have always had a more favorable position regarding Refit's demands. At an ANP meeting in December, Maia even pointed out problems in the Manguinhos inspection that, according to him, should have been reported to other directors. The position was criticized at the time by Watt and Pietro. Of the current ANP directorate members, Watt and Pietro were sworn in this year under the Lula administration, with mandates through 2029 and 2028, respectively. Daniel Maia and Fernando Moura began their terms during Jair Bolsonaro's administration in 2022 and remain until October and November of this year. Symone joined the directorate in 2020, was reappointed in 2022, and will end her term in 2027.
