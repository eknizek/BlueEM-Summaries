# Oil Markets Show High Volatility Eight Weeks Into Middle East Conflict

Oil continues to be the market's focus since the start of the Middle East war. Eight weeks into the conflict, the market continues to react with high volatility, reflecting greater concern about supply risks and their impact on inflationary pressures, as revealed by Scotiabank's Weekly Report.

"Although there are signals of possible negotiations, the market seems to assume that any ceasefire would be fragile while the Strait of Hormuz remains closed," Scotiabank explained in its report.

In that regard, Texas oil began the week with a 6% increase, once again surpassing the US$100 per barrel mark, after President Trump announced his plans to impose a blockade on ships related to Iranian ports transiting through the Strait of Hormuz, in response to the failure to reach an agreement over the weekend. The surge came after crude prices fell more than 10% on Wednesday of the previous week following the announcement of a two-week ceasefire. This demonstrates the fragility of the truce or that any ceasefire could be ephemeral.

During the first quarter of 2026, crude prices averaged US$72 per barrel, while as of April, it registers an average close to US$104 per barrel. Year-to-date, the average price stands around US$76 per barrel, reflecting a sharp break between first quarter performance and the start of the second quarter.

For reference, during the first year of the Russia-Ukraine war, oil remained around US$100 per barrel for five consecutive months, then gradually declining. Thus, with two quarters of elevated prices, the annual WTI average was US$92 per barrel in 2022.

On the other hand, if the market faced only one quarter with prices around US$100, the 2026 annual average would be closer to US$75 per barrel.

The current context presents relevant differences. In 2022, the price increase was mainly due to Russia's weight as a key producer, the sanctions imposed by the U.S. and Europe, and logistical risks—factors that the market gradually internalized. However, in the current scenario, tension stems from the disruption of the Strait of Hormuz route, through which approximately 20% of global oil trade transits, introducing a larger physical shock and reducing the market's capacity for self-regulation.

Regarding the physical market, Scotiabank recorded a surplus balance at the beginning of the year with a slight deficit for 2026. The monthly balance began showing deficits from March, following the start of the war on February 28, as a result of the Hormuz disruption. This change in balance not only reflects a specific supply shock but also the market's limited capacity to absorb it in the short term.

According to the U.S. Department of Energy (EIA), the physical deficit was recorded mainly in the second quarter of 2026 and is due to reduced supply associated with Middle Eastern countries. Although production outside OPEC continued growing, led by the U.S., this increase was not sufficient to compensate for Middle Eastern disruptions.

In the case of the United States, oil production is higher in the April report, exceeding projections recorded in early February and positioning itself, on average, around 1% above what was anticipated. On the other hand, OPEC is the most affected bloc, with a significant decline during the second quarter of 2026 of approximately 20% compared to projections.
