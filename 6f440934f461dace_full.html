# Cargo Transport in Mexico Faces Critical Challenges from Insecurity and Infrastructure Deficits

The cargo transport sector in Mexico faces a critical scenario where insecurity and deficient road infrastructure operate as the main obstacles to economic growth. José Carlos Gilibert Peña, vice president of the National Chamber of Cargo Transport (Canacar), warned that despite the industry's willingness to contribute to an expanding economy, the lack of minimum conditions of certainty on national routes is suffocating operations.

"For us, growth and continuity are very difficult issues because we have a very serious problem today at the national level, which is the country's insecurity," the executive said.

The official explained that one of the most harmful effects of crime is the alteration of logistics flows, as fear of theft has caused a massive shift of operations to daytime hours, saturating general communication routes and toll plazas. This situation not only raises operating costs but also degrades service quality for all users.

"If we cannot circulate at night because we have a high theft rate on certain highway stretches, logically we will circulate during the day, which results in saturated toll booths, miles of lines, and poor toll service."

He added that the efficiency demanded by the current supply chain is unattainable without "completely different highways and toll systems with the dynamism and technology" necessary to expedite transit.

Luis Hernández, director general of Transportation at SICT, indicated that work has been done on digitalization and administrative simplification processes to help cargo move more easily both within national territory and internationally. He mentioned that work is underway on a self-regulation project that will facilitate companies ensuring cargo compliance with standards from the start and avoid delays.

Higher Insurance Premiums

The Mexican Association of Freight Forwarders (Amacarga) revealed that the increase in diesel prices and insurance policy premiums, which have resulted from the war between the United States and Iran, have generated an increase of between 25 and 30% in the sector's operating costs.

"Insurance policies (for shipping companies) have doubled or even tripled, because you no longer just have transportation insurance, you have an extra war insurance that the shipping company is imposing on you," highlighted Eva Maria Muñoz, president of the organization, during her participation in the Forum of the National Association of Vehicle Tracking and Protection Companies (ANERPV).
