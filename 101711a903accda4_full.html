# Chile Concerned About Argentina's RIGI Mineral Incentives: "Milei Achieved in 18 Months What We Didn't in 10 Years"

The announcements of mining investments in Argentina following the implementation of the Large Investment Incentive Regime (RIGI), approved in July 2024, have triggered alarms in Chile, a country with a strong mining tradition. Manuel Viera Flores, president of Chile's Mining Chamber, wrote an article praising Javier Milei's policy and expressed serious concern about the future of the sector in his country.

With copper as the primary mineral, mining in Chile drives over 10% of GDP, and for the Mining Chamber's leader, the trans-Andean country lost the last decade. In his column titled "Chile Before the Argentine Mirror: React or Watch the Copper Supercycle Pass By," published in Cooperativa, the civil engineer emphasizes the benefits of RIGI for Argentina, to the detriment of Chile's situation.

"Milei achieved in 18 months what Chile hasn't managed in 10 years. The Large Investment Incentive Regime (RIGI) is a remarkable public policy instrument, and Chile must take it as a reference. It's the first time in decades that a South American country has offered a truly competitive fiscal framework globally. Look at the result: very quickly investors of the caliber of RTZ, BHP, Lundin, First Quantum, etc. arrived with large investment amounts, as clear as that," wrote Viera Flores.

"The RIGI offers tax invariability for 30 years standard and 40 years for strategic projects like Vicuña. This is what mining investors need: long-term certainty that aligns with mining project horizons; it allows profit repatriation without currency controls. For BHP, Lundin, First Quantum, Rio Tinto, this was a historical barrier in Argentina. Today it's no longer," added the former director of Enami and of Chile's Institute of Mining Engineers.

In another section of his lengthy column, he detailed some figures: "Massive scale of attraction. In 18 months, RIGI captured US$33.876 billion in applied projects, of which 73% is copper mining. There are 12 projects already approved and 11 in process. Chile didn't achieve this volume of new investment in the last decade. The crown jewel of BHP and Lundin Mining, Vicuña, with US$18 billion under the special PEELP category (Long-Term Strategic Export Projects), has 40-year benefits. It's potentially one of the 5 largest copper mines in the world. That's what Milei wins for Argentina, not Chile."

Viera Flores, who holds a master's degree in Industrial Engineering and a doctorate in Mining Economics and Finance from the Autonomous University of Madrid, summarizes RIGI with two words: "Simplicity and speed."

"RIGI has an approval period of 45 days by the Evaluation Committee. In Chile, a project would wait months just for an Environmental Qualification Resolution. Speed is an enormous competitive advantage. Chile cannot fall asleep: it must compete to attract investments. Chile's boom has already passed. Now we need to pursue a different strategy with the advantages the country offers, for example, increasing smelting and refining capacity," emphasizes the Chilean.

"With due respect to Argentine sovereignty, RIGI is a lesson in pro-investment public policy. It doesn't mean copying it identically, but rather learning its principles: long-term fiscal stability, regulatory simplicity, free currency availability, preferential treatment for strategic projects, and—fundamentally—the State's commitment not to change the rules," Viera Flores stresses.

The Mining Chamber president continued with praise for the Argentine president: "Milei understood something Chile forgot: capital is cowardly and goes where there is certainty. RIGI is not a gift to mining companies; it's a State strategy to capture global investment. Chile urgently needs its own RIGI, adapted to our institutions, but with the same ambition, thinking big again. Argentina is today the emerging global lithium powerhouse. It has 30% of the South American Lithium Triangle, brines with concentrations exceeding 800 mg/L—like Hombre Muerto West with 883 mg/L—and DLE technology in full implementation. Rio Tinto's Rincón project with US$2.724 billion is a global reference. And Salta alone has 35 lithium projects in different phases."

He also identifies certain paradoxes in the Argentine model: "Argentina has the world's most promising greenfield copper projects, but doesn't produce copper today. Vicuña (Josemaría + Filo del Sol) with 13 Mt of contained copper in resources, plus 25 Mt inferred, is qualified by Lundin Mining as the largest greenfield copper discovery in the last 30 years. El Pachón is one of the largest undeveloped ore deposits on the planet. Los Azules could be South America's largest copper project according to some estimates," Viera Flores specifies.

However, the trans-Andean engineer raises a concern: "But 'having' is not 'producing.' Argentina's first copper production will be Josemaría around 2030. Between 2030 and 2035, Argentina could reach between 1 and 2 million tons annually. That would make it the 4th or 5th largest world producer, still far from Chile's 5.4 million."

He concludes: "Chile didn't lose mining leadership. What it lost was speed. If we recover speed with a Chilean-style RIGI, single-window permitting, and a national exploration plan, we become the mining power of the 21st century again. Chilean mining has always been a winner, but this time we can't win with 20th-century reflexes. Chile has two paths: defend what it has and gradually lose ground, or attack with an ambitious agenda and recover leadership. The first option is tempting, but it's slow-motion defeat. The second requires political will and a sense of urgency. My message to authorities is clear: Let's choose the path of ambition!"

"The 21st century will be the century of South American copper. The question is whether we'll be the protagonists or spectators, because 'in mining there are no shortcuts. Success is built with mature engineering, genuine social license, and clear rules,'" concludes Manuel Viera Flores, president of the Mining Chamber.
