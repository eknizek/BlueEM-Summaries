# Concern in Chile over Argentina's RIGI mining incentive advance: "Milei did in 18 months what we couldn't achieve in 10 years"

The announcements of mining investments in Argentina following the implementation of the Regime of Incentives for Large Investments (RIGI), approved in July 2024, has set off alarms in Chile, a country with a mining tradition. Manuel Viera Flores, president of Chile's Mining Chamber, wrote an article praising Javier Milei's policy and expressed serious concern about the future of the sector in his country. With copper as the main mineral, mining in Chile drives more than 10% of GDP, and for the Mining Chamber's leader, the trans-Andean country lost the last decade.

In his column titled "Chile Before the Argentine Mirror: React or Watch the Copper Supercycle Pass By," published in the media outlet Cooperativa, the civil engineer weighs the benefits of RIGI for Argentina, to the detriment of Chile's situation.

"Milei did in 18 months what in Chile we have not achieved in 10 years. The Regime of Incentives for Large Investments (RIGI) is a remarkable public policy instrument, and Chile must take it as a reference. It is the first time in decades that a South American country has offered a truly competitive fiscal framework at a global level. See the result: very quickly investors of the caliber of RTZ, BHP, Lundin, First Quantum, etc. arrived with large investment amounts, it's that clear," wrote Viera Flores.

"The RIGI offers tax invariability for 30 years as standard and 40 years for strategic projects like Vicuña. This is what mining investors need: long-term certainty that coincides with the horizons of mining projects; it allows profit repatriation without exchange controls. For BHP, Lundin, First Quantum, Rio Tinto, this was a historical barrier in Argentina. Today it no longer is," added the former director of Enami and the Institute of Mining Engineers of Chile.

In another section of his lengthy column, he detailed some numbers: "Massive scale of investment capture. In 18 months, RIGI attracted $33.876 billion in applied projects, of which 73% is copper mining. There are 12 projects already approved and 11 in process. Chile did not achieve this volume of new investment in the last decade. The crown jewel of BHP and Lundin Mining, Vicuña, with $18 billion under the special PEELP category (Long-Term Strategic Export Projects), has 40-year benefits. It is potentially one of the 5 largest copper mines in the world. Milei wins that for Argentina, not Chile."

"Chile's boom has already passed": the raw confession from the trans-Andean Mining Chamber regarding RIGI in Argentina

Holder of a Master's degree in Industrial Engineering and a doctorate in Finance and Mining Economics from the Autonomous University of Madrid, Viera Flores summarizes RIGI with two words: "Simplicity and speed."

"The RIGI has a 45-day approval period by the Evaluation Committee. In Chile a project waited months just for an Environmental Qualification Resolution. Speed is an enormous competitive advantage. Chile cannot fall asleep: it must compete to attract investments. Chile's boom has already passed, now we need to find a different strategy with the advantages the country offers, for example, increasing smelting and refining capacity," highlights the Chilean.

"With due respect for Argentine sovereignty, RIGI is a lesson in pro-investment public policy. It doesn't mean copying it identically, but rather learning its principles: long-term fiscal stability, regulatory simplicity, free currency availability, preferential treatment for strategic projects, and—fundamentally—the State's commitment not to change the rules," emphasizes Viera Flores.

The president of Chile's Mining Chamber continued with praise for the Argentine president: "Milei understood something that Chile forgot: capital is cowardly and goes where there is certainty. The RIGI is not a gift to mining companies, it is a State strategy to capture global investment. Chile urgently needs its own RIGI, adapted to our institutions, but with the same ambition, to think big again. Argentina is today the great emerging power of global lithium. It has 30% of the South American Lithium Triangle, brines with concentrations exceeding 800 mg/L—like Hombre Muerto West with 883 mg/L—and DLE technology in full implementation. Rio Tinto's Rincón project with $2.724 billion is a global reference. And Salta alone has 35 lithium projects in different phases."

He also establishes certain paradoxes in the Argentine model. "Argentina has the world's most promising copper greenfield projects, but does not produce copper today. Vicuña (Josemaría + Filo del Sol) with 13 Mt of contained copper in resources, plus 25 Mt inferred, is classified by Lundin Mining as the largest copper greenfield discovery in the last 30 years. Pachón is one of the largest undeveloped ore deposits on the planet. Los Azules could be South America's largest copper project according to some estimates," Viera Flores specifies.

## RIGI for mining in Argentina: the warning coming from Chile

However, the trans-Andean engineer raises a caveat: "But 'having' is not 'producing'. Argentina's first copper production will be Josemaría around 2030. Between 2030 and 2035, Argentina could reach between 1 and 2 million tons annually. That would put it as the 4th or 5th largest world producer, still far from Chile's 5.4 million."

And he concludes: "Chile did not lose mining leadership. What it lost was speed. If we recover speed with a Chilean-style RIGI, a single-window permitting system, and a national exploration plan, we will be the mining powerhouse of the 21st century. Chilean mining has always been a winner, but this time we cannot win with 20th-century reflexes. Chile has two paths: defend what it has and gradually lose ground, or attack with an ambitious agenda and recover leadership. The first option is tempting, but it is slow-motion defeat. The second requires political will and a sense of urgency. My message to the authorities is clear: Let's choose the path of ambition!"

"The 21st century will be the century of South American copper. The question is whether we will be the protagonists or spectators, because 'in mining there are no shortcuts. Success is built with mature engineering, genuine social license, and clear rules,'" concludes Manuel Viera Flores, president of the Mining Chamber.
