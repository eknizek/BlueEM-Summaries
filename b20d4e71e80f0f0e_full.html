# Mixue Opens First International Factory Outside China; Launches Flagship Store in São Paulo

Chinese tea and ice cream chain larger than McDonald's and Starbucks opens first store in the country this week

The Chinese company Mixue, which is targeting opening hundreds of stores in Brazil, plans to inaugurate a factory in the country to serve the regional market in the Americas — the brand's first factory outside of China. The plans are part of a R$ 3.2 billion investment announced by the company last year.

Mixue's debut begins on April 11 on the third floor of Shopping Cidade São Paulo on Avenida Paulista. The first store will have 80 square meters and will allow order pickup for customers in "grab and go" format or through delivery via fellow Chinese company Keeta.

The next location should be Rua 25 de Março, one of São Paulo's highest foot-traffic areas. The store is in final construction phase and should open soon, according to Mixue. "The presence in these two locations allows Mixue to be close to different consumer profiles, with accessible prices," explains Tian Zhezhong, the executive responsible for Mixue's operations in Brazil and an employee of the Chinese tea and ice cream chain for over 10 years.

The expansion should include other high-traffic locations with different store models (street, shopping mall, and flagships) in the southwest, central, and east zones of São Paulo, with the first unit outside the capital by year-end. After that, expansion should follow a franchise model without a master franchisee.

The Chinese company's operation begins with 20 employees in the country, with an office on Avenida Paulista. The expectation is that operations, training, marketing, sales, and supply chain departments will be strengthened in the coming months.

In Brazil, Mixue wants to replicate the accessible profile of chilled beverages that became a success in Asia and Oceania, where the company has over 47,500 stores with beverages costing around US$ 1. Without anticipating prices to be practiced in Brazil, the idea is to maintain a low ticket value. The brand serves fruit and tea-based beverages, with special appeal in the frozen segment. Beyond classic lemonades — the company is China's largest lemon buyer — and ice cream cones, coffee and tropical flavors should feature on the Brazilian menu.

Mixue's expansion through Brazil happens as the Chinese company focuses on growth outside Asia. This year, it opened stores in Los Angeles, New York, and Mexico City, still without presence in Europe. Beyond consumer market, Brazil should act as a logistics and production hub. In announcing the operation alongside President Lula in Beijing, the company committed to increasing purchases of Brazilian agricultural products to meet global demand.

The factory plans in the country stem from this. With a facility in Brazil, the company can produce packaging and pack products for the country and the Americas. Currently, the company imports and packages items in three factories in China called D.co. "The pace and volume of investments will be defined according to the development of Mixue's business in Brazil," says Zhezhong. "Brazil is a long-term strategic market."
