Challenges facing the team overseeing major environmental system reform

24 April 2026 · 09:21 PM

# Environmental Ministry Prepares for Major System Reform: Who is Leading It

A dozen measures related to environmental processing of projects were presented by the government in the national reconstruction law initiative. Among the most notable are the one that enables reimbursement of invested resources if an Environmental Qualification Resolution (RCA) is revoked in court, the one that grants more powers to the SEA to dismiss rulings from other agencies, and the one that limits the grounds for challenging RCAs.

In addition to being reviewed by the Chamber's Finance Commission, the project will go through the Environment Commission—which has an official majority—an instance that will be key for the Executive's environmental team to explain the reasons behind the major overhaul they want to make to the system.

Led by the ministry's minister, Francisca Toledo, and Undersecretary José Ignacio Vial, the team also includes the executive director of the Environmental Evaluation Service (SEA), Arturo Farías, and the director of the Biodiversity and Protected Areas Service (SBAP), Tomás Saratscheff. They will have to face some criticism from the opposition, which accuses the ministry of being subordinated to Finance.

"I can confirm that the minister and undersecretary have considerable experience and knowledge and will be key in processing the project. I believe they should clearly lead the technical-political dialogue on these issues. In such a complex project, one would hope that all ministries are coordinated," says Horizontal researcher Juan Pablo Escudero.

However, sources linked to the environment suggest there could be tension because both Toledo and Vial hold favorable views of environmental institutions and the SEA. Additionally, Farías is a man who has worked in the Environmental Evaluation Service for eight years and served as metropolitan regional director in the previous administration, so he also understands the entire framework and its importance.

The same sources, however, highlight that the Secretary of State is "studious and thorough" and will be capable of balancing the debate while knowing that the environmental system has room for improvement.

Those familiar with Toledo's working style indicate that if tension exists, it doesn't stem from what is being proposed in the project, but rather from how it is being carried out. They point out that doubts will be cleared up during appearances before the thematic commissions to which the text was referred. Whether Quiroz appears alone or accompanied by the environmental team, for example, could be telling.

These sources, who know Toledo, suggest that the challenge is to demonstrate in the Commission that this is not about more or less environmental protection, but about dismantling the caricatures created around statements by other ministers or the famous "goodbye environmental guides" that President Kast professed during his campaign. They also need to establish that when evaluating projects, costs and benefits must be weighed.

Regarding content, former SEA director Valentina Durán argues that "it is necessary to look at reforms jointly and have an economic perspective, because if we don't, we can make serious errors and may have measures that instead of accelerating investment could make it more complicated and potentially make project authorizations more vulnerable."

Other voices linked to the environment have expressed concern about the expense reimbursement mechanism, which is handled by a commission appointed by Finance that will define the amount to be reimbursed. They say this shows that in this project all actors, including the Environment Ministry, are operating according to what is stated at Teatinos 120 (Finance Ministry headquarters).

Former SBAP director Aarón Cavieres, meanwhile, trusts in the level of professionals in the Service: "We have to see it in action. I know the team will be fully willing to comply with the law in what it seeks and in the perspective that SBAP has, which is to protect nature. And if there's a mismatch, it will probably be for other reasons. I hope it doesn't happen, but for now I prefer to see what happens. I'm fairly confident they have the capacity to face it and hope the government gives them the space for that to occur."

The debate will resume the first week of May—next week is district-focused—and will be the first major test for Minister Toledo and the services in question to argue and defend a project that only bears the signature of the President of the Republic and Minister Quiroz.
