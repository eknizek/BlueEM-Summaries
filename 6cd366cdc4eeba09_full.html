Where is the Dollar Headed in Latin America? Citi Issues New Forecasts

02 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Where is the Dollar Headed in Latin America? These are Citi's New Projections

Citi's Chief Economist for Latin America, Ernesto Revilla, stated that the combination of a weaker dollar along with "relatively elevated" commodity prices has helped the region withstand the effects of the Middle East conflict for now.

"The dollar has weakened significantly since April of last year, losing close to 10%, obviously it has had some fluctuations in recent weeks, but it remains in a range that is relatively weak," said Ernesto Revilla during the presentation of the Economic Outlook.

This environment of U.S. currency weakness has acted as a tailwind for Latin American currencies. "When the dollar is weak, Latin American currencies are strong." This movement, according to Revilla, would have been difficult to anticipate 18 months ago, even in a context of trade and geopolitical tensions. "Probably no one would have believed it."

He also reinforced that the price of major commodities serves as support for local currencies against the dollar. The rise in oil prices benefits several regional producing countries, such as Colombia, Ecuador, Mexico, and Brazil. Additionally, he noted that prices of metals such as gold, silver, and copper remain at favorable levels, which also provides support to countries that export these commodities. In other words, "we have a good external combination that has kept Latin America very resilient," he noted.

## Determinants of the Dollar's Price

"We see a slight depreciation in Latin American currencies toward the end of 2026, but nothing serious, still at levels that are very favorable," according to the Citi economist.

Looking ahead over the coming quarters, the Citi analyst warned that the dollar remains in a phase of equilibrium between two forces. On one hand, possible episodes of greater global risk aversion, such as an escalation in the Middle East, could drive its strengthening as a safe-haven asset. "If the Middle East conflict intensifies and the market becomes very nervous, obviously there will be a flow toward safe assets and that is fundamentally the dollar," he noted.

On the other hand, the interest rate differential between central banks of advanced economies will continue to influence it. He explained that central banks of advanced economies have adopted a more hawkish stance, with messages suggesting higher interest rates for longer. In contrast, the Federal Reserve would shift toward a more dovish policy, with rate cuts in the U.S., especially with the arrival of a new president, Kevin Warsh, who would be more flexible than Jerome Powell.

"If the United States tends to lower interest rates while others raise them, that will tend to weaken the dollar," noted Citi's chief economist for Latin America, Ernesto Revilla.

In this context, Citi expects both effects to offset each other in the short term, keeping the dollar "relatively flat" at least through the third quarter of this year. However, Citi's scenario is that in the fourth quarter the U.S. economy will be influenced by midterm elections. In this context, a "dynamic" economy is expected, with political incentives to keep it strong toward year-end.

Within this framework, toward the last quarter, the bank projects a slight strengthening of the dollar between 3% and 4%, which could translate into slight depreciation pressure for Latin American currencies toward the end of the period.
