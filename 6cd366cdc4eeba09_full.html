# Where is the dollar heading in Latin America? These are Citi's new projections

Ernesto Revilla, chief economist at Citi for Latin America, noted that the combination of a weaker dollar along with "relatively elevated" commodity prices has helped the region so far withstand the effects of the Middle East conflict.

"The dollar has weakened significantly since April of last year, losing close to 10%, obviously it has had some fluctuations in recent weeks, but it remains in a range that is relatively weak," said Ernesto Revilla during the presentation of the Economic Outlook.

This environment of US currency weakening has acted as a tailwind for Latin American currencies. "When the dollar is weak, Latin American currencies are strong." This movement, said Revilla, would have been difficult to anticipate 18 months ago, even in a context of trade and geopolitical tensions. "Probably nobody would have believed it."

He also reinforced that the price of major commodities serves as support for local currencies against the dollar. The rise in oil prices benefits several regional producing countries, such as Colombia, Ecuador, Mexico, and Brazil. He also noted that prices for metals such as gold, silver, and copper remain at favorable levels, which also supports exporting countries for these commodities. In other words, "we have a good external combination that has kept Latin America very resilient," he pointed out.

## Determinants of dollar price

"We see a slight depreciation in Latin American currencies toward the close of 2026, but nothing serious, still at levels that remain very favorable," according to the Citi economist.

Looking ahead to the coming quarters, the Citi analyst warned that the dollar remains in a phase of balance between two forces. On one hand, possible episodes of greater global risk aversion, such as an escalation in the Middle East, could drive its strengthening as a safe-haven asset. "If the Middle East conflict intensifies and the market becomes very nervous, obviously there will be a flow toward safe assets and that is fundamentally the dollar," he noted.

On the other hand, the interest rate differential between central banks in advanced economies will continue to have an influence. He explained that central banks in advanced economies have adopted a more hawkish stance, with messages suggesting higher interest rates for longer. In contrast, the Federal Reserve would shift toward a more dovish policy, with rate cuts in the US, especially with the arrival of a new president, Kevin Warsh, who would be more flexible than Jerome Powell.

"If the United States tends to lower interest rates while others raise them, that will tend to weaken the dollar," noted Citi's chief economist for Latin America, Ernesto Revilla.

In that context, Citi expects both effects to offset each other in the short term, keeping the dollar "relatively flat" at least through the third quarter of this year. However, Citi's scenario is that in the fourth quarter the US economy will be influenced by midterm elections. In that context, a "dynamic" economy is expected, with political incentives to keep it strong toward year-end.

In this framework, toward the last quarter, the bank projects a slight strengthening of the dollar of between 3% and 4%, which could translate into slight depreciation pressure for Latin American currencies toward the end of the period.
