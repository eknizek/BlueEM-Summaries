# Fund uses CBios to guarantee barter operations

Decarbonization Credits (CBios), created so that Brazilian biofuel distributors participating in the RenovaBio program can meet their greenhouse gas emission reduction targets, may be used as a "currency" in a new fund created by the asset manager Iwá. It will be possible to use fund quotas to guarantee barter operations with biofuel plants, agricultural input suppliers, and distributors. Distributors will now be able to buy CBios on credit.

Created in 2024 by Iwá and managed by Daycoval, the Nova Economia multi-market investment fund was structured over the past year and a half to serve as an intermediary between biofuel producers, input companies, and fuel distributors. The idea is that, by trading its quotas with these agents, the fund will turn CBios into a derivative, giving "economic functionality" to this asset and ensuring greater liquidity to the market, according to Heloisa Baldin, a partner at Iwá.

A first pilot operation was carried out at the beginning of the year with the participation of Bayer, of the Japungu group, which has sugarcane ethanol plants in Brazil's Center-South and Northeast regions, and of an undisclosed large fuel distributor. In the operation, Japungu contributed CBios to the fund and granted Bayer shares in the fund in exchange for inputs. On the other end, the Nova Economia fund arranged the sale of the CBios it received from Japungu to a distributor, on credit. Finally, the proceeds from the sale of the CBios were paid to Bayer in exchange for the fund shares that the German company had received from Japungu.

With this structure, the fund enabled the triangulation between the input supplier, the biofuel plant, and the fuel distributor. Baldin explains that this structure allows the CBio to be used as part of a barter operation, which would not be possible without the fund, since the CBio market does not allow the identification of buyers and sellers.

The use of CBio as "currency" in the fund's operations was enabled by Resolution 175 of the Brazilian Securities and Exchange Commission (CVM), from 2023, which classified CBio as a financial asset, and therefore eligible for acquisition by funds.

"The fund is a major intermediary, because we, as Bayer, cannot do this [buy CBios] with the plant and trade the CBio with the distributor due to the secrecy [of the CBio market]," says Pollyana Morais, Bayer's barter operations manager.

According to her, the advantage for the ethanol plants is in securing the supply of inputs with part of their production, locking in part of their cost. For the distributor, the advantage is to buy the CBio, which is mandatory by law, on credit, amortizing the volatility of prices in the physical market, adds Baldin.

Since RenovaBio requires distributors to retire (withdraw from circulation) CBios within the calendar year to prove compliance with that year's target, a distributor that buys a CBio on credit and wants to use it to meet the target for the same year will have to settle the payment by the end of December. If the installment plan for the purchase extends into the following year, the CBio can only be retired when the payment is made.

Iwá and Daycoval expect to expand the scale of these operations and reach R$500 million in CBio transactions per year, equivalent today to about 10% of the financial volume of CBios sold annually.

"The estimate is that this will grow significantly in the coming months," says André Kurbet, head of new business in fiduciary services at Daycoval.
