# Government submits Senate bill to pay $171 million to holdout funds with 10-day approval deadline

Ten years after paying a group of vulture funds that sued Argentina for the 2001 default, the Government is now aiming to finalize an agreement worth $171 million with a handful of plaintiff funds that remained in litigation in American courts.

Through a bill that the Executive Branch sent to the Senate this Friday, the Casa Rosada requested Congressional authorization to pay this amount to a group of vulture funds. The operations are led by Bainbridge and another consortium headed by Attestor Value Master Fund. The lawyers representing Argentina in New York courts, where lawsuits over bonds that defaulted in 2001 were conducted, reached an agreement with the litigants to complete the definitive payment that would close this judicial front. It includes a haircut on the disputed capital.

According to the official text, the transaction contemplates a haircut exceeding 30% on the disputed amounts and fixes payments of $67 million for Bainbridge and $104 million for the Attestor group, under a "delivery versus payment" mechanism, meaning the Government receives the bonds in question for cancellation. Additionally, it is established that legal costs are borne by the creditors.

The agreement was reached in March but has a specific deadline for payment before the possibility of judicial claims could potentially reopen. The bill mentions the "urgency of legislative treatment" since "the agreements establish April 30, 2026 as the closing deadline ('Outside Date')."

"The lack of parliamentary approval after that date would cause the automatic resolution of the transactions, obliging the Argentine Republic to resume litigation under significantly more burdensome financial conditions," stated the document, signed by Justice Minister Juan Bautista Mahiques and Chief of Staff Manuel Adorni.

Another point that the Government highlighted as decisive is that with the approval of this bill, embargo lawsuits on Argentine assets will be dropped. Among other points, the finalization of lawsuits is expected, including attempted embargoes on state stakes in companies such as YPF, Banco Nación and Aerolíneas Argentinas, as well as the closure of asset discovery processes that creditors were pushing forward.

According to the bill's specifications, payment to the two funds in question will be made with the budget allocated for debt service payments.

In April 2016, the Ministry of Finance and Treasury, then headed by Alfonso Prat Gay, announced the closure of an agreement with the main vulture funds suing Argentina in New York courts over unpaid bonds from the 2001 crisis. The plaintiffs at that time had favorable rulings in first and second instance, and a rejection from the U.S. Supreme Court to reconsider the appeals court ruling, so the courts determined that the country had to negotiate an exit through an agreed payment with the funds. One of the negotiators was Luis Caputo, then Secretary of Finance. The two main funds were NML Capital and Aurelius Capital Management.

To complete the payment, Congress approved the Sovereign Payment Law and the repeal of the "lock" and "sovereign payment" provisions that prevented improving the terms offered in previous exchanges. To obtain these funds, Mauricio Macri's Government issued bonds in international markets for approximately $16.5 billion, in what was one of the largest placements by an emerging country at that time. It represented Argentina's return to the international debt market.

The payment was completed on April 22, 2016, and agreements with holdouts worth around $9.3 billion were settled. A group of smaller funds chose to stay out of that understanding and continued in courts seeking a more favorable agreement. The vulture fund business consisted of buying defaulted securities at very low prices after their value collapsed and then litigating in New York to demand full payment. According to official estimates, at the time the agreement closed, the operation yielded up to 1000% profit for the holdouts.
