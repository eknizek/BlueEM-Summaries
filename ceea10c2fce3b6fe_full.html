# Rappi Issues Strong Warning to Delivery Workers After Protests: "Platform Safety is Non-Negotiable"

The protests by delivery workers recorded on Monday, April 20 in northern Bogotá occur amid the impact of the recent sanction by the Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) against the Rappi delivery application, which exceeds $4 billion. The decision, motivated by service failures, unauthorized charges, and problems in user service, reignited tensions within the platform's ecosystem.

In this context, delivery workers denounced alleged "arbitrary account blockages," which, according to them, directly affects their income and working conditions. The demonstrations concentrated on strategic points in the city, making visible a discontent that goes beyond the sanction and points to the relationship between the company and those who operate as delivery workers.

## Rappi's Response

In response to the situation, Rappi reiterated its institutional position, stating that it receives the authorities' decisions "with respect" and that it maintains willingness to work jointly on improving the service. The company also defended the scale of its operations in the country, affirming that it "operates millions of orders daily," the vast majority completed satisfactorily. However, it acknowledged that there are aspects to improve: "every case counts and we must continuously improve our users' experience," it stated in its pronouncement.

In parallel, the company confirmed that it will file legal remedies against the sanction and will continue investing in technology and strengthening its service channels, with the objective of reducing response times and resolving service failures.

## Structural Tensions in the Model

The background of the conflict reveals deeper tensions regarding the business model of digital platforms. The SIC has maintained that Rappi does not act solely as an intermediary, but as a provider, having control over payments, deliveries, and customer service, which implies greater responsibilities toward users and allies.

In this scenario, delivery workers' complaints about blockages and lack of guarantees add to regulatory questioning, broadening the debate on working conditions in the platform economy. While the protests evidence discontent at the operational base, the company's response points toward defending its functioning and correcting failures without altering the core of its model. The outcome of this tension—between regulation, operation, and working conditions—could set the course for digital platforms in Colombia.
