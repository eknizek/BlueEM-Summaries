# Buy and Trade: How Vinland is Navigating Equity Volatility

"Buy and hold" may have once been a common stock strategy among fundamentalist managers. But in such a dynamic and volatile world, where a tweet can change the direction of the stock market or the dollar, this is out of the question at Vinland. "Here it's buy and trade, sell and buy again. We rotate the portfolio quite a bit," says Rodrigo Andrade, partner and head of equities at the firm, in an interview with Pipeline. He is keeping an eye on short-term decisions and fluctuations but also on structural challenges – making tactical adjustments to the portfolio as he evaluates, for example, which sectors will be most affected by costs if the project changing the 6x1 work schedule is approved and which operations will gain or lose with the advancement of artificial intelligence.

Andrade also cites the "culture of workarounds," ranging from the growth of betting sites as an attempt at short-term gains to the corruption scandal at Banco Master, and the still unforeseen consequences of the war in the Middle East, whose only consensus is more inflation – this while a series of corporate crises, of different sizes, are frightening stock and credit investors, including Braskem, Oncoclínicas, Raízen, and Aegea. "These are the main themes we think about in allocation decisions. We evaluate the macro, but also the capillarity of these events and how global and local trends permeate our decision-making," says Andrade. "Which companies are prepared to surf this new reality in a way that allows them to survive?"

Exposure to the Brazilian stock market was reduced after the recent rally, driven not only by prices but also by the performance of big techs abroad – which led to swap operations, such as selling TOTVS to buy Microsoft, which traded at similar multiples. "In the consumer sector, given budget constraints from higher family indebtedness, high interest rates, and global inflationary outlook, we also don't have large allocations," says the Vinland partner, emphasizing that the projection on the interest rate curve changed from a reduction of 350 basis points to 150 after the war broke out – which has also led to deterioration in agribusiness balance sheets, with an increase in defaults that already impacts banks like BB.

"But we increased our position in defensive, regulated sectors, such as services adjusted for inflation and companies with the ability to generate high and stable dividends, such as utilities and telecom," says the manager. One of the names is Equatorial, which is already a shareholder in Sabesp and may compete for a position in the privatization of the mining company Copasa. The manager also mentions road concessions. "And, although linked to consumption, shopping centers also benefit from inflation pass-through in rents and a percentage of sales, and are seen as a safe haven for family leisure. Allos, which has active portfolio management, has a dividend yield of 12%," exemplifies Andrade about a portfolio holding.

The weak dollar call is still a relevant bet for the firm, but Andrade evaluates that Donald Trump will have quite a challenge in the second half, with the midterm elections – which could lead to changes in his political and economic strategy, already in a period close to Brazilian elections, which promises volatility. "The oil and gas sector still doesn't fully reflect the new level of oil prices, which are unlikely to return to levels of $60 and $70. Oil isn't jumping $20 in a day, but rises $2 every day," he says.

Brazil, with pre-sal and new frontiers like the Amazon Basin and the potential revitalization of Venezuela, could benefit from a new geopolitical rearrangement of investments in the post-war period, he evaluates. Vinland divested from Brava Energia after the announcement of Ecopetrol's entry and maintains positions in Petrobras and Prio. Andrade avoids companies with governance problems or uncertain turnarounds, such as Hapvida, and prefers Rede D'Or in the health sector. The approach is skeptical about IPOs, given the catastrophic track record of recent batches, but there are exceptions: Vinland invested and continues to bet on Orizon, which has a circular economy model and consolidation in waste management.

The manager considers that the flow of foreign investors tends to continue in the coming months in the country, and one of the thermometers for this is the increased demand from institutional investors for meetings and due diligence processes at local managers, including Vinland. "We've had quite a lot of interest from foreigners wanting to talk to us, from different regions, and with proposals in different formats," says the fund's partner. They range from first loss structures to portfolio replication and direct allocations from sovereign wealth funds. This is because, after almost a decade in which concentration of investments in the United States proved to be a highly successful strategy, Trump's modus operandi has led global managers to diversify their portfolios.

After almost a decade in which concentrating everything in the United States was the winning strategy, large global allocators realized they need to diversify. What could drain this flow, as seen last week, is a resumption of allocations to big techs, if American companies in the sector continue to beat consensus results, he ponders. "The perception of risk by foreign investors regarding Brazil is different from local investors. Issues such as fiscal risk and the electoral scenario, which generate local apprehension, are relativized by foreigners, who see fiscal problems as a post-pandemic global phenomenon. A potential fourth term for Lula, for example, is seen as 'more of the same'," he evaluates.

But, for the manager, it's too early for any electoral trade in Brazil. "The confrontation hasn't really started yet and there are two main candidates with high rejection rates, which makes the scenario indicated in surveys somewhat fragile still," he evaluates.
