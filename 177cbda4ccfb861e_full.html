# Without CEO and Under Cash Pressure, Berkmann Reviews Premium Wine Strategy in Brazil

Charles Marshall began lunch at the Cala del Tanit restaurant in São Paulo, trying to break the tension building around British wine importer Berkmann Wine Cellars' operations in Brazil. "As the old saying goes, any publicity is good publicity," said the company's strategy director, a member of the controlling holding's board, before loosening his tie and discussing the operation in the country. The irony was pointed. Days earlier, the CEO of the operation in Brazil, Mariano Levy, had prematurely announced his departure on social media after just six months. The news generated noise in the country's premium wine market and fueled rumors about the health of the importer's operation.

During a press conference focused on the company's business, Marshall said he had advanced his trip to Brazil to counter the rumors and formalize a fundamental restructuring of Berkmann's operation in the country.

He admitted cash flow pressure due to excess inventory but denied crisis, said Levy's departure was already stipulated in the contract as a temporary position, and defended the importance of Brazil's operation—small in relation to the British one.

Berkmann has 65 years in the United Kingdom, revenue of £118 million, 220 employees, and over 100 exclusive agencies in its portfolio. According to him, the British importer, present in Brazil since 2015, will no longer have a local CEO. The operation will be coordinated directly from London by Marshall and Rupert Berkmann, global CEO and second generation of the founding family, in collaboration with senior Brazilian leadership. Nevertheless, "nothing changes" regarding the importance of Brazil to the company, he repeated more than once during the interview.

The decision seeks to close a cycle of instability in leadership. In the second half of 2025, Paulo Bruno Cordeiro left the operation after ten years. Levy, co-founder of Grand Cru, took over with the stated goal of bringing Brazilian revenue to R$ 100 million in 2026 (up from R$ 74 million the previous year). In April, he left pointing to a 63% sales growth in the first quarter.

When discussing the new structure, Marshall did not mention the R$ 100 million target for 2026 that Levy had announced in an interview with Bloomberg Línea. The focus is now different. It is about concentrating efforts on a few global brands and developing penetration within each of them.

"Levy entered into a temporary contract to really stabilize the boat," Marshall said. The executive credited Levy with the commercial focus that unlocked the quarter. After his departure, there will be no replacement leading Brazil in the short term, he said. "Rupert and I will be in the market," Marshall said. "We have a very strong senior leadership team, and the strategy is already defined. It's about execution."

Marshall directly addressed rumors about the company's financial difficulties. He acknowledged the problem without qualifying it as a crisis. "Yes, we've had a cash flow squeeze as a company, and there's no secret about it," he said. "Tell me one company in today's market that doesn't have some cash flow problem. I would be amazed."

According to the executive, the cause was a calibration error. The company bought labels based on an optimistic reading of the market and accumulated SKUs that did not match demand. "We had too many wines in our portfolio that were not aligned with where the market is today," he explained. "They sit idle in the warehouse attracting costs and draining cash."

The solution was to slim down the portfolio and rely on support from the British parent company to get through the period. "Strategically, we streamlined our portfolio to truly focus on brand building and leading key strategic agents."

Despite admitting difficulties, Marshall justifies maintaining the bet on Brazil in terms of margin. The wine sector in the United Kingdom operates with returns between 3% and 6%, according to the executive. In Brazil, with the adjusted operation, margins can reach double digits. "It's a dynamic market, compared to the United Kingdom, where everything is negative," he said. "Here, it's like breathing fresh air."

According to him, despite still being a small operation in Berkmann's context, Brazil also fits the company's search for international markets with long-term growth potential. "We were looking for markets that are rapidly 'gentrifying.' With scale, given that it has over 200 million people, and that truly have quite a European palate."

He also admitted an alignment of consumer profile between Brazil and the United Kingdom, two countries without much winemaking tradition and where the market is open to labels from around the world—in places like France and Italy, consumption is almost entirely dominated by wines from their own country.

The executive also evaluated the importance of the Mercosur-European Union agreement, which enters into force this Friday (1st), for advancing the importer's wines in Brazil. According to him, the change could favor European labels, which will compete on more favorable terms with those from Latin America. "It will make much of our portfolio, particularly the European side, much more attractive and competitive, without losing the premium and luxury positioning," he said.

He also explained that the on-trade channel, which accounts for only 15% of Brazilian wine consumption, appears as another external variable with potential to accelerate the thesis.

However, execution depends on the new structure without local leadership functioning. "When you run remote operations, you need boots on the ground to understand what's happening. Managing things from afar is always a challenge."

Italian winery Antinori is the centerpiece for Berkmann's positioning. Marshall was emphatic in denying rumors that the Tuscan house would leave the importer in Brazil. "They are not leaving Berkmann," he said. "We've worked with them for 30 years in the United Kingdom. We are two family businesses that work well together."

According to data from Ideal BI consultancy, Villa Antinori is the Italian label with the highest sales revenue in Brazil, and Tignanello (from the same brand) appears in third place. Marshall argues that there are still 19 brands with low penetration in the country that could gain traction. Similar logic applies to other international brands the company works with, such as Familia Torres, Viña Montes, Riccitelli, and others.
