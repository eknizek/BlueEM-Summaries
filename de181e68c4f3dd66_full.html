# Beef prices decline 10%, reducing inflation pressure from livestock sector

In recent weeks, the mentioned scarcity of animal supply has not been acute enough to counteract the crisis in beef consumption—which fell to 47.3 kg per person per year, the lowest level in two decades—and fat cattle prices have entered a readjustment phase that has resulted in declines of around 10%.

The question now is whether this drop will be reflected on butcher shop boards.

Johnny Perkins, a market operator, states in dialogue with Clarín Rural that as of April "prices began to calm down." "It was a product of the strong increase that occurred in February and March. After that, meat sales on the street began showing strong resistance to increases and, as expected, this had an impact on the market. Prices began to fall. I would say we had a drop of between 8% and 10% from that moment until today, and the outlook doesn't seem likely to change for now," he describes.

In mid-March, the INMAG (Novillo Market Agro-Livestock Index), the reference indicator for Argentina's livestock market, was at 4,492 pesos. In the last week, however, it stood at 4,166 pesos.

A recent Rosgan report details that if you observe fat cattle values in the MAG from the second half of last year to the present, the change in trend is evident. "In constant terms, between October 2025 and February of this year—when historic highs were reached—steers rose 28%, while young steers and heifers rose 32% and 33%, respectively. Since that peak, in March and during the first twenty days of April, prices began to correct: steers fell 9% and young steers and heifers fell 6%," it indicates.

The entity explains that historically, March and April typically concentrate the largest price increases for fat cattle, given the greater demand fluidity that transfers from retail counters. "However, in this campaign the adjustment occurred earlier in February and to a magnitude that consumption cannot completely absorb," it warns.

From the retail perspective, the diagnosis is similar. "For a long time meat was very expensive and that caused many people to turn to chicken or pork. Today, for example, eating beef barbecue or flank steak has become almost a luxury. Some specific cuts sell more, but there isn't general demand. There are customers who keep buying without looking too closely at the price, but also many who come, ask, and end up taking other meats because beef seems expensive to them," says Romina Uz, a butcher in the Buenos Aires neighborhoods of Belgrano and Saavedra.

## Maybe it won't drop, but at least it won't increase

Whether the drop in livestock prices will transfer to butcher shop prices is uncertain. What is highly probable is that beef prices will no longer increase in the short term.

In a recent report, the president of the Federation of Regional Argentine Refrigeration Industries (Fifra), Daniel Urcía, described how the animal protein consumption matrix has changed: "Poultry and pork meat are definitely incorporated into the diet and when discussing meat consumption, the three species must inevitably be considered, also for measuring the inflation index in which beef has strong weight over others," he said.

And he added: "The price of beef will stop being news in May when it no longer has an impact on constructing the consumer price index."

According to Urcía, chicken meat consumption is 50 kilos per inhabitant per year, and if you add 20 kilos of pork meat, consumption of these meats is 60% higher than beef. "Therefore, measuring the evolution of their prices—which was much lower than beef—would do justice to measuring that macro indicator and would reduce pressure on the meat business," he says.

For Ariel Morales Anton, president of the Chamber of Slaughterers and Suppliers (CAMyA), the recent drop in livestock prices could partially transfer to retail counters, although not automatically or in equal proportion. "This is because within the chain there are costs—such as logistics, energy, and salaries—that have not decreased and continue to pressure the final price. Additionally, consumption remains weak, which generates caution in different links when adjusting prices," he explains.

In this context, Morales Anton considers that any correction in retail prices will be partial and gradual. "If the downward trend in livestock prices consolidates, it could begin to reflect more clearly in consumer prices in the coming weeks," he adds.

Perkins agrees that a consumption rebound will not be immediate. "Sales remain stuck: Doña Rosa doesn't have money to spend and, besides, there is strong competition from other meats like chicken and pork, with very competitive prices. The outlook is discouraging for now; we'll have to wait for the economy in general to settle a bit more," he warns.

Uz, for her part, adds her daily business perspective: "For us as merchants the situation is complicated. When prices go up, it's not just meat: chicken, pork, rent, and other costs also increase. So you have to adapt to all of that, not just demand. What I see is more cautious consumption and very conditioned by prices."
