Beef Prices No Longer Adding Pressure to General Inflation

23 April 2026 · 06:54 PM

# Beef Prices No Longer Adding Pressure to General Inflation

In recent weeks, the mentioned scarcity of animal supply was not sufficiently acute to counteract the crisis in beef consumption—which fell to 47.3 kg per person per year, the lowest level in two decades—and fattened cattle prices entered a readjustment phase that, so far, has resulted in declines of around 10%.

The question now is whether that drop will be reflected on butcher shop price boards. Johnny Perkins, a market operator, states in conversation with Clarín Rural that starting in April "prices began to calm down." "It was a product of the sharp increase that occurred in February and March. After that, meat sales on the street began to show strong resistance to increases and, as was to be expected, that had an impact on the market. Prices began to fall. I would say we had a drop of between 8% and 10% from that moment until today, and the outlook doesn't seem like it will change for now," he describes.

Indeed, in mid-March the INMAG (Novillo Market Agricultural-Livestock Index), the reference indicator for Argentina's livestock market, was at 4,492 pesos. In the last week, however, it positioned itself at 4,166 pesos.

A recent Rosgan report details that if you look at fattened cattle values in the MAG from the second half of last year until now, the change in trend is evident. "In constant terms, between October 2025 and February of this year—when historical highs were reached—cattle increased by 28%, while young cattle and heifers did so by 32% and 33%, respectively. From that peak, in March and during the first twenty days of April, prices began to correct: cattle fell back 9% and young cattle and heifers 6%," it indicates.

The entity explains that historically, March and April usually concentrate the largest price increases for fattened cattle, given the greater demand fluidity that transfers from retail counters. "However, in this campaign the adjustment came early in February and to a magnitude that consumption cannot completely absorb," it warns.

From the retail counter, the diagnosis is similar. "For a long time meat was very expensive and that caused many people to turn to chicken or pork. Today, for example, eating grilled meat or flank steak has become almost a luxury. Some specific cuts sell better, but there isn't generalized demand. There are customers who keep buying without looking too much at the price, but also many who come in, ask questions, and end up taking other meats because beef is too expensive for them," says Romina Uz, a butcher in the Buenos Aires neighborhoods of Belgrano and Saavedra.

## Perhaps It Won't Go Down, But At Least It Won't Go Up

Whether the drop in livestock prices will be passed on to meat prices at the counter is a mystery. What is highly probable is that beef prices will no longer increase in the short term. In a recent report, the president of the Federation of Regional Argentine Refrigeration Industries (Fifra), Daniel Urcía, described how the matrix of animal protein consumption changed: "Poultry and pork meat are definitely incorporated into the diet and when talking about meat consumption, the three species must inevitably be considered, also for measuring the inflation index in which beef has strong weight over the others," he said.

And he added: "The price of beef will stop being news in May when it no longer has an impact on the construction of the consumer price index."

According to Urcía's detail, chicken meat consumption is 50 kilos per inhabitant per year, and if you add 20 of pork meat, the intake of these meats is 60% higher than beef. "Therefore, measuring the evolution of their prices—which was much lower than beef—would do justice to that macro indicator measurement and would reduce pressure on the meat business," he says.

For Ariel Morales Anton, president of the Chamber of Slaughterers and Suppliers (CAMyA), the recent drop in livestock prices could be partially passed on to retail counters, although not automatically or in equal proportion. "This is because within the supply chain there are costs—such as logistics, energy, and salaries—that have not decreased and continue to put pressure on the final price. Furthermore, consumption remains weak, which generates caution in the different links when adjusting prices," he explains.

In this context, Morales Anton believes that any correction in retail prices will be partial and gradual. "If the downward trend in livestock consolidates, it could begin to be reflected more clearly in consumer prices in the coming weeks," he adds.

Perkins agrees that a rebound in consumption will not be immediate. "Sales remain stuck: Doña Rosa doesn't have money to spend and, besides, there is strong competition from other meats like chicken and pork, with very competitive prices. The outlook is not encouraging right now; we'll have to wait for the general economy to stabilize a bit more," he warns.

Uz, for her part, adds the day-to-day perspective of the business: "For us as merchants the situation is complicated. When prices go up, it's not just meat: chicken, pork, rent, and other costs also increase. So you have to adapt to all of that, not just demand. What I see is more cautious consumption and very conditioned by prices."
