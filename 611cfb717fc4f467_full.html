# Felipe Harboe: "Companies Purchasing Clipping Services Must Require Agencies to Comply with Law"

In Chile, as in other countries, there is a market for press clipping services where companies and communications agencies group news published in different media outlets and commercialize them without paying copyright fees, violating the Intellectual Property Law. However, those who contract these services also bear responsibility and could face the same fines and penalties.

This argument is part of the analysis presented by Felipe Harboe, senior partner at H&CO Abogados and former senator, in a column published in La Tercera and elaborated in an interview with DF, where he warned that both clipping service providers and the organizations that hire them would be violating the law.

"Companies that purchase clipping services must require providers (companies and communications agencies) to comply with the law; otherwise, they could be participating in an illegal activity," Harboe stated.

What should happen, he noted, is that service provider companies pay licensing fees to media outlets, as occurs in other countries. Article 79 of the Intellectual Property Law establishes fines of up to 1,000 UTM—approximately $69.8 million—and minimum sentences of imprisonment between 61 and 540 days for violators.

"The impact of technological changes on traditional advertising models and clipping services that do not pay licensing fees could affect the continuity of media outlets."

**Where does your concern about clipping originate?**

First, under the Intellectual Property Law (17.336), news articles are considered intellectual creations and are therefore protected by law. So we asked ourselves at the law firm: What happens with systems that group news by topic and sell them? Second, technological change has had a very strong impact on the traditional advertising model of media outlets. That, combined with clipping services that sell others' works without paying licensing fees, could affect the continuity of media outlets and democracy.

**What crime could communications agencies and companies offering this service be committing?**

Article 19 of Law 17.336 protects the author's exclusive right to reproduce their work by any means. Therefore, reproduction without authorization is illegal and gives rise to fines and the obligation to compensate the damages caused to the author. Articles 79 and following establish fines of up to 1,000 UTM, classifying it as a crime when works are used without authorization from the holder or without payment of corresponding royalties for profit, with a sentence of minimum imprisonment—that is, 61 to 540 days.

Those who sell clipping services are violating intellectual property regulations. And those who purchase these services could be sanctioned, because the Economic Crimes Law (21.595) recognizes the Intellectual Property Law violation as a base crime and assumes that whoever contracted that service knew or should have known that it was being offered with legal infraction. Additionally, Law 20.393—regarding criminal liability of legal persons in the crimes it indicates—extends liability to the legal entity, that is, to the purchasing company.

For this reason, those who contract clipping services must conduct prior due diligence and require their providers to comply with the Intellectual Property Law and the licensing system for journalistic works they reproduce. Otherwise, they could be accomplices to an illegal act and their responsibility could be pursued. In fact, many government ministries have clipping services. So government authorities will also have to review whether service provider companies are complying with rights payments as they should. And if not, they should review contract continuity.

**How have other countries addressed the protection of media journalistic content?**

In Europe, there is an entire system with licensing payments to rights management societies. Since 2019, there is Directive 2019/790 from the European Parliament and the Council of the European Union. Countries like Spain, France, Germany, Italy, and Belgium already have it regulated. France was the pioneer, and in 2021, it achieved agreements with Google, for example, signed with the French press association and French TV groups. These discussions occur ahead of time in countries with higher development levels and greater intellectual property awareness. In Chile, unfortunately, intellectual property is viewed as a second-category right, and that is a cultural problem we must overcome. Societies that advance in recognizing intellectual property foster creation and innovation, because creations are respected.
