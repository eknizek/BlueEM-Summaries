What scenario are you buying when you invest?

02 May 2026 · 09:00 PM

# What Scenario Are You Buying When You Invest?

Three friends receive the same news: interest rates are high. The first rushes to invest in fixed-rate bonds, locking in future interest rates. The second invests in bonds indexed to IPCA (official inflation). The third decides to follow monetary policy decisions and goes with bonds indexed to CDI and Selic. In the end, each one believes they are being prudent and conservative in their choice. After all, all three will earn an interest rate on their investments and are protecting themselves against what concerns them most. But in practice, each one is making a different bet about the future, which may be more or less favorable than the other two alternatives.

Investing is never neutral. The risk lies in not understanding what you are choosing to protect yourself against and which scenario you are investing for. The suggestion to highlight these conditions came from friend and reader Felipe Avezani.

In any of the choices, it is important to understand what the market already considers in the prices being traded. For example, just because you believe the Selic will fall does not mean you invest in a fixed-rate bond. You must first know what the market already factors in regarding future rate drops.

So let's start with the fixed-rate bond. In fixed-rate bonds, there is no sense of shelter. There is conviction. You look at the rate available today and decide to lock it in because you believe that in the future, it will no longer be there. In other words, you believe the market will be surprised by stronger drops in the Selic rate. It is a choice that rewards those who anticipate interest rate cuts. But it also exposes the investor to an inevitable discomfort. For example, at this moment, the public bond "Tesouro Prefixado 2029," maturing on 12/31/2028, is traded at 13.69% per year. But the Selic is at 14.5% per year. Therefore, you start with a return lower than the Selic and will only gain over it if it falls below 13% per year quickly enough so that the average until the end of 2028 is lower than the contracted rate. The mistake here does not appear only at maturity. It appears along the way, testing the discipline of those who may have believed too early.

When you choose a bond tied to IPCA, the feeling can be one of protection. But protection against what? By choosing this bond, you are protecting yourself for an adverse scenario where inflation surprises. But you are also making a bet similar to the one mentioned in the fixed-rate bond. Only on real interest rates. But there is an uncomfortable detail: if the scenario turns out to be more stable than imagined—inflation controlled and higher real interest rates—your bond's return could fall below the CDI. The protection exists, but it charges a premium.

And then comes the CDI, often treated as the absence of opinion. Or protection against market fluctuations. But there is no absence of opinion in the market. When you invest in floating-rate bonds, you are, in practice, believing that the market is already too optimistic about the Central Bank's moves and therefore prefers to follow monetary policy decisions. Or that the Central Bank will not be surprised by a jump in inflation, causing the real interest rate implicit in the Selic to be lower than expected. That is, you are betting that inflation may remain under pressure, above target, but still controlled, with the economy still heated, and that members of the Central Bank's Monetary Policy Committee will be committed to keeping interest rates elevated longer than the market expects. The problem is that this choice also has consequences. If interest rates fall faster than expected, the profitability of those who chose this indexer falls along with it.

Notice that none of the three investors is "right" by definition. They simply chose different scenarios they wished to protect themselves against or expose themselves to. This is where the central point lies. The investor rarely fails due to lack of access to information. They fail because they do not understand the nature of the decision itself. IPCA, fixed-rate, or CDI are not just indexers. They are distinct ways of positioning yourself in the face of uncertainty. And ignoring this does not eliminate risk—it only makes it invisible.

In the end, investing in fixed income does not avoid bets. You must consciously choose which risk you are willing to carry and protect yourself. No matter which indexer you chose to concentrate your investments, the greatest risk, almost always, is believing that you are not running any risk at all.
