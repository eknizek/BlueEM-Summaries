What scenario are you buying when you invest?

02 May 2026 · 09:00 PM

# What Scenario Are You Buying When You Invest?

Three friends receive the same news: interest rates are high. The first rushes to invest in fixed-rate securities, locking in future interest rates. The second invests in securities indexed to IPCA (official inflation). The third decides to follow monetary policy decisions and stays with securities indexed to CDI and Selic. In the end, each believes they are being prudent and conservative in their choice. After all, all three will earn an interest rate on their investments and are protecting themselves against what concerns them most. But in practice, each one is making a different bet about the future, which can be more or less favorable than the other two alternatives.

Investing is never neutral. The risk lies in not understanding what you are choosing to protect yourself against and what scenario you are investing for. The suggestion to highlight these conditions came from friend and reader Felipe Avezani. In any of the choices, it is important to understand what the market already considers in negotiated prices. For example, it is not because you believe the Selic will fall that you invest in a fixed-rate security. You must first know what the market already considers regarding future rate decreases in negotiated rates.

So let's start with the fixed-rate security. In the fixed-rate option, there is no sense of shelter. There is conviction. You look at the rate available today and decide to lock it in because you believe that in the future it will no longer be there. In other words, you believe the market will be surprised by stronger Selic rate cuts. It is a choice that rewards those who anticipate interest rate movements. But it also exposes the investor to inevitable discomfort. For example, at this moment, the public security Tesouro Prefixado 2029, maturing on 12/31/2028, is traded at 13.69% per year. But the Selic is at 14.5% per year. Therefore, you start with a return lower than the Selic and will only gain from it if it falls below 13% per year quickly enough that the average through the end of 2028 is less than the contracted rate. The error here does not appear only at maturity. It appears along the way, testing the discipline of those who may have believed too early.

When you choose a security tied to IPCA, the feeling can be one of protection. But protection against what? By choosing this security, you are protecting yourself for an adverse scenario in which inflation surprises. But you are also making a bet similar to the one mentioned in the fixed-rate security. Only on real interest rates. But there is an uncomfortable detail: if the scenario is more stable than imagined—controlled inflation and higher real interest rates—your security's return could fall below the CDI. Protection exists, but it charges a premium.

And then comes the CDI, often treated as the absence of opinion. Or protection against market fluctuations. But there is no absence of opinion in the market. By investing in post-fixed securities, you are practically believing that the market is already too optimistic about the Central Bank's movements and therefore prefers to follow monetary policy. Or that the Central Bank will not be surprised by a jump in inflation, causing the real interest rate implicit in the Selic to be lower than expected. That is, you are betting that inflation may remain pressured, above target, but still controlled, with the economy still heated, and that members of the Central Bank's Monetary Policy Committee will be impartial in maintaining elevated interest rates longer than the market expects. The problem is that this choice also has consequences. If interest rates fall faster than expected, the profitability of those who chose this indexer falls with it.

Notice that none of the three investors is "right" by definition. They simply chose different scenarios against which they desired to protect themselves or be exposed. This is where the central point lies. The investor rarely errs from lack of access to information. They err from not understanding the nature of their own decision. IPCA, fixed-rate, or CDI are not just indexers. They are distinct ways of positioning yourself in the face of uncertainty. And ignoring this does not eliminate risk—it only makes it invisible.

In the end, investing in fixed income does not avoid bets. You must consciously choose what risk you are willing to carry and protect yourself accordingly. No matter which indexer you chose to concentrate your investments in, the greatest risk, almost always, is believing that you are not taking any risk at all.
