# US Stock Futures Rise Driven by Tech Earnings; Oil Advances

U.S. stock index futures advanced, driven by late-hour gains from tech giants, as the artificial intelligence sector remained a key engine for markets, while oil prices surged amid growing uncertainty about the war with Iran.

Nasdaq 100 index contracts, which are heavily weighted toward technology, rose 0.9%, and S&P 500 index contracts rose 0.4%, in early Asian trading, following broadly positive results from megacap companies. Shares of Alphabet Inc. (GOOGL) and Amazon.com Inc. (AMZN) climbed in after-hours trading, while Meta Platforms Inc. (META) plummeted over spending concerns. Chip maker Qualcomm Inc. (QCOM) surged 13% following advances in the data center market. Samsung Electronics Co. earnings also beat estimates.

Brent crude rose 1.9% at the Thursday open to $120.30 per barrel, after rebounding Wednesday to its highest level since June 2022. Oil is rising without signals of progress toward resolving the war with Iran that has roiled global markets following the near-closure of the crucial Strait of Hormuz.

Asian stocks fell at the open. Rising oil prices and the Federal Reserve's hawkish stance weighed on Treasury bonds, with the 10-year bond yield rising more than eight basis points to 4.43% during the U.S. session. Australian bonds followed Treasury bonds lower in early Thursday trading, as expensive oil fueled inflation concerns. The 10-year Japanese bond yield rose to its highest level since 1997.

"As the Magnificent Seven's earnings come in, the money printing of their businesses and their profitability stands in stark contrast to the story of U.S. debt and yields that keep rising," said Martin Whetton, head of financial market strategy at Westpac Banking Corp.

From the Iran war-driven oil price spike to a divided Federal Reserve holding rates steady and megacap tech profits, traders face a barrage of outsize headlines. With oil climbing to four-year highs and 10-year Treasury bond yields at their highest since July, the backdrop tests a global equity rebound that has erased war-related losses and driven U.S. markets to new highs.

On Thursday, the European Central Bank and the Bank of England will announce their policy decisions. Following that, Apple Inc. (AAPL) is scheduled to report earnings. More U.S. economic data will then be released.

In other market corners, the yen strengthened slightly in early Thursday trading after the currency extended its decline beyond 160 per dollar to its weakest mark this year, raising the risk that authorities may intervene in the market to provide support. Gold rose 0.3% to $4,560 per ounce, while bitcoin advanced to $75,790.

Amazon.com Inc. (AMZN) shares rose 5% in after-hours trading after reporting the strongest growth in its cloud unit's sales in more than three years. Alphabet Inc. also gained in late trading after reporting quarterly revenue and earnings that beat projections. Meta plummeted 6.5% due to growing concerns about AI spending. Additionally, Anthropic PBC has begun weighing a new funding round that would value the artificial intelligence developer at more than $900 billion, according to people familiar with the matter, potentially surpassing longtime rival OpenAI as the world's most valuable AI startup.

Oil movements came as President Donald Trump told Axios he will not lift a naval blockade of Iran's ports until he secures a deal with Tehran to address the country's nuclear program, extending a standoff over the Strait of Hormuz that has caused a global energy crisis.

The widespread market shift toward forecasting a prolonged conflict has intensified focus on U.S. supplies, now more crucial than ever to offset disruptions to Middle East flows. Government data released Wednesday shows national oil reserves are declining while U.S. exports reach historic highs.

"The longer the strait stays closed, the higher prices will go," said Dennis Kissler, senior vice president of trading at BOK Financial Securities Inc. "A prolonged holding pattern is a near-term bullish catalyst for crude prices, however, it may just be the recipe needed to bring the conflict to an end."

Earlier, the Fed held rates unchanged but revealed an increasingly deep division over monetary policy prospects. Traders have virtually abandoned bets on a rate cut this year and have begun pricing in the possibility of a hike in 2027. Fed officials tweaked their statement, saying that "developments in the Middle East are contributing to a high level of uncertainty about the economic outlook." They repeated language about the "pace and timing of additional" rate adjustments.

Jerome Powell's press conference was his last leading the central bank after the Department of Justice dropped a controversial criminal investigation into the Fed, clearing the way for Senate confirmation of Kevin Warsh as the next chairman. Powell said he will remain at the central bank as a governor.

The meeting revealed an increasingly deep division. Cleveland Federal Reserve President Beth Hammack, along with Neel Kashkari of Minneapolis and Lorie Logan of Dallas, "supported maintaining the target range for the federal funds rate, but did not support inclusion of an easing bias in the statement."
