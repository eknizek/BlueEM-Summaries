# 'No Matter How Powerful AI Is, That Tool Has Limits'

Interview with renowned consultant Patrick Dixon, an expert in examining global trends to project long-term scenarios.

Patrick Dixon studied medicine at the University of Cambridge in England and even practiced it for a time. But really, Patrick Dixon describes himself as a futurist—that is, someone who examines current trends and projects scenarios two or three decades ahead, which serve companies in different sectors to plan long-term investments. Author of eighteen books, some of which have made bestseller lists, he is the keynote speaker at the Fourth Infrastructure Meeting organized by the National Development Finance Corporation, which will take place in Bogotá this week. Prior to the event, EL TIEMPO interviewed the British expert exclusively. This is the edited version of that conversation.

You are known as someone specialized in looking at the future and discerning what is to come. Is it valid to say that this work has become more difficult in these times?

I would say yes and no. It's true that when you hear the news or read the headlines, the message you receive is that there is chaos in the world. There is no doubt that it is impossible to predict what Donald Trump will do tomorrow, to speak of a well-known case. But I have been in this profession for 30 years and I advise companies that make substantial decisions, such as those in the pharmaceutical sector. My work consists of laying out scenarios until the middle of the century or beyond, essential to justify investments with long-term returns. In general, the conclusion is that betting on a better future is the right choice.

Is that valid for all sectors?

At least for the majority. In the energy field, the same exercise is valid, as it is in fiber optic networks or in the development of a port. It sounds risky with so much volatility, but when you have perspective, the vision is different.

Why?

Because the horizon is bigger than Trump, to return to the same example. Presidents come and go, as do oil crises or even wars. The irrefutable fact is that the history of humanity continues and the world keeps moving forward. In that sense, the importance of good infrastructure remains very high, something that ranges from closing gaps behind us to adapting and responding to new needs in that field.

We are in a time of many transformations…

Undoubtedly, but most take a long time. And there are also wrong predictions. At the beginning of this century we were told that printed books were going to disappear and there they are—which, by the way, is good for me because I dedicate myself to writing books. Actually, I think it has a great future. So we need some common sense when looking at what's coming.

How does that translate into practice?

I'll answer with a personal experience. I like to sail in a sailboat I have and five years ago I arrived in Cartagena, where I spent unforgettable days. If I compare it with Cartagena today, I don't think the differences are that big, either positive or negative, although I am inclined to think that progress will have been made. It remains a wonderful city, even with its problems. It will have grown, but in essence it is the same and will continue to be. That said, I accept that there are fundamental changes that we cannot ignore.

For example?

Demographic reality. In the case of Colombia, when I look at the percentage of the population that is under ten years old today, I venture to predict the future. Because this group will be the secondary and university students in the next decade. And later, it will make up the workforce. In this matter, as in much of the world, things look very different now.

What use is that information?

For planning. In a city like Bogotá you can determine with a high degree of certainty what it will be like in ten or twenty years, what type of public goods it will need or what the demand will be.
