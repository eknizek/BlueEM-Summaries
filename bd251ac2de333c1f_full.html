Argentine Central Bank accumulates US$6.76B in dollar purchases in under four months despite stable exchange rate

24 April 2026 · 09:56 PM

# Argentine Central Bank accumulates US$6.76B in dollar purchases in under four months despite stable exchange rate

The Central Bank purchased US$80 million this Friday and, with a week remaining until the end of April, has accumulated US$2.379 billion. For the year to date, it totals US$6.765 billion. Friday was a record day in more than a year, with US$954 million traded in the market—the highest daily volume since March 14 of last year, when the amount reached US$1.134 billion, according to economist Salvador Vitelli.

On January 5 of this year, the Government reversed its exchange rate policy and enabled the Central Bank's purchases, which had been absent from the market since the removal of currency controls a year ago. Between April 14 of last year and December 30, the Central Bank did not purchase a single dollar. The Government argued it would only buy when the wholesale exchange rate exceeded the currency band ceiling. However, this never occurred: today the wholesale rate stands at $1,399 while the ceiling is at $1,693.

The Government's rationale for keeping the Central Bank out of the market was to protect disinflation: to buy dollars, the Central Bank would have to emit pesos, and the economic team wanted to avoid those pesos pressuring prices and the Central Bank's purchases raising the exchange rate. This changed at year's end in response to warnings from various analysts, particularly the International Monetary Fund, regarding difficulties in building reserves. The year 2025 closed with US$41.167 billion in reserves, and today they stand at US$46.184 billion.

With a new direction, the Central Bank began purchasing in a context of abundant foreign currency: exports, private debt placements, and dollar loans lubricated the market and allowed the exchange rate to fall 4% year to date.

## Where the dollars come from

Data from consulting firm 1816 shows the commercial balance was US$5.508 billion in the first quarter—the highest in nominal terms in history and the highest in real terms since 2009. Additionally, companies have liquidated US$6.8 billion from negotiable obligations issued since October, according to information the BCRA presented to investors in Washington. "While not all that balance ended up being net foreign currency income (because part was used for debt repurchases and amortizations), the flow was very relevant in macroeconomic terms, especially because it concentrated in the months prior to the harvest," notes 1816.

To this add that the stock of dollar bank loans, which are liquidated instantly in the free exchange market when granted, increased US$3.525 billion year to date.

In this framework, President Javier Milei's prediction to Central Bank President Santiago Bausili during Argentina Week in New York in early April seems to be coming true: "Get ready, dollars are going to come out of your ears."

However, in this exchange rate lull, some data is creating noise. Friday's high trading volume and the Central Bank's relatively low purchase amount for the day indicate that foreign currency demand is rising. Until now the market had been quiet due to carry trade: with the dollar declining, it was more profitable to position in pesos and take advantage of high yields. Now with peso rates declining, the carry is losing appeal, which could reactivate foreign currency demand.

This occurs in a context where other economic indicators are concerning: persistent inflation around 3% monthly, rising unemployment, and stalled activity are red lights for the team led by Luis Caputo. In this framework, the Central Bank is approaching its annual target of purchasing US$10 billion faster than expected—a goal that could stretch to US$17 billion in 2026.

For Juan Manuel Franco, chief economist at the SBS group, "during the peak harvest season, the streak should continue, supported also by hydrocarbon exports, with the energy balance setting a record in March, without the full effect of oil prices following the Middle East war." "Regardless, some points to monitor in the exchange market come from the peso market, with negative rates in real terms. We believe this level of real rate may have been sought to attempt some reactivation in the most lagging real economy sectors, but the potential impact on the exchange rate should be monitored, even in a context where flows seem to be playing in Argentina's favor," Franco adds.
