With the dollar stable, Argentina's Central Bank has purchased $6.765bn in less than four months

# With the dollar stable, Argentina's Central Bank has purchased $6.765bn in less than four months

The Central Bank purchased $80 million this Friday and within a week of the end of April it accumulated $2.379 billion. For the year so far, it totals $6.765 billion. Friday was a record day in more than a year, because $954 million were traded in the market, the highest daily volume since March 14 of last year, when the amount had reached $1.134 billion, according to economist Salvador Vitelli.

On January 5 of this year, the Government reversed the exchange rate policy it had been applying and enabled Central Bank purchases, which had been absent from the market since the lifting of exchange controls a year ago. Then, between April 14 of last year and December 30, the Central Bank did not purchase a single dollar. The reason is that the Government argued that they would only buy when the wholesale exchange rate exceeded the ceiling of the exchange rate band. But that never happened: today the wholesale rate is at $1.399 and the ceiling is at $1.693.

The Government's argument for keeping the Central Bank out of the market was to protect disinflation: to buy dollars the Central Bank had to issue pesos and the economic team wanted to avoid those pesos pressuring prices. And that in turn, Central Bank purchases would raise the exchange rate.

This changed at the end of last year, in response to warnings from several analysts, but particularly from the International Monetary Fund regarding difficulties in adding reserves. 2025 closed with $41.167 billion in reserves and today they stand at $46.184 billion.

With a new direction, the Central Bank launched into buying and did so in a context of abundance of foreign currency: exports, private debt placements and dollar loans oiled the market and allowed the exchange rate to fall 4% so far this year.

## Where the dollars come from

Data from consultancy 1816 details that the trade balance was $5.508 billion in the first quarter, the highest in nominal terms in history and the highest in real terms since 2009. On the other hand, companies have liquidated $6.800 million for negotiable obligations they issued since October, according to information provided by the BCRA in a presentation to investors in Washington.

"Although not all of that balance ended up being net foreign currency income (because part was used for debt buybacks and amortizations), the flow was very relevant in macroeconomic terms, especially because it was concentrated in the months prior to the harvest," notes 1816.

To this is added the fact that the stock of dollar bank loans, which are settled immediately in the free exchange market when granted, increased $3.525 billion so far this year.

In this framework, it seems to be fulfilling the prediction made by President Javier Milei to Central Bank President Santiago Bausili during Argentina Week in New York in early April: "Get ready, dollars are going to come out of your ears."

But in this brief period of exchange rate calm, data is starting to create noise. The high trading volume on Friday and the Central Bank's purchase amount for the day, one of the lowest of the month, show that demand for foreign currency is rising. Until now the market was quiet because of the carry trade: with the dollar retreating it was more profitable to position oneself in pesos and take advantage of attractive interest rates. Now with interest rates in pesos falling, the carry trade is starting to lose appeal, which could reactivate demand for foreign currency.

This occurs in a context where other economic indicators are complicated: the persistence of inflation in the 3% monthly range, rising unemployment and a halt in activity are red lights for the team led by Luis Caputo.

In this framework, the Central Bank is approaching its annual goal of purchasing $10.000 billion faster than expected, a target that could stretch to $17.000 billion in 2026.

For Juan Manuel Franco, chief economist of the SBS group, "in the midst of peak harvest season the streak should continue, supported furthermore by hydrocarbon exports, with an energy balance that set a record in March, without the full effect of oil prices following the war in the Middle East."

"In any case, some points to follow in the exchange market come from the peso market, with negative real interest rates. We believe that this level of real interest rates may have been sought to attempt some reactivation in the lagging sectors of the real economy, but the potential impact on the exchange rate should be monitored, even in a context where flows seem to be playing in Argentina's favor," adds Franco.
