# With the dollar stable, the Central Bank has purchased $6.765B in less than four months

The Central Bank purchased $80 million this Friday and, one week before the end of April, it accumulated $2.379 billion. Year-to-date, it totals $6.765 billion. Friday was a record day in over a year, with $954 million traded in the market—the highest daily volume since March 14 of last year, when it had reached $1.134 billion, according to economist Salvador Vitelli.

On January 5 of this year, the Government reversed its exchange rate policy and enabled Central Bank purchases, which had been absent from the market since the lifting of exchange controls a year ago. Between April 14 of last year and December 30, the Central Bank did not purchase a single dollar. The Government's argument was that they would only buy when the wholesale exchange rate surpassed the ceiling of the exchange band. But that never happened: today the wholesale rate stands at $1.399 and the ceiling at $1.693.

The Government's argument for keeping the Central Bank out of the market was to protect disinflation: to purchase dollars, the Central Bank would have to emit pesos, and the economic team wanted to avoid those pesos putting pressure on prices and the Central Bank's purchases raising the exchange rate. This changed at year-end, as a response to warnings from various analysts, but particularly from the IMF regarding difficulties in accumulating reserves. 2025 closed with $41.167 billion in reserves; today they stand at $46.184 billion.

With a new direction, the Central Bank launched into purchases in a context of abundant foreign currency: exports, private debt placements, and dollar loans oiled the market and allowed the exchange rate to fall 4% year-to-date.

## Where the Dollars Come From

Data from consultancy 1816 shows that the trade balance was $5.508 billion in the first quarter—the highest in nominal terms in history and the highest in real terms since 2009. Additionally, companies have liquidated $6.800 billion from negotiable obligations they issued since October, according to information provided by the BCRA in a presentation to investors in Washington.

"While not all of that balance ended up being net foreign currency income (because part was used for debt buybacks and amortizations), the flow was very relevant in macroeconomic terms, especially for concentrating in the months before the harvest," notes 1816.

Add to this that the stock of dollar bank loans, which are liquidated instantly in the free exchange market when granted, rose $3.525 billion year-to-date.

In this framework, it appears that President Javier Milei's preview to Central Bank President Santiago Bausili during Argentina Week in New York in early April is coming true: "Get ready, you're going to have dollars coming out of your ears."

But in this exchange rate lull, some data are beginning to create noise. Friday's high traded volume and the Central Bank's daily purchase amount—one of the lowest of the month—show that dollar demand is rising. Until now the market had been quiet due to the carry trade: with the dollar declining, it was more profitable to position oneself in pesos and take advantage of attractive rates. Now, with peso rates falling, the carry trade is beginning to lose appeal, which could reactivate dollar demand.

This occurs in a context where other economic indicators are troubling: persistent inflation in the 3% monthly range, rising unemployment, and economic activity stagnation are red flags for the team led by Luis Caputo.

In this framework, the Central Bank is approaching its annual goal of purchasing $10 billion faster than expected—a target that could stretch to $17 billion in 2026.

For Juan Manuel Franco, chief economist of the SBS group, "in the midst of peak harvest, the streak should continue, supported also by hydrocarbon exports, with an energy balance that set a record in March, without the full effect of oil prices following the Middle East war."

"In any case, some points to monitor in the exchange market come from the peso market, with negative real rates. We consider that this real rate level could have been sought to attempt some reactivation in the more lagging sectors of the real economy, but the potential impact on the exchange rate will need to be monitored, even in a context where flows seem to be working in Argentina's favor," Franco adds.
