Two waterway concessions return to auction agenda

# Two waterway concessions return to auction agenda

The Ministry of Ports and Airports (MPor) has decided to move past the problems with indigenous communities in the Amazon, which in February threatened to derail the General Waterway Concessions Plan before it even got off the ground. Even under pressure from an election year calendar, the ministry is resuming institutional steps to enable, as soon as possible, auctions for the concession of two waterways distant from the Amazon and with more advanced modeling—the Paraguay Waterway, in the Center-West Region, and the Lagoa Mirim Waterway, in the South Region. This is revealed in an interview with NeoFeed by Otto Luiz Burlier, Secretary of Waterways at MPor.

"We understand that waterway concession is a public policy that is here to stay," he says about the waterways concessions plan—the last large-scale transport mode in the infrastructure sector to be integrated into the current government's concessions policy.

Brazil has 42,000 kilometers of navigable waterways, with approximately 19,000 kilometers having economic viability—that is, where ships and barges transport grains, bulk liquids and fuels, vehicles, fertilizers, containers, and general cargo. The objective with waterway concessions is to triple the capacity of rivers with economic viability. Currently, highways account for 62.2% of total cargo transported in the country, while 27% circulates on railways and only 4.3% passes through waterways, according to a study by the Dom Cabral Foundation.

"Just as it has happened with other modes, the first steps to enable new concessions are always more difficult, because everything new generates resistance and questioning," says the secretary, referring to pressure from indigenous leaders, who organized an invasion of an area linked to Cargill's terminal in Santarém (PA), interrupting progress on the concessions of the Madeira, Tapajós, and Tocantins waterways.

In a controversial decision criticized by the productive sector, the federal government, through the Civil House, decided to revoke the decree that included three Amazon waterways in the National Privatization Program (PND) following the episode.

According to Burlier, the ministry decided to act in two directions. In one, adopting a conciliatory approach regarding criticisms from indigenous communities and environmental NGOs about Amazon waterways. "There is much lack of information about how the Amazon waterways will operate, there is no such thing as 'river privatization,' as many claim, but we will discuss with them the prioritization of operational solutions, paying attention to environmental demands, focusing on draft maintenance and signaling, avoiding major works to deepen the channel and rectifications," reveals Burlier, suggesting that the government is open to receiving suggestions.

The ministry's objective is to show the benefits of concession. In the case of the Madeira waterway, for example, it provides for charging a modest fee for transporting large cargo—R$ 1.50 per ton—with an expected reduction in freight costs of 24%. "The magic is that, with the concession, the winner will have predictability of more than 20 years to offer a series of services, including security, plus exemption from charges for passenger transport," he says.

On another front, MPor intends to accelerate the waterway concessions schedule by advancing where possible. In this respect, the processes for organizing the auction of the Paraguay Waterway and the Lagoa Mirim (RS) take the lead. Burlier explains that the technically most advanced project is the Paraguay Waterway, with studies completed in early last year, consultations and hearings in the first semester, and submission of the process to the Court of Accounts of the Union (TCU) in October. "This waterway involves stretches in Brazil, Bolivia, and Paraguay, requiring a tripartite agreement," he says.

With this, a meeting is planned in Brasília in early May to advance on the draft, with the intention of approval in the Parliaments of the three countries in the second half and resumption of negotiations with the TCU afterward. The estimated investment for the Paraguay River Waterway concession auction is R$ 63.8 million for the first years of the contract. The project aims at improvements in navigability, including dredging and signaling, covering a stretch of approximately 600 km between Corumbá (MS) and the mouth of the Rio Apa. "There is a possibility of publishing the notice still this year, despite being an election year," continues the secretary, emphasizing that eventual delays in the process would be normal. "It is necessary to ensure governance and technical security; this is a 25-year concession."

The other in the crosshairs, the Lagoa Mirim Waterway, still has to meet a series of requirements, including a public consultation scheduled for this month or early May, followed by consolidation of contributions and submission to the TCU. The chosen modeling, however, is innovative. "The idea is to integrate the waterway concession auction in a single tender that would include those for access channels to the ports of Rio Grande, Pelotas, and Porto Alegre," says Burlier, adding that the notice could even be issued this year, but the auction is unlikely within the same period, given the sequence of steps.

The project is part of the integrated system that connects Lagoa Mirim, the São Gonçalo Canal, Lagoa dos Patos, and the ports of Rio Grande, Pelotas, and Porto Alegre. In practice, it functions as a single logistics route, even with a link between Brazil and Uruguay. "By concentrating the concession of the waterway and the access channels of the ports in a single package, we intend to capture synergies between waterways and ports and improve viability," he reveals. "Most of the dredging investments are concentrated in Rio Grande, and integration with the waterway improves operations and planning, strengthening the logistics competitiveness of the South."

The final values will be detailed in the public consultation. The ministry estimates, however, a total investment of R$ 134 million, with R$ 90.5 million provided for works in the Lagoa Mirim Waterway concession. The operating cost over 25 years is R$ 5.7 billion, with the project's estimated revenue in the range of R$ 7.5 billion. The anticipated movement is 1.7 million tons of grains in year 10 and 2.1 million tons in the final year of the concession.
