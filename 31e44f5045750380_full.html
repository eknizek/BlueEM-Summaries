Two waterway concessions return to auction agenda

15 April 2026 · 01:53 PM

# Two Waterway Concessions Return to Auction Agenda

The Ministry of Ports and Airports (MPor) has decided to move past the problems with indigenous communities in the Amazon, which in February threatened to derail the General Waterway Concessions Plan before the initiative even got off the ground. Despite pressure from an election year calendar, the ministry is resuming institutional steps to expedite auctions for two waterway concessions located outside the Amazon with more advanced modeling — the Paraguay Waterway in the Center-West Region and the Lagoa Mirim Waterway in the South Region. This is revealed in an interview with NeoFeed by Otto Luiz Burlier, Secretary of Waterways at MPor.

"We understand that waterway concessions are a public policy that has come to stay," he says about the waterway concessions plan — the last large-scale transportation mode in the infrastructure sector to be integrated into the current government's concessions policy.

Brazil has 42,000 kilometers of navigable waterways, with approximately 19,000 kilometers having economic viability — that is, where ships and barges transport grain, liquid bulk and fuel, vehicles, fertilizers, containers and general cargo. The objective with waterway concessions is to triple the capacity of economically viable rivers. Currently, highways account for 62.2% of total cargo transported in the country, while 27% travels by rail and only 4.3% by waterway, according to a study by the Dom Cabral Foundation.

"Just as has happened in other modes, the first steps to enable new concessions are always more difficult, because anything new generates resistance and questioning," says the secretary, referring to pressure from indigenous leaders who organized an invasion of an area linked to Cargill's terminal in Santarém (PA), interrupting progress on concessions for the Madeira, Tapajós and Tocantins waterways.

In a controversial decision criticized by the productive sector, the federal government, through the Civil House, decided to revoke the decree that included three Amazon waterways in the National Privatization Program (PND) following the episode.

According to Burlier, the ministry decided to act in two directions. In one, adopting a conciliatory approach regarding criticism from indigenous communities and environmental NGOs about Amazon waterways. "There is much lack of information about how the Amazon waterways will operate, there is no such thing as 'river privatization,' as many claim, but we will discuss with them the prioritization of operational solutions, paying attention to environmental demands, focusing on draft maintenance and signaling, avoiding major works to deepen the channel and straighten it," reveals Burlier, suggesting that the government is open to receiving suggestions.

The ministry's objective is to show the benefits of concessions. In the case of the Madeira waterway, for example, it provides for a modest charge per large cargo transport — R$ 1.50 per ton — with an expected freight cost reduction of 24%. "The magic is that with the concession, the winner will have predictability for more than 20 years to offer a series of services, including security, plus exemption from charges for passenger transport," he says.

On another front, MPor intends to accelerate the waterway concessions schedule by moving forward where possible. In this regard, the processes to organize the auction for the Paraguay Waterway and Lagoa Mirim (RS) are taking the lead.

Burlier explains that the technically most advanced project is the Paraguay Waterway, with studies completed in early last year, consultations and hearings in the first semester and submission of the process to the Court of Auditors (TCU) in October. "This waterway involves stretches in Brazil, Bolivia and Paraguay, requiring a tripartite agreement," he says.

A meeting in Brasília is scheduled for early May to advance the draft, with intentions for approval in the three countries' legislatures in the second semester and resumption of negotiations with the TCU afterward. The estimated investment for the Paraguay River Waterway concession auction is R$ 63.8 million for the first years of the contract. The project aims at navigability improvements, including dredging and signaling, covering a stretch of approximately 600 km between Corumbá (MS) and the mouth of the Apa River.

"There is a possibility of publishing the public notice still this year, despite being an election year," continues the secretary, emphasizing that eventual delays in the process would be normal. "It is necessary to ensure governance and technical security; this is a 25-year concession."

The other in the sights, the Lagoa Mirim Waterway, still has to meet a series of requirements, including a public consultation scheduled for this month or early May, followed by consolidation of contributions and submission to the TCU. The chosen modeling, however, is innovative.

"The idea is to integrate the waterway concession auction into a single bidding process that would include those for access channels to the ports of Rio Grande, Pelotas and Porto Alegre," says Burlier, adding that the notice could even come out this year, but the auction is unlikely within the same period given the sequence of steps.

The project is part of the integrated system that connects Lagoa Mirim, São Gonçalo Canal, Lagoa dos Patos and the ports of Rio Grande, Pelotas and Porto Alegre. In practice, it functions as a single logistics route, including a link between Brazil and Uruguay. "By concentrating the waterway concession and port access channel concessions into a single package, we intend to capture synergies between waterways and ports and improve viability," he reveals. "Most of the dredging investments are concentrated in Rio Grande, and integration with the waterway improves operations and planning, strengthening the logistical competitiveness of the South."

Final values will be detailed in the public consultation. The ministry estimates, however, total investment of R$ 134 million, with R$ 90.5 million planned for works on the Lagoa Mirim Waterway concession. The operating cost over 25 years is R$ 5.7 billion, with estimated project revenue in the range of R$ 7.5 billion. The anticipated movement is 1.7 million tons of grain in year 10 and 2.1 million tons in the final year of the concession.
