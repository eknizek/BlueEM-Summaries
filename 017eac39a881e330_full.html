# HYBE's Journey: From Crisis-Hit Agency to Global K-pop Empire Worth $10 Billion

When BTS takes the stage in Peru in October, we will be able to appreciate everything behind their spectacle, the lightsticks, the screams and the euphoria. And it is that, with the band, one of the most disruptive business stories in the global music industry also lands: HYBE Corporation.

What today is a multinational conglomerate with operations on three continents started almost quietly in 2005. At that time, Bang Si-hyuk founded Big Hit Entertainment with limited resources and few guarantees of success in an industry dominated by giants like SM Entertainment, YG Entertainment and JYP Entertainment. For years, the company survived more than it grew. There were moments when closure seemed inevitable. But the bet was different. It was not just about producing music, but about building a direct relationship between artists and fans, at a time when social networks were just beginning to redefine the industry.

## The Turning Point: When Fandom Became Business

The story changed in 2013, with the debut of BTS. Unlike other groups, the South Korean artists not only sang but also narrated, interacted and built community. While the industry continued to bet on traditional formulas, Big Hit Entertainment understood that the true asset was not just the artist, but the fandom. That bond between the fandom called Army ultimately proved more powerful than any marketing campaign. In a few years, BTS went from being a promise to filling stadiums, leading global rankings and generating billions of dollars in economic impact. And for the company, this process was more than a success—it meant the validation of their model.

## From Agency to Global Ecosystem

With BTS's growth, the company stopped thinking like an agency and began operating as a platform. In 2021, Big Hit Entertainment changed its name to HYBE Corporation, a move that marked its structural transformation. But the change was not just about branding—it was about business architecture. Big Hit Entertainment became a piece within a larger system.

Under the new structure, HYBE today functions as a global holding and technology platform, while Big Hit Music exists as an independent label responsible for artists like BTS, Tomorrow X Together (TXT), Cortis and soloist Lee Hyun.

The reorganization was built on three major pillars: record labels, where Big Hit Music, Pledis, Source Music, ADOR and other labels coexist; solutions focused on content, intellectual property, video games and experiences; and platforms with Weverse (an application designed to connect K-Pop and global pop fans and artists) as the central axis of digital business, monetizing everything from exclusive content to merchandise.

This shift allowed the company to diversify beyond music. The acquisition of Superb, a video game developer specializing in music games, opened the door to a new segment, while the expansion of co-branded initiatives broadened their reach into new markets like Latin America.

By early 2026, its stock market value was around $10 billion, equivalent to over 15 trillion South Korean won. This is the story of how HYBE transformed from a small agency on the brink of collapse into a global entertainment empire.
