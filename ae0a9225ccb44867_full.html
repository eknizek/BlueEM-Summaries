New OFAC licenses to Venezuela create economic opportunities for Colombian enterprises

08 April 2026 · 09:12 PM

# How New OFAC Licenses to Venezuela Can Bring Economic Advantages for Colombia

At the beginning of 2026, the Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) began issuing and expanding general licenses linked to Venezuela that authorize activities that were previously restricted or prohibited under U.S. sanctions. These licenses—such as 46B, 48A, 49A, 51A, and 52—expand the scope of what economic actors can do, including everything from oil and petrochemical trade to investments, supply of goods and services, and activities in sectors such as electricity and mining.

In this regard, since the Venezuelan landscape began to change with the capture of former dictator Nicolás Maduro, discussion about the once-great oil giant, Petróleos de Venezuela (PDVSA), has become a constant in regional public discussions. However, how easy is it to establish business with the oil company? And how can Colombia take advantage of this easing of sanctions by the United States? Elías Esteban, an attorney at the U.S. firm Leech Tishman, explained the steps Colombian companies must follow when making alliances with the neighboring oil producer.

**Among the licenses that have been granted, which do you think has the most impact on opportunities for foreign companies?**

Rather than one particular license, I would speak of trends. When the opening began, OFAC—the entity that regulates sanctions—focused on the oil industry. The first general licenses, such as 46, 48, and 49, covered oil. Later, more recently, they were extended to electrical infrastructure and, third, to the mining sector. Some licenses are more relevant than others. For example, general license 52 is very broad because it allows various commercial activities with PDVSA. It is even broader than 48B, since it allows for joint ventures with local companies in Venezuela, something that was not clear before.

The characteristic of these general licenses is that if their requirements are met, no special authorization from OFAC is needed. However, other regulations must be complied with: export, banking regulation, and asset controls. There are two licenses—one in oil and another in mining—focused on large-scale investment projects. These allow for contracts subject to contingency. Once an MOU (Memorandum of Understanding) or preliminary agreement is signed, you must go to OFAC to obtain special authorization. This applies to investment projects in oil platforms or mining.

**If a Colombian company wants to get involved in business in Venezuela, what is the first step?**

That's a very good question. If a Colombian company wants to do business directly with Venezuela—for example, with the Government or PDVSA—the United States would have no jurisdiction, unless there is some specific regulation. This is because OFAC applies to U.S. persons. If the Colombian company does not fall into that category, the United States does not control operations. However, if the U.S. financial system is used, regulations must be complied with.

**What happens if payments are made through the United States?**

It depends on how the operation is structured. In that case, the focus will not be OFAC, but banking regulation. The receiving bank will require compliance with money laundering matters, documentation, explanations, and possibly a comfort letter. Everything depends on the structure of the business.

The Government has told us constantly that for Ecopetrol to do business with PDVSA, it is necessary to lift sanctions. Of course, because at the government level, no one wants to be involved in business with a sanctioned entity. If you decide to do business with someone sanctioned, the risk and responsibility are yours.

**Is there a common mistake when thinking about doing business with Venezuela?**

It is essential to be well advised on regulatory matters. There must be clarity on how the business will be structured, in what sector, who the counterpart will be, and how payments will be guaranteed. Venezuela has new regulations, such as changes in hydrocarbon law and in the mining sector, in addition to new figures such as CPPs. That's why it's key to understand the local system and also exposure to other jurisdictions, such as the United States.

**Does the expansion of licenses represent an opportunity for Colombian companies?**

The United States is a benchmark in the region. Before, many avoided doing business with Venezuela because of the sanctions regime. The current opening is gradual, not immediate, but it is a strong signal. This creates opportunities. Additionally, Colombia facilitates alliances with Venezuelan companies.

**Is it easier today to establish relationships with PDVSA?**

It depends on the type of activity, but there is greater openness, willingness, and need in Venezuela. Entities are more open to this type of relationship, which represents an opportunity.

**Does lifting sanctions mean greater security?**

Yes. It is a signal of gradual opening for American companies, which increases competition. More actors in the market generate opportunities and greater confidence.

**Are we facing a structural relaxation of sanctions or are these temporary measures?**

OFAC can amend or reverse decisions, but the recent trend is the opposite: to expand and increase the scope of action for companies. Mining activity, for example, was not originally contemplated and was incorporated on March 27.
