# Mid-Sized Companies "Emerge From Shadows" and Drive 22.5% Increase in Debt Issuance

The debt capital market began 2026 at an even more accelerated pace than last year, which ended with a historic record of R$ 838.8 billion in public offerings. According to data compiled by Bloxs, an Investment Banking as a Service fintech that connects issuers to investors, the consolidated volume for the first quarter reached R$ 192.4 billion, up 22.5% compared to the same period of the previous year. The expansion occurred even in an environment of still-elevated interest rates, with the Selic at 14.75%.

For the issuance platform, the advancement of private credit has been opening space for a group of companies that, until recently, remained off the radar of major market structures. On one hand, bank credit for legal entities continues to operate at rates between 20% and 27% per year. On the other, the capital market offers costs between 16% and 18% per year for mid-sized companies and from CDI+2% for AAA-rated issuers. This differential continues to attract new issuers.

However, preference for instruments is changing. Debentures remained dominant with R$ 73 billion in issuances and 62.02% participation, but declined 14.3% in year-over-year comparison. FIDCs (Credit Receivables Investment Funds) were the highlight, with an increase of 38.09%, reaching R$ 29.7 billion and 25.23% participation. CRIs (Real Estate Receivables Certificates) advanced 11.3% to R$ 7.4 billion, while CRAs (Agribusiness Receivables Certificates) fell 72% to R$ 2.1 billion, showing a more challenging scenario for agribusiness. Commercial notes declined 35% to R$ 5.5 billion, indicating lower appetite for this simpler type of issuance during a period of greater credit scrutiny.

In Bloxs' assessment, FIDCs are consolidating as the "gateway for the middle market," driven by retail investor access brought by CVM 175 and a cycle in which more issuers seek structures suited to receivables, working capital, and dispersed financing. The report also highlights that the opening of FIDCs to retail investors, limited liability for quota holders, and increased transparency have helped catalyze this movement.

"We see a capital market growing year after year, but much of this growth does not necessarily occur because it has become better than other alternatives," says Felipe Souto, CEO of Bloxs. "There is considerable growth of those small middle-market companies, more family-oriented, outside major centers, that have begun to discover the capital market."

In the sector breakdown, the debt capital market in the first quarter was led by electricity (estimated 28% of volume), followed by sanitation (18%), agribusiness (15%), and logistics and transportation (12%). While electricity and sanitation led in the consolidated market, the picture on the Bloxs platform is different. The real estate sector accounted for 51% of the company's deals in the quarter, followed by other/diversified (21%), agribusiness (10%), and credit/FIDC (8%). According to the company, the weight of real estate reflects both the maturity of the CRI as an instrument and the depth of its advisor network in this segment.

Through the end of 2026, Bloxs' expectation is for a more liquid market favorable to new issuances, as the Selic rate cut cycle helps compress spreads and expand appetite for risk. In the house's market report, the reading is that this movement tends to benefit especially smaller issuers and provide additional traction to instruments like FIDCs and tokenization, although the scenario remains subject to tax and regulatory risks.

However, much of the issuances occurred during this first quarter itself. "Because of elections at year-end and the volatility they bring to the market, companies are rushing to issue earlier," says Souto.
