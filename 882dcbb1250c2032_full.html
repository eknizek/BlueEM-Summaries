# Peru Poverty: Five More Years Needed to Erase Crisis Aftermath

In 2020, poverty skyrocketed due to the pandemic, affecting 30.1% of the population. Five years later, Peru has failed to recover pre-crisis health levels. Now, amid a year marked by presidential elections and geopolitical conflicts, the prospects point to the "goal" of returning to pre-pandemic levels remaining distant.

Economist and Macroconsult associate Yohnny Campana noted that the latest projections from the consulting firm estimate that poverty would have been reduced to 26.2% in 2025, and for 2026, a rate of 25.1% is expected. However, the specialist clarified that reaching 2019 levels could take us about an additional five years while maintaining moderate economic growth rates like the current one.

"Under the base scenario, it should be reduced to the pre-pandemic level in 2030. However, under a pessimistic scenario, with the economy growing more slowly, it could take even longer," he told Gestión.

Former Minister of Development and Social Inclusion Carolina Trivelli agreed that 2025 would be expected to have continued this poverty reduction trend, but highlighted that the current gap reflects significant lag compared to other countries in the region.

"This is a tragedy because almost all Latin American countries have already returned to pre-pandemic poverty levels or have less poverty than before. The only ones who haven't achieved it are Argentina, Honduras, Colombia, and us. Peru is among the furthest away," she stated.

According to Trivelli, while the reduction observed in recent years responds largely to labor market recovery in sectors such as construction, commerce, and services, the outlook for 2026 is more uncertain. Although positive economic growth is still expected for Peru this year, this effect could be limited by a possible increase in inflation associated with the impact of the war in the Middle East, which has increased oil prices.

"For people in poverty situations or those near the poverty line, inflation hits them hard. The increases force them to reduce food consumption levels," she noted.

From the Roundtable for the Fight Against Poverty, president Federico Arnillas warns that the challenge is greater since adding the 27.6% poverty rate with the 31.8% of the population in vulnerable situations leaves almost 60% of Peruvians in an alert condition. For the specialist, the State faces the challenge of generating structural changes.

## The Risk of an Election Year

The electoral context introduces an additional element of uncertainty. Rosa Luz Durán, economist and professor at the University of Lima, recalled that instability in the political environment usually directly affects economic dynamics.

"In electoral contexts, the Peruvian economy is affected by a combination of institutional paralysis and fiscal populism, which also includes a deterioration of business confidence. We are in a situation of uncertainty for private investment because economic agents don't know who will govern or what political position they will have, so they postpone decisions and investments are paralyzed," commented the researcher from the Economic, Financial and Social Observatory of U. Lima.

For her part, Trivelli emphasized that the next Government's decisions will be decisive in the poverty trajectory.

"The reduction could be halted if there is less growth and/or more inflation. That is possible with a Government that questions, for example, the autonomy of the Central Reserve Bank or affects growth engines," she warned.

But growth alone is not enough. According to the economist, only half of the people who fall into poverty manage to get out on their own with controlled growth and inflation; the other half needs State support.

"[In these elections] practically nothing has been discussed about poverty, hunger, or vulnerability. There is little hope of sufficient political commitment," she affirmed.

Currently, according to estimates from the University of Lima, 32% of the population lacks access to basic services, which means that more than 10 million Peruvians lack access to health, education, housing, employment, and services such as drinking water, sanitation, and connectivity.

"It is crucial to reduce access problems. There is a risk situation with unsustainable fiscal policies. Electoral populism translates into generalized subsidies and spending without much criteria that deteriorates our fiscal accounts and scares away investment. Business confidence is affected and the efficiency of social spending is reduced, which is what we need to combat poverty more effectively," she stated.

## Can Poverty Reduction Be Accelerated?

Trivelli emphasizes that accelerating the reduction would require a more targeted approach. She therefore proposes the need for economic growth oriented toward sectors such as small agriculture, commerce, and local entrepreneurship, accompanied by a more effective social policy agenda.

The economist recalled that tools designed for the situation from ten years ago are still being used, but today's poverty is different, which requires a new diagnosis that allows adapting the development and social inclusion strategy, which also implies the need for "political commitment, leadership, and resource allocation."
