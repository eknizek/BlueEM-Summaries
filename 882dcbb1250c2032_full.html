Poverty in Peru: Five More Years Needed to Fully Reverse Economic Crisis Damage

27 April 2026 · 10:00 PM

# Poverty in Peru: Five More Years Needed to "Erase" Crisis Aftermath

In 2020, poverty skyrocketed due to the pandemic, affecting 30.1% of the population. Five years later, Peru has failed to recover pre-pandemic crisis levels. Now, amid a year marked by presidential elections and geopolitical conflicts, the prospects point to the "goal" of returning to pre-pandemic levels remaining distant.

Economist and associate at Macroconsult, Yohnny Campana, noted that the latest projections from the consulting firm estimate that poverty would have been reduced to 26.2% in 2025 and, for 2026, a rate of 25.1% is expected. However, the specialist clarified that reaching 2019 levels could take us nearly an additional five years while maintaining moderate economic growth rates like the current one. "Under the base scenario, it should be reduced to pre-pandemic levels in 2030. However, under a pessimistic scenario, with the economy growing more slowly, it could take even longer," Campana told Gestión.

Former Minister of Social Development and Inclusion, Carolina Trivelli, agreed that a continued trend of poverty reduction would be expected in 2025, but emphasized that the current gap reflects significant lag compared to other countries in the region. "This is a drama because almost all Latin American countries have already returned to pre-pandemic poverty levels or have less poverty than before. The only ones who haven't achieved it are Argentina, Honduras, Colombia, and us. Peru is one of the furthest away," she stated.

According to Trivelli, while the reduction observed in recent years is largely due to labor market recovery in sectors such as construction, commerce, and services, the outlook for 2026 is more uncertain. Although positive economic growth is still expected for Peru this year, this effect could be limited by a possible increase in inflation associated with the impact of the Middle East war, which has increased oil prices. "For people in poverty or those near the poverty line, inflation hits them hard. The increases force them to reduce food consumption levels," she indicated.

From the Roundtable for the Fight Against Poverty, its president Federico Arnillas warns that the challenge is greater since adding 27.6% poverty with 31.8% of the population in vulnerable situations results in more than half of Peruvians in an alert condition. "The 59.4% of the population we have is in a quite delicate or very delicate situation even. It challenges us in terms of what actions and commitments the Peruvian State in particular and Peruvian society as a whole should prioritize to reverse this situation," he said.

Arnillas pointed out that structural changes are required related to key factors in the population's well-being, that is, improvements related to access to water, comprehensive nutrition, and education. This, he added, requires a proactive dynamic "that positively involves the work of the public sector with efficiency, transparency, and without corruption."

## Electoral Year Risk

The electoral context introduces an additional element of uncertainty. Rosa Luz Durán, economist and professor at the University of Lima, recalled that instability in the political environment usually directly affects economic dynamics. "In electoral contexts, the Peruvian economy is affected by a combination of institutional paralysis and fiscal populism, which also includes a deterioration of business confidence. We are in a situation of uncertainty for private investment because economic agents don't know who will govern or what political position they will have, so they postpone decisions and investments are paralyzed," commented the researcher from the Economic, Financial and Social Observatory of U. Lima.

Trivelli also highlighted that decisions by the next Government will be decisive in the trajectory of poverty. "The reduction could be halted if there is less growth and/or more inflation. That is possible with a Government that questions, for example, the autonomy of the Central Reserve Bank or affects growth engines," she warned.

But growth in GDP alone is not enough. According to the economist, only half of the people who fall into poverty manage to get out on their own with controlled growth and inflation; the other half needs state support. "[In these elections] almost nothing has been discussed about poverty, hunger, or vulnerability. There is little hope that there will be sufficient political commitment," she stated.

Considering multidimensional poverty—which measures lack of access to basic services—Durán believed that if fiscal populism, staff turnover, and low infrastructure project execution prevail in the new Government, the reduction of gaps would be further limited. Currently, according to University of Lima estimates, 32% of the population has basic service deficiencies, which means there are more than 10 million Peruvians without access to health, education, housing, employment, and services such as drinking water, sanitation, and connectivity.

"It is crucial to reduce the problems of lack of access. There is a risk situation with unsustainable fiscal policies, electoral populism translates into generalized subsidies and spending without much criteria that deteriorate our fiscal accounts and scare away investment. Business confidence is affected and the efficiency of social spending is reduced, which is what we need to combat poverty more effectively," she said.

## Can Poverty Reduction Be Accelerated?

Trivelli emphasizes that accelerating reduction would require a more targeted approach. Therefore, she proposes the need for economic growth oriented toward sectors such as small-scale agriculture, commerce, and local entrepreneurship, accompanied by a more effective social policy agenda. The economist recalled that tools designed for the situation from ten years ago are being used, but today's poverty is different, which requires a new diagnosis.
