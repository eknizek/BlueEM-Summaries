# Labor reform: two months on, Argentine companies still measuring impact amid implementation delays

Last February, when the Government had already obtained initial approval of the labor reform law, Economy Minister Luis Caputo complained on social media X because business owners were not celebrating the reduction in employer contributions for new employees, one of the changes in the regulation. The law was finally approved on February 27 and enacted on March 6; the courts halted it and later reinstated it.

Despite two months having passed since the vote, some regulations are still missing, a delay that contradicts the Government's anxiety to implement the regulation supposedly designed to boost employment. Along the way, the enthusiasm Caputo demanded from business owners remains absent.

Evidence of this is a recent survey by human resources consulting firm WTW among 419 companies. When asked about the different changes brought by the law, the majority response is, in almost all cases, "the matter is under analysis" or "it will have no impact on the company." This includes everything from hour banks to vacations or the extension of the trial period.

The highest value corresponded to the Labor Assistance Fund (FAL). Eighty-one percent of firms responded that they had not taken a position regarding mandatory incorporation into that fund for indemnifications, which has not yet been regulated but was supposed to start in June. Companies' first response to all questions about the FAL was "we still cannot evaluate the impact," followed by "moderate impact" and only as a third response "positive impact."

Regarding the hour bank, 59% say it is under analysis, but 25% say they do not consider adopting it and 12% use similar systems. Only 4% say they will implement it in some areas.

Regarding the incorporation or expansion of variable compensation or productivity schemes under the new legal framework for dynamic variable compensation, after 44% say it is under analysis, 30% do not consider it appropriate for their company, 22% say they will not implement it because they already have a bonus system, and barely 4% say yes, but only for some employee groups or areas.

The same applies to new regulations on vacations, which allow them to be split throughout the year and make it more flexible for employees not to take them in summer: 52% maintain that they already operate with a similar scheme and 37% say it "does not apply."

They also do not see changes in the extension of the trial period: only 5% indicate it will be "a central tool for attracting talent," the rest assure that it will not have a strategic role (49%), that they are analyzing it (37%), and that it will be used selectively (9%).

The Government is analyzing allowing SMEs to "share" the same fund for dismissals.

Many companies are already working with hour banks.
