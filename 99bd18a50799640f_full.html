# For BTG Pactual, Stone Has Attractive Valuation, But Operations Remain Weak

Stone's extraordinary dividends from the Linx sale may have given some momentum to the company's thesis at a moment when valuation is quite low. But BTG Pactual analysts warn: without significant operational improvement, the shares will remain at current levels. They assert that time is running out for Stone to demonstrate it can achieve a feasible turnaround.

"At this moment, one of the main questions is whether Stone will be able to get out of this situation on its own, just through execution, or whether the story might eventually require some form of strategic alternative or mergers and acquisitions to unlock value," says an excerpt from the report signed by analysts Eduardo Rosman, Ricardo Buchpiguel, and Antonio Pascale, who recommend buying the stock with a price target of $19—the stock is being traded near $15.

Looking at valuation, BTG Pactual analysts highlight that Stone's shares are trading at a P/E of 6 times. The multiple is lower than the 6.1 times recorded by Banco do Brasil, which faces severe problems related to default in the agricultural sector, while the payment processor shows good cash flow and higher profitability. But poor operational prospects represent a significant drag, as became clear the day after the dividend announcement. Despite being welcome, the dividend did not cause the shares to jump as would be expected from such news.

Year-to-date, the shares are up 1.26%, bringing the market value to $3.6 billion.

"The problem is that the operational pace remains weak, and the market seems increasingly reluctant to pay for a recovery that has not yet clearly materialized," says an excerpt from the report.

Expectations are not positive for Stone following signals the company gave in March about what to expect from first-quarter results. The situation caused the shares to fall more than 19% on March 3rd. BTG Pactual analysts project that the total payment volume (TPV) of the micro, small, and medium-sized enterprises segment should remain stable relative to the first quarter of 2025. The estimate is in line with what the company had forecast in its last conference call—if Pix is included, it comes in slightly above the previous year's result.

Another negative point is asset quality, resulting in increased provisions, driven by the growth of non-performing loans (NPLs) in some larger accounts and weaker performance of recent vintages.

"Risk cost could reach something above the 17% recorded in the fourth quarter," says an excerpt from the report.

Revenues should increase almost 5% relative to the previous year but fall 5% relative to the previous quarter. Despite expectations that operating profit will decline in the low single digits relative to the previous year, analysts say that the lower tax rate should allow adjusted net income from continuing operations to increase slightly relative to the previous year, to around R$ 540 million.

The analysts acknowledge that Stone has the correct diagnosis of the situation, working on diverse fronts such as execution, cost control, and commercial engagement, plus experience with these issues. But they question whether it will be sufficient, stating that there is no single factor explaining Stone's poor performance.

"If the problems were clearly tied to one variable, such as price, the path to recovery could be more obvious, but also much more costly from a unit economics perspective. Instead, the current diagnosis suggests the need to 'tighten many screws' across the entire value proposition," says an excerpt from the report.

Around 12:26 p.m., Stone's shares were up 0.94% to $15.06.
