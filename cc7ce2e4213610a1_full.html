# USA Rare Earth Acquires Serra Verde for $2.8 Billion Rare Earths Deal

While some Brazilian politicians begin discussing the creation of a state-owned rare earths company, Americans have been gradually securing control over the first reserve of these minerals discovered in the country. USA Rare Earth announced today an agreement to acquire 100% of mining company Serra Verde, with operations in Goiás, for $2.8 billion – nearly 14 billion reais at today's exchange rate.

The buyer of Brazilian assets received a $1.6 billion injection from the Trump Government at the beginning of the year – and the United States now has an equity stake of approximately 10% in the company. Serra Verde itself had been moving closer to the US, with a $565 million loan from a Washington agency, the US International Development Finance Corporation, which also involved an offtake of future production.

The Trump administration, eager for alternatives to China in supplying rare earths, arranged a deal to guarantee the purchase of 100% of the initial production from the Brazilian sector pioneer. This agreement, with a term of 15 years, was signed by Serra Verde with a vehicle capitalized by US government agencies and private investors, said USA Rare Earth CEO Barbara Humpton. She assessed that the deal "demonstrates the global importance" of the asset.

The CEO also said the purchase of reserves in Brazil is "a transformational step" in the American company's ambition to be a "global champion" in rare earths. The USA Rare Earth transaction for Serra Verde involves $300 million in cash and the remainder in new shares to be issued by the company, which is listed on Nasdaq. The deal is expected to be finalized in the third quarter, subject to regulatory approvals.

Valued at $4.35 billion before the M&A, USA Rare Earth evaluates that production in Brazil will end 2027 at a level sufficient to generate annualized EBITDA of between $550 million and $650 million. Beyond the first phase of its projects, expected to be 100% operational by the end of next year, Serra Verde has "significant growth potential with its phase 2," according to the Americans. USA Rare Earths expects to reach, with its operations and new reserves in Brazil, annualized EBITDA of $1.8 billion by the end of 2030.

While Americans conduct business, Brazil brought the rare earths issue to the arena of political polarization. The PT caucus, party of President Lula, presented a project for creating "TerraBras" – a state company to "manage critical and strategic minerals and guarantee national sovereignty over these resources." In a text defending the proposal, the PT said that Lula's probable rival in this year's presidential elections, Flávio Bolsonaro, "signaled the intention to hand over the exploitation of these minerals to the USA." The PT project would create a mineral exploration sharing model, similar to that used with pre-sal oil, so the Government would be a direct partner of miners, receiving part of the production.

Regardless of political questions, the BTG Pactual team is optimistic about the development of a rare earths supply chain in the West, and sees potential for Brazil to become an important player in this market. "We believe this could mark the beginning of a larger wave of transactions in the sector, with companies in early stages of development increasingly becoming targets for acquisitions, especially as projects advance toward production," wrote analysts Leo Correa, Marcelo Arazi and Rodrigo Gotardo.

For now, Serra Verde is the only active rare earths mine in Brazil, with most new projects expected to begin operating after 2028. Producing since 2024, the pioneering mine in Goiás received funding from two American private equity firms, Denham Capital and Energy & Minerals Group, and British Vision Blue. Production has been sold to Asia – under a contract that expires at the end of 2026 and will be replaced by the agreement with the Americans.
