# Oil Opens Week Higher, Rebounds Above $100 After Failed US-Iran Negotiations

Oil prices rose at the opening of this week's trading following the failure of peace negotiations between the United States and Iran, as well as an announcement by Donald Trump to impose a naval blockade on the Strait of Hormuz. Brent, the global benchmark, advanced nearly 8%, reaching $102.80 per barrel, while WTI (West Texas Intermediate), used in the United States, surpassed $104. Before the start of the war in Iran, the barrel was trading around $70.

A ship in the Strait of Hormuz on Sunday (12) - Reuters

Analysts expect Asian stock exchanges to fall on Monday. US stock futures fell Sunday night, setting the stage for losses on Monday. S&P 500 futures fell 1% and Nasdaq 100 futures retreated 1.2% at market opening. The euro fell about 0.5%, to $1.1672. The two main Wall Street indices had been rising since the end of March, when a diplomatic solution became more likely. Both indices expanded their gains last week after the US and Iran announced a ceasefire.

"This is an absolute reversal of any optimism that existed before the peace negotiations, with the dollar taking on a safe-haven role, oil skyrocketing, and the selling of everything else," said Fiona Cincotta, senior market analyst at City Index. "On the other hand, we've seen markets exaggerate sometimes. And I think especially in this scenario, the market is having difficulty pricing correctly, because there's a lot of uncertainty, a lot of unknowns."

The movements at the beginning of Monday dragged many asset prices back to near where they were trading in the middle of last week, before the two-week ceasefire.

"The market is now largely back to pre-ceasefire conditions, except that the US will also now block remaining flows of up to 2 million barrels linked to Iran through the Strait of Hormuz," said Saul Kavonic, analyst at MST Marquee in Sydney. "The main question that remains is whether the US will resume attacks on Iran, increasing the risk of attacks on energy infrastructure across the region, which could have a lasting impact beyond the duration of the war."

Risk-sensitive currencies, such as the Australian dollar and the British pound, came under pressure, falling 0.7% and 0.5%, respectively. The dollar rose 0.3%, to 159.78 yen.

With growing expectations of a resumption of inflation, investors priced in the possibility of several central banks, such as the European Central Bank and the Bank of England, leaning toward raising interest rates this year.

The increase in oil prices came after a week of relief in prices. The barrel closed Friday's trading (10) at $94, accumulating a weekly decline of 13% amid positive investor expectations for Saturday's (11) meeting. However, the meeting between American and Iranian representatives in Pakistan ended without an agreement. The failure brought uncertainties about the future of the ceasefire announced last week.

Trump's plan for the Strait of Hormuz deepened the scenario of uncertainties and prospects for higher fuel prices.

"From now on, the United States Navy, the best in the world, will begin the process of blockading every ship that attempts to enter or leave the Strait of Hormuz," Trump wrote on his social network. "We have also instructed our Navy to search for and interdict all vessels in international waters that have paid a toll to Iran."

If the strait blockade is confirmed, the flow of Iranian oil to other markets will be blocked.

"Until now, the US allowed Iranian crude oil and derivative exports, and even loosened sanctions so that more buyers could import these cargoes, since the US was very focused on keeping oil prices low," said Amrita Sen, founder and director of Market Intelligence at Energy Aspect, to the Financial Times. "But if there is a genuine blockade, that's another 1.5 million to 1.7 million barrels per day [b/d] of oil exports that would be halted—on top of the more than 10 million b/d already interrupted."

Before the war, about one-fifth of all oil traded in the world passed through the strait, a maritime passage between Iran and Oman. Iran's Revolutionary Guard responded with a statement, warning that military vessels approaching the strait will be considered a ceasefire violation and treated with severity and firmness, emphasizing the risk of a dangerous escalation.
