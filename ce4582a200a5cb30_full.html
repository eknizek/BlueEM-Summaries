# Oil prices rise above $100 as U.S.-Iran negotiations fail and Trump announces Hormuz blockade

Oil prices rose at the opening of this week's trading following the failure of peace negotiations between the United States and Iran and an announcement by Donald Trump to impose a naval blockade on the Strait of Hormuz. Brent, the global benchmark, advanced nearly 8%, reaching $102.80 per barrel, while WTI (West Texas Intermediate), used in the United States, surpassed $104. Early in the morning (in Brazil), values remained at the same level. Before the war in Iran began, the barrel was trading around $70.

Analysts expect Asian stock markets to fall on Monday. U.S. stock futures fell Sunday evening, setting the stage for losses on Monday. S&P 500 futures fell 1% and Nasdaq 100 futures retreated 1.2% at market opening. The euro fell about 0.5%, to $1.1672.

The two main Wall Street indices had been rising since the end of March, when a diplomatic solution became more likely. Both indices expanded their gains last week after the U.S. and Iran announced a ceasefire. "This is an absolute reversal of any optimism that existed before the peace negotiations, with the dollar assuming a safe haven role, oil skyrocketing and the sell-off of everything else," said Fiona Cincotta, senior market analyst at City Index. "On the other hand, we've already seen markets overreact sometimes. And I think especially in this scenario, the market is having difficulty pricing things correctly, because there's a lot of uncertainty, a lot of unknowns."

The movements early Monday dragged many asset prices back to near where they were trading mid-last week, before the two-week ceasefire. "The market is now largely back to pre-ceasefire conditions, except that the U.S. will now also block remaining flows of up to 2 million barrels linked to Iran through the Strait of Hormuz," said Saul Kavonic, analyst at MST Marquee in Sydney. "The main question that remains is whether the U.S. will resume attacks on Iran, increasing the risk of attacks on energy infrastructure throughout the region, which could have a lasting impact beyond the duration of the war."

Risk-sensitive currencies, such as the Australian dollar and the British pound, came under pressure, falling 0.7% and 0.5%, respectively. The dollar rose 0.3%, to 159.78 yen.

With growing expectations of a resumption of inflation, investors priced in the possibility of several central banks, such as the European Central Bank and the Bank of England, leaning toward raising interest rates this year.

The rise in oil prices comes after a week of relief in prices. The barrel closed Friday's trading at $94, accumulating a weekly drop of 13% amid investors' positive expectations for Saturday's meeting. However, the meeting between American and Iranian representatives in Pakistan ended without agreement. The failure brought uncertainty about the future of the ceasefire announced last week.

The plan announced by Trump for the Strait of Hormuz deepened the scenario of uncertainties and prospects for higher fuel prices. "From now on, the United States Navy, the best in the world, will begin the process of blockading any and every ship attempting to enter or leave the Strait of Hormuz," Trump wrote on his social network. "I have also instructed our Navy to search for and interdict all vessels in international waters that have paid a toll to Iran."

If the blockade of the strait is confirmed, the flow of Iranian oil to other markets will be blocked. "Until now, the U.S. allowed Iranian crude oil and derivative exports, and even relaxed sanctions so more buyers could import these shipments, as the U.S. was very focused on keeping oil prices low," Amrita Sen, founder and director of Market Intelligence at Energy Aspect, told the Financial Times. "But if there is a genuine blockade, that's another 1.5 million to 1.7 million barrels per day [b/d] of oil exports that would be halted on top of the more than 10 million b/d already disrupted," she stated.

Before the war, about one-fifth of all oil traded in the world passed through the strait, a maritime passage between Iran and Oman. Iran's Revolutionary Guard responded with a statement, warning that military vessels approaching the strait would be considered a violation of the ceasefire and treated with vigor and firmness, emphasizing the risk of a dangerous escalation.
