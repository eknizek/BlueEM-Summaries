# High Diesel Costs Draw Attention to Ethanol Engine Projects

The war in the Middle East has driven up oil prices, raising the cost of fossil fuel products such as diesel, the main fuel used in agricultural machinery. This scenario has further highlighted the importance of equipment powered by renewable fuels. At Agrishow in Ribeirão Preto (São Paulo)—Latin America's largest agricultural technology fair—some of the industry's leading manufacturers presented ethanol engine projects currently under development.

Although visitors can already see these innovations, the technology to power tractors and other agricultural machines with biofuel remains in the testing phase, which precedes full validation. At Agrishow, Valtra is the only company already providing estimates for a commercial launch. "The machines have already completed more than 10,000 hours of testing on partner sugar-and-ethanol mills' farms. We are now in the fine-tuning phase, such as adjusting the power curve, but we are ready to move decisively into the market by 2029," said Cláudio Esteves, sales director at AGCO, Valtra's parent company.

Massey Ferguson, also part of AGCO, has begun testing an ethanol-powered tractor with 200 to 300 horsepower. The brand showcased the AGCO Power engine at Agrishow, a project entirely developed by its engineering team in Brazil.

Among technologies aimed at reducing fossil fuel use in agriculture, one of Fendt's main bets—another AGCO brand—is the electric engine, already available in European and U.S. markets. However, the company is also testing other fuel options, noted Marcelo Traldi, vice president of Fendt and Valtra in South America. Torsten Dehner, global vice president of Fendt, said the electric tractor, developed in Germany, can deliver up to 20% fuel savings in field operations. The company is also developing a hybrid tractor.

Case, part of CNH, has been working on ethanol-powered machinery since 2024, when it conducted 800 hours of testing with a two-row harvester in sugar-and-ethanol mills operated by São Martinho. At this year's Agrishow, the company presented the Cursor 13 engine, developed in Brazil in partnership with FPT Industrial. Tests with the ethanol-powered harvester were conducted without interruption and delivered satisfactory results, said Paulo Arabian, CNH's vice president of agricultural sales for Latin America. In the current harvest, the manufacturer is testing a single-row harvester. "If the results were good with two rows, we expect them to be even better with a single-row machine," he said. "In terms of power, we have already reached our targets, and in terms of consumption, we are getting there."

He added that CNH is also working on alternative fuels, such as biomethane and electricity, in projects at various stages of development. Case Construction is also developing an ethanol-powered wheel loader, aiming to reduce costs for sugar-and-ethanol mills. The machine has been tested in industrial settings and will now move to trials on client farms.

Jacto, one of the leaders in spraying equipment, is also investing in an ethanol engine for input application, noted Carlos Haushahn, the company's chief executive officer (CEO). The technology is still in testing, with no launch date yet set.
