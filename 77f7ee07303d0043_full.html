# We lack true dialogue between public and private sectors to discuss agriculture: Grupo Evo

Grupo Evo has consolidated a business model that integrates fertilizer importation, transformation and distribution, financing for producers, and logistics operations, with the objective of addressing bottlenecks in Colombian agriculture and boosting productivity. In this process, its CEO, René Puche Restrepo, warned that Colombia uses only 12% of its arable land and that 85% of farmers, mostly small-scale producers, remain outside the financial system, which limits their development. For this reason, the executive highlighted the business work that the group is doing for the country's agriculture.

**How is Grupo Evo performing?**

I am convinced that Colombia has truly unprecedented potential for agricultural growth and sustainability. A few years ago we conducted an evaluation and decided to create this business group, where we combine what we call solutions for Colombian agriculture. On one hand, we have a business unit focused on the importation, transformation and distribution of fertilizers. We have Evo Crédito, where we have dedicated ourselves to financing farmers, whether agro-industrial or small farmers. We understand that in the Colombian market, 15% of all farmers are agro-industrial and 85% are small farmers. We also have a logistics unit called Trading, through which we handle ship unloading, port operations, internal transport, at ports and in different warehouses.

**What led you to finance farmers?**

We saw that 85% of small farmers practically don't exist for the financial system. Additionally, in Colombia only 12% of the cultivable area is used out of a total of 40 million hectares—we practically only use 6 million. Also, we only apply 30% of the amount of fertilizers we should be applying. For this reason, we understand that Colombia not only has the agricultural capacity to be self-sustaining and stop importing food, but has tremendous potential to be a generator of labor, economic and social opportunities.

**What is your added value?**

We have the capacity to handle all the transformation we do with fertilizers, mixing and so on. But it also gives us the opportunity to provide logistics services, not only to the group, but also to third parties. We do this thanks to the company, with 18 years of experience, that we acquired in November 2024. In the agriculture sector, through our majority shareholder XTS, who are bankers from the United States, we have closed the virtuous business cycle. Through them, we had the ability to give farmers the freedom to pay us with part of their production, and a fraction of that we use so that, through XTS, it can be exported.

**What products are you exporting?**

Right now, we are focused on coffee and palm oil. Last year we made exports of approximately 75,000 sacks of coffee and about 30,000 tons of palm oil. This is a barter business that is also giving us a bit more traction in this entire agricultural model, and it also gives us the opportunity to continue understanding and identifying opportunities that will take us forward.

**How is the group growing?**

We have been experiencing interesting growth. The fertilizer market in Colombia has historically been around two million tons. For this reason, with the business models we are implementing, we are seeking to grow the market. We are at roughly 10% of the market. Last year we reached almost 190,000 tons, with a very interesting presence in markets like coffee, although we reach all crops. Our credit unit also started, mostly in coffee, financing coffee growers through cooperatives.

**How much have you placed in financing?**

In terms of financing we have placed US$30 million, through factoring lines and some foreign investment from foreign funds. In the small farmer sector, we are going to start a pilot in the second half of this year, in Cundinamarca, Boyacá, Cauca and Nariño. We are selecting between 100 and 150 farmers to finance fertilizers for harvest. We even want to receive the harvest as part payment, understanding that one of the challenges our farmers face is the disruption in supply and demand, and to get it circulating through supermarket lines.

**Where are your plants located?**

We have one plant in Barranquilla and another in Buga. We import through those two ports, Buenaventura and Barranquilla. Right now, we are conducting the proper investigations, because we have great interest in entering the Urabá zone, with the launch of Puerto Antioquia.
