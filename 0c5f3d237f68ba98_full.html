The R$37 billion "blackout" in the FIDC market

20 April 2026 · 12:10 PM

# The R$37 billion "blackout" in the FIDC market

A class of funds that has grown the most in Brazil in recent years, Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) are known for their complex structures involving different types of receivables. But at this moment, the complexity has combined with a kind of "blackout."

The delays in disclosing monthly FIDC reports have become recurrent in recent months and have demanded a more active stance from the Securities and Exchange Commission (CVM). The document, which brings information such as assets, credit volume and default levels, is the main reference for those who regularly monitor these portfolios.

According to a survey by Uqbar conducted at NeoFeed's request, 76 FIDCs had not yet reported February data as of April 10 — the deadline for delivery was March 15. The gap is R$37 billion, considering the last net equity reported by the funds. The volume corresponds to 5% of the entire FIDC industry and 50% of all net fundraising from last year.

The study considered only active FIDCs that had reported monthly reports in November, when the delays began to worsen, according to Alfredo Marrucho, partner at Uqbar. Marrucho has followed the FIDC market for more than 10 years and states that non-compliance in delivering reports is something unprecedented, especially in these proportions.

"Some administrators claimed that the year-end transition would have interfered. But the argument doesn't hold, given that in early 2025 there wasn't the same volume of delays. It was about 1% of the industry," he says.

Reag, currently being liquidated by the Central Bank, is the administrator with the largest number of FIDCs that did not deliver February data, with 37. Planner comes next, with 11 FIDCs with delayed reports. Of the 76 FIDCs that did not publish February statements, 30 were with at least two overdue reports. Another 39 FIDCs failed to present reports from December and January.

These gaps, according to Marrucho, have become increasingly frequent and have hampered industry monitoring. "Even with FIDCs raising capital, a survey we conducted pointed to a decline in assets, due to delays in disclosures. We no longer have a notion of the actual size of this market."

Of the FIDCs that have not yet presented the February balance sheet, the largest is the ANNA FIC FIDC Non-Standardized. The fund, administered by Reag and managed by CBSF Trust, reported net equity of R$15.7 billion in December. Created in late 2023, the fund invested primarily in shares of other investment funds and never had an audited financial statement.

Of the R$37 billion that are in the dark, the funds administered by Reag account for R$23.66 billion. "Reag is having operational problems today. The Central Bank, in the liquidation, is keeping an eye on these portfolios, many of them don't have adequate backing," says a fund administrator.

These delays, according to this source, may be generating a cascading effect. "If a FIDC doesn't send the information, another fund that invests in it ends up not having enough data to account for its portfolio."

One of Reag's funds with reports is the Rover FIDC, which came to have a significant portion in credits assigned by EntrePay, according to NeoFeed reporting, before the institution entered liquidation by the Central Bank at the end of last month. As a result of EntrePay's liquidation, the Redwood manager suspended one of its funds for redemption at the beginning of this month. Two other funds from the manager, which totaled more than R$130 million in assets in November, have had payments delayed for months.

The scenario is similar to Fictor FIDC, which, as NeoFeed showed, also closed for redemptions amid liquidity problems in operations. The fund, which until January declared R$201 million, also did not publish its February report.

## CVM on alert

The recurring delays in submitting monthly reports have also drawn the attention of the CVM. Faced with the problem, the agency published an official letter early this year, reinforcing that delays are subject to automatic fines of up to R$60,000 per fund. In the letter, the CVM informed that "it has received an impressive volume of appeals filed by administrators" due to fines applied for delay or failure to deliver information.

"Sometimes administrators claim in their appeals that they were surprised by the volume of fines applied and that this will impact their assets. However, such claims reinforce that internal controls are not adequate, including to identify delays that have occurred and account, in a timely manner, for provisions and expenses for future payment of fines," says the CVM letter.

For a businessman in the FIDC business, the lack of transparency in publishing reports has generated an "immense" reputational risk for the industry. "There is enormous fear, including among major fintech market players. It's a class that has become increasingly institutional and a bad administrator disrupts the entire operation," he says.

This source, who spoke with NeoFeed under the condition of anonymity, also stated that even published data may be inaccurate due to the difficulty of processing more complex portfolios. "I feel that many times what is being reported is not exactly what is happening in real time within the portfolio. FIDCs are becoming a class of assets that institutional investors are allocating increasingly to — and institutional investors don't tolerate this level of little information, wrong information."

In his assessment, it's a matter of time before the CVM toughens the rules for the FIDC market, especially now that it will have the help of the BC to supervise this class of funds. "The Central Bank, in November of last year, raised the bar for DTVMs. It's a move that the Central Bank has already been very categorical about and I think the CVM should certainly do the same soon. So many administrators will be left behind."

When questioned about the delay in delivering reports, Planner informed that the FIDCs identified in the survey "are funds recently transferred from other administrators, at different stages of regularization." According to Planner, "most present pending issues that already existed with the previous administrator on the date of transfer" and, in two of them, the delay stems from "operational issues in implementing the internal system."

The company further stated that "there is no chain effect" on other funds.
