The R$37 billion 'blackout' in the FIDC market

20 April 2026 · 12:10 PM

# The R$37 billion 'blackout' in the FIDC market

A class of funds that has grown the most in Brazil in recent years, Credit Rights Investment Funds (FIDCs) are known for their complex structures involving different types of receivables. But at this moment, complexity has been compounded by a kind of "blackout."

Delays in the disclosure of monthly FIDC reports have become recurrent in recent months and have demanded a more active stance from the Securities and Exchange Commission (CVM). The document, which contains information such as assets, credit volume and delinquency levels, is the main reference for those who regularly monitor these portfolios.

According to a survey by Uqbar conducted at NeoFeed's request, 76 FIDCs had not yet reported February data as of April 10 — the deadline for submission was March 15. The gap is R$37 billion, considering the last net assets reported by the funds. The volume corresponds to 5% of the entire FIDC industry and 50% of all net capital raised last year.

The study considered only active FIDCs that had reported monthly reports in November, when delays began to worsen, according to Alfredo Marrucho, partner at Uqbar. Marrucho has been following the FIDC market for over 10 years and states that non-compliance in the delivery of reports is unprecedented, particularly in these proportions.

"Some administrators claimed that the year-end transition caused problems. But this argument doesn't hold, given that at the beginning of 2025 there wasn't the same volume of delays. It was about 1% of the industry," he says.

Reag, currently in liquidation by the Central Bank, is the administrator with the largest number of FIDCs that failed to deliver February data, with 37. Planner comes next, with 11 FIDCs with delayed reports. Of the 76 FIDCs that did not publish February balances, 30 had at least two delayed reports. Another 39 FIDCs failed to present reports for December and January.

These gaps, according to Marrucho, have become increasingly frequent and hindered industry monitoring. "Even with FIDCs raising capital, a survey we conducted showed a decline in assets due to delays in disclosures. We no longer have a true sense of the size of this market."

Of the FIDCs that have not yet presented the February balance sheet, the largest is the ANNA FIC FIDC Non-Standard fund. The fund, administered by Reag and managed by CBSF Trust, reported net assets of R$15.7 billion in December. Created at the end of 2023, the fund primarily invested in shares of other investment funds and never had an audited financial statement.

Of the R$37 billion in the dark, funds administered by Reag account for R$23.66 billion. "Reag is experiencing operational problems today. The Central Bank, in the liquidation process, is keeping an eye on these portfolios, many of which don't have adequate backing," says a fund administrator.

These delays, according to this source, may be generating a cascading effect. "If one FIDC doesn't send information, another fund that invests in it ends up not having sufficient data to account for its portfolio."

One of Reag's funds with reports is Rover FIDC, which at one point had a significant portion in credit assigned by EntrePay, according to NeoFeed reporting, before the institution entered liquidation by the Central Bank at the end of last month. As a result of EntrePay's liquidation, manager Redwood suspended one of its funds for redemptions at the beginning of this month. Two other funds from the manager, which totaled more than R$130 million in assets in November, have had payments delayed for months.

The scenario is similar to Fictor FIDC, which, as NeoFeed showed, also closed for redemptions amid liquidity problems on the operational front. The fund, which until January declared R$201 million, also did not publish its February report.

## CVM on alert

The recurrent delays in delivering monthly reports have also caught the attention of the CVM. In light of the problem, the agency published a memo at the beginning of this year, reiterating that delays are subject to automatic fines of up to R$60,000 per fund. In the memo, the CVM reported that "it has received a significant volume of appeals filed by administrators" due to fines applied for late or non-delivery of information.

"Sometimes administrators claim in their appeals that they were surprised by the volume of fines applied and that this will impact their assets. However, such allegations reinforce that internal controls are not adequate, including to identify delays that have occurred and timely account for provisions and expenses for the future payment of fines," says the CVM memo.

For a businessman in the FIDC business, the lack of transparency in the publication of reports has generated an "immense" reputational risk for the industry. "There is enormous fear, including among major players in the fintech market. This is a class that has become increasingly institutional and one bad administrator disrupts the entire operation," he says.

This source, who spoke with NeoFeed on condition of anonymity, also stated that even published data may be imprecise due to the difficulty of processing more complex portfolios. "I feel that often what is being reported is not exactly what is happening in real time within the portfolio. FIDCs are becoming a class of assets that institutional investors have been allocating to increasingly — and institutional investors don't tolerate this level of lack of information, of incorrect information."

In his assessment, it is only a matter of time before the CVM tightens rules for the FIDC market, especially now that it will have the help of the BC to supervise this class of funds. "The Central Bank, in November of last year, raised the bar for DTVMs. It's a move in which the Central Bank has already been very categorical and I think the CVM will certainly do the same soon. Then many administrators will be left in the middle of the road."

When questioned about the delay in delivering reports, Planner stated that the FIDCs identified in the survey "are funds recently transferred from other administrators, at different stages of regularization." According to Planner, "most present pending issues that already existed at the previous administrator at the time of transfer" and, in two of them, the delay stems from "operational issues in implementation in the internal system."

The company also stated that "there is no chain effect" on other funds.
