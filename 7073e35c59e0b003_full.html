# AI, Data Centers and Energy: Why Batteries Become the Critical Link in Latin America

Buenos Aires — The advancement of artificial intelligence is driving a new wave of investments in digital infrastructure, but it is also beginning to strain a key front: energy. In Latin America, the growth of data centers — led by giants like Amazon Web Services, Microsoft and Google — opens a central question: how to supply a growing, constant electrical demand that is increasingly demanding in environmental terms.

"Data centers are expected to be a concentrator of very intense, very large electrical demand," says Alfredo Solar, regional manager of Atlas Renewable Energy for Chile and the Southern Cone. That demand is not only high, but also constant: "Basically it is a unit that will be consuming those 100 megawatts for 8,700 hours of the year."

In that context, the impact on electrical systems can be significant. "Data centers may require 30% or 40% more installed capacity than a country has over a time horizon of, say, 10 years," he adds. According to the International Energy Agency, global electricity consumption by data centers, artificial intelligence and cryptocurrencies could more than double by the end of the decade, in line with growing demand for computing capacity to train AI models.

In this scenario, Latin America appears as a region with advantages: abundant renewable resources, relatively low costs and space to expand infrastructure. However, the challenge is not just generating clean energy, but guaranteeing constant supply. "They are basically looking for two things when it comes to energy. Of course, they are looking for countries that are stable so they can make their investments, but fundamentally that the energy is renewable, clean, certified and that it can be supplied to them 24 hours a day," Solar points out.

## The Role of Batteries: Delivering Solar Energy 24/7

This is where storage begins to play a central role. The expansion of renewable energies — particularly solar — created new opportunities, but also imbalances in electrical systems. Solar explains that in markets like Chile there is an oversupply of energy during the day, with very low or even near-zero prices, while at night generation depends on more expensive sources, generally fossil fuels.

"So there is a commercial opportunity to store large amounts of energy during the day, when energy is very cheap, and inject it into the grid later when the price is much higher," he states. This differential is the basis of the battery business model, which relies on price arbitrage and capacity payments within the electrical system. In parallel, the fall in technology costs has allowed this type of investment to become profitable.

## Chile, Regional Laboratory

The most advanced development of this trend is happening today in Chile, where the combination of abundant solar resources and specific regulation allowed storage to take off. "Today we should already be reaching about 6,000 megawatts of installed storage capacity and construction continues," Solar notes. Additionally, he projects investments on the order of $10 billion toward 2027-2028.

But leadership is not only energetic. Latin America is experiencing a boom in data centers, with accelerated growth in installed capacity in recent years. In that context, along with Brazil, Mexico and Colombia, Chile concentrates a large part of the projects and developments in the region. Unlike other markets, the country managed to establish clear rules for battery operation within the electrical system, which allowed for large-scale investments to be viable.
