# Sleeping Budget: 1,442 Districts Have Not Used a Quarter of Funds for Public Works

In Peru, the calendar advances, but public management does not keep pace with it. Gestión reviewed the spending capacity of Peru's 1,891 municipalities and discovered that budget execution for local projects is setting off alarm bells. It is not just slow work: in some cases, it has been nonexistent through April 2026.

To date, districts and provinces have S/ 23,147.9 million available for their projects. As of April 17, they have invested 19.1% of this amount. The data comes from the Economic Transparency platform, a digital tool of the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), which allows monitoring how public entities in the country spend money.

## A Critical Execution Problem

A critical finding is that 97 of the 1,891 municipalities in the country have not executed a single sol through April. Their spending level is zero in a context where demand for basic services, infrastructure, and security continues to increase. Amazonas, Áncash, Apurímac, Arequipa, Ayacucho, Cajamarca, Huancavelica, Junín, Lambayeque, Lima, Pasco, Piura, Puno, and San Martín are home to these municipalities.

But there are more numbers in the red: when expanding the view, 1,442 municipalities were detected as not exceeding 25% budget execution through the evaluated period. That is, 76.26% of Peru's municipalities register a harmful spending pattern: money paused at the beginning of the year and accelerated at the end of it.

Karla Gaviño, a Public Management professor at the University of the Pacific (UP), explains this trend but first points out its implications: "We are in April. That there are municipalities with 0% budget execution means that they are not even paying advances for technical files, designing, or convening for the physical execution of works, so we are quite far from them being able to make a financial movement."

She adds: "This leads us to a recurring scenario every time there is a change in government, in which during the first months there is little budget execution, but in the last months there is a kind of race to use all the money possible. The punishment is concentrated in the quality of execution."

Álvaro Cubas, an economist at the Private Council for Competitiveness (CPC), agrees: "Unfortunately, it is habitual behavior for municipalities, but one always tries to think that, being the last year of management, they should go against the current: spend more to close more, but that is not what we are seeing."

He adds: "We could affirm that rushed execution also translates into low-quality execution. For example, in a hypothetical case, they gave S/ 1 million to a project, but between November and December S/ 800,000 was spent. Then, it is understood that, over the course of the year, the demands that the work might have had were not met. [...] Delayed spending will also cause the general project progress to be 'kicked down the road.'"

## The "Race" Before Year-End

When there is a change in subnational governments, the probability increases that ongoing projects will be paralyzed because each administration typically pursues its own agenda. "The risk is greater when a change of authority occurs. According to what we saw at the CPC, that is when the number of discontinued projects is largest. [...] It will matter greatly whether the incoming mayor has the political commitment to continue the project that the previous authority left. What we end up having as citizens is the risk that what was promised will not be fulfilled, that the spending that was done will not translate into works or well-being," Cubas warns.

Gaviño is emphatic on this point: "There is a very perverse objective, which is to close the year precisely because the administration ends. So, in the last months, the spending that is executed is financial spending that does not necessarily match the physical execution of work."

"This leads us to infer, potentially, that we are talking about works done with considerable haste and with reduced quality. And not only with reduced quality, but also with spending converted into simplistic expenses." She refers to the fact that the urgency to show results usually tips the balance toward interventions that do not address deeper needs: "They will not necessarily build works that generate greater impact on social gaps, but rather those that are more populist and remembered in the voter's memory."

## Geographic Disparities

The lag also has marked geography. Áncash, with 132 municipalities, and Lima, with 126, lead the list with the highest number of municipalities that fail to execute a quarter of their budget in the first months of the year. They are followed by Junín with 102, Ayacucho with 100, and Cajamarca with 95.

Cubas makes a note about this: "Many municipalities are wealthy municipalities [...]. The saddest part of all this is that, beyond delays, when a project is delayed in execution and years pass, its cost will increase due to reactivation. A project that cost S/ 100 million will cost S/ 300 million. It is not that the project scope increased, but rather S/ 200 million more is being spent simply because it was paralyzed and requires other additional expenses, administrative especially, so that it gets back on track."

The academic emphasizes that the situation is especially concerning in regions that receive canon revenue, such as Áncash or Cajamarca. Having more resources and having the numbers against you reflects a lack of technical capacity. "It is not healthy that Áncash and Cajamarca do not have accompaniment in budget allocation, there is neither political priority nor technical spending capacity. These are regrettable indicators."

"And there is another additional fact. It is a problem that crosses the entire country [the lack of strategy in municipal budget execution]. We cannot speak of a focus on a specific zone. [...] Hence the importance of the MEF, which must provide support from the public investment and budget oversight departments."
