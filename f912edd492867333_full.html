Budget Execution Crisis: 1,442 Peruvian Districts Have Used Less Than 25% of 2026 Budgets

20 April 2026 · 11:29 PM

# Budget "Dormant": 1,442 Districts Have Not Used Even a Quarter of Their Money for Works

In Peru, the calendar advances, but public management does not move at the same pace. Gestión reviewed the spending capacity of Peru's 1,891 municipalities and discovered that budget execution for projects at the local level sets off an alarm signal. It is not only a matter of slow work: in some cases it is nonexistent through what has elapsed of 2026.

To date, districts and provinces have S/ 23,147.9 million for their projects. Of this amount, as of April 17, they have invested 19.1%. The data comes from the Economic Transparency platform, a digital tool of the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), which allows monitoring how public entities in the country dispose of money.

## Critical Execution Data

A critical figure is that 97 of the 1,891 municipalities in the country have not executed a single sol through April. Their spending level is zero in a context where demand for basic services, infrastructure, and security continues to increase. Amazonas, Áncash, Apurímac, Arequipa, Ayacucho, Cajamarca, Huancavelica, Junín, Lambayeque, Lima, Pasco, Piura, Puno, and San Martín are home to these municipalities.

But there are more numbers in "red": when broadening the view, it was detected that 1,442 municipalities do not exceed 25% budget execution through the evaluated period. That is, 76.26% of Peru's municipalities register a harmful spending pattern: money paused at the beginning of the year and accelerated at the end of it.

Karla Gaviño, Public Management professor at the University of the Pacific (UP), explains this trend, but first points out its implications: "We are in April. That there are municipalities with 0% budget reduction means they are not even paying advances for technical documents, designing, or calling for the physical execution of works, which means we are quite far from them being able to make a financial movement."

She adds: "This leads us to a recurring scenario each time there is a change of government, in which during the first months there is no major spending execution, but in the last months there is a kind of race to use all the money possible. The punishment is concentrated in the quality of execution."

Álvaro Cubas, economist at the Private Council for Competitiveness (CPC), agrees: "Unfortunately, it is habitual behavior by municipalities, but one always tries to think that, being the last year of management, they should go against the grain: spend more to close more, but that is not what is being seen."

He adds: "We could affirm that hasty execution also translates into low-quality execution. For example, in a hypothetical case, they gave S/ 1 million to a project, but between November and December S/ 800,000 was spent. Then it gives the sense that, in the course of the year, the demands the work could have had were not met. [...] Delayed spending will also cause the project's overall progress to be delayed."

## The "Race" Before Year-End

When there is a change in subnational governments, the probability increases that projects underway will be paralyzed because each administration usually pushes its own agenda. "The risk is greater when there is a change of authority. According to what we saw at the CPC, that is when the number of discontinued projects is largest. [...] It will weigh heavily whether the incoming mayor has the political commitment to continue the project the previous authority left. What we end up having as citizens is the risk that what was promised will not be fulfilled, that the spending that was done will not translate into works or wellbeing," warns Cubas.

Gaviño is emphatic on this point: "There is a very perverse objective, which is to close the year precisely because the management ends. So, in the last months, the spending that is executed is financial spending that does not necessarily match the physical execution of works."

"This leads us to infer, potentially, that we are talking about works done in quite a hurry and with reduced quality. And not only with reduced quality, but also with spending converted into simplistic spending. She refers to the fact that the urgency to show results often tilts the balance toward interventions that do not address deeper needs: "Works will not necessarily be done that generate greater impact on social gaps, but rather those that are more populist and will be remembered in the voter's memory."

## Lima Cases

In Lima province, two districts registered 0% progress: Barranco and San Bartolo. The jurisdictions have S/ 1.8 million and S/ 1.9 million for their projects, respectively. In the case of Metropolitan Lima, which has S/ 886.1 million for its works, 33.4% has already been executed.

## Cross-Cutting Diagnosis

The lag, moreover, has a marked geography. Áncash, with 132 municipalities, and Lima, with 126, top the list with the greatest number of municipalities that fail to execute a quarter of their budget in the first months of the year. They are followed by Junín, with 102, Ayacucho, with 100, and Cajamarca, with 95.

Cubas makes a note on this: "A large part of the municipalities are wealthy municipalities [...]. The saddest part of all this is that, beyond the delays, when a project is slow to execute and years pass, its cost will increase due to reactivation. A project that cost S/ 100 million will cost S/ 300 million. And it is not that the project scope increased, but S/ 200 million more is being spent simply because it was halted and requires other additional, administrative expenses, above all, to get it back on track."

For her part, the academic emphasizes that the situation is especially worrying in regions that receive canon revenue, such as Áncash or Cajamarca. Having more resources and having numbers against you reflects a lack of technical capacity. "It is not healthy that Áncash and Cajamarca do not have accompaniment in budget allocation, there is neither political priority nor technical spending capacity. These are regrettable indicators."

"And there is another additional fact. It is a transversal problem throughout the country [the lack of strategy in municipal budget execution]. We cannot speak of a focus on a determined zone. [...] Hence the importance of the MEF, which must provide support from the public investment and budget governance roles."
