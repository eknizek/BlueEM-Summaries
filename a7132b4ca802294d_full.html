Cocamar sues Patria over grain storage units dispute

13 April 2026 · 10:51 PM

# "He gave the green light and disappeared." Why Cocamar took Patria to court

Patria viewed Cocamar's messages — and never responded. This summarizes a legal battle between the alternative asset manager and the Paraná-based cooperative whose revenue exceeds R$ 11 billion.

It all started in December when Patria put four grain storage units up for sale in Sabáudia, Assaí, Bela Vista do Paraíso, and Cambé, all in Paraná. At the time, Cocamar negotiated the purchase of the four assets for R$ 125 million and received an email from the manager saying the deal was closed. "Thank you for sending the revised proposal — we agree to proceed on the terms of the attached proposal," the December 23rd email said, initiating discussions for contract signing.

Cocamar closed the deal — but didn't take possession. According to court records, after that email Patria disappeared, and in January announced the sale of the assets to Coamo, another Paraná cooperative, for a slightly better price: R$ 136 million.

Shocked, Cocamar went to court — and in late February obtained an injunction at the appellate level that prevents the sale or encumbrance of the assets until the case is decided on its merits. (Cocamar had lost in the first instance.) The decision has already been registered in the property records — which in practice blocks any transfer attempt. The case is under court secrecy and further appeals are still possible.

Cocamar's argument is that a binding proposal, once accepted, creates obligations between parties, especially when accompanied by acts indicating the intention to close the deal, such as drafting contracts and beginning due diligence. The cooperative filed a lawsuit seeking performance of the offer — that is, for the assets to be transferred to it under the originally agreed conditions.

"But Cocamar would be willing to negotiate with Patria. The company remains interested in the assets," a person close to the cooperative told Brazil Journal.

Patria has not yet responded, but its defense may follow what was decided in the first instance. At the 4th Civil Court of Maringá, Judge Aline Koentopp recognized that negotiations occurred but considered mere communication between the two sides fragile. In the judge's assessment, the communication indicates the deal had not been formally concluded, leaving room for potential damages. Still, the judge authorized the registration of the lawsuit's existence on one of the four properties.

Cocamar appealed, and at the appellate level Judge Domingos José Perfetto understood there were sufficient elements to indicate the probability of the cooperative's rights and the risk of harm if the assets were transferred to third parties. With this, Paraná's Court of Justice extended the measure to all units involved in the negotiation, determining registration in property records and prohibiting the sale or encumbrance of properties.

In other words, in practice, the assets cannot be transferred until the courts make a final decision, which will ultimately determine whether Patria was merely impolite — by disengaging overnight and leaving a buyer without a response — or violated Cocamar's rights.

Patria said it cannot comment on a case under court secrecy.
