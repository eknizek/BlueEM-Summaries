# "He Agreed and Disappeared." Why Cocamar Took Patria to Court

Patria read Cocamar's messages — and never responded. This is the summary behind a legal battle between the alternative asset manager and the Paraná-based cooperative whose annual revenue exceeds R$ 11 billion.

Everything started in December, when Patria put four grain storage units up for sale in Sabáudia, Assaí, Bela Vista do Paraíso, and Cambé, all in Paraná. At the time, Cocamar negotiated the purchase of the four assets for R$ 125 million and received an email from the asset manager saying the deal was closed. "Thank you for sending the revised proposal – we agree to proceed on the terms of the attached proposal sent," said the email from December 23rd, beginning negotiations for contract signing.

Cocamar closed the deal — but didn't take possession. According to court documents, after that email, Patria disappeared, and in January announced the sale of the assets to Coamo, another Paraná cooperative, for a slightly better price: R$ 136 million. Shocked, Cocamar went to court — and in late February obtained an injunction in the appeals court that prevents the transfer or encumbrance of the assets until judgment on the merits. (Cocamar had lost in the lower court.)

The decision has already been registered on the property records — which, in practice, blocks any attempt at transfer. The case is under court secrecy and can still be appealed.

Cocamar's thesis is that a binding proposal, once accepted, creates obligations between parties, especially when accompanied by actions indicating the intention to close the deal, such as contract preparation and the beginning of due diligence. The cooperative filed a lawsuit requesting performance of the offer — that is, that the assets be transferred to it under the originally agreed terms.

"But Cocamar would be willing to negotiate with Patria. The company remains interested in the assets," someone close to the cooperative told Brazil Journal.

Patria has not yet commented, but its defense could follow the line decided in the lower court. In the 4th Civil Court of Maringá, Judge Aline Koentopp acknowledged that there was negotiation, but considered the mere communication between the two sides fragile. In the judge's view, the communication indicates that the deal had not been formally concluded, opening space for a possible resolution in damages.

Nevertheless, the judge authorized the registration of the lawsuit's existence on one of the four properties. Cocamar appealed, and in the appeals court, Justice Domingos José Perfetto understood that there are sufficient elements to indicate the probability of the cooperative's rights and the risk of damage if the assets are transferred to third parties. With this, the Paraná Court of Justice extended the measure to all units involved in the negotiation, ordering registration and prohibiting the sale or encumbrance of the properties.

In other words, in practice, the assets cannot be transferred until the final word from the courts, which will ultimately decide whether Patria was merely impolite — by disengaging overnight and leaving a buyer without a response — or violated Cocamar's rights.

Patria said it cannot comment on a case under court secrecy.
