# Cocamar sues Patria over failed grain storage unit sale

Patria viewed Cocamar's messages—and didn't respond. This is the summary behind a legal battle between the alternative asset manager and the Paraná-based cooperative whose revenue exceeds R$ 11 billion.

It all began in December, when Patria put four grain storage units up for sale in Sabáudia, Assaí, Bela Vista do Paraíso, and Cambé, all in Paraná. At the time, Cocamar negotiated the purchase of the four assets for R$ 125 million and received an email from the manager saying the deal was closed. "Thank you for sending the revised proposal – we agree to proceed on the terms of the attached proposal sent," the December 23 email said, initiating negotiations for contract signing.

Cocamar closed—but didn't acquire. According to court records, after that email, Patria disappeared, and in January announced the sale of the assets to Coamo, another Paraná cooperative, for a slightly better price: R$ 136 million. Shocked, Cocamar went to court—and in late February obtained an injunction in the second instance that prevents the transfer or encumbrance of the assets until the case is decided on its merits. (Cocamar had lost in the first instance.) The decision has already been registered in the property records—which, in practice, blocks any transfer attempt. The case is under court confidentiality and can still be appealed.

Cocamar's thesis is that a binding proposal, once accepted, creates an obligation between the parties, especially when accompanied by actions indicating the intention to close the deal, such as drafting contracts and initiating due diligence. The cooperative filed a suit seeking performance of the offer—that is, that the assets be transferred to it under the originally agreed conditions.

"But Cocamar would be willing to negotiate with Patria. The company remains interested in the assets," a person close to the cooperative told Brazil Journal.

Patria has not yet responded, but its defense may follow the line decided in the first instance. In the 4th Civil Court of Maringá, Judge Aline Koentopp acknowledged that there was negotiation, but considered the mere communication between the two sides to be fragile. In the judge's assessment, the communication indicates that the deal had not been formally concluded, leaving room for possible resolution with damages.

Nevertheless, the judge authorized the registration of the existence of the suit on one of the four properties. Cocamar appealed, and in the second instance, Judge Domingos José Perfetto understood that there are sufficient elements to indicate the probability of the cooperative's right and the risk of harm if the assets are transferred to third parties. With this, the Paraná Court of Justice extended the measure to all units involved in the negotiation, determining registration in the property records and prohibiting the sale or encumbrance of the properties.

In other words, in practice, the assets cannot be transferred until the final word from the courts, which will ultimately decide whether Patria was merely inelegant—by disengaging overnight and leaving a buyer without a response—or violated Cocamar's rights.

Patria said it cannot comment on a case under court confidentiality.
