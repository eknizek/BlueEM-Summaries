# With Credit Frozen, Companies Accelerate Capital Increases to Cut Debt

Capital increases have regained traction as one of the main solutions for indebted companies amid an increasingly expensive and selective credit market. In the year's cumulative total, the volume of shares issued by listed companies stands at R$ 7.5 billion, in operations that largely focus on reducing leverage and strengthening capital structure. The largest contributions came from Azul, which conducted a follow-on of nearly R$ 5 billion as part of its restructuring, including debt-to-equity conversion, and Hypera Pharma, which raised R$ 1.5 billion in a private offering restricted to company shareholders.

The volume of primary emissions this year is already 85% higher than registered in the same period of 2025, and has conditions to more than double in the short term, considering only open operations. The emission with the greatest potential is the BRB's issuance of R$ 536 million to R$ 8.8 billion in shares, which seeks to restructure its capital after significant losses with Banco Master. Another relevant offer on the radar involves the Simpar group, which is structuring capital increases to reduce its debt and that of its subsidiaries. The holding — whose net debt corresponds to about 3 times its Ebitda — could raise up to R$ 2 billion, while its subsidiaries Vamos and Movida seek up to R$ 600 million and R$ 750 million, respectively. This week, Movida reported that it had already reached the minimum amount of R$ 500 million with its shareholders, making the offer viable.

Vitru Educação is also moving to raise capital without resorting to more debt. On Wednesday, April 8, the owner of Uniasselvi and Unicesumar colleges filed a public share offering with potential to raise up to R$ 300 million, with part of the funds intended for debt amortization. The list of companies that have already turned to the stock market this year to try to reduce debt also includes Grupo Mateus, Pague Menos, Gafisa, Infracommerce, and Paranapanema. Though publicly traded but off the exchange, Aegea raised R$ 1.2 billion from its main shareholders to try to equalize its debt.

"In general, these operations appear in more pressured companies. If the company were doing well, it wouldn't need to raise funds, unless there was a major investment opportunity," says Ricardo Simon, director of the Eclipseon family office. For Simon, the worsening inflation expectations tied to the effects of wars in the Middle East may have aggravated this scenario. "There was an expectation of a greater drop in long-term interest rates, but it doesn't seem that will materialize, at least in the short term. So these companies that have higher leverage levels may end up having more difficulty."

In the Focus Bulletin, expectations for the Selic at the end of this year rose from 12.13% to 12.5% in the last four weeks, while projections for the IPCA rose from 3.91% to 4.36%. The greater effect, however, has been on fixed income, directly affecting the resource source for public companies.

**More Restricted Credit**

With expectations of higher inflation and interest rates, the benchmark for infrastructure funds closed March with negative performance of 1.74%, while spreads rose 34 basis points. The scenario, according to analysts at ABC bank, has similarities to that faced by the industry in October last year, when negative results dried up the volume of fundraising. In March this year, infrastructure funds had net fundraising of R$ 4.1 billion, according to ABC bank's survey.

"What is not yet known is the level of redemptions, given that the industry works with D+30 or longer periods. After October's poor performance, redemptions reached R$ 1 billion per day, but only in December, 45 days later," says the ABC report. In private credit funds, net fundraising fell from R$ 23.2 billion in January and R$ 8.4 billion in February to R$ 600 million in March. With less new money in credit funds and risk of withdrawals, the expectation is for a decline in debt issuance volumes.

In an ABC survey of credit managers, only 16% said that the primary market volume should increase, while 50% expect a decline in activity in the next three months. Pessimism is also regarding spread behavior, with 75% of managers expecting a price deterioration, compared to 24% six months ago. "The credit market has worsened. I wouldn't say the tap is completely closed, but it's dripping slowly. It's obvious this scenario makes things immensely difficult. Everyone was expecting the beginning of a major monetary relief cycle," says Daniel Utsch, manager at Nero Capital.

Capital increase, says the manager, is always a positive signal to the creditor, given that reference shareholders are putting new money into the company. He emphasizes, however, that these operations involve minority shareholder dilution if they don't follow the offering. "It's a strategic decision by the controlling shareholder, based on what he's seeing from creditors. In the end, it's a dispute between controlling shareholders and creditors to define where resources will come from to maintain company operations," states the manager.

With less new money to finance debt for companies that are already highly leveraged, another way out has been judicial or extrajudicial restructuring, as Raízen and GPA did last month. "In Raízen's case, for example, it's clear that creditors would prefer a major capital increase from Cosan and Shell, rather than an institutional reprieve."

Utsch's expectation is that most of the main restructurings on the exchange are already underway, without the need for major capital increases in the short term or bankruptcy filing requests. "If there's much more, then it's already a very bad scenario."

One case that remains open is Oncoclínicas. In November, the company conducted a capital increase of R$ 1.4 billion, with conversion of part of the debt, but continues seeking a solution with creditors, since it's on the verge of reaching the maximum leverage limit provided for in debt contracts, which would allow acceleration of maturity.
