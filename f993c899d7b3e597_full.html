# Financial Decisions After First Round

By Paul Rebolledo

Presidential elections typically impact the performance of local financial assets, generating episodes of volatility, at least temporarily. It is enough to recall the "stress scenarios" of 2011 and 2021, when candidates perceived as "anti-market" advanced to the second round. In those moments, nervousness was quickly reflected: stock market declines exceeding 15%, currency depreciation close to 10%, and increases in sovereign rates of up to 100 basis points. Local variables tend to decouple from the global environment. However, in both cases, some subsequent moderation allowed financial market stability to be recovered.

Although the results of the first round were not yet known at the time of writing this article, the current behavior of assets has been more influenced by international factors than local ones. In this context, how should we make investment decisions in the lead-up to the second round?

First, it is key to avoid hasty decisions driven by nervousness. Liquidating investments during moments of high volatility rarely proves profitable. Experience shows that many investors miss subsequent market rebounds. Therefore, it is essential to respect the risk appetite defined initially.

Second, one should avoid "playing the market" when there are short-term liquidity needs. If operations are required, such as exchanging currency to cover upcoming expenses, it is preferable to do so immediately. Attempting to anticipate better conditions can be counterproductive in an environment dominated by polls and changes in political discourse.

Third, diversification becomes even more relevant. Reducing local bias and expanding exposure to international assets helps mitigate risks associated with the political cycle. Likewise, prioritizing more resilient sectors—such as those linked to domestic demand—can offer greater stability against sectors more exposed to regulatory or political changes.

In sum, maintaining consistency with risk profile, avoiding impulsive decisions, and building a diversified portfolio are key to navigating electoral volatility with greater peace of mind.
