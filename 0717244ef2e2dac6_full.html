Zero-fare policy: Contribution may replace transport vouchers but increases costs for 95% of companies

# Zero-fare policy: Contribution may replace transport vouchers but increases costs for 95% of companies

BRASÍLIA — A contribution on the payroll could replace transport vouchers and finance a zero-fare policy in Brazil, but the cost would increase for 95% of companies, according to an unprecedented study by the Institute of Applied Economic Research (Ipea).

The researchers studied the impacts of the measure on public bus transportation (excluding trains and subways) in 12 Brazilian capitals: Belém, Belo Horizonte, Campo Grande, Curitiba, Florianópolis, Fortaleza, Manaus, Porto Alegre, Recife, Rio de Janeiro, Salvador, and São Paulo.

At the end of last year, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) asked the Ministry of Finance to develop studies to implement zero-fare transportation in Brazil. The government has not yet adopted the measure, but one of the proposals under analysis is to replace the current transport voucher with a contribution that would finance a subsidy.

The transport voucher covered 1.09 million formal workers in the 12 capitals in 2024, the most recent data available, representing approximately 9.5% of the total formal workers in these cities. The Ipea estimates, however, that this number is underestimated in official databases.

Under current rules, the employee bears the cost of their trips from home to work up to the limit of 6% of their basic salary. The company, in turn, is obligated to pay the entire amount exceeding those 6%.

Replacing the transport voucher with a social contribution from companies would require a constitutional amendment and is difficult to implement, according to Ipea, as it involves creating a new tax and depends on municipal adhesion and federative coordination.

In most of the analyzed cities, a rate of between 0.5% and 1% on payroll would be sufficient to double the current level of subsidy to bus systems. This scenario would represent the lowest cost for companies. To reduce the fare by half, rates between 1.7% and 2.6% would be necessary. To implement zero-fare, that is, completely free, the contribution would need to be between 2.4% and 8.5%, potentially reaching 13.7% in Belém, where there is no municipal subsidy.

The researchers estimate that about 70% of workers who receive transport vouchers (566,000 people) in the 12 capitals would benefit from replacing the voucher with a contribution that reduces the fare by 50%. For these workers, average monthly public transportation spending would fall by almost 40%, from R$79 to R$48.

"The creation of a Social Contribution for Urban Public Transportation (CTPU) would primarily benefit the low-income population. This would include both lower-income formal workers and the rest of the population outside the formal market (informal and unemployed), who would experience a reduction in costs for all their public transportation trips, not just home-work trips," the researchers say.

Companies, on the other hand, would face increased expenses. For 95% of establishments, the average monthly cost with employee transportation would rise from R$60 to R$1,100 to R$1,300 per company, corresponding to 1.8% to 2.1% of payroll or an increase of R$65 per employee per month. In the zero-fare scenario, the monthly cost per company would be R$2,710, equivalent to 4.37% of payroll, potentially reaching R$3,200 (R$166 per employee per month) in the scenario of greatest demand increase.

The increase in costs for companies occurs because, while the transport voucher is paid only to specific employees who live far away or earn less, the contribution would be a fixed rate on the company's entire gross payroll.

"The estimates above show that the cost of implementing CTPU would fall on employers, even in scenarios replacing the transport voucher. These effects are, however, short-term. In the medium and long term, it is likely that the additional cost imposed on employers will be passed on, in some way, to employees. For formal employees earning above minimum wage, the additional cost should be offset by lower salary increases," the study states.

São Paulo, for example, has the largest public transportation system among the analyzed capitals. A rate of 0.5% would already be sufficient for the city to raise R$1.419 billion per year, equivalent to the cost of building one kilometer of subway in the capital, given the number of companies and employed workers. The current subsidy is R$6.7 billion and already covers approximately 58% of system costs. To reduce the fare by half, a rate between 1.6% and 2% would be necessary. If the city council wants to grant total free fares, the contribution would need to be between 3.8% and 4.8%.

The study points to challenges in implementing the measure. Success would depend on careful institutional and regulatory design, metropolitan integration of public transportation, readjustment of concession contracts with transparency regarding management and operator remuneration rules.

"Creating the contribution would require a constitutional amendment, national regulation, and voluntary adhesion by municipalities or metropolitan regions. There is also the risk of fiscal competition between municipalities if the contribution is instituted in isolation, which reinforces the importance of metropolitan coordination."

A side effect considered in the study is that the increased cost for companies could increase informality and independent contractor arrangements. "Another challenge is that the policy would increase social charges between one and three percentage points. Additionally, the high informality of the Brazilian labor market, which limits the tax collection base and may reduce the policy's long-term sustainability. Finally, higher CTPU rates tend to incentivize independent contracting of these positions, especially in the middle and upper income ranges."
