Brazil Weighs Tax Breaks for Critical Minerals

30 April 2026 · 07:00 PM

The rapporteur for the bill that establishes the Brazilian National Policy for Critical and Strategic Minerals, Congressman Arnaldo Jardim, said Wednesday (29) that it should be submitted next Monday and will include tax incentives, such as a reduction in import taxes on products for the sector and exemption from income tax on technology patents and licenses.

"As we move along the [critical minerals] chain, these incentives will grow," he said, citing as an example the possibility of facilitating the import of equipment in the absence of national equivalents. Jardim participated in the event "Transição Energética: O Brasil como potência na energia limpa" (or "Energy Transition: Brazil as a clean energy powerhouse"), held by Valor and O Globo under the sponsorship of Vale.

The lawmaker rejected the idea of creating Terrabras, a state-owned company, as suggested by sectors of the Workers' Party (PT) to research, explore, and manage reserves considered crucial for national sovereignty and the energy transition. "The [government] should have an inductive, regulatory role, not a provider role."

The measure was also rejected by the Lula administration, which received technical reports against the creation of the company. One explanation was that the federal government already has a monopoly on the exploration of the subsoil and its mineral resources, according to the legislation, but state-owned companies cannot hold exclusive rights to use these resources.

The debate surrounding rare-earth production gained traction after U.S.-based USA Rare Earth announced the purchase of the Brazilian mining company Serra Verde, the only one that produces these elements on a large scale outside of Asia. Rare earths are essential for energy transition, an area in which Brazil, despite having a cleaner electricity mix than the global average and great potential in renewables, still faces obstacles to consolidating itself as a leader, according to authorities and experts gathered at the event.

Among the points identified as bottlenecks are the low density of the sector's production chain—solar panels and wind turbines are mostly imported—challenges in the exploration of critical minerals, and difficulties in including social and environmental risks in investment decisions.

André Luís Ferreira, executive director of the Institute of Energy and Environment (IEMA), observed that Brazil should follow the global trend of expanding electrification in the coming years, a movement that will demand infrastructure and equipment consistent with the needs of the energy transition, especially in transportation, which he says accounts for almost 60% of fossil fuel consumption in the country.

"Today, trucks emit as much [greenhouse gases] as the entire electricity sector combined. Brazil has a successful biofuel program, but when you look at the solutions, the biggest challenge is long-distance freight transport," said the expert.

The topic gained prominence due to the rise in diesel prices following the blockage of the Strait of Hormuz after the attacks on Iran. "Today, with the ongoing geopolitical conflicts, countries are looking more favorably at electrification to reduce their dependence on imported fossil fuels," said Bruna Mascotte, senior partner at Catavento Consultoria.

Although Brazil's emissions profile is very different from the global average—land-use change and deforestation account for about 40% of Brazilian emissions, and energy, 20%; in the world, on average, energy accounts for about 70%—the energy transition is also part of the decarbonization effort, according to the executive.

"We can move forward in road transport, maintaining the high renewability of the electricity sector, using batteries to deal with intermittent sources, and eventually thermal power plants, but without going back on what we have already achieved," she said.

Another factor that could elevate Brazil to the status of a global player is the development of technologies capable of mitigating damage already done to the environment, according to Alexandre Strapasson, a professor at the Center for Sustainable Development at the University of Brasília (UnB).

"Our tech industry can be catalyzed precisely by this opportunity presented by renewables. We can develop technologies to remove carbon from the atmosphere, such as mass reforestation and bioenergy; we can enable carbon markets that are more costly in other countries," said the professor.

To that end, it is crucial to combine sectoral planning with territorial planning, that is, with the priorities of the territories where projects will be installed, argued Ferreira, from IEMA. "[There are] social and environmental risks, and we need to internalize this in the decision-making process for investment in mining," he said, also highlighting the government's low capacity to meet the demand for requested licenses.

The director of corporate strategy at Casa dos Ventos, Erik Lima, in turn, defended the review of incentives for distributed generation (DG) of solar energy. The oversupply of renewable energies has caused problems such as generation curtailments to ensure grid stability.

"It may sound controversial but controlling DG or coupling batteries are important remedies to regulate expansion," he said.

For Clinger Barros, head of energy at the startup Lemon, lack of planning is at the root of the problems evidenced in the Brazilian energy sector. "We do not have, either from the government's or the administration's point of view, any national guideline on the electricity sector that we want in Brazil," he stated.

The lack of direction and coordination is harmful and also affects how legislation for the sector evolves, with "add-ons" that reflect diverse interests, and not a vision for the future of the country, according to Barros. "It will no longer be possible to talk about renewable energy without thinking about the planning of data centers or mining. We need to use the surplus energy for some project in Brazil that we want to implement," he argued.

Mascotte, from Catavento, highlighted that the high cost of capital in Brazil limits innovation to the academic environment. "Macroeconomic factors, such as the high interest rate, impact the cost of capital and are an obstacle to attracting resources," she said.

In addition, the expansion of the electricity sector and of renewable sources also faces challenges in workforce qualification, from the factory floor to the corporate and executive level, according to Lima, from Casa dos Ventos. "The shortage is brutal," he said.
