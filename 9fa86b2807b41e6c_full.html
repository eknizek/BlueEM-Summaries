# Oncoclínicas Reports Deteriorating Leverage; Cites 'Relevant Uncertainty' on Operational Continuity

As expected by the market, Oncoclínicas presented a sharp deterioration in its leverage levels in the annual balance sheet released Thursday evening, April 9. In the period, the net debt to Ebitda ratio rose from 2.6 times to 3.5 times — a level that is already approaching the company's covenant limits. Considering adjustments required by debt contracts, however, the indicator reaches 4.3 times.

To avoid early maturity of obligations, Oncoclínicas had been attempting to negotiate with creditors to obtain a waiver for covenant breaches before the balance sheet disclosure. The company, however, failed even to convene the general assembly of debenture holders called to address the matter, due to lack of minimum quorum, postponing deliberation on the request and keeping negotiations with creditors open.

Carlos Gil, CEO of Oncoclínicas, stated in the results call on Friday morning, April 10, that the company has made progress in negotiations with concentrated creditors, but has faced difficulty in cases where credit is dispersed, such as Real Estate Receivables Certificates (CRIs) and some debentures. "With these dispersed debts, we did not obtain waivers. That's why the covenant breach. But our debt is not due. We have no formal declaration of early maturity at this moment," said the CEO, an internal solution found by board members to replace founder Bruno Ferrari.

According to Gil, the company already has some covenant breach waivers, but as they were not complete, the debt was reclassified as short-term, given the risk of early maturity. The company reported in the balance sheet that of the R$ 3.23 billion in debt volume, R$ 2.9 billion are compromised by covenant breaches. Of this amount, Oncoclínicas states it has not yet obtained waivers for R$ 1.88 billion.

According to the balance sheet, the company had approximately R$ 520 million in cash at the beginning of the year against short-term financial obligations of R$ 3.43 billion. Facing this risk, Oncoclínicas' own management admitted in the balance sheet a "relevant uncertainty that may raise significant doubt as to the ability to continue operations." According to the company, continuity "depends substantially" on the success of negotiations with creditors to obtain the waiver and on raising new funds.

The uncertainty was highlighted in Deloitte's opinion, the independent auditor of the balance sheet. Deloitte's emphasis on business continuity risks was cited by Gil in the investor call but downplayed, with the CEO saying there are no overdue debts to date.

In the balance sheet, Oncoclínicas stated that some alternatives are being evaluated to meet its financial obligations, including refinancing financial debts and capital contribution from shareholders.

The company's liquidity problems were aggravated by two extraordinary events during the year, which impacted the company's cash by more than R$ 1.2 billion. On one hand, the default of Unimed FERJ, which stopped paying approximately R$ 800 million to the company. On the other, the loss of approximately R$ 430 million (after an additional provision of R$ 213 million) in funds that were in financial notes from Banco Master at the time of its liquidation. Last year, Master had 20% of the company. There was a guarantee that if the bank failed, the Oncoclínicas shares it held should be returned to the company. But after the liquidation of Daniel Vorcaro's bank, the shares were transferred to BRB, which triggered a judicial dispute for enforcement of the guarantee.

"These events combined with decreased revenue and lack of fixed cost dilution greatly impacted the company's short-term liquidity, reflecting on indebtedness," said the CEO.

The situation would have been more critical, according to the executive, had it not been for a R$ 1.4 billion capital increase made by the company in November. "We would have closed the fourth quarter with net debt of R$ 4.1 billion, but we managed to reduce it to R$ 2.9 billion."

In this perfect storm, Oncoclínicas' net loss jumped 400% last year, ending 2025 at R$ 3.67 billion — a "quite negative" result in the CEO's own words.

Despite the moment of fragility, the annual results call was sparsely attended, with no questions during the Q&A session. Management also did not elaborate on the proposed sale to Porto Seguro, which in recent weeks has generated friction within the company.

## Internal Rift

Despite attempts at renegotiating with creditors, the strategy has been criticized by some company shareholders. In a letter sent to the company last month, Mak Capital — a shareholder with approximately 6.3% of capital — classified the attempt to obtain waivers from creditors as "complex and with limited effects," citing high debt dispersion as a factor that hampers quorum achievement and reduces success chances within the timeframe.

The company has also faced friction in its board of administration. The catalyst was Porto Seguro's proposal for a possible sale of its operations. In a meeting to deliberate on the proposal, board members Marcos Grodetzky and Raul Rosenthal positioned themselves in opposition, pointing out possible flaws in the process conduct.

In their joint statement, the board members assert that negotiations were conducted by shareholders — not by management — which, in their assessment, would compromise the process' credibility with creditors. Grodetzky and Rosenthal also criticized the fact that the company granted exclusivity to Porto, which could weaken its bargaining power and limit alternatives.

In the proposed structure, the operation would result in the creation of a new entity controlled by Porto, which would concentrate the company's main operational activity, while the remaining Oncoclínicas would hold relevant economic participation but with less influence over management.

Within management, the proposal culminated in the departure of CFO Camille Faria, who had assumed the position little over a month earlier and opposed the negotiated terms.
