# Retailers Seek to Boost 65-inch TV and Premium Smartphone Sales During World Cup, but Fear Bad Debt

The World Cup typically represents a new Black Friday for electronics retailers. For this year, expectations are that the excitement of following Brazil's games will boost sales of large-screen TVs, such as 65-inch or larger models. These are smart TVs, connected to the internet and equipped with artificial intelligence for automatic sound and image adjustment. They are ready for interactive TV 3.0, whose signal was promised by the government to begin during this edition of the tournament.

Meanwhile, 5G smartphone technology, compatible with high mobile connection speeds, allows more streaming transmissions—the best option for those at work or in transit who want to follow the games. These devices cost more than R$ 1,000.

But to score goals in first-half sales, retailers and manufacturers need to navigate consumer default rates, which hit a record 44.4% of Brazilian adults in March this year—the highest level since January 2015, when the credit bureau SPC Brasil began tracking the data. In other words, so many consumers are in debt that more than 4 out of every 10 have stopped paying their bills.

Retailers are banking on money freed up by the government's exemption of income tax for those earning up to R$ 5,000 monthly—a measure from the Lula administration (PT) that took effect this year. The government is also discussing releasing up to 20% of FGTS account balances to pay off debts for workers earning up to five minimum wages (R$ 8,105).

"If that happens, it will be very positive," says Silvio Stagni, president of electronics distributor Allied, which sells brands like Apple, HP, Microsoft, Acer, and Lenovo to retailers and manages Samsung stores in Brazil. "But only the injection expected from the income tax exemption, around R$ 30 billion, can partially offset family debt."

It is precisely in the bracket of three to five minimum wages (R$ 4,863 to R$ 8,105) where the number of defaulters grew the most in the past year, from 13% in 2025 to the current 20%, according to the "Portrait of Default 2026" study by CNDL (National Confederation of Store Managers) and SPC Brasil, obtained exclusively by Folha.

The study, however, points out that default is not linked to unemployment: 82% of defaulters work (48% are employees, 23% are self-employed, and 11% are entrepreneurs). "Although inflation has been more balanced lately, accompanied by better employment and income indices, this is not enough to control default," says Merula Borges, a CNDL financial specialist. "Prices remain at very high levels, it's harder to balance accounts at month's end, and consumers are more impulsive, shopping a lot online."

Of the total consumers who said they were blacklisted this year, 41% are so because of credit cards (from banks or stores), compared to 23% who cited the same reason last year—a jump of 18 percentage points. The second main reason for consumers to have their "names blacklisted" was failure to pay loans from banks or financial institutions—the share rose from 16% of defaulters to 26%. Following that is store credit: 23% of blacklisted consumers said they have debts in this category, compared to 12% last year.

The survey was conducted online between March 6 and 17 this year with 609 respondents. It has a margin of error of 4 percentage points for a 95% confidence interval.

Faced with the dilemma of increasing sales while risking defaults, retailers have been seeking a middle ground, restraining credit offers while making payments easier. At Magalu, for example, it is possible to buy a 65" LG TV for R$ 3,699 in 21 interest-free installments using the store's credit line (on regular credit cards, the price is divided into 12 interest-free installments). At the company, the default rate (debts over 90 days) stands at 7.5% of its credit portfolio, which totaled R$ 20.8 billion in December. Compared to December 2024, the portfolio grew 3%.

"We have always been cautious with credit offers," says Luiz Fernando Rego, commercial director at Magalu. At the company, 68% of sales happen online—only 10% of these sales come from Luizacred, a partnership between the retailer and Itaú. In stores, Luizacred accounts for 40% of sales.

At Casas Bahia, the watchword continues to be controlling credit offers to keep default rates stable, below 9%. "We have been very conservative in credit approval; even when I approve, I demand a slightly larger down payment, for installment plans with more discipline, nobody knows what tomorrow will bring," Casas Bahia CEO Renato Franklin told Folha in March. The company owns the fintech BanQi but issues store cards with Bradesco. Sales on store credit and Casas Bahia cards represent 23.8% of total sales.

As with every World Cup, investment in marketing is consistent: spending on campaigns using the World Cup theme is expected to reach R$ 5.5 billion, according to calculations by advertising executive Luiz Lara, president of the board of Cenp (Self-Regulation Forum of the Advertising Market). This amount represents about 20% of all advertising investment last year. The majority, R$ 4.2 billion, comes from official sponsorship fees.

"The World Cup is the biggest media event of the year and will catalyze investments, mainly from food and beverage brands," says Lara, who also believes in the boost to TV sales, despite the high default rates.
