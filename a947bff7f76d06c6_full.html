# Retail seeks to sell more 65-inch TVs and expensive phones during World Cup but fears bad debt

The World Cup typically represents a new Black Friday for electronics retailers. For this year, the expectation is that enthusiasm for following Brazil's games will boost sales of large-screen TVs, such as 65-inch or larger models. These are smart TVs, connected to the internet and equipped with artificial intelligence for automatic sound and image adjustment. They are ready for interactive TV 3.0, whose signal was promised by the government to start during this tournament edition.

Meanwhile, 5G smartphone technology, compatible with high-speed mobile connections, allows more streaming transmissions, the best option for those at work or in transit to follow the games. These are devices that cost over R$1,000.

But to score big on first-semester sales, retailers and industry need to overcome consumer default rates, which hit a record 44.4% of Brazilian adults in March this year—the highest index since January 2015, when credit bureau SPC Brasil began tracking it. In other words, so heavily indebted, more than 4 out of every 10 consumers have stopped paying their bills.

Retail's hope rests on money freed up from the exemption of income tax charges for those earning up to R$5,000 monthly—a measure by the Lula government (PT) that came into effect this year. The Executive is also discussing releasing up to 20% of FGTS balances for debt payment for workers earning up to five minimum wages (R$8,105).

"If that happens, it will be very positive," says Silvio Stagni, president of electronics distributor Allied, which sells brands like Apple, HP, Microsoft, Acer, and Lenovo to retailers, in addition to managing Samsung stores in Brazil. "But only the injection expected from the income tax exemption, around R$30 billion, can partially offset family indebtedness."

It is precisely in the range of three to five minimum wages (R$4,863 to R$8,105) where the number of defaulters has grown most over the past year, from 13% in 2025 to the current 20%, according to the "Profile of Default 2026" survey by CNDL (National Confederation of Store Managers) and SPC Brasil, obtained exclusively by Folha.

The study, however, points out that default is not linked to lack of employment: 82% of defaulters work (48% are formal employees, 23% are self-employed, and 11% are entrepreneurs). "Although inflation has been more balanced recently, accompanied by better employment and income indices, this is not sufficient to control default," says Merula Borges, CNDL finance specialist. "Prices remain at a very high level, it's harder to balance accounts at the end of the month, and consumers are more impulsive, buying much online."

Of the total consumers who said they were in default this year, 41% are due to credit cards (from banks or stores), compared to 23% who cited the same reason last year—an 18 percentage point jump. The second main reason consumers end up with their "name on the blacklist" was failure to pay loans taken from banks or finance companies—the share rose from 16% of defaulters to 26%. Following that is store credit: 23% of protested consumers said they had debts in this modality, compared to 12% last year.

The survey was conducted online between March 6 and 17 this year with 609 respondents. It has a margin of error of 4 percentage points for a 95% confidence interval.

Caught between increasing sales and risking default, retailers have been seeking middle ground, restraining credit offerings while easing payments. At Magalu, for example, it's possible to buy a 65" LG TV for R$3,699 in 21 interest-free installments through store credit (on a regular credit card, the price is divided into 12 interest-free installments). At the company, the default rate (debts over 90 days) stands at 7.5% of the credit portfolio, which totaled R$20.8 billion in December. Compared to December 2024, the portfolio grew 3%.

"We've always been cautious with credit offerings," says Luiz Fernando Rego, commercial director of Magalu. At the company, 68% of sales happen online—only 10% of these sales come from Luizacred, the retailer's partnership with Itaú. In stores, Luizacred accounts for 40% of sales.

At Casas Bahia, the watchword continues to be controlling credit offerings to keep default below 9%. "We've been very conservative in credit approvals; even when I approve, I demand a slightly larger down payment, for more disciplined installment payments, nobody knows what tomorrow will bring," Casas Bahia's CEO, Renato Franklin, told Folha in March. The company owns fintech BanQi but issues store cards with Bradesco. Sales in store credit and Casas Bahia cards represent 23.8% of total sales.

As in every World Cup, marketing investment is consistent: spending on campaigns using the World Cup theme is expected to reach R$5.5 billion, according to calculations by advertising executive Luiz Lara, president of the Cenp Council (Self-Regulation Forum of the Advertising Market). This amount represents about 20% of everything invested in advertising last year. The bulk, R$4.2 billion, comes from official sponsorship quotas. "The World Cup is the biggest media event of the year and will catalyze investments, mainly from food and beverage brands," says Lara, who also believes in the boost to TV sales despite high default rates.
