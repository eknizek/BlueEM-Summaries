# Brazil and Spain sign agreement on critical minerals cooperation

Brazil and Spain signed an agreement on Friday (17) to expand cooperation in the exploration of critical minerals, amid a global race between the United States and China for the sector. The text signed by the Minister of Mines and Energy, Alexandre Silveira, provides for the exchange of technical information, the encouragement of reciprocal investments and the search for means to finance projects, mentioning European Union funds. With an initial validity of five years, the document establishes, for example, joint working groups to promote research, prioritizing sustainable practices. The agreement, however, does not yet provide for immediate payments or legal bindings.

"No one but Brazil will own our natural wealth," said President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) alongside Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez, who received him for a bilateral meeting in Barcelona and will host a meeting of progressive leaders on Saturday (17). The PT member added that critical minerals are a matter of national security and that "we will not repeat with critical minerals and rare earths what happened with iron ore and bauxite."

Later, in a meeting with Spanish and Brazilian businesspeople at the hotel where he is staying, the president repeated that "this time we want to build partnerships with companies from various countries in the world that want to do the transformation process within Brazil."

"So that we can share resources and technological knowledge, so that Brazil becomes an industrialized country," he added.

After this meeting, Aloizio Mercadante, president of BNDES (National Bank for Economic and Social Development), declared to journalists that Spain is the second country with the most investments in Brazil and that he hopes to leave the visit with more interested parties. He said there is a balance because "Brazil has a surplus with Spain, but the quality of trade is favorable to Spain," which exports more added value.

Brasília is also negotiating an agreement on critical minerals with the United States, despite diplomatic tensions between the two. Washington sees the country as a potential target for billions of dollars in investments, according to a US embassy spokesman.

The issue also entered the Brazilian electoral campaign. Ronaldo Caiado (PSD), governor of Goiás and pre-candidate for the Presidency against Lula, signed a memorandum of understanding for cooperation on the topic with the American government last month without consulting the Union. Goiás has reserves of lithium, niobium and is the headquarters of the only company that commercially produces rare earths in Brazil, Serra Verde, supported by Americans. However, an agreement of this magnitude cannot be applied without approval from the federal government.

The sector is dealing with a delay in the approval of legal framework. The bill that establishes a National Policy on Critical and Strategic Minerals has been discussed since mid-last year in the Chamber, but successive requests for postponement have delayed the approval of the text. The government caucus of the Chamber of Deputies presented, on last Friday (10), two bills that would place the State as a partner of companies in the sector, in a sharing system similar to the pre-sal—which was criticized by miners.

The Critical Minerals Association argues that "value aggregation in the country is a correct objective, but it does not derive, by itself, from the creation of a state-owned company."

"There is a risk of creating a formally ambitious structure, but with low practical effectiveness, capable even of generating additional regulatory noise instead of accelerating solutions," said the entity in a note.
