# Household debt raises alarm bells in Brazil's beef industry

The high level of debt among Brazilian households, which is already affecting food consumption, has raised a red flag among meatpackers, especially in a year when beef exports to China—Brazil's main customer—are expected to decline.

Roberto Perosa, president of the Brazilian Association of Meat Exporting Industries (ABIEC), told reporters on Tuesday (6) that the situation worries the beef industry because the domestic market, the destination of about 70% of national production, could absorb some of the meat that China will no longer import this year. But this level of household debt reduces Brazilian consumption.

China imposed a quota of 1.1 million tonnes without charging an extra 55% tariff on imports from Brazil this year. The volume is less than the total of 1.7 million tonnes exported by Brazilian meatpackers to China in 2025. Even with the rising protein consumption in the Asian market as a whole, there are no buyers with an appetite equivalent to the Chinese, said Perosa.

"If we are going to have a sharp drop in exports to China, where will that volume go? We want domestic consumption to increase to offset what we will not be able to export," he said.

Considering that shipments of Brazilian beef to China within the quota this year will be around 35% lower than in 2025, ABIEC estimates that Brazilian exports could fall by around 10% in 2026. Even if the possibility that Chinese importers will continue to buy Brazilian beef despite the extra tariff, shipments to the country tends to decrease, being resumed more strongly only from October onwards, so that they arrive in the Asian country at the beginning of 2027.

Perosa pointed to the increase in Brazilians' spending on online betting platforms as one factor increasing indebtedness and affecting consumption. According to him, the topic was discussed at a meeting on Monday with Brazil's Vice President Geraldo Alckmin, which was also attended by representatives of retailers. The participants presented data collected by Nielsen showing that low-income households consumed 9.6% less in the last quarter of 2025, compared with the same period in 2024. Meanwhile, the sports betting and online gaming segment moved around R$360 billion.

In the meeting with Alckmin, which also included the presence of the CEO of the Assaí cash-and-carry chain and board president of the Brazilian Association of Self-Service Wholesalers (ABAAS), Belmiro Gomes, the participants also presented proposals to limit the encroachment of expenses with sports betting, especially illegal betting, on income, said Perosa.

Also citing Nielsen research, Perosa added that the expectation is that in this World Cup year, spending on gambling platforms will double, to around R$600 billion, including operations in legalized and illegal betting apps. According to a study by the consulting firm LCA, commissioned by the Brazilian Institute for Responsible Gaming (IBJR), spending on online betting represents only 0.46% of consumption, a level close to that of alcoholic beverages, at 0.5%.

In this scenario of reduced sales to China and a drop in domestic demand, ABIEC seeks to open new markets and expand those already conquered. However, there are limitations of various kinds, including the uncertainty about the opening of markets that Brazilian exporters have long desired, such as Japan, Turkey, and South Korea, which could compensate for the reduction in beef sales to China.

"We are looking for new markets, but the problem is volume; there is no market that can replace the Chinese one," said Perosa. "I still have hope that Japan can open its market this year," he said.

A Japanese technical mission came to Brazil a month ago to inspect the country's beef production system and visited two meatpacking plants, in Paraná and Rio Grande do Sul. According to Perosa, the expectation is that the Japanese government will soon comment on the visit, at which time it should inform whether additional data will be needed or whether the process will move forward.
