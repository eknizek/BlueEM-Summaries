# Nestlé drives cold coffee beverage expansion in Brazil, targeting 30% sales by 2028

Coffee has cooled down at Nestlé – literally. The multinational food company projects that the cold beverage segment should grow from the current 10% to approximately 30% of Nescafé sales in Brazil by 2028, in a growth movement that does not replace the traditional hot cup but rather increases overall beverage consumption.

Nescafé only entered the cold coffee market in 2023, lagging behind brands like Starbucks that had already established themselves with frappés, cold brews, and espresso tonics. The category attracts younger consumers, especially Generation Z, and creates new consumption occasions with social aspects (cafeterias, happy hours, and gourmet recipes to showcase on social media) and less domestic contexts (breakfast).

"This is a historic change in the coffee category," says Valeria Pardal, Nescafé director for Brazil at Nestlé. Currently, the category includes ready-to-drink beverages (on-the-go consumption), instant coffee, and Dolce Gusto capsules, representing 15 SKUs for cold consumption – by 2028, the portfolio could expand to 30.

Cold beverages have helped position Nescafé in underexplored niches – such as the beach. From February to April, Nestlé maintained a kiosk on the Copacabana waterfront to establish a new consumption habit among audiences accustomed to iced mate, coconut water, and açai. "During summer months, we had more limited operations, so cold beverages drive the new category forward," says the executive.

Cold coffee also complements hot coffee consumption as a diversification strategy. If someone previously drank three cups per day, cold coffee enters as a fourth cup by replacing a soft drink, she explains. "There is no cannibalization, but rather an increase," she says.

Nescafé was developed globally in the 1930s at the request of the Brazilian government as a way to utilize excess coffee generated by the 1929 crisis – which collapsed global demand for the commodity – transforming the bean into an easy-to-store and prepare instant format. However, Nescafé only arrived in Brazil and began being produced in the 1950s.

Today, there are more than 100 SKUs, including gourmet lines such as Gold, with growth exceeding 20% in the past year. The company partners with 3,800 coffee farms across the country, and factories are located in Araras and Montes Claros, from where products are exported to more than 50 countries.
