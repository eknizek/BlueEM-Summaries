# Embraer closes largest single-country sale for C-390 cargo aircraft

Embraer received the largest international order from a single country for its C-390 Millennium cargo aircraft — the largest plane ever developed by the Brazilian company. The deal was closed with the United Arab Emirates (UAE) and signed with the Tawazun Council for Defence Enablement, the country's national entity responsible for promoting and regulating the defense and security industry, which purchased ten aircraft with the option to expand the order to twenty more.

The contract includes the development, in collaboration with a UAE company, of maintenance, repair, and overhaul capabilities, as well as other post-sale support services for the fleet. In a statement released on Monday, May 4, Embraer highlighted that the C-390 will allow the Armed Forces of the purchasing country to "perform a wide range of missions, including cargo and troop transport, aerial drop operations, humanitarian assistance, aeromedical evacuation, operations on unpaved runways, and full interoperability with national, allied, and partner forces."

"We are extremely proud that the UAE has selected the C-390 Millennium to enhance its air transport capabilities," said Bosco da Costa Junior, president of Embraer Defense, also in a statement. "This revolutionary and mission-proven aircraft will provide the Air and Air Defense Force with the versatility and performance needed to conduct a wide range of missions, anytime and anywhere, for decades to come," he added.

The news of the cargo aircraft sale boosted the company's stock price on Monday. At 4 p.m., shares rose 3.26% and were quoted at R$ 79.55.

Citi analysts wrote in a report that the deal could add approximately $700 million (R$ 3.5 billion) to Embraer's order backlog, considering that each aircraft has an average price of $70 million. This amount represents 16% of what the company's defense division currently has in firm orders.

"This is the largest international order for the aircraft and consolidates its presence in the Middle East," the analysts André Mazini, Kiepher Kennedy, and Piero Trotta highlighted. According to them, with this new order, Embraer's production line should be practically fully occupied until the end of the decade. The company has been taking approximately 30 months to deliver each cargo aircraft.

Announced in 2009, the C-390 program cost $4.5 billion (approximately R$ 22 billion) and was developed in partnership with the Brazilian Air Force. Despite impressing with technical quality, the aircraft — which is called KC-390 when it has the in-flight refueling option — initially disappointed the financial market in sales.

The cargo aircraft began registering more concrete advances in 2023. Until 2022, the company had closed sales contracts for the model only with Portugal (in 2019), Hungary (2020), and the Netherlands (2022). In 2023 alone, however, it reached agreements with Austria, the Czech Republic, and South Korea. The most promising conversations are ongoing with Saudi Arabia and India.

In India, for example, Embraer is competing to sell between 40 and 80 aircraft. To try to close the deal, the company has already announced a local partnership with the Mahindra group to manufacture the aircraft in the country. There are also negotiations with Saudi Arabia, where a fleet of 60 aircraft needs to be retired (23 of them with a profile suitable for replacement by the C-390).

To sell to the country, Embraer may have to establish a final assembly line there or transfer maintenance, training, and parts manufacturing activities. These requirements are common in sales of equipment to armed forces.

One difficulty for the C-390, however, is market restriction. If Embraer intended to sell, for example, a cargo aircraft to Israel, its aircraft could not have any parts manufactured in a nation considered an enemy of the country. Today, the Brazilian company's market consists of countries aligned with the United States.

Even in Brazil, the C-390 has faced important challenges. In 2021, the Air Force unilaterally decided, citing budgetary restrictions, to reduce its order from 28 to 15 cargo aircraft. After months of negotiations, it was agreed that there would be 19 units — despite the contract stipulating that the order value could be reduced by a maximum of 25%, which meant the sale of 21 units.

The company's calculations, however, still indicate that over the next 20 years, 490 cargo aircraft will need to be replaced worldwide, a $60 billion market (nearly R$ 300 billion) in which the C-390 is positioned to stand out.
