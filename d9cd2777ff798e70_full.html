# 'Money Under the Mattress': Argentines Resist Milei's Appeal and Keep $170 Billion at Home

Nobody imagines how dollar bills become damp and foul-smelling when stored under a mattress for years, says Alejandro Lamas, when he has received so many stacks of them from Argentines to whom he sold used cars over the years that he has become something of an expert. Try to pass a fake bill to him and you will notice it on first touch.

It is a skill as important to him today as it was when Lamas sold his first used car in Buenos Aires four decades ago, and it seems it will continue to be in the near future. President Javier Milei's latest attempt to convince Argentine savers to deposit their money in dollar accounts is having difficulty gaining traction, highlighting the singular distrust that citizens still have toward the government and banks.

Almost 25 years after a financial crisis that saw the government forcibly convert dollar deposits to pesos that quickly lost 75% of their value, attempts to restore confidence have barely gotten off the ground. So when savers have extra money to save for a rainy day or a major purchase, they buy dollars; and many of them keep them in cash.

"Some people are still afraid," Lamas said in his sales lot, where an almost brand new Toyota Corolla Cross was advertised for about 30,000 dollars. "The governments here have done all kinds of crazy things in the past. How could you trust them?"

Authorities estimate that about $170 billion is outside the banks. Unlocking even a fraction of this, putting the money to work in the formal financial system, could help revive the second largest economy in South America and trigger the growth that Milei promised as a reward for enduring harsh austerity measures after years of debt-financed spending.

Although Wall Street has embraced Milei, rewarding his fiscal discipline with purchases of bonds and other assets, a new set of incentives called Tax Innocence has so far failed to provoke a significant shift among savers. Dollar deposits have risen less than $1 billion since its debut in February.

The program seeks to encourage Argentines to bring undeclared savings back into the system, relaxing reporting requirements and significantly reducing the risk of scrutiny by tax authorities. Those who deposit dollar savings "will not have to give explanations," the tax agency said earlier this year.

Strong Demand

Argentines buy about $2 billion in foreign currency every month, a figure that can jump to more than 6 billion during periods of political stress, such as elections. Authorities estimate that about 10% of these purchases are kept in cash, hidden at home or in private safes, or sent abroad.

"It is a revolutionary law, but you cannot force things," Milei said in a recent interview, showing some frustration. In response to questions, a spokesman for the Economy Ministry cited data showing that dollar deposits are at their highest level since at least 2001.

The Argentine habit of keeping money at home is so deep that it has its own local peculiarities. The older $100 bills are called "cara chica," or small face, because they feature a smaller portrait of Benjamin Franklin, in a series that stopped being issued about 30 years ago.

They are usually traded at a small discount compared to the newer "cara grande" bills in the Argentine informal market, which happens on street corners, shopping mall kiosks and backyard shops in places like the commercial district of Calle Florida in Buenos Aires. Dealing with this money has become second nature to local merchants, especially those who sell high-value items, where customers are more likely to pay in dollars.

"Argentine merchants can identify fake dollars better than Americans," said Lamas. Often, according to him, it all comes down to touch, running your fingers over the bills to test the paper, a sensitivity honed by years of handling worn notes. "It is a skill you acquire quickly here, even if a bill is old and worn, you notice it right away."

Financial Life Jacket

Marcelo Capobianco, a butcher in a working-class suburb of Buenos Aires and a Milei supporter, says the dollar has always served as his financial life jacket, especially in times of political stress. "When the opposition started winning elections last year, it was a big scare and everyone ran to buy dollars again," he recounted. "When your money loses value, everyone runs to the dollar. Nobody here saves in pesos, and if you do, it's game over."

Argentine distrust of banks is rooted in the country's 2001 financial collapse, when the government, desperate to keep dollars in the economy, imposed the so-called "corralito," which converted dollar deposits and limited cash withdrawals. The years that followed brought repeated exchange rate crises, inflation spikes and political upheaval, reinforcing the habit of saving outside the financial system and buying dollars as protection against devaluation. The central bank allows the currency to weaken gradually, and it has already lost 99% of its value in the last decade.

Still, Economy Minister Luis Caputo has repeatedly insisted that things are different now, urging Argentines to bring their savings into the formal system. He presents it as a win-win situation: Argentina can boost economic activity while savers earn returns on money that would otherwise sit idle at home.

"Many people keep their money at home, losing value, when they could take it to the bank," he said in an interview with local TV this month. The fear of a return to spendthrift government, according to him, is holding many people back, especially with presidential elections approaching in 2027.

The state-owned Banco Nación has also been pushing this effort, resorting to a dose of humor. Using the slogan "Relieve Your Mattress," the bank launched advertisements featuring worn-out beds complaining that they cannot sleep because they are "stuffed" with their owners' anxieties.

It is true that confidence in the Milei government is the highest for any government in decades. Dollar deposits in the banking system have risen to nearly 40 billion dollars. "The dollars coming in through tax innocence are still coming in dribs and drabs," said Adrián Yarde Buller, chief economist at Facimex Valores. "The potential is huge, given the scale of assets Argentines keep outside the system, but it will take more than this law to change behavior. You need to rebuild trust in institutions, and that takes time."

Lamas says he agrees with the government's message that it is safe to save within banks. He deposits his business profits. But many Argentines remain unconvinced, continuing the age-old practice of keeping their dollars close to home.
