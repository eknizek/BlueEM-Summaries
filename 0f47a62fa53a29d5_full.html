# Black Women Rely More on Credit and Pay Higher Interest Rates, Central Bank Report Shows

Black women are the portion of the low-income population that pays the highest interest rates in the country, according to the Central Bank's 2025 Financial Citizenship Report, released this Monday (13). This is the first time the institution has released this data segmented by gender and race. The last survey on the subject was published in 2021 and did not include this breakdown.

The report brings together studies for the formulation of public policies aimed at financial inclusion and education, and presents information about access, use, and cost of credit in Brazil, based on 2024 data.

In relation to the general population, the new report showed a 55% increase in the use of debt-generating modalities with interest on credit cards (revolving credit and invoice installments) over the last four years. In 2020, there were 34 million people in this situation; in 2024, the number jumped to 52.8 million—more than half of the total 96.6 million users with active credit cards.

In the case of black women, the data reveals almost universal financial inclusion in the low-income segment (97.8%, above the general average of 96.4%), but with warning signs regarding the quality of this use. Among those registered in Cadastro Único (Single Registry), 62% are credit borrowers and face average rates reaching 140% per year. For white women, the rate is 128%; for black men, 110%; and for white men, 97%.

Part of this higher cost is associated with the greater concentration of debts in credit card revolving and installment plans, which represent approximately 23% of the group's credit portfolio. Although they are the largest users of productive microcredit—loans intended for micro and small entrepreneurs to boost their businesses—black women are pushed toward more costly modalities of the financial system, explained Luis Mansur, head of the Central Bank's Department for Promotion of Financial Citizenship, during a live event about the report this afternoon.

"This means that revolving and installment plans represent about 23% of total contracted credit, while microcredit accounts for only 4%. Thus, when we look at the overall composition of the portfolio, we see that this concentration in more expensive credit lines ends up leading to a higher interest rate for black women."

In general, risky debt is more prevalent among the female population in Brazil, affecting 15.4% of female credit borrowers, compared to 12.1% of men. The historical gender disparity in access to technical financial knowledge also harms women, who are more susceptible to expensive credit modalities. They also have more difficulty accessing financing for small businesses. Female entrepreneurs pay interest rates approximately 4 percentage points higher than men, according to data from Sebrae (Brazilian Service for Support to Micro and Small Enterprises) included in the Central Bank's report. This occurs due to the perception of greater risk by financial institutions, lack of formal credit history, and difficulty in offering guarantees, since they own fewer assets registered in their names.

## Bankarization Advances Among Brazilians

The number of adults with relationships with financial institutions reached 175 million at the end of 2024, representing 96.4% of the adult population. The average Brazilian maintains 6.7 relationships with financial institutions. In all analyzed groups, more than 90% of people have access to the financial system, including indigenous people (93.7%), quilombo communities (97.2%), rural residents (96.5%), people with disabilities (90.4%), and the homeless population (99.2%).

According to the Central Bank's report, this expanded access is a direct result of the creation of Pix, whose adoption, by the end of 2023, had already reached more than 70% of the low-income population. Another factor cited is the rise of fintechs—digital financial services companies that reached 123 million customers in 2024, attracting especially younger audiences through user-centered and lower-cost solutions.

The mandatory opening of bank accounts to receive Bolsa Família (Family Allowance) is another explanation for the high access rate, especially among women, who are the program's priority beneficiaries. According to Izabela Correa, director of Citizenship and Conduct Supervision at the Central Bank, the advancement of bankarization and credit access must be accompanied by financial education—promoted both by schools and by banks themselves, which since 2024 are required to offer this type of awareness to consumers.

"We know that it is not the only pillar we will have to mobilize to advance on this issue, but it is an important pillar," she states.
