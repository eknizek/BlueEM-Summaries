# Stone Navigates Payments Hardware Pressure Amid New Business Expansion

Stone & Co's stock fell 20% in just over a month, a movement that began with the disclosure of below-expected fourth quarter results and disappointing guidance. Although the payments business has high margins, management's own vision is that the company needs to go beyond this to grow consistently.

"We are reorganizing so that the company increasingly looks like a bank and less like a payment machine that does other things," CEO Mateus Scherer told Brazil Journal. The question investors have is whether Stone is capable of delivering this before being overtaken by competitors.

"Banks are using payment acquiring as just another service within a broader package than Stone can currently offer. With this, they make better pricing and extract value more easily," said a manager who bought the stock after the recent selloff because he thinks the paper became so cheap that it compensates for the risk.

BTG Pactual doesn't see a quick fix for the business, but believes the company can deliver more in coming years – and that the current valuation already prices in much of the uncertainty. "The short-term question is whether investors interpret the current competitive environment and Stone's own challenges as a 'glass half full' or 'glass half empty' situation," ponders Eduardo Rosman, the analyst responsible for the financial sector at BTG, who has a buy rating on the stock.

Stone built one of the largest distribution channels in the country, with strong penetration among micro, small and medium-sized retailers. In 2025, it earned R$ 2.5 billion, 17.5% more than the previous year, with an ROE of 26%. The company estimates it has around 20% market share in payments in its main segment, SMEs. But in credit and banking – which includes checking accounts and cash management services – participation is "low single digits," between 1% and 2%, according to Scherer.

Although credit is growing rapidly – the portfolio more than doubled in 2025 – the scale is still small: the total portfolio totaled R$ 2.8 billion in December. Stone estimates its total addressable market to be R$ 100 billion in revenue, divided relatively evenly among these three verticals. Today, however, results are still disproportionately dependent on payment acquiring – a highly competitive, volatile segment with margins that tend to be smaller over time, not just in Brazil.

In the fourth quarter, growth in TPV (total volume processed) fell short of expectations, with high churn. Part of this is due to a worse macroeconomic environment, with weakening consumption. But stronger online expansion – where Stone has less exposure – over physical retail, more aggressive competition offers and execution problems also weighed. "If the company raises prices and loses market share, okay. But it lost on both sides. It entered an equilibrium that worries me," said a manager who zeroed out his Stone investment at year-end.

According to an analyst, the problems are diffuse depending on the retailer profile and region. "In some cases, it's a lack of credit; in others, it's price. And there are those who complain about old machines." For Scherer, the challenge is to demonstrate "the value of the complete solution."

"We are the sector player focused on SMEs, and our product offering evolved a lot last year but became complex for the customer. We need to simplify and improve execution," said the executive, who became CEO in March after 11 years at the company, three of them as CFO. "We always had a big focus on sales. We need to develop the relationship throughout the customer's lifetime."

For now, neither the company nor the market expects major changes in the short term. Stone gave guidance for gross profit of R$ 6.6 billion to R$ 7 billion for 2026 and R$ 7.2 billion to R$ 8.3 billion for 2027, below what analysts estimated. It also predicts TPV growth of around 5%, below the expected expansion for SG&A (sales, general and administrative expenses) of 10% for this year and 20% for next, according to BTG.

CFO Diego Salgado noted that few companies give guidance for two years, and that the market became more frustrated with expectations for 2027. "Longer guidance embeds a greater degree of uncertainty, because we make projections based on the information we have today."

For Scherer, there is room to pursue better numbers, especially in expenses, incorporating developments in technology and artificial intelligence. "Yes, we have to make a lot of investments to evolve from a payment machine to a more complete platform, but the world is evolving very quickly, and it's hard to anticipate everything that's coming," said the CEO.

The uncertainty about when and how the company will deliver on its promises led to the selloff of the past month. "Stone has always been a sales company, not a technology company. It's true that it's growing in other verticals, but the core is declining more rapidly," said the manager who sold the stock.

Those who bought in recent weeks are watching the paper discount – and the dividend potential. Stone trades at 5.7x 2026 earnings, one of the lowest P/Es in the sector, below mid-sized banks, which typically trade at lower multiples because they have lower ROEs, according to BTG.

The valuation excludes the R$ 3.1 billion received from the sale of Linx – which should be used for share buybacks and dividend payments (the company said it will communicate this in April). With the distribution of these resources, dividend yield should reach 17%, according to Citi. The company also announced it should use around R$ 2 billion to repurchase shares.

Stone also has around R$ 10 billion in non-remunerated deposits – which, with 15% interest rates, generated a result of around R$ 1.5 billion in 2025. For Citi, the fair price for the stock implies a multiple of 8.3x.

Rosman compared Stone's situation to Banco do Brasil at the time of Lula's re-election, when BB was trading below 4x earnings despite having excess capital and high provisions. "At the time, we argued that the dividend yield was so high that the asymmetry seemed attractive – profits would have to deteriorate very rapidly for the thesis not to work."

At these multiples, bankers and analysts believe Stone could be a target for M&A. In the view of Gustavo Schroder, Citi's financial analyst, visible reinvestment opportunities in the core business are "limited". Moreover, he wrote, the company "seems to be migrating to a leaner operating structure, prioritizing efficiency and capital discipline, rather than expansion initiatives, which makes it more attractive to a financial or strategic player."
