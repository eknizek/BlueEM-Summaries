# Stone navigates POS competition headwinds while betting on new business lines

Stone&Co's stock fell 20% in just over a month, a decline that began with fourth-quarter results below expectations and disappointing guidance. Although the payments business has high margins, management itself recognizes that the company needs to go beyond this to grow consistently. "We are reorganizing ourselves so that the company increasingly looks like a bank and less like a POS machine that does other things," CEO Mateus Scherer told Brazil Journal.

The question investors are asking is whether Stone can deliver this before being overwhelmed by competitors. "Banks are using acquiring as just one service within a broader package than what Stone can currently offer. This allows them to price better and extract value more easily," said one manager who bought the stock after the recent selloff because he thinks the stock has become so cheap that it compensates for the risk.

BTG Pactual doesn't see a quick fix for the business, but believes the company can deliver more in the coming years – and that the current valuation already prices in much of the uncertainty. "The short-term question is whether investors interpret the current competitive environment and Stone's own challenges as a 'glass half full' or 'glass half empty' situation," reflects Eduardo Rosman, the analyst responsible for the financial sector at BTG, who has a buy rating on the stock.

Stone has built one of the largest distribution channels in the country, with strong penetration among micro, small and medium-sized retailers. In 2025, it earned R$ 2.5 billion, 17.5% more than the previous year, with an ROE of 26%. The company estimates it has approximately 20% market share in payments in its main segment of SMEs. But in credit and banking – which includes checking accounts and cash management services – participation is "low single digits," between 1% and 2%, according to Scherer.

Although credit is growing rapidly – the portfolio more than doubled in 2025 – the scale is still small: the total portfolio totaled R$ 2.8 billion in December. Stone calculates that its total addressable market is R$ 100 billion in revenue, relatively evenly divided among these three verticals. Today, however, results are still disproportionately dependent on acquiring – a highly competitive, volatile segment with margins that tend to decrease over time, not just in Brazil.

In the fourth quarter, TPV (total payment volume) growth fell short of expectations, with high churn. Part of this is due to a worse macro environment, with weakening consumption. But stronger online expansion – where Stone has less exposure – over physical retail, more aggressive competition offers, and execution problems also weighed. "If the company raises prices and loses market share, okay. But it lost both ways. It entered a balance that concerns us," said one manager who exited their Stone investment at year-end.

According to an analyst, the problems vary by retailer profile and region. "In some cases, it's a lack of credit; in others, it's a price issue. And some complain about old machines." For Scherer, the challenge is demonstrating "the value of the complete solution." "We are the sector player focused on SMEs, and our product offering evolved significantly last year but became complex for customers. We need to simplify and improve execution," said the executive, who became CEO in March after 11 years at the company, three as CFO. "We've always had a big focus on sales. We need to develop relationships throughout the customer's lifetime."

For now, neither the company nor the market expects major changes in the short term. Stone provided guidance of gross profit of R$ 6.6 billion to R$ 7 billion for 2026 and R$ 7.2 billion to R$ 8.3 billion for 2027, below analyst estimates. It also forecasts TPV growth of around 5%, below the projected SG&A expense (sales, general and administrative) growth of 10% this year and 20% next year, according to BTG.

CFO Diego Salgado reminded that few companies provide two-year guidance, and that the market was more frustrated with 2027 expectations. "Longer guidance embeds a greater degree of uncertainty, because we make projections based on information we have today."

For Scherer, there is room to seek better numbers, especially in expenses, incorporating advances in technology and artificial intelligence. "Yes, we need to make a lot of investments to evolve from a POS machine to a more complete platform, but the world is evolving very fast, and it's difficult to anticipate everything that's coming," said the CEO.

The uncertainty about when and how the company will deliver on its promises led to last month's selloff. "Stone has always been a sales company, not a technology company. It's true that it's growing in other verticals, but the core is declining more rapidly," said the manager who sold the stock.

Those who bought in recent weeks are focused on the stock's discount – and dividend potential. Stone trades at 5.7x 2026 earnings, one of the lowest P/Es in the sector, below mid-size banks, which typically trade at lower multiples because they have lower ROEs, according to BTG.

The valuation excludes the R$ 3.1 billion received from selling Linx – which should be used for share buybacks and dividend payments (the company said it will communicate this in April). With the distribution of these resources, dividend yield should reach 17%, according to Citi.

The company also announced it will use approximately R$ 2 billion to repurchase shares. Stone also has approximately R$ 10 billion in non-interest-bearing deposits – which, at 15% interest rates, generated results of around R$ 1.5 billion in 2025.

For Citi, the fair price for the stock implies a multiple of 8.3x. Rosman compared Stone's situation to Banco do Brasil at the time of Lula's reelection, when BB was trading below 4x earnings despite having excess capital and high provisions. "At the time, we argued that the dividend yield was so high that the asymmetry seemed attractive – earnings would have to deteriorate very rapidly for the thesis not to work."

At these multiples, bankers and analysts believe Stone could be an M&A target. In the view of Gustavo Schroder, Citi's financial analyst, visible reinvestment opportunities in the core business are "limited." Moreover, he wrote, the company "appears to be migrating to a leaner operating structure, prioritizing efficiency and capital discipline instead of expansion initiatives, which makes it more attractive to a financial or strategic player."
