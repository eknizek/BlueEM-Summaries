# Senate Approves New Minimum Cocoa Percentage for Chocolate, Eliminates Dark and Semi-Dark Categories

The Senate approved on Wednesday (15) a bill that establishes minimum cocoa percentages in chocolate production. The text now moves to President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva (PT) for signature.

The proposal originated in the Senate itself and had already been analyzed by the Chamber of Deputies, where it underwent changes. Because of this, it returned to the House for a new vote and was approved symbolically.

One of the main changes is the elimination of the terms "dark chocolate" and "semi-dark chocolate." Products with at least 35% total cocoa solids will now be classified simply as chocolate, without qualifiers. These products also cannot contain more than 5% vegetable fats.

The current Anvisa (National Health Surveillance Agency) rule provides for a minimum of 25% cocoa solids for all types of chocolate except white chocolate, which requires at least 20% cocoa butter. The 25% percentage will now be permitted in two types of products: one already known to consumers and another created by the bill.

For traditional milk chocolate, the rule approved by the Senate remains the same: a minimum of 25% cocoa solids and 14% total milk solids or derivatives. The novelty is the creation of sweet chocolate, which must have at least 25% total cocoa solids, with at least 18% cocoa butter and 12% fat-free solids.

In the report presented in this final stage, Senator Angelo Coronel (Republicanos-BA) included changes that loosen labeling rules and technical criteria for defining cocoa solids. These points will be detailed later through Executive regulations.

The requirement to display the cocoa percentage of each product on labels was one of the targets of criticism from industry. In a statement, industry associations—Abia (food industry), Abicab (chocolate, peanut and candy industry), and Aipc (cocoa processors)—said the sector had just completed an "extensive packaging adaptation process to present consumers with changes in the nutrition table," referring to changes mandated in 2020.

If signed into law, the new law will have a one-year period to enter into force. Until then, current rules remain valid.

When the bill was approved in the Chamber of Deputies in February, industry associations released a technical note stating that the modifications diverge from current Anvisa regulations.
