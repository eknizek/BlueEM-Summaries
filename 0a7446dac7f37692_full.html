# Brazil Legislative Agenda Stalled: Gasoline Tax, Debt Relief, and 6x1 Work Schedule in Limbo

It is hard to believe that a majority of deputies and senators would vote against the approval of bills that distribute money or have gained popular support. One such project that should enter discussion soon is the reduction of working hours, the change to the 6x1 schedule. The reduction of federal gasoline tax was on the government's urgent agenda. Perhaps the new plan to support the renegotiation of debts for individuals, Desenrola 2, requires some legislative measure. In theory, part of these projects would be approved, the government imagined, which will gather fractions of a percentage of popularity and votes however it can, since the reelection of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva hangs in the balance at the moment. In theory.

After Wednesday's (29th) setback, it is unclear how the opposition will try to balance its hunger for votes with the desire to bury Lula's third term and prevent a fourth. The emboldened opposition now has about 70% of the Chamber and more than half of the Senate. Opposition leaders say these "agendas," projects of interest to Lula, are not government priorities and will be discussed, but on Congress's terms. That Lula will have to dance carefully. Etc.

In summary, it has become more difficult, but not impossible, although the government's political articulation is even more inexperienced than at the beginning of Lula's third term. The economic and financial horizon has become more sour in the short term. There are certain aspects of the economy with still very good results, such as employment and wages, as seen in the March Pnad. But, as we know, this performance has not bolstered Lula's popularity.

Interest rates will remain higher for longer. With Desenrola and everything else, financial pressure will decrease little. Average inflation has begun to rise because of the war, but the worse effect is still to come. Food prices will likely rise again, possibly to the same level as the IPCA by year's end, to more than 4% at least. Given that food price levels have skyrocketed since the epidemic and have barely fallen since, even a modest increase irritates. It irritates even more because a considerable part of income gains is being consumed by interest, particularly for those earning up to three minimum wages.

This is not a doomsday scenario painting. It is one of additional difficulties in a situation of Lula's popularity deficit. To summarize:

1) Small or no impact from measures that should be popular, such as income tax exemption for those earning up to R$5,000;

2) Reheating of prices, with risk of food becoming more expensive;

3) Persistently high interest rates;

4) Doubts about the approval of emergency economic and electoral projects (fuel, Desenrola) and what will remain of the work schedule reduction project.

The problem goes beyond Lula's third term. According to the most recent "market" forecasts, those from the Focus Bulletin, the Selic rate will only drop to 13% by the end of 2026. In the market, money negotiations indicate Selic at more than 13.5% at year's end. With IPCA forecast at 3.5% for 2029, Selic would still be at 9.75%. That is, the real interest rate would remain above 6% after three-quarters of the next president's term have passed; there are other serious macroeconomic issues.

Without an impactful economic plan or miracles, this scenario does not change much. If Lula's third term succeeds, his fourth will begin in a difficult situation.
