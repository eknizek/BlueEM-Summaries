# Aegea's Annual Results Reveal Revenue Decline and Higher Provisions Amid Accounting Review

The market cautiously received Aegea's annual financial statements, released in the early hours of Saturday, April 11, following a series of delays caused by a comprehensive accounting review of the company's financial statements. The main changes involved the methodology for revenue recognition and the calculation of Expected Credit Losses (PECLD), adopting a more conservative approach to assessing the company's ability to recover credits.

The new provision model introduces a loss matrix based on delinquency history, which considers portfolio evolution across different delay brackets. The longer the time overdue, the lower the probability of recovery. It also incorporates the risk of losses in credits not yet due, giving greater weight to customers' payment history. Renegotiations overdue for more than 30 days were written off.

As a result, the provision for Expected Credit Losses (PECLD) now equals 105% of overdue credits in the Aegea ecosystem. At the end of 2025, the company recorded R$ 978 million in overdue accounts, not counting renegotiations. This figure is 65% higher than the revised 2024 numbers and also exceeds the balance of performing credits of R$ 850 million.

The company also changed its revenue recognition model to better align results with cash generation. In the previous model, revenue was recognized after service delivery, while delinquent amounts were later recorded as credit losses. Under the new methodology, revenue from customers with balances overdue for more than six months or with incomplete or outdated registration is recognized only after actual payment.

On a consolidated basis, the pro forma revised net revenue for 2024 was R$ 15.16 billion, approximately R$ 1 billion below the originally reported value before the review. In 2025, pro forma net revenue totaled R$ 18.3 billion.

The concession most affected by the review was Águas do Rio, which, according to the company, is still "in a maturation and customer portfolio conversion process." Águas do Rio serves more than 20 municipalities in Rio de Janeiro state and more than 100 neighborhoods in the state capital. The delay also affected Aegea's results through equity accounting, reducing the contribution of affiliates linked to Águas do Rio and the value of the investment recorded on the balance sheet. In 2025, Aegea's pro forma billing from this concession grew only 2%, well below the 8% growth of overall operations.

In the market, the delay in releasing financial statements had been feeding distrust, with Fitch and S&P downgrading the company's credit ratings. In the week before the balance sheet release, Aegea debentures led the spread widening in the credit market. The AEGP17 paper suffered the most, with spreads opening 3.76 percentage points and closing the week at 6.3%.

"The balance sheet helped calm things. It came in line with what we expected," says a senior credit analyst from a major bank. Among funds with the greatest exposure to the company were Vinland XP Fundo Incentivado, Icatu Vanguarda Iporã, and Polo Crédito Corporativo, which had 24.4%, 12.6%, and 10.5% of their portfolios allocated to Aegea securities, respectively.

In a call with investors on Monday, April 13, CEO Radamés Casseb stated that the adjustments were necessary to align results with cash generation and that the changes were merely accounting-related. "The increase in delinquency was exclusively related to the methodology change. There is no impact from genuine deterioration of our receivables. When we look at Rio de Janeiro, there is actually an improvement, and also in the ecosystem," Casseb stated.

According to the CEO, the delay was due to the complexity of the company's financial statements following strong growth in recent years. "We went from 50 cities in December 2020 to 892 cities in 2025, from 4,000 employees to 25,000, and from 10 concessions to 65 concessions," he said. "We redid the balance sheets for the last five years of all concessions."

According to the executive, the adjustments had an impact of approximately 50 basis points on leverage. Aegea ended 2025 with pro forma net debt of R$ 47.05 billion, equivalent to 4.51 times Ebitda. In 2024, the ratio was 4.13 times, already considering the revised numbers.
