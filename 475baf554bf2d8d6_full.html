# Climate change mobilizes coffee growers' cooperatives

Climate change is expected to alter the world map of Arabica coffee production in the coming decades. Rising temperatures, irregular rainfall, and other extreme weather events are expected to make planting unfeasible or difficult in traditional coffee-growing regions.

Aware of this scenario, cooperatives, which account for more than half of Arabica coffee production in Brazil, are adopting measures to minimize the impact of climate change. "We are facing a real challenge that requires a technical response, planning, and coordination. Cooperatives are very close to the reality of the farmer and understand that adaptation will be increasingly crucial to maintaining competitiveness," says Ronaldo Scucato, president of the Ocemg System.

Currently, according to a Rabobank study, 8% of the areas used worldwide for planting Arabica coffee are already considered unsuitable. Projections indicate that this percentage could reach 20% by 2050 due to climate change. In recent years, Brazil and Colombia, the two largest world producers of Arabica coffee, have already experienced crop failures due to extreme weather events.

Today in Brazil 81% of Arabica coffee is produced in areas considered suitable, yielding an average of 32.6 60-kilogram bags per hectare. Another 11% is produced in marginally suitable areas (with an average productivity of 28.3 bags per hectare). And 4% are in areas considered unsuitable (with productivity of up to 28 bags per hectare).

In northern Minas Gerais, for example, cultivation in unsuitable areas has become possible thanks to high investments in irrigation. Even so, the average productivity is 29 bags per hectare.

According to Rabobank, by 2050, suitable areas will decrease from 81% to 62% of today's coffee farms. Areas considered marginally suitable will increase from 11% today to 27%, and unsuitable areas will grow from 6% to 9%.

In Colombia, the share of coffee planted in unsuitable areas is expected to rise from 7% to 18% by 2050. Planting in suitable areas will fall from 56% to 45%.

In Ethiopia, however, the expectation is for improvement. Suitable areas will cover 50% of the planted area by 2050, compared with 39% today.

In Brazil, the areas that will be most affected are mainly in the Cerrado (Brazilian savanna), in northern Minas Gerais, western Bahia, Goiás, Mato Grosso, Mato Grosso do Sul, Tocantins, and Maranhão.

Scucato, from Ocemg System, says that coffee growers' cooperatives have been investing in regenerative agriculture to restore soil health, increase biodiversity, improve water retention, and make crops more resilient to climate change.

The Cerrado Coffee Growers Cooperative (EXPOCACER), in Patrocínio, Minas Gerais, was the first to obtain the international Regeneragri certification for regenerative agriculture. Simão Pedro de Lima, president of EXPOCACER, says that 25% of the productive area (13,000 hectares) of the cooperative members' farms is currently dedicated to regenerative coffee farming. "We aim to reach up to 25,000 hectares. We want to have 35% of the area with regenerative coffee [farming]," says Lima.

He adds that regenerative practices have reduced carbon emissions from the crop from an average of 8 tonnes per hectare to 3 to 4 tonnes per hectare.

Farlla Gomes, technical sustainability manager at EXPOCACER, explains that regenerative practices involve actions such as using bio-inputs instead of chemical pesticides, shifting to fertilizers with a lower carbon footprint, and planting cover crops between the rows of coffee plants. Another technique is planting trees around the coffee plants, forming a protection barrier.

"Cover crops sequester greenhouse gases and reduce the demand for herbicides by 40% because they curb the growth of weeds," she says. Cover crops, crop rotation, and less soil disturbance are practices that increase the organic matter content and keep carbon fixed in the soil.

"In southern Minas Gerais, cooperatives that have adopted regenerative practices are reporting reductions of up to 30% in pest attacks," notes Scucato.

One example is the Regional Cooperative of Coffee Growers in Guaxupé (COOXUPÉ), the largest coffee cooperative in the world. In April, COOXUPÉ began selling carbon credits generated from the afforestation of coffee plantations. The project involved 12 cooperative members and an area of 43.27 hectares with regenerative systems. 649.94 tonnes of carbon were sequestered, generating R$104,600 in revenues.

In the Zona da Mata region of Minas Gerais, the Lajinha Region's Coffee Growers Cooperative (COOCAFÉ), which has 11,200 members and produces 1 million bags of coffee per year, is betting on biochar, a biomass-based soil conditioner that improves water retention, carbon storage, and nutrient availability in the soil.

In 2023, COOCAFÉ installed an industrial biochar production plant in partnership with the French greentech company NetZero. The unit processes 16,000 tonnes of coffee straw and husks per year and produces 4,000 tonnes of biochar, enough to supply up to 3,000 hectares. The raw material is supplied by 400 COOCAFÉ members.

"Foreign markets are looking for coffees with low carbon emissions. We also work with fertilizers that emit less carbon, incorporating cover cropping," says Valdean Lourenço Teófilo, technical development manager at COOCAFÉ.
