# Winners and Losers in the Libertarian Economy

If the measure of things in these times of libertarian economy Javier Milei-style is based on growth rates, mining and financial activity compete head-to-head for the top spot: 9.6% versus 7.7%, according to the latest official records for 2025. Far behind, construction appears with a modest 0.5%; manufacturing industry shows a negative 2.6% and commerce, wholesale and retail, another negative 3.2%. INDEC's monthly estimator that paints the complete picture and can be likened to an advance of GDP marks 1.9% positive in line with the 3.3% annual projection by the Central Bank's survey of expectations conducted among consultancies, specialized institutes and financial entities. Placed there, economic activity falls just short of the 4.4% with which 2024 had closed. Data, once again data, now speaks about the impact of the economic situation on the labor market. Nothing to celebrate: instead of creating jobs, the sectors leading the ranking lost employment. According to reports from the UIA's Center for Studies based on figures from the Labor Department, mining lost almost 8,100 registered, formal jobs, and 4,307 in so-called financial intermediation. Central piece of a generalized collapse, manufacturing industry faced nothing less than 38,971 lost jobs; commerce, 12,400 and construction, 2,960. We're talking about how things are going in three sectors that together represent 40% of the country's formal labor force. From one period to another, in 2025 alone there was a gap equivalent to 88,848 jobs that climbs to 236,857 if the count stretches until August 2023, that is, until shortly before Javier Milei and company's arrival in government and when the then president-elect warned about the "critical situation" he was inheriting and promised "drastic changes, with no room for gradualism."

If we focus on industry, we have a sector that truly operates at half capacity, with 46% of its production capacity unused and idle, with records similar to those from March 2024; that is, from no less than two years ago. Affected sectors include automotive, textiles, metalworking and plastics. Explainable by what we see yet inevitably disheartening, the list of causes and consequences that link without pause continues with labor income that calculated in real wages show five months of continuous decline; with retail sales that accumulate eleven months downward, since May 2025, and beef consumption installed at its worst level in the last 20 years. Sources: specialized research centers and business chambers. Scattered though also striking data from the price index for March 26 against March 25, of the last year so to speak, tell us that in Greater Buenos Aires the cost of rents rose 52% and 88.9% in Patagonia and that in public transportation there was 46% for Greater Buenos Aires and 55% in Cuyo. They also reveal a disordered price structure, similar to the absence of a price policy as necessity demands. It's worth insisting: as much as they have the habit of boring, data usually explains reality better than words and also better than more than a few official or pro-government speeches or both conveyed together. Precisely, Minister Luis Caputo has stressed these days the importance that investment has in sustained and long-term economic growth, like someone suggesting he holds a trump card. True from start to finish, only the INDEC data on investment progress since he became Economy Minister sow doubts about whether he will really materialize his statements: they show declines of 2.8% and 5.3% in the third and fourth quarters of 2025 and a 17.7% drop during 2024. On the same track he asks for patience for what's going rough and announces with questionable precision: "The next 18 months will be the best of the last decades." Patience reinforced this time, Caputo's 18 months stretch until November 2028. Meanwhile, President Milei clings to the chainsaw almost as if it were his only instrument of economic policy. He has said: "It keeps running. I will not resign fiscal balance." In reality, no one is asking him to do such a thing: it's what in the absence of a growth model he intends to make people believe. By Alcadio Oña
