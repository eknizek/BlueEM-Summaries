With Tigo Operator Strengthened, Fixed Internet Becomes New Battleground in Colombian Telecom Sector

26 April 2026 · 04:51 PM

# With the Tigo Operator Strengthened, Fixed Internet Becomes the New Battleground in the Colombian Telecommunications Sector

After the auction in which the Treasury sold its shareholding stake in Coltel was finalized, and in which Millicom, the parent company of Tigo, resulted as the awarded investor, questions are beginning to emerge about the impact this operation will have on the telecommunications sector. One of the key fronts will be the fixed home internet market, where greater competition is anticipated in the coming months, especially due to Tigo's strengthening in this segment.

According to the most recent figures from the ICT Ministry, at the close of the fourth quarter of 2025, the fixed internet access market in Colombia was still led by Claro, which registered 3.48 million connections. It was followed by UNE EPM (Tigo) with 1.82 million, Movistar with 1.60 million, and ETB with 560,000 accesses. Further behind are other players with smaller market shares, but which are also part of the competitive ecosystem. Among them are Starlink Colombia with 160,000 accesses, Celsia Internet with 150,000, HV Television with 130,000, and EMCALI with 80,000 connections. In the segment of smaller operators, Fiesta Telecomunications and Fibrazo report 70,000 accesses each, while the group of "other" companies accounts for 2.09 million connections in total, which shows an important base of regional and local operators. Additionally, emerging providers like Somos Internet reach 55,630 fixed accesses, reflecting the growth of new business models.

## Tigo Arrives Strong

One of the most striking aspects following the operation is Tigo's strengthening in the fixed internet segment. After integrating accesses with Movistar, the operator would reach approximately 3.42 million connections, significantly reducing the gap with respect to Claro, the current market leader. This move would reconfigure the competitive landscape. According to José Otero, a telecommunications expert and executive director of ICT Development Consulting, the market would enter a new stage marked by greater balance among the main players. In his view, a counterweight would be configured against Claro's dominance, with three clearly differentiated blocs: Claro, Tigo, and a set of smaller operators. "In this scenario, Tigo would gain economies of scale and greater presence, which would allow it to pressure the market leader through more aggressive commercial strategies, such as promotions and service packages," Otero comments.

## Is the Playing Field Becoming Unbalanced?

However, the impact would not be uniform throughout the country. Otero points out that rural areas, where smaller operators have greater prominence, would not experience significant changes, since competition between the major players would be concentrated mainly in urban and suburban areas and in business segments. Greater effects are anticipated in major cities. "Operators like ETB and EMCALI, which have presence in urban markets, would face more intense competitive pressure from companies with greater scale, national coverage, and more robust portfolios," Otero notes. For the expert, both Claro and Tigo have international presence, which could make them more attractive to corporate clients or multinationals operating in different countries, which would reinforce their positioning in the business segment. "The increase in competition between major players could also have collateral effects on smaller operators. If the dispute translates into more aggressive offers, these could see their market share reduced or be forced to compete via prices, which would impact their profit margins," Otero comments.

## The Landscape Remains Unclear

For Geusseppe González, associate director for Latin America at Access Partnership, what is occurring in Colombia goes beyond a simple business operation; "it is a fundamental transformation in the structure of the telecommunications market." In his view, the movement between Millicom and Telefónica is part of a broader dynamic marked by technological evolution and the advancement of small and medium operators, who are increasingly gaining ground with more competitive offers. However, he also notes that the effect on competition remains uncertain and will not necessarily translate into a more dynamic market. Although Millicom could substantially increase its user base, González focuses on an actor that often goes unnoticed: small and medium operators, which already represent approximately 18% of the market. "Added to this is the emergence of new technologies such as low-earth orbit (LEO) satellites, which are growing steadily and are beginning to compete for internet access, especially in remote areas of the country."

In this scenario, González points out that the future of the sector will not depend solely on major players. The lack of clear public policies for this new context leaves much of the direction in the hands of the market, "in the decisions of operators, both traditional and emerging, and in users' response to these new options."

For now, the landscape remains open. According to González, it will be necessary to wait between 10 and 12 months to understand more clearly how the market has been rearranged, where users migrated, and above all, whether competition really intensified or ended up concentrating even further.
