# With Strengthened Tigo Operator, Fixed Internet Becomes New Battleground in Colombian Telecom Sector

After the auction in which the Treasury sold its shareholding stake in Coltel, and in which Millicom, Tigo's parent company, emerged as the awarded investor, questions are beginning to arise about the impact this operation will have on the telecommunications sector. One of the key fronts will be the fixed home internet market, where greater competition is anticipated in the coming months, especially due to Tigo's strengthening in this segment.

According to the most recent figures from the ICT Ministry, at the close of the fourth quarter of 2025, Colombia's fixed internet access market continued to be led by Claro, which registered 3.48 million connections. It was followed by UNE EPM (Tigo) with 1.82 million, Movistar with 1.60 million, and ETB with 560,000 accesses. Further back are other players with smaller market shares, but who also form part of the competitive ecosystem. Among them are Starlink Colombia with 160,000 accesses, Celsia Internet with 150,000, HV Televisión with 130,000, and EMCALI with 80,000 connections. In the segment of smaller operators, Fiesta Telecomunicaciones and Fibrazo report 70,000 accesses each, while the group of "other" companies combines 2.09 million connections, which evidences an important base of regional and local operators. Additionally, emerging providers such as Somos Internet reach 55,630 fixed accesses, reflecting the growth of new business models.

## Tigo Arrives Strong

One of the most striking aspects following the operation is Tigo's strengthening in the fixed internet segment. After integrating accesses with Movistar, the operator would reach approximately 3.42 million connections, significantly reducing the gap compared to Claro, the current market leader. This move would reconfigure the competitive landscape. According to José Otero, a telecommunications expert and executive director of ICT Development Consulting, the market would enter a new stage marked by greater balance among the main players. In his view, a counterweight would be configured against Claro's dominance, with three clearly differentiated blocs: Claro, Tigo, and a set of smaller operators. "In this scenario, Tigo would gain economies of scale and greater presence, which would allow it to pressure the market leader through more aggressive commercial strategies, such as promotions and service packages," comments Otero.

## Does the Playing Field Become Unbalanced?

The impact, however, would not be homogeneous across the country. Otero points out that rural areas, where smaller operators have greater prominence, would not experience significant changes, since competition between major players would concentrate mainly in urban and suburban areas and in business segments. Where greater effects are anticipated is in major cities. "Operators such as ETB and EMCALI, which have presence in urban markets, would face more intense competitive pressure from companies with greater scale, national coverage, and more robust portfolios," Otero specifies. For the expert, both Claro and Tigo have international presence, which could make them more attractive to corporate or multinational clients operating in different countries, which would reinforce their positioning in the business segment. "The increase in competition among major players could also have collateral effects on smaller operators. If the dispute translates into more aggressive offers, these could see their market share reduced or be forced to compete via prices, which would impact their profit margins," comments Otero.

## The Panorama Remains Unclear

For Geusseppe González, associate director for Latin America at Access Partnership, what is happening in Colombia goes beyond a simple business operation; "it is a fundamental transformation in the structure of the telecommunications market." In his view, the movement between Millicom and Telefónica is part of a broader dynamic marked by technological evolution and the advance of small and medium-sized operators, which are increasingly gaining ground with more competitive offers. However, he also notes that the effect on competition is still uncertain and will not necessarily translate into a more dynamic market. Although Millicom could significantly increase its user base, González focuses on an actor that often goes unnoticed: small and medium-sized operators, which already represent about 18% of the market. "Added to this is the emergence of new technologies such as low-earth orbit (LEO) satellites, which are growing steadily and beginning to compete for internet access, especially in remote areas of the country." In this scenario, González points out that the sector's future will not depend solely on major players. The lack of clear public policies for this new context leaves much of the course in the hands of the market, "the decisions of operators, both traditional and emerging, and the response of users to these new options." For now, the panorama remains open. According to González, it will be necessary to wait between 10 and 12 months to better understand how the market was restructured, where users migrated, and above all, whether competition really intensified or ended up concentrating even more.
