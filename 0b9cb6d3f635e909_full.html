SulAmérica obtains injunction and accuses autism clinic of fraud

18 April 2026 · 10:00 AM

# SulAmérica Obtains Injunction and Accuses Autism Clinic of Fraud

In a preliminary injunction decision, the 3rd Civil Court of Tatuapé, in the east zone of São Paulo, prohibited Behave ABA - Multidisciplinary Clinic, specialized in treating people with ASD (Autism Spectrum Disorder), from serving beneficiaries of SulAmérica health insurance plans and authorized the operator to deny reimbursement requests for services provided by the clinic. It also allowed patients to be transferred to the operator's credentialed network within 60 days.

SulAmérica accuses Behave of irregularly moving R$ 11 million between 2022 and 2025 through services not rendered and payments made to the clinic only after the reimbursement values were transferred by the health plan.

According to lawyer Marcelo Válio, who defends Behave, this is a strategy by the operator to avoid reimbursing specialized and quality clinics that treat people with ASD. This would force parents to take them to facilities credentialed by SulAmérica—clinics that, according to him, offer "miserable" services. Válio also states he is defending five other lawsuits filed by the operator against spaces specialized in ASD: "They are all identical."

In the preliminary injunction decision, Judge Luiz Fernando Nardelli evaluates that there are "strong indications of irregularities" given the accusation that the clinic instructed the omission of ASD diagnosis when health plan beneficiaries contracted coverage and gave instructions on how to prepare medical reports to enable actions to obtain coverage.

In the decision, Nardelli determines that Behave itself communicate the service prohibition to beneficiaries, without prejudicing any eventual developments in the criminal sphere.

Behave's defender accuses SulAmérica of practicing so-called "fishing," an illegal practice in Brazilian law in which investigations are generic, without a defined or probable target, in hopes of finding some evidence. He claims this and the "thousands of documents" included in the lawsuit misled the judge. He also accuses SulAmérica of "stalking," harassment of patients. "They pursue, go to people's houses, knock on doors, take photos without authorization. They don't want to pay clinics that aren't credentialed and sell the story that the others are committing fraud," he accuses.

Válio contests that the R$ 11 million figure is exaggerated. He states it fails to account for the number of children treated by the clinic and the cost of each specialized session with quality professionals.

When consulted by the column, SulAmérica states it does not comment on cases under judicial secrecy. However, the company contests the lawyer's accusations and states that its credentialed network, including clinics specialized in treating ASD patients, "is formed by carefully selected professionals and institutions, committed to clinical protocols based on evidence. Unlike models where profit supersedes care, SulAmérica does not compromise on ethics or patient well-being. This is a line the company does not negotiate."

The operator states it fully complies with its legal and contractual obligations and combats, with the approval of the Judiciary, frauds in charges and services "that do not correspond to any reasonable clinical reality and that divert resources that should reach those who genuinely need care." It states it has filed more than 850 crime reports in the last two years. It has also filed approximately 500 lawsuits, identifying R$ 237 million in fraud in 2025 alone. The company states these actions are not against patients, but in their defense.
