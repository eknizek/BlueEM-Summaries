# Green Fertilizer Project Challenges Industry Dependence on Natural Gas

A UK-listed green fertilizer group is developing a pioneering project in Paraguay to test whether low-cost renewable energy can sustain ammonia production without natural gas, as the Middle East crisis disrupts fertilizer markets.

Atome announced it has made a final investment decision on its US$665 million (R$3.3 billion) Villeta plant, which will use hydroelectric power to produce approximately 260,000 tonnes per year of fertilizer for regional markets.

"We've proven it's possible to close and finance a green fertilizer facility at industrial scale," said CEO Olivier Mussat. "This has never been done before."

The decision comes as war in Iran disrupts gas and fertilizer supplies, exposing the strong dependence of fertilizer production on natural gas and a small number of exporting regions. Most nitrogen fertilizer is produced by combining hydrogen derived from natural gas with nitrogen from the air to produce ammonia. Green fertilizer uses hydrogen separated from water with renewable electricity.

Since gas represents the majority of production costs in traditional ammonia manufacturing, shocks in supply and prices quickly spread to food systems. The conflict has also blocked important trade routes. Approximately one-quarter to one-third of global nitrogen fertilizer exports pass through the Strait of Hormuz, while declining gas shipments have already raised prices and reduced production, triggering warnings of a global food crisis.

Mussat believes the project should be viewed primarily as a response to these vulnerabilities. "It's not a green story," he said. "Actually, it's a food security story."

Latin America's agricultural sector is a major global exporter of crops but depends heavily on imported fertilizers, leaving it particularly exposed. "Half the region's fertilizers come from behind the Strait of Hormuz, so that's a big problem," Mussat said. "It's about geopolitical resilience and ensuring they can continue producing the food we eat, even in Europe."

Agriculture is also the "backbone of economies of countries like Brazil," he said, adding that the project would function as a "structural protection" against geopolitical volatility and fossil fuel price swings.

The Paraguay plant is backed by a group of development creditors, including the International Finance Corporation and the European Investment Bank, with capital investments led by Hy24, a hydrogen investor. Norwegian fertilizer group Yara International agreed to purchase the plant's entire production under a 10-year contract.

Atome said the US$665 million financing package included approximately US$420 million (R$2.1 billion) in debt and US$245 million (R$1.2 billion) in equity.

Hy24 CEO Pierre-Etienne Franc said recent geopolitical tensions reinforced arguments for such investments. "The fertilizer industry is fundamentally dependent on gas prices," he said. "If you want to be less dependent and have cheap sources of energy that aren't fossil fuels, you have a pathway to green fertilizer that will be localized."

He added that the Middle East crisis "put back on the agenda the need not to depend on fossil energy," even though earlier periods of lower gas prices had weakened arguments for alternative production.

The vulnerability reflects how the fertilizer industry developed, with large-scale production concentrated in regions with cheap gas, particularly the Middle East and Russia. These facilities then export ammonia and urea worldwide, leaving agricultural regions dependent on long and fragile supply chains.

Franc said Latin America was a natural location for new production because it combines large agricultural demand with a lack of domestic gas resources. "You're localizing, you're bringing local jobs and you're not depending on natural gas-driven commodity prices," he said.

He said investors were targeting returns in the "double digits," reflecting country risk, but sustained by strong counterparties and long-term contracts.

The Villeta project will be relatively small in global terms—representing less than 1 percent of the nitrogen fertilizer market—but its supporters argue it could provide a model for similar developments in other import-dependent markets.

Electricity costs, a key determinant of whether green fertilizer can compete with conventional production, are expected to be around US$30 per megawatt-hour under a long-term power agreement, according to the company.
