# Face-Off Over Rising Interest Rates: Finance Minister's Criticism and What the Central Bank Refutes

Finance Minister Germán Ávila brought his confrontation with the Central Bank of Colombia to an economic forum organized by the institution this Wednesday. There, the official once again questioned the appropriateness of recent rate hikes and the Central Bank's control strategy.

For the official, his withdrawal from the March session was not the "substantial fact," but rather the board's decision to implement a second consecutive 100 basis point increase in the interest rate. He also questioned the absence of the Central Bank's manager, Leonardo Villar, from the event, who excused himself by arguing that his presence would have "political implications" in the country's current circumstances.

"The manager perhaps does not realize that the decisions they are making also have implications in the current electoral situation and are not separate from that political moment," said Minister Ávila.

The official launched this criticism despite having announced days earlier that he would not return to the Central Bank sessions. This stance opens the door to a possible institutional paralysis, as experts argue that the board cannot deliberate or decide without his presence.

## Effects on the Economy

The minister also insisted on his discontent with the direction of monetary policy and asserted that Colombia is "the only country in the world that would think to raise 200 basis points" its reference rate. Furthermore, he described as "absolutely ineffective" attempting to control inflation pressures derived from global tensions through increases in the local interest rate.

"If the entire world is being prudent, it makes no sense that the Central Bank, through orthodox interpretation and inflation control measures, ends up making a decision of these magnitudes," the minister stated.

According to his calculations, the 200 basis point increase would subtract 0.36 percentage points from economic growth in 2026; in fact, he argued that without these increases, the economy could have grown up to 2.9 percent this year.

## Central Bank's Position

From the Central Bank's perspective, co-director Bibiana Taboada assured in an interview with El Tiempo that "accelerating economic growth through different fiscal and monetary stimuli" is not the correct path. Instead, she proposed taking structural measures that guarantee sustained growth while maintaining low and stable inflation in the long term for the benefit of Colombians.

Co-director Mauricio Villamizar agrees that greater economic growth must be sustainable. "Growth for a day or a year can be followed by lean times for a long time afterward. We need patience and caution," he said in a recent interview.

## Risk to Employment and Finances

During his intervention at the forum, Finance Minister Germán Ávila emphasized that while inflation has declined from 13.34 percent to 5.2 percent, high interest rates threaten the downward trend in unemployment and could destroy approximately 48,000 jobs.

Another effect of those 200 basis points would be felt in the cost of public debt, which would increase by approximately 1.8 trillion pesos, collaterally affecting sectors such as industry, construction, and agriculture.
