Commission to debate end of 6x1 work schedule will have government command and PEC authors among members

29 April 2026 · 06:42 PM

# Commission to debate end of 6x1 work schedule will have government command and PEC authors among members

BRASÍLIA - The special commission that will debate the merits of the constitutional amendment proposal (PEC) on ending the 6x1 work schedule will have government leadership and will be composed primarily of allied parliamentarians or those who have already expressed support for the issue - signaling that the PEC will face little resistance in the committee. The commission was installed this Wednesday, the 29th. The leadership will be in the hands of deputies allied with the government, who ran as a single slate. In total, there were 28 favorable votes and three blank votes. The Chamber president, Hugo Motta (Republicanos-PB), set May 28 as the deadline for voting on the PEC in the plenary.

The president will be Alencar Santana (PT-SP). The first vice-president will be deputy Daiana Santos (PC do B-RS), author of a project that reduces working hours from 44 to 40 hours per week. The second vice-president will be Luiz Gastão (PSD-CE), who prepared a report on Daiana's project in a subcommittee of the Chamber's Labor Commission. And the third vice-president will be Mauro Benevides Filho (União-CE), who was with the PDT until the party switching window.

Of the 38 regular members, at least 21 have a government-friendly profile or are sympathetic to the proposal, including the two authors of PECs that end the 6x1: Reginaldo Lopes (PT-MG) and Erika Hilton (PSOL-SP), who popularized the debate. The opposition is also present, but in smaller numbers. Deputy Mauricio Marcon (PL-RS), who filed a PEC defending hourly wage payment, managed to be nominated by the PL to join the committee. Former PSDB member Lucas Redecker (PSD-RS), who expressed concern about the impact on companies during proceedings in the Constitutional and Justice Commission, is also among the regular members.

The deadline for submitting amendments opens this Thursday, the 30th, and will last ten sessions. Alencar Santana plans to hold at least two meetings per week and also supports holding a hearing in another state. "We will have sufficient time to dialogue, listen to suggestions, debates from broad sectors that will bring their contributions, obviously listening to the government, which also has its proposal," he says.

For him, the committee has already demonstrated that it holds positions predominantly in favor of ending the 6x1 schedule. The commission president also signals that he should use the minimum processing time for the PEC in the committee, which is ten plenary sessions. This would allow voting on the text in the plenary within the deadline set by Motta. "Obviously, in addition to the three (sessions during the week), president Hugo Mota has the prerogative to call a session on Monday and, eventually, on Friday," he says. "So we will have sufficient time. It just takes political will, just wanting to."

Part of the committee is already beginning to indicate that the debate on ending the 6x1 schedule will fall by the wayside and that the text should focus on reducing working hours without salary reduction. At the press conference where he announced the rapporteur, deputy Leo Prates (Republicanos-BA), Motta did not explicitly speak of ending the 6x1 schedule, although he emphasized that the PEC should address reducing hours without reducing salaries. According to some center-aligned parliamentarians, only more radical deputies from the government base will insist on ending the 6x1 schedule, so as not to lose the discourse.

The rapporteur of the commission was also responsible for preparing the report on Daiana's project in the Labor Commission. In an interview with Estadão, the rapporteur defended that compensation would help smooth the change, but said that a decision on this is up to the government. Labor Minister Luiz Marinho has already said several times that he is against compensation, which also faces resistance in the Finance Ministry. Prates also believes that a transition rule would mitigate the impact of the reduction in working hours. "I want to mitigate as much as possible the problems being raised by the productive sector without taking away the improvement in workers' quality of life," Prates says. "I consider a transition rule coherent and I also consider that the market needs time to adapt. Nobody wants to do this by force," he said.

He also defended that the government withdraw constitutional urgency from the bill sent in mid-April that provides for reducing working hours from 44 to 40 hours per week, without salary reduction. However, the government's leader in the Chamber, deputy Paulo Pimenta (PT-RS), signaled that this would only happen after the final PEC report was closed in the special commission. Second vice-president deputy Luiz Gastão stated that micro and small companies cannot be allowed to lose their competitiveness. "We cannot allow micro and small companies to lose competitiveness, to lose their consumption capacity," he argues. "We will already have a tax reform on consumption that will bring them losses in competitiveness. If they also have an increase in costs with this competitiveness, we will be dooming them to concentrate more businesses in large companies and end up losing jobs."

For him, it would be necessary to discuss transition rules and compensation for micro and small companies, as well as "legal certainty for companies as a whole."
