# Temporary Suspension: Magnicharters Halted Due to Financial Insolvency

The Federal Civil Aviation Agency (AFAC) determined the temporary suspension of the Air Operator Certificate (AOC) of Magnicharters due to its financial insolvency. The authority informed the company that it has been given a deadline to present a plan addressing its financial problems, or otherwise it will proceed with the definitive revocation of its concession title and AOC, which would imply the permanent cessation of its commercial operations.

It is worth noting that the authority detected the financial problem since January, following an audit, despite which the airline continued operating normally. "Since January 2026, the AFAC conducted a technical-administrative verification (VTA) of Magnicharters, in accordance with Article 84 of the Civil Aviation Law, in which findings were identified and notified. In this verification it was clarified that they complied with all operational safety measures to continue flying; however, they were requested to resolve observations regarding financial matters and to provide evidence of solutions," said the AFAC.

The aeronautical authority noted that due to the identified financial capacity, it was detected that this could represent a risk to operational safety. "The company will be given a deadline to present a plan that addresses the findings detected and guarantees compliance with the necessary conditions to operate safely. In case of failing to prove the required solvency, the definitive revocation of the concession title and AOC will proceed, which would imply the permanent cessation of its commercial operations," the AFAC added.

## Background

This week, the airline's workers called a strike because the company owes them more than six months of salaries and travel allowances. Previously, the crews had already publicly denounced violations of the collective labor agreement. In December of last year, a pilot of the airline detained a flight at Mexico City International Airport headed to Cancun, arguing that he would not take off until the company addressed the request for overdue payments. At that moment, the crew member alleged there was a delay of more than five months.

The crew member detained the aircraft for several hours until he was arrested by elements of the Navy. By the close of 2025, Magnicharters operated a fleet of three aircraft with an average age of 29.7 years, equipment among the oldest in the country's commercial air fleet.
