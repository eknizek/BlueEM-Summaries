# Oil Executives Pressure Venezuela's President for Investment Guarantees

A group of U.S. oil sector executives met last week with Venezuela's interim president in Caracas and pressured her for guarantees that the country is safe for investments, while the U.S. government under President Donald Trump encourages them to rebuild Venezuela's struggling energy sector.

The group, accompanied by a high-ranking U.S. Department of Energy official, included Continental Resources CEO Doug Lawler, Texas oil businessman Bryan Sheffield, and former House Speaker Kevin McCarthy, who currently sits on the board of Denver-based drilling company Aspect Holdings.

The executives pressured interim president Delcy Rodríguez for details on how Venezuela's state oil company would structure contracts, what royalties would look like, and whether they could invest without the risk of having their assets confiscated, according to one person who participated in the meeting.

The meeting represents, so far, the clearest indication that interest among U.S. oil companies is expanding beyond Chevron and other major firms, in response to President Trump's call to restart production in Venezuela.

Russell Freeman, chief executive of Dallas-based HKN Energy, participated and said he was encouraged by the potential of the Latin American country. "We had positive discussions with Venezuela's leadership," Freeman said in an email statement, whose company has investor Ross Perot Jr. and operates in Iraq. "HKN Energy brings experience operating in complex environments and would welcome the opportunity to contribute to Venezuela's economic growth."

Executives from several of the companies—including Continental, HKN, and Aspect—participated in a White House meeting in January, when Trump urged the sector to invest billions of dollars in Venezuela following the capture of former president Nicolás Maduro.

Last week's meeting follows a similar meeting held last month in Caracas by U.S. Interior Secretary Doug Burgum with oil sector executives and government authorities.

"Venezuela is back on the map," Sheffield, managing partner of Austin, Texas-based investment firm Formentera Partners, said in a text message. "The pieces are finally falling into place."

Venezuela possesses some of the world's largest oil reserves, but its energy infrastructure is in ruins after years of neglect and corruption.

Companies have been cautious in heeding the U.S. president's appeal to help rebuild it, given political instability and a history of nationalizations—the country confiscated assets from oil companies in the 1970s and again in 2007.

In just over three months since U.S. forces captured Maduro, the U.S. has assumed control of Venezuela's oil sales, gradually eased sanctions, and issued a series of licenses allowing more foreign companies to operate in the country. Venezuelan authorities, in turn, reformed hydrocarbon law to attract back international companies.

During the meeting with sector executives, Rodríguez stated that the U.S. needs to go beyond issuing short-term licenses and offer broad, lasting sanctions relief to stimulate long-term investments, according to a brief video of the meeting shown by Venezuelan state television.

Sheffield said that for him, the trip to Caracas was personal. His grandfather was about to assume the presidency of Arco operations in Venezuela before the country took control of the oil sector in the mid-1970s.

"Rodríguez only reinforced my conviction in Venezuela's potential and why I'm excited to invest in the country again," he said.
