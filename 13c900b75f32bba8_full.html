Informal Employment Predominates in Mexico, Reports INEGI

04 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Informal Employment Predominates in Mexico, Reports INEGI

During March of this year, the employed population increased by 421 thousand 746 people compared to the same month last year, revealed data from the National Survey of Occupation and Employment from the National Institute of Statistics and Geography (INEGI).

Of this figure, practically all of it was generated in informal employment: 504 thousand 641 positions were created in the informal sector with respect to a reduction of 82 thousand 895 positions in the formal sector.

A similar pattern was observed with the accumulated figures for January-March 2026: in that period there has been a net reduction of 227 thousand 155 jobs, of which informality has increased by 82 thousand 895 people, while formality has fallen by 230 thousand 119 people.

With this, the rate of labor informality in the country is on the rise, as it marked 54.4% of the employed population in March 2025, rising to 54.6% in December of that same year, and for March of this year, the level is 54.8% of those employed in the country.

Thus, in Mexico 32.9 million people work in the informal sector, a figure that has remained relatively stable in recent months.

## Behavior

Regarding INEGI's figures related to informality, Beatriz Robles, director of Operations at Manpower Mexico, explained that "employment dynamics in Mexico show that informality persists and continues absorbing more people. The 33 million informal workers represent an extremely vulnerable sector, as they lack social security and access to legal benefits."

The specialist added that "formal employment is the foundation of the country's sustainable economic development because it guarantees workers' rights and strengthens the country's economic structure, but informality presents a structural challenge that absorbs much of the country's employment growth."

For this reason, the director of Operations at Manpower Mexico emphasized that regarding the Football World Cup, "it is relevant to ensure that this economic boost that is generated translates into formal employment, and not into a possible increase in informal work. The way we address informality during the 2026 World Cup will be a test of maturity for the Mexican labor market. It is not enough to generate employment; we must generate temporary formal employment."

## By Gender

The informal employment problem was more pronounced among women compared to men during March of this year. In total, only 2 thousand 546 jobs were created for women compared to March 2025. This figure was composed of a loss of 419 thousand 955 jobs in the formal sector, while 422 thousand 501 positions were generated in the informal sector.

In contrast, for men, employment was more dynamic in the third month of the year. 419 thousand 200 positions were created compared to March 2025, of which 337 thousand 60 were in the formal sector, while 82 thousand 140 positions were created in the informal sector.

Meanwhile, in the year-to-date accumulation, the employment loss for women was 129 thousand 536 positions, compared to 97 thousand 619 positions for men.
