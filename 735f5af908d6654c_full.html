# Central Banks Send "Message" on War (And It's Not Good)

Six central banks will meet between April 28 and 30 to discuss the economic impacts of the Middle East war, which has now lasted two months. The focus is on inflation caused by rising oil prices, especially due to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Economists warn that the situation is recessionary and inflationary, requiring central banks to decide whether to allow economic activity to decline with more or less inflation. In Brazil, a 0.25 percentage point cut in interest rates is expected, but with a warning that easing will be more cautious. Stagflation is a real concern, and the inflation target is being discussed, especially as elections approach. The new Fed president, Kevin Warsh, may also influence monetary decisions in the US. The situation is complex, with significant challenges for the Brazilian Central Bank and the global economy.

In a busy final week of April with fiscal data and labor market releases in Brazil, within 72 hours, six central banks will deliver their message on the economic impacts of the Middle East conflict completing two months. And it is far from ending, despite the indefinite ceasefire announced by Donald Trump. In chronological order, from April 28 to 30, the Bank of Japan, Federal Reserve, Central Bank of Brazil, Bank of Canada, European Central Bank, and Bank of England will discuss monetary policy. The US-Iran war does not tend to impose classical coordinated action on monetary decisions. But central banks are indeed, with their "fingers on the trigger" to contain inflationary effects of the oil escalation. And the adoption of more conservative rhetoric is on the script.

The imperative condition for decisions that coincidentally concentrate at the end of April is the oil supply shock represented by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and damage of all kinds to production structures, especially oil and gas. The interruption of commodity flow, even if intermittent as seen in recent weeks, maintains pressure on prices and logistics costs that result in more widespread inflation and prospects for declining economic growth. "The supply shock is recessionary and inflationary," observes Gino Olivares, chief economist at Azimut Brasil Wealth Management. "Central banks are left to manage inflation because the oil increase and its scarcity will cause economic activity to fall. An example of this effect is the impact on air transport."

Flight reductions are inevitable and ticket price increases likewise. As for flights, monetary policy cannot do anything. Therefore, the choice to be made by central banks and governments is whether activity will fall with more or with less inflation." In an interview with NeoFeed, Olivares warns that the conflict is not an isolated shock. "From 2000 to now, we've had the pandemic, a supply and demand shock; the invasion of Ukraine, a supply shock; Trump's tariffs, another supply shock just a year ago; and, two months ago, the outbreak of war in Iran." The shocks may be temporary, Olivares adds, but economies have taken blow after blow in five years with no time for recovery. In the post-pandemic period, the economist recalls, inflation soared, central banks reacted, raised rates and inflation subsided, but did not return to targets. Therefore, the Iran war arrived without the central banks' work being finished.

Olivares, also a professor at Insper and former economist at the Economic Studies Management of Peru's Central Bank, assesses that the European Central Bank and the Bank of England are first in line to raise rates. European economies were most affected by the shocks and monetary cycles were completed or nearly so. In Brazil, Olivares expects a 0.25 percentage point cut on the 29th for the institution to honor its commitment to the "calibration" cycle of monetary policy. However, Copom should make clear that the bar has been raised for reductions. "The March cut actually occurred because of the commitment made by Gabriel Galípolo from January to March. And he delivers what he promises to build and maintain the institution's credibility. The March decision to maintain the risk balance, despite worsening, was to avoid an explanation for the Selic reduction," he says.

Olivares recalls, however, that in Washington, where he participated in the IMF and World Bank meeting, director Paulo Picchetti acknowledged that the risk balance was aggravated. "It's a signal. The reduction of another 0.25 percentage point should occur, but the pace of easing will change. And the outlook is what Focus points to: by year-end, the Selic will be higher than estimated before the war." Also likely to be veiled or explicitly noted in central banks' communications is the stagflation scenario, seen as a real threat by monetary authorities that currently practice the following basic rates: Bank of Japan, 0.75%; Federal Reserve (Fed), 3.5% to 3.75%; Central Bank of Brazil, 14.75%; Bank of Canada, 2.25%; European Central Bank (ECB), 2%; and Bank of England, 3.75%.

The Fed, which should maintain its rate, is going through a peculiar moment. It will change presidents on May 15, with command passing from Jerome Powell to Kevin Warsh if he, nominated by Donald Trump, is approved by the US Senate. In hearings on Tuesday the 21st, Warsh defended Fed independence, said Trump never asked him to commit to rate cuts, and showed concern about the inflation target. However, simultaneously, Trump again criticized Powell for being "behind on rate cuts" and said he expects different behavior from his successor.

In Brazil, concern about the target emerges in a document prepared by the PT for the party's 8th National Congress, starting on the 24th. Among the proposals that in theory should underpin the economic program for a Lula 4.0 administration is reviewing the inflation target, delimiting Central Bank autonomy, changing the terms of its board which would again coincide with the presidential term, and keeping interest rates below 10%. A difficult situation for Galípolo to manage in the coming months leading up to the election and another two years ahead if Lula is reelected. Although a government program doesn't ruffle financial markets, after a long period of silence, Lula has already expressed displeasure with the pace of Selic reductions. An obstacle to rebalancing the financial lives of thousands of indebted Brazilians—but voters.
