# Central Banks Send "Message" on War (and It's Not Good News)

Six central banks will meet between April 28 and 30 to discuss the economic impacts of the Middle East war, which will have lasted two months. The focus is on inflation caused by rising oil prices, especially due to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Economists warn that the situation is both recessionary and inflationary, requiring central banks to decide whether to allow economic activity to fall with more or less inflation. In Brazil, a 0.25 percentage point cut in interest rates is expected, but with a warning that easing will be more cautious. Stagflation is a real concern, and the inflation target is being discussed, especially with elections approaching. The new Fed chair, Kevin Warsh, may also influence monetary decisions in the US. The situation is complex, with significant challenges for Brazil's Central Bank and the global economy.

In the last week of April with a packed agenda of fiscal data and labor market figures in Brazil, within 72 hours, six central banks will deliver their message on the economic impacts of the Middle East conflict that will complete two months. And it is far from over, despite the indefinite ceasefire announced by Donald Trump. In chronological order, from April 28 to 30, the Bank of Japan, Federal Reserve, Central Bank of Brazil, Bank of Canada, European Central Bank, and Bank of England will discuss monetary policy. The US-Iran war does not tend to impose classic coordinated action on monetary decisions. But central banks are indeed "finger on the trigger" to contain inflationary effects of the oil escalation. And adopting more conservative rhetoric is in the script.

The imperative condition for decisions coincidentally concentrated at the end of April is the oil supply shock represented by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and damage of all kinds to oil and gas production structures. The interruption of the commodity flow, even if intermittent as seen in recent weeks, maintains pressure on prices and logistics costs that result in more widespread inflation and prospects for falling economic growth.

"The supply shock is recessionary and inflationary," observes Gino Olivares, chief economist at Azimut Brasil Wealth Management. "Central banks are left to manage inflation because the rise in oil and its scarcity will cause economic activity to fall. An example of this effect is the impact on air transport. The reduction in flights is inevitable, and the increase in fares is the same. As for flights, monetary policy can do nothing. Therefore, the choice to be made by central banks and governments is whether activity will fall with more or less inflation."

In an interview with NeoFeed, Olivares warns that the conflict is not an isolated shock. "From 2000 onwards, we have had the pandemic, a supply and demand shock; the invasion of Ukraine, a supply shock; Trump's tariffs, another supply shock just a year ago; and, two months ago, the outbreak of war with Iran." The shocks may be temporary, adds Olivares, but economies have been hit one punch after another in five years without time for recovery. After the pandemic, the economist notes, inflation surged, central banks reacted, raised rates, and inflation subsided, but did not return to targets. Therefore, the Iran war arrived without the central banks' work being finished.

Olivares, also a professor at Insper and former economist at the Economic Studies Management of Peru's Central Bank, assesses that the European Central Bank and Bank of England are first in line to raise rates. European economies were the hardest hit by shocks and monetary cycles were completed or nearly so. In Brazil, Olivares expects a 0.25 percentage point cut on April 29 for the institution to honor its commitment to the "calibration" cycle of monetary policy. However, the Copom should make clear that the bar has been raised for reductions. "The March cut actually occurred due to the commitment made by Gabriel Galípolo from January to March. And he delivers what he promises to build and maintain the institution's credibility. The March decision to maintain the risk balance, despite deterioration, was to avoid an explanation for the Selic reduction," he says.

Olivares recalls, however, that in Washington, where he participated in the IMF and World Bank meeting, director Paulo Picchetti acknowledged that the risk balance was aggravated. "It is a signal. The reduction of another 0.25 percentage point should occur, but the pace of easing will be altered. And the outlook is what Focus points to: by year-end, the Selic will be higher than estimated before the war."

The stagflation scenario, viewed as a real threat by monetary authorities, should also be veiled or explicitly pointed out in central bank communications. Currently, the following basic rates are being practiced: Bank of Japan, 0.75%; Federal Reserve (Fed), 3.5% to 3.75%; Brazil's Central Bank, 14.75%; Bank of Canada, 2.25%; European Central Bank (ECB), 2%; and Bank of England, 3.75%.

The Fed, which is expected to maintain its rate, is going through a peculiar moment. It will change presidents on May 15, with Jerome Powell being replaced by Kevin Warsh if he, nominated by Donald Trump, is approved by the US Senate. In a hearing on Tuesday the 21st, Warsh defended Fed independence, said Trump never asked for a commitment to rate cuts, and expressed concern about the inflation target. However, simultaneously, Trump again criticized Powell for being "late on rate cuts" and said he expects different behavior from his successor.

In Brazil, concern about the target springs from a document prepared by the PT for the party's 8th National Congress, starting Friday the 24th. Among the proposals that are supposed to underpin the economic program for a Lula 4.0 administration is reviewing the inflation target, limiting Central Bank autonomy, changing the terms of its board members that would again coincide with the presidential term, and keeping rates below 10%. A thorny issue for Galípolo to untangle in the coming months before the election and another two years ahead if Lula is reelected.

Although a government program does not tickle the financial market, after a long period of silence, Lula has already expressed displeasure with the pace of Selic reductions. An obstacle to rebalancing the financial lives of thousands of indebted Brazilians – but voters.
