# Central Banks Send "Message" About the War (and It Is Not Good News)

Six central banks will meet between April 28 and 30 to discuss the economic impacts of the Middle East war, which has already lasted two months. The focus is on inflation caused by rising oil prices, especially due to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Economists warn that the situation is recessionary and inflationary, with central banks needing to decide whether to allow economic activity to fall with more or less inflation. In Brazil, a 0.25 percentage point cut in interest rates is expected, but with a warning that easing will be more cautious. The stagflation scenario is a real concern, and the inflation target is being discussed, especially with elections approaching. The new Fed president, Kevin Warsh, could also influence monetary decisions in the US. The situation is complex, with significant challenges for the Brazilian Central Bank and the global economy.

In a busy final week of April with fiscal data and labor market releases in Brazil, in 72 hours, six central banks will deliver their message on the economic impacts of the Middle East conflict that will complete two months. And it is far from over, despite the indefinite ceasefire announced by Donald Trump.

In chronological order, the Bank of Japan, Federal Reserve, Brazilian Central Bank, Bank of Canada, European Central Bank, and Bank of England will discuss monetary policy from April 28 to 30. The US-Iran war does not tend to impose classical coordinated action on monetary decisions. But central banks are indeed "trigger-happy" to contain inflationary effects of escalating oil prices. And adopting a more conservative tone is on the script.

The imperative condition for decisions coincidentally concentrated at the end of April is the oil supply shock represented by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and damage of all kinds to production structures, especially oil and gas. The interruption of commodity flow, even if intermittent as seen in recent weeks, maintains pressure on prices and logistics costs that result in more widespread inflation and prospects for economic growth decline.

"The supply shock is recessionary and inflationary," observes Gino Olivares, chief economist at Azimut Brasil Wealth Management. "Central banks are left to care about inflation because the rise in oil and its scarcity will cause activity to fall. An example of this effect is the impact on air transport." The reduction in flights is inevitable and the rise in fares as well. As for flights, monetary policy can do nothing. Therefore, the choice to be made, by central banks and governments, is whether activity will fall with more or less inflation."

In an interview with NeoFeed, Olivares warns that the conflict is not an isolated shock. "From 2000 onwards, we had the pandemic, a supply and demand shock; the invasion of Ukraine, a supply shock; Trump's tariff increase, another supply shock just a year ago; and, two months ago, the outbreak of war in Iran." The shocks may be transitory, adds Olivares, but economies have taken one punch after another in five years without time to recover. In the post-pandemic period, the economist recalls, inflation soared, central banks reacted, raised rates and inflation eased, but did not return to targets. Therefore, the Iran war arrived without the work of the central banks being finished.

Inflation Target

Also a professor at Insper and former economist at the Economics Studies Division of the Central Bank of Peru, Olivares assesses that the European Central Bank and the Bank of England are first in line to raise rates. European economies were the hardest hit by shocks and monetary cycles were completed or almost. In Brazil, Olivares' expectation is that a 0.25 percentage point cut will occur on April 29 for the institution to honor its commitment to the "calibration" cycle of monetary policy. However, the Copom should make clear that the bar has been raised for reductions.

"The March cut actually occurred because of Gabriel Galípolo's commitment from January to March. And he delivers what he promises to build and maintain the institution's credibility. The March decision to maintain the risk balance, despite the deterioration, was to avoid an explanation for the Selic reduction," he says.

Olivares recalls, however, that in Washington, where he participated in the IMF and World Bank meeting, director Paulo Picchetti acknowledged that the risk balance had worsened. "It's a signal. The reduction of another 0.25 percentage point should occur, but the pace of easing will change. And the prospect is what Focus points to: by year-end, the Selic will be higher than estimated before the war."

The stagflation scenario should also be veiled or explicitly pointed out in the central banks' communications, seen as a real threat by monetary authorities that currently practice the following basic rates: Bank of Japan, 0.75%; Federal Reserve (Fed), 3.5% to 3.75%; Brazilian Central Bank, 14.75%; Bank of Canada, 2.25%; European Central Bank (ECB), 2%; and Bank of England, 3.75%.

The Fed, which is expected to maintain its rate, is going through a peculiar moment. It will change presidents on May 15, with command passing from Jerome Powell to Kevin Warsh if he, nominated by Donald Trump, is approved by the US Senate. At a hearing on Tuesday the 21st, Warsh defended the Fed's independence, said Trump never asked him to commit to interest rate cuts, and expressed concern about the inflation target. However, simultaneously, Trump again criticized Powell for being "late in cutting rates" and said he expects different behavior from his successor.

In Brazil, concern about the target springs from a document prepared by the PT for the party's 8th National Congress, starting on Friday the 24th. Among the proposals that, in theory, should underpin the economic program for Lula 4.0 administration, is reviewing the inflation target, delimiting the Central Bank's autonomy, changing the mandates of its board members that would again coincide with the presidential term, and keeping interest rates below 10%. A tough challenge for Galípolo to navigate in the coming months leading up to the election and two more years in charge if Lula is reelected.

Although a government program does not tickle the financial market, after a long silence Lula has already expressed displeasure with the pace of Selic rate reductions. An obstacle to rebalancing the financial lives of thousands of indebted Brazilians—but voters.
