# Agis Construtora Completes Line 17-Gold Metro Project; Eyes New Infrastructure Challenges

The Agis Construtora has solved a major urban mobility problem in São Paulo that lasted over a decade: the completion of Line 17-Gold, which connects to Congonhas Airport. The project's original deadline was for the 2014 FIFA World Cup held in Brazil.

The construction company, which belongs to businessman Eduardo Capobianco from a family with a tradition in civil construction, took over the construction of Line 17-Gold in August 2023 to resume the stalled work. The São Paulo State Government's promise at the time was to serve tourists attending the games. It was finally completed almost three World Cups later.

After completing the nearly seven-kilometer metro line with seven stations, from the Morumbi terminal to the airport, Agis has now begun its next challenge: the first phase of the Intercities Train between Jundiaí and Campinas. The company will be responsible for the initial stretch of approximately 50 kilometers.

Governor Tarcísio de Freitas participated on Wednesday, April 8, in the ceremony marking the start of construction. Agis was hired by the TIC Trains consortium, formed by the Comporte group and China's CRRC, which won the bidding in February 2024 in an auction held at B3.

The total project, which aims to connect São Paulo to Campinas, will require R$ 14.2 billion in investment with operations expected to begin in 2031. In this phase, Agis will execute earthmoving and physical infrastructure works (bridges and overpasses). The deadline for the work under the contractor's responsibility is 21 months.

"Since the Comporte group does not execute works, they go to the market and contract companies with the capacity to perform these activities," says José Lima, president of Agis Construtora, in an interview with NeoFeed.

After completing this service, the concessionaire will make new hiring for the permanent track work, adjacent to the existing railway line on the route. Agis also intends to participate in new bids.

The Intercidades Train project will help the company reach R$ 2 billion in revenue in 2026, representing a 67% increase over 2025 revenue of R$ 1.2 billion. At least 40% of the company's revenue comes from public contracts. The remaining 60% comes from corporate contracts, including models like the Intercidades Train, which, despite being public works, is contracted by a private company.

Although public power contracts have clauses that guarantee monetary correction, it is challenging, according to Agis's president, to predict the impact of scenarios with high interest rates, particularly when dealing with a Selic rate of 15% per annum for much of last year.

"This is not easy. Operating with a 15% rate is higher than the contract's own margin. Just as there is rigor in the technical engineering aspect, we were also careful about financial matters. The impacts of a project this size are significant because we need to pay beforehand," Lima explains.

At the Line 17-Gold inauguration event on March 31, Tarcísio also announced studies for the monorail expansion with an additional 4.6 kilometers and four more stations, including one in the Paraisópolis community. There is no timeline yet. Agis is also expected to participate in this new bid.

**Brazil's 'Second 7-1'**

In practice, the World Cup in Brazil left two embarrassing moments: the 7-1 defeat to Germany and the failure to meet the Line 17-Gold delivery deadline, which was part of a major logistics plan designed as a global showcase and legacy of the competition.

"As a citizen, it was frustrating to see that unfinished work on Avenida Roberto Marinho near Congonhas. But as an executive, it was a great opportunity for us to meet this challenge and deliver this project to society," Lima says.

When the company took over, at least 40% of the work still needed to be completed. But in practice, nothing was ready. It was an open-air skeleton on one of São Paulo's most important traffic corridors, crossing Brooklin and the surroundings of Avenida Luís Carlos Berrini.

"Taking over a leftover mobility project is much more difficult. Everything had been started and nothing finished. No roads, no stations, nothing. We had to touch everything to deliver on time," Lima explains.

Another issue was ensuring minimal impact on São Paulo traffic during the final phase, particularly with concrete beams, some weighing more than 100 tons, installed at a height of about 15 meters where stations are located.

"The traffic interference had to be minimal. We even closed Marginal Pinheiros on a Friday night to install one curved beam and had to reopen it by Sunday. The responsibility was enormous," the executive recalls.

According to Lima, one of the main challenges in completing the monorail was meeting all technical specifications for the correct "fit" of trains supplied by Chinese manufacturer BYD, marking the carmaker's first venture into this type of monorail in Brazil.

"It was integrated engineering with the rolling stock owner. The tolerance was minimal—a maximum of three millimeters. We held weekly meetings to adjust everything necessary. It worked well," he explains.

This was not the first unfinished work—taking over a decade to complete—that Agis took over. The company also participated in the construction of the São Francisco River Transposition in the Northeast.

"Both carry similarity in the symbolism and importance of these works for their regions. They also had this characteristic of being leftover projects. We completed the water passage to Ceará in 2022. It was also a great challenge," Lima recalls.

Although delivered and already operating under the initial management of São Paulo Metro, Agis will still complete the final station of this Line 17 phase, Washington Luís. According to the president, the remaining physical work will be delivered within 90 days. The Água Espraiada yard will also be completed.
