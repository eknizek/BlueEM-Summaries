The Ping-Pong of Gasoline in Brazilian Ports

23 April 2026 · 10:50 PM

# The Ping-Pong of Gasoline in Brazilian Ports

The war between Iran and the United States and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz generated a significant increase in gasoline imports through the Port of Santos, which quadrupled its volume in March, with a growth of 278% compared to February. The movement of fuels in Brazilian terminals also grew, totaling 134,914 tons in March. The rise in oil prices and the appreciation of the real made imported gasoline more attractive. The ANP implemented measures to guarantee supply and monitor prices. The Port Authority prioritized the docking of ships with fuel, reducing waiting times.

March was a month marked by speculation and increased fuel imports in the domestic market. The period was also characterized by coastal shipping, with intense movement between local ports to transfer barrels of oil. This "ping-pong" effect of fuel is explained by the real risk of fuel shortages in Brazil, especially in the Southeast region, due to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz caused by the war between Iran and the United States.

An example of this movement is seen in the numbers from the Port of Santos, which practically quadrupled its gasoline import volume compared to February's result, with a 278% increase in the quantity unloaded. When looking at coastal shipping, Latin America's largest port complex registered an increase of 11.5%.

Data from the Santos Port Authority (APS) shows that last month, 134,709 tons of gasoline arrived in Santos, compared to 36,575 tons imported in February. Among Brazilian port terminals, fuel movement also grew, precisely to meet this demand, serve the national market, and avert the danger of shortages at gas stations. There were 134,914 tons in March, compared to 120,934 tons registered in February.

"There was great speculation that generated a spike in oil prices and the risk of the Strait remaining closed. These factors caused a rush to quickly unload gasoline to supply the Brazilian market," says Anderson Pomini, president of the Santos Port Authority (APS), to NeoFeed.

Another point raised by the executive to explain the volume increase was the recent appreciation of the real against the dollar, which, in practice, made the imported product relatively more attractive, especially at a moment of uncertainty about supply guarantees.

In March, the National Agency for Petroleum, Natural Gas and Biofuels (ANP) adopted a series of measures to guarantee gasoline and diesel stocks in the country. Among them, it implemented stricter monitoring of volumes for each distributor and oversight of possible price abuses charged to consumers. The agency's data show that in 2025, Brazil recorded a 27.6% increase in gasoline import volume. Last year, all fuel movement at the Port of Santos, including loading and unloading of gasoline, fuel oil, and diesel, was 9.6 million tons. In just the first three months of 2026, three million tons have already passed through the terminals.

"Beyond Petrobras, many other independent fuel suppliers, who sell to unbranded gas stations, also increased their orders. What we perceived was a drive to increase supply and ensure market security," says Pomini.

The cargo arriving at the Port of Santos comes from various locations, such as the United States, Russia, and the Middle East itself. In practice, orders were accelerated so that the impact on prices would be minimal.

Beyond the direct growth of vessels in Santos over the last 30 days due to market necessity, the Port Authority adopted a priority system for docking ships carrying fuel. In practice, this movement causes the vessel to "skip the queue" and reduce the average time it waits in the anchorage area for a berth to be released. On average, a ship takes about three days to dock. Now, those carrying petroleum derivatives barely wait.

According to the port director, the trend is that this accelerated pace will be maintained at least until the end of the geopolitical conflict. Today, the Port of Santos has 66 docking berths to receive all cargo, including grains, containers, liquid bulk, and fuels.

"We had cases of preference requests. At this moment, fuel unloading at the Port of Santos is a priority. No one knows what could happen regarding the war," says the APS president.

Pomini believes that with this maritime rush, the trend is for revenue in the Santos port terminal to show growth in the first quarter, not yet finalized. "Only the Port Authority can break the queue when there is public interest and a situation that could affect the country's economy. That's what we're experiencing right now."

A few weeks before the start of the tariff increases imposed by U.S. President Donald Trump in April last year, the Santos port faced a rush of ships, but in the opposite direction from now. "We're seeing something similar, but in the other direction. Back then, meat exporters didn't want to be impacted by the new import tax. And now companies want to guarantee gasoline stocks while waiting for the war to end," says Pomini.

In the first quarter of 2026, cargo movement in Santos reached 42.8 million tons, combining all products. The volume is higher than what was achieved in the entire year of 1999, with 42.7 million tons. The Santos terminal currently moves almost 30% of all Brazilian commercial cargo. China was the main partner. The value transacted between January and March with the Asian country was US$ 13 billion, compared to US$ 4.4 billion from the United States.

Last year, the Santos Port Authority closed with revenue of R$ 1.4 billion and R$ 4 billion in cash.
