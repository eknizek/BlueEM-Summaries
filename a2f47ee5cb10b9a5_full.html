# Ten Oil Spills Detected in Gulf of Mexico Over Past 7 Months

Satellite images in the Gulf of Mexico have recorded a series of hydrocarbon spills, in several cases involving unidentified vessels ("dark vessels"). SkyTruth reported at least 10 events between August 2025 and March 2026, with records occurring practically monthly and from diverse sources ranging from fully identified ships to vessels with no active signal and possible offshore oil infrastructure.

The first identified case was documented on August 6, 2025, when a 12-kilometer slick was detected in deep waters of the Gulf, associated with the ship MTM Amazon. It is an oil and chemical tanker currently in the Gulf of Mexico under Singapore flag.

From that point on, events occurred in succession. On September 27, another 34-kilometer slick was linked to the German vessel BBC NILE off Veracruz coasts. On October 17, satellite images detected a 26-square-kilometer slick associated with a "dark vessel"—an unidentified ship with an estimated length of 380 meters.

By November 30, a 34-kilometer spill was linked to the Mexican ship Seacor Viking, and on December 6, the platform identified a 13-kilometer slick associated with the Carnival Valor cruise ship, expanding the spectrum of involved vessels. Eight days later, on December 14, another 18-kilometer slick was detected linked to the XENA vessel, with Panamanian flag.

On January 2, 2026, a 15-kilometer slick was associated with the Mexican ship Amparo Paola off Veracruz, and on February 14, 2026, the platform documented two separate events on the same day in the Campeche Sound. One was linked to offshore infrastructure with a 43-square-kilometer slick, and another associated with a "dark vessel" with a 53-kilometer trail. The coincidence of both events in the same zone and date broke the hypothesis of a single origin and pointed to simultaneous spills in a strategically important oil region.

The most recent record is from March 3, 2026, when a 7-kilometer slick was detected associated with the Nordic Ann vessel, under Danish flag.

Most events are linked to fully identified vessels, but the presence of ships with no active signal introduces a component of operational opacity, while evidence of infrastructure opens the possibility of failures or leaks in oil installations. The events are concentrated in the Campeche Sound and off the coasts of Veracruz, Tabasco, and Tamaulipas, a key zone for hydrocarbon production and transport in Mexico, where intensive maritime traffic, energy infrastructure, and possible irregular offshore operations converge.
