# Wine: Management adjustment at Berkmann Brasil importer favors Italian and Spanish wineries

The departure of Argentine executive Mariano Levy from his position as CEO of wine importer Berkmann Brasil has led the English parent company to change how it manages its Brazilian subsidiary. The decision is not to have a CEO in the short and medium term in Brazil and to increase the presence of English professionals Rupert Berkmann, president of Berkmann Global, and Charles Marshall, Strategy Director, in Brazil. The objective is to maintain the main wineries in its Brazilian portfolio, focusing on Italian Marquesi Antinori and Spanish Familia Torres, which were dissatisfied with the results of their wines in Brazil.

The decision was announced by Marshall himself, who advanced his trip to Brazil by a week to prevent Levy's departure from becoming a crisis at the company and in the market. He will spend the next three weeks here, already in this new action plan. "Both Rupert and I will take turns and spend more time here in Brazil," the executive states.

As Estadão reported earlier, Levy anticipated the official announcement and last weekend posted on his social networks notice of his departure with thanks to partners, suppliers and clients. With the repercussions, the post was taken down.

Marshall, who would have announced Levy's departure online to clients and suppliers, got on a plane and came to Brazil in person. In person, he guaranteed that Antinori will not leave Berkmann's Brazilian operation portfolio. According to him, this was agreed in an in-person meeting between the main executives of Antinori, Renzo Cotarella, president of the winery, and Stefano Leone, commercial director, with Rupert Berkmann and Marshall himself, 15 days ago, during Vinitaly, an important Italian wine fair held in Verona.

Antinori has a 30-year relationship with English Berkmann, a company that currently bills £116 million (about R$675 million, at Thursday's exchange rate, the 30th) per year — the Brazilian subsidiary has revenue of R$74 million. It arrived in the Brazilian portfolio in 2019. The Italians were not satisfied with the positioning and market prices of their wines in Brazil, despite 60% growth in sales here during Levy's management. "The Brazilian subsidiary had problems with its financial situation and cash flow pressure," explained Marshall. This led Berkmann to review its portfolio, including discontinuing labels that did not perform well in Brazil.

## Focus on the on-trade wine market

According to Marshall, the focus remains on the on-trade wine market, an English term that defines bars, restaurants and hotels, and if necessary the parent company can invest to help the Brazilian subsidiary. He also guaranteed the presence of other Berkmann executives, such as Saler Talger, commercial director; Heitor Soubihe, operations director, and Jessica Marinzheck, head of marketing and products. Marshall also stated that Brazil remains challenging for the English, especially due to regulatory and tax terms. "Now it's about investing in brands and recovering margins," he commented.

The most important wineries in the Brazilian portfolio are, besides Antinori and Torres, Chilean Montes, Argentine Riccitelli, French Simonnet-Febvre and Italian Allegrini.

Levy had assumed the presidency of Berkmann six months ago, when his non-compete contract with the Víssimo group expired, resulting from the merger of importers Grand Cru and Evino. The Argentine executive arrived in Brazil in 2002, then to found the Grand Cru importer, which currently integrates the Víssimo group. Levy will now dedicate himself to his importer, Vinessence, which he has in partnership with sommelier Massimo Leoncini.
