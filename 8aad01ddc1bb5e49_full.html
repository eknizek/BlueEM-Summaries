# Why Aegea is Delaying Publication of Its Balance Sheet

Aegea's sanitation bonds have been under pressure in recent weeks after the company postponed the publication of its fourth quarter balance sheet multiple times — fueling market speculation and triggering credit rating downgrades by rating agencies. The situation has drawn attention to one of Brazil's largest infrastructure players at a moment when Aegea is preparing for an IPO in the next market window. The company — controlled by the Equipav group and with GIC as a shareholder holding 34% of capital and Itaúsa with 13% — is also among the companies evaluating the privatization of Copasa.

Sources familiar with the matter told Brazil Journal that the balance sheet delays are related to KPMG, which has audited the company for two years. The audit firm recently changed its interpretation of various accounting topics in the company's income statement — leading to the reopening of the company's balance sheets since 2020. One of these sources said that the accounting impact of these revisions is in the "billions of reais," but that there is no cash effect, being purely "accounting matters."

According to this source, the revisions involve issues such as "revenue recognition in some concessionaires, provisions in others, and the way the company accounted for grant interest." This source also stated that financially, the company is in a "very solid" position, with more than R$ 5 billion in holding cash and no major near-term maturities.

The uncertainties from the multiple balance sheet delays have had harmful effects on the company's credit. S&P and Fitch recently downgraded Aegea's rating to speculative levels (junk) and placed the company under review for possible further downgrades. S&P also warned that Aegea has until today, the 10th, to publish its balance sheets, under penalty of declaring a default event on some of its debentures, which in turn could trigger cross-default clauses on bonds listed abroad.

A KPMG accounting team is allocated at Aegea, racing against time to deliver the balance sheets by end of business today, said one of the sources. The debentures that would be impacted by this default event total around R$ 500-700 million. One alternative would be for Aegea to use its cash to pay off these debts before the early maturity is declared (avoiding cross-default), said people familiar with the company's plans.

KPMG, which audited Banco Master's balance sheets through December 2024, is under scrutiny for not signaling any problems in the numbers, which led to greater involvement of the global firm in the Brazilian operation. This scenario appears to have led the firm to be more conservative in auditing its clients' balance sheets, according to one of the sources.

The partner responsible for auditing Aegea's balance sheet was replaced shortly after the company's latest bond issuance in September, when Aegea raised US$ 750 million with a green bond.

Aegea's bonds maturing in 2036 — which are the most liquid — are being traded today near 70% of face value. At the end of March, they were trading at 92%. Meanwhile, securities maturing in 2029 and 2031 were trading today near 80% of face value.
