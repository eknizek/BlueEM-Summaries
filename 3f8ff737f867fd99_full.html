# Soy continues rally in international markets amid Middle East tensions; local market impact

The new escalation in the war in the Middle East, pitting the United States and Israel against Iran, has further pressured international agricultural commodity values, which continue their upward trend as the conflict remains unresolved and the Strait of Hormuz stays closed.

During today's session, soy presented the largest increases: in the Chicago market, the July soybean contract rose $7 to $449.1 per ton, while oil advanced $31.52 to $1,688.43 and meal rose $1.65 to $353.62 per ton. Cereals also closed higher on the North American exchange: corn registered an improvement of $2.07 to $191.13 per ton, while wheat rose $1.29 to $235.62 per ton.

The reasons explaining this new increase stem from two main issues: the jump in oil prices due to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz and expectations arising from a new meeting between U.S. President Donald Trump and his Chinese counterpart, Xi Jinping.

In this regard, market analyst at the Rosario Stock Exchange (BCR), Matías Contardi, detailed that "negotiations were expected to eventually open the strait at some point, and yet, since we began to have the pause (ceasefire), attacks continued. In reality, there was no clear direction toward immediate opening or toward negotiations that would reach fruition."

"So, today, the market is basically in a cloud of general uncertainty," Contardi stated, a situation that again drives up oil prices, with values around $112 per barrel of Brent crude, a price at maximums since the conflict began.

Thus, the specialist indicated that the current situation "is a fairly complex scenario. Energy prices have been dragging down vegetable oils, because increasingly these function as an energy input, as they are used to produce biofuels." With oil at highs since mid-2022 and with an upward trend continuing, "it pulls the soybean price up significantly. Uncertainty grows, energy prices rise, dragging oil prices, and in this way its fundamental input, soybean. And to top it off, everything related to agro commodities is a good refuge for speculative funds."

On the other hand, analysts at the Granar brokerage indicated that "given the imminence of Trump's trip to China – May 14-15 – to meet with his Chinese counterpart Xi Jinping, traders began to speculate about possible agreements that would favor agricultural trade."

## Domestic Market

However, the rise in soy values in Chicago is not reflected in improved prices in the local market, due to the abundant supply that is facilitating harvest operations. In fact, the price for available soy fell in Rosario by 6% in the last three weeks, standing at $305 per ton.

"It was to be expected that it would drop significantly due to harvest pressure," explained to Clarín Rural market analyst and financial advisor Mariela Brandolin. In this sense, she noted that "many trucks are entering the port and producers are accelerating harvest because rainy days are coming, trying to take advantage of current favorable weather conditions. Clearly, as more trucks arrive at the port, this ends up impacting the price downward."

The case of corn and wheat is different, as both local prices have shown increases for weeks: in the case of the former, the increase was 7% in April to $190 per ton, while in the latter the improvement was 10%, closing last month at $205 for available merchandise.

For Brandolin, while there was a "small harvest pressure at the end of February and beginning of March, afterward the corn price began to rebound, because the producer focused on trying to harvest soy and set the cereal aside, in a context where there are many ships to load corn, which is keeping demand firm."

Regarding wheat, Brandolin explained: "we currently have a value that rebounded quite a bit, from $160 it had in December (full harvest), today we have it at $205 and this is basically a bit due to the rise in price at the international level and demand that has also been improving."
