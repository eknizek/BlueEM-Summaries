# Why I Changed My Mind About the Energy Transition

Until recently, conservative circles maintained a skeptical view of the energy transition, often perceived as excessively associated with ideological narratives and less grounded in economic or strategic fundamentals. This reading, which I also shared, seemed reasonable given the dominant debate at the time. Recent events – particularly the war involving Iran – impose, however, a reassessment of this perspective.

The war exposed, once again, a structural fragility of the global economy: critical dependence on energy from geopolitically unstable regions. The debate on energy transition, frequently treated as an environmental or ideological agenda, needs to be repositioned. It is no longer about political preference. It is about risk management.

Few points illustrate this vulnerability as clearly as the Strait of Hormuz. Approximately 20% of all oil consumed in the world passes daily through this narrow route, located in one of the most volatile regions of the planet. It is a true bottleneck of the global energy system: any interruption, even partial, has the immediate potential to disrupt markets, pressure prices, and generate inflationary effects on a global scale.

Recent events in the Middle East show that this risk is far from theoretical. Tensions involving Iran call into question not only regional stability but the functioning of entire supply chains around the world. Oil, in this context, ceases to be merely a commodity and becomes an instrument of power – and potentially of coercion.

This reality raises an uncomfortable but inevitable question: is it rational that global economic growth depends, to a large extent, on regions under the influence of unstable or ideologically radicalized regimes? From a risk management perspective, the answer is clearly no.

It is in light of all this that the energy transition needs to be reinterpreted. For a long time, it was treated as an environmental agenda or associated with progressive causes. While relevant, this framing is limited. Diversification of the energy matrix should be understood first and foremost as a strategy for reducing systemic risk. Reducing dependence on oil – especially from sensitive regions – means decreasing exposure to external shocks beyond the control of national economies. It also means reducing the capacity of regional actors to use energy as an instrument of global pressure.

Beyond the geopolitical dimension, there is a direct impact on social stability. Shocks in oil prices quickly translate into inflation, increased transportation costs, food, and energy. This effect is regressive and affects the most vulnerable populations with greater intensity. In other words, energy dependence is not just economic risk: it is a factor of social instability.

The energy transition should not be seen as an abrupt rupture, but as a strategic process of diversification. Renewable energies, energy efficiency, and new technologies are not only environmental alternatives; they are instruments of economic and political resilience. More than that, the current debate is no longer limited to the substitution of energy sources, but expands to a broader agenda of reconfiguring supply chains, including movements of onshoring and regionalization, as well as the development of new frontiers of energy production – including within the oil industry itself.

The pursuit of energy security thus walks hand in hand with the expansion of industrial autonomy and the reduction of exposure to concentrated external risks. Historically, energy shocks have redefined global geopolitics. The current scenario indicates that we are facing one of these moments – with the difference that, this time, there is a viable path to reduce this structural vulnerability.

In the end, the question is not environmental or ideological – it is one of structural vulnerability. The energy transition, in this context, ceases to be a future agenda and becomes an immediate strategy of economic protection and social stability.

Edison Ticle is CFO of Minerva Foods.
