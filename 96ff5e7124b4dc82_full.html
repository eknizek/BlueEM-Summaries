# From Pergola to Luxury: Brazil's Best-Selling Wine Finances Campestre's Premium Bet

The Pergola table wine has just completed 12 years as Brazil's best-selling wine. With 45 million liters sold annually, growth of more than 10%, and annual revenues exceeding R$ 500 million, the simple and affordable beverage has become the foundation for diversification in Vinícola Campestre's business, with an eye toward the long term and leadership in fine wines as well. In 2014, the same year it achieved leadership in the national market, entrepreneur João Zanotto purchased a deactivated slaughterhouse in Vacaria, in the Rio Grande do Sul highlands, and began transforming it into a new winery for Campestre, the brand's owner.

This marked the true beginning of a new project focused on fine wines of greater prestige, surfing on the strength and revenue of the sweet and simple beverage that conquered millions of consumers. The decision to enter the premium segment did not come because of success in cheap drinks. Years before starting the new winery, the company was already testing and producing fine wines for its own consumption, and in 2015 came the decision to transform production into a commercial product.

"We hope to balance the market, have the market of one to begin in the other," Zanotto told Bloomberg Línea during a visit to the winery in Vacaria.

Among vineyard strolls, tastings and even the experience of treading grapes, the CEO explained the diversification logic guiding Campestre's new bets, which wants recognition not just for size, but for quality.

The logic is simple in formulation, complex in execution: Pergola finances these bets. The company operates with a target of 5% net profit margin on revenue and reinvests another 5% in technology, expansion and new businesses.

Pergola helps sustain everything, the CEO explained when discussing the long-term logic. "We'll have this big wing until fine wine can walk on its own." This "big wing," as he defines it, is what allows Campestre to invest in a business that still doesn't generate relevant returns, but could redefine its positioning in the future.

With revenue exceeding R$ 500 million annually — a figure Zanotto confirmed in a previous interview with Bloomberg Línea and growing about 10% to 15% per year — the model generates sufficient resources to finance long-term bets that, for now, don't pay for themselves.

## A Business Within a Business

Fine wine currently represents 2% of Campestre's revenue. The investment to reach this point, according to Zanotto, was "very substantial." The original timeline for return was 10 years. Later, it extended to 15. "When we reach 15 years, I think it will be paid for and some profitability begins," he said. This means the company has been financing, for nearly a decade, a division that consumes capital without generating equivalent returns.

The justification cannot be reduced merely to the prestige and recognition of fine wines, and is both strategic and financial. "The fine wine margin is much better," Zanotto stated. "These are wines with greater added value. When someone likes a type of wine, they have no problem paying more."

This is evident during a visit to the winery, where fine wines are on sale. While Pergola can be found in any supermarket for about R$ 20, among fine wines the simpler ones carry the Villa Campestre brand and can be purchased for about R$ 40. Premium labels are called Zanotto and have prices starting from the R$ 70 range and going up to R$ 189, as in the Roots line. There's even a special line, Zanotto Troppo Maturo, made with grapes dehydrated in the vineyard, which costs over R$ 400 per bottle. These are wines that have also achieved international awards. The Sauvignon Blanc received a gold medal from the Decanter World Wine Awards in 2025, for example.

The comparison he uses is with the luxury perfume market: those who like it pay. The problem is getting the person to try it. And that's the strategy for building market share: making the consumer know the beverage. This is why there's a bet on wine tourism, but also investment in bringing the drink to consumers. One way to do this was opening a store with a wine bar in São Paulo, where Campestre began offering a "rodizio" — an experience where the customer pays a fixed amount and can taste all the brand's wines in any quantity they want.

In the company's view, consistent quality is the only way to compete in a market where consumers still favor imported labels. This applies to both fine wine and table wine, which has also undergone improvements over the decades. "Without quality, you can't think about anything else," Zanotto said.

The coexistence between the two segments — popular and premium — is not seen as an internal conflict. On the contrary: they play distinct roles within the company's strategy.

Every 15 days, the team brings together employees of different profiles to taste the house's wines blind alongside competing labels — Brazilian and imported, from the same vintage. "Our objective is always to be first and second," Zanotto said. "That's how you anticipate the market."

In this sense, Campestre's fine wine follows different logic from Pergola, which reached the top through scale, capillary distribution and a product that found an accessible price point in supermarkets throughout Brazil. Fine wine — marketed under the Zanotto line — goes to specialty stores and restaurants. "The work with table wine was the same as is now done with wine grapes. Without haste," Zanotto said.

## New Terroir

The separation between the two segments goes beyond the market. It's structural. In table wine, Campestre doesn't plant a single grape: it supplies its production from about 840 supplier families within a 120-kilometer radius around the original unit in Campestre da Serra. In fine wine, the company produces its own raw materials across the 28 hectares of vineyard it cultivates in Vacaria — with 14 varieties, both French and Italian.

"Table wine is wine meant to be young. We wanted to make aging wines, wines to win international awards," Zanotto explained. "So it was invested, as we started 60 years ago, everything was difficult, it was all little by little."

André Donatti, chief enologist and general manager of the winery in Vacaria, elected Enologist of the Year 2024 by the Brazilian Enology Association, has led operations for 20 years. For him, the objective was never just to sell a different product. "The objective is to create something of quality for the public, for the client who wants to taste and explore a new region," he said. "We want to have a new terroir with distinct wines and specificity from the region."
