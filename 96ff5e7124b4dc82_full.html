# From Pérgola to Luxury: Brazil's Best-Selling Wine Finances Campestre's Premium Bet

The Pérgola table wine has just completed 12 years as Brazil's best-selling wine. With 45 million liters sold annually, growth of over 10%, and annual revenue exceeding R$ 500 million, this simple and affordable beverage has become the foundation for diversification in Vinícola Campestre's business, with an eye toward long-term growth and leadership in fine wines as well. In 2014, the same year it achieved market leadership, entrepreneur João Zanotto purchased a defunct refrigeration facility in Vacaria, in the Rio Grande do Sul highlands, and began transforming it into a new winery for Campestre, the brand's owner.

A new project began in earnest there, focused on fine wines of greater prestige, riding on the strength and revenue of the sweet, simple beverage that conquered millions of consumers. The decision to enter the premium segment did not come because of success in cheap beverages. Years before starting the new winery, the company was already testing and producing fine wines for its own consumption, and in 2015 came the decision to transform production into a commercial product. "We expect to balance the market, have one market to begin in the other," Zanotto told Bloomberg Línea during a visit to the winery in Vacaria.

During walks through the vineyards, tastings, and even grape-treading experiences, the CEO explained the diversification logic guiding Campestre's new bets, which wants recognition not only for size, but for quality.

The logic is simple in formulation, complex in execution: Pérgola finances these bets. The company operates with a target of 5% net profit margin on revenue and reinvests another 5% in technology, expansion, and new businesses.

Pérgola helps sustain everything, the CEO explained when discussing the long-term logic. "We'll have this big wing until fine wine can walk on its own." This "big wing," as he defines it, is what allows Campestre to invest in a business that doesn't yet generate relevant returns, but could redefine its positioning in the future. With annual revenue exceeding R$ 500 million—a figure Zanotto confirmed in a previous interview with Bloomberg Línea and which grows approximately 10% to 15% annually—the model generates sufficient resources to finance long-term bets that, for now, don't pay for themselves.

## A Business Within a Business

Fine wine currently represents 2% of Campestre's revenue. The investment to reach this point, according to Zanotto, was "very substantial." The original timeline for return was 10 years. Later, it extended to 15. "When we reach 15 years, I think it will be paid off and some profitability will begin," he said. This means the company has been financing, for nearly a decade, a division that consumes capital without generating equivalent returns.

The justification cannot be reduced merely to prestige and recognition of fine wines, and is both strategic and financial. "The fine wine margin is much better," Zanotto stated. "These are wines with greater added value. When people like a type of wine, they don't mind paying more."

This is evident during a visit to the winery, where fine wines are for sale. While Pérgola can be found in any supermarket for around R$ 20, the simplest fine wines bear the Villa Campestre brand and can be purchased for around R$ 40. The premium labels are called Zanotto and have prices starting at R$ 70 and going up to R$ 189, in the case of the Roots line. There's even a special line, Zanotto Troppo Maturo, made with grapes dehydrated in the vineyard, costing over R$ 400 per bottle. These wines have also achieved international awards. The Sauvignon Blanc received a gold medal from the Decanter World Wine Awards in 2025, for example.

The comparison he uses is with the luxury perfume market: those who like it, pay. The problem is getting people to try it. And that's the strategy for building a market: making consumers know the beverage. That's why there's a bet on wine tourism, but also investment in bringing the beverage to consumers. One way of doing this was opening a store with a wine bar in São Paulo, where Campestre began offering a "rodízio," an experience where customers pay a fixed amount and can taste all the brand's wines in any quantity they want.

In the company's view, consistent quality is the only way to compete for space in a market where consumers still favor imported labels. This applies to both fine wine and table wine, which has also improved over the decades. "Without quality, you can't think about anything else," Zanotto said.

The coexistence between the two segments—popular and premium—is not seen as internal conflict. Quite the opposite: they fulfill distinct roles within the company's strategy.

Every 15 days, the team gathers employees of different profiles to blindly taste house wines alongside competing labels—Brazilian and imported, from the same vintage. "Our goal is always to be first and second," Zanotto said. "That's how you anticipate the market."

In this sense, Campestre's fine wine follows different logic from Pérgola, which reached the top through scale, capillary distribution, and a product that found an accessible price point in supermarkets throughout Brazil. The fine wine—marketed under the Zanotto line—goes to specialty stores and restaurants. "The work with table wine was the same as what is now being done with vinifera grapes. Without rushing," Zanotto said.

## New Terroir

The separation between the two segments goes beyond the market. It's structural. In table wine, Campestre doesn't plant a single grape: it supplies its production from approximately 840 supplier families within a 120-kilometer radius around the original unit in Campestre da Serra. In fine wine, the company produces its own raw materials from the 28 hectares of vineyard it cultivates in Vacaria—with 14 varieties, both French and Italian. "Table wine is a wine meant to be young. We wanted to make aging wines, wines to win international awards," Zanotto explained. "So we invested, as we started 60 years ago, everything with difficulty, everything little by little."

André Donatti, head winemaker and general manager of the Vacaria winery, named Enologist of the Year 2024 by the Brazilian Enology Association, has headed operations for 20 years. For him, the objective was never merely to sell a different product. "The objective is to create something of quality for the public, for the customer who wants to taste and explore a new region," he said. "We want to have a new terroir with distinct wines and specificity by region." Campestre has already identified the varieties that thrive best in Vacaria's microclimates and soil composition, establishing itself as a reference for a region that was previously known more for table wine than for fine wines with aging potential.
