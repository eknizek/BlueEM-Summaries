# PEC on 6×1 Work Schedule Opens New Round Between Congress and Private Sector

With government support, the Constitutional and Justice Committee (CCJ) of the Chamber of Deputies approved on Wednesday, April 22, the PEC that ends the 6x1 work schedule. But the business sector has already prepared to wage a battle in the special commission that will be created to debate the issue. The intention is to push the vote to after the elections, according to NeoFeed's reporting.

The strategy designed by business entities and confederations aligned with Center-Right and Right-wing deputies is to gain time and postpone voting as much as possible in committees before the proposal goes to the plenary. Should it pass in the Chamber—Chamber president Hugo Motta (Republicanos-PB) intends to vote still in the first semester—the bet will be on the Senate, where president Davi Alcolumbre (União-AP) has already signaled in a recent meeting with business sectors that voting could be postponed until after the October elections.

After the CCJ, which only assessed the constitutionality of the PEC, a special commission should be formed to debate the merits. If approved, the text proceeds to the Chamber plenary. Subsequently, the proposal must be voted on in the Senate.

During Wednesday's CCJ session on April 22, rapporteur Paulo Azi (União-BA) considered the day "historic" and said he hoped the government and workers would reach a consensus and produce modern, balanced legislation.

The task of business segments is arduous, but "Operation Secure PEC," according to a source closely following negotiations, seeks to prevent lawmakers opposed to the PEC from having to vote on the proposal in plenary or accept the burden of rejecting it at a moment when the issue has great popular appeal.

The business segment argues that if the current schedule (six working days and one day off) is extinguished without any transition, it will result in increased costs for employers, possible increase in informality, and need for higher hiring in some cases. The sector also advocates for a system based on hours worked and, in part, compensation such as payroll tax relief (though some sectors are not supporting the idea, nor is the government's economic team, which fears undesired fiscal impact on the National Treasury).

Recently, 175 employer entities (CNI, CNA, Fiesp, Abimaq, CNT, CNC, etc.) signed a manifesto defending more time to debate the subject on technical grounds. "It is considered advisable that deeper discussion of this agenda occurs outside the environment of electoral disputes, at a more propitious moment for building lasting consensuses and balanced solutions, avoiding that pressured decisions could compromise the quality of public policies and their effects on the country," says the statement.

Nearly isolated in his vote on the PEC in the CCJ today, deputy Lucas Redecker (PSD-RS) noted that with the end of the 6x1 schedule, it will be necessary to hire more in various business sectors, "but that doesn't mean the employer will have money to hire more." He advocated that the special commission be the space to debate forms of compensation and transition for ending this work schedule.

"My concern is: who will pay this bill? Because if we reduce working hours without salary reduction, and this is very good for the worker, it will bring a deficit for companies and this will also fall on the consumer's account," Redecker said, asking for time in the CCJ last week to postpone the vote. "There is rushing, a hurry to have this project approved as quickly as possible, but we need to create alternatives to maintain jobs," he added.

The government and its base, however, have been mobilizing to accelerate the issue's processing in Congress. Besides the PECs being processed in the Chamber—in addition to the one approved, authored by deputy Reginaldo Lopes (PT-MG), there is another by deputy Erika Hilton (PSOL-SP)—President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva's (PT) government sent a bill to Congress on April 15. The text provides for a new 5x2 work schedule (five working days and two days off per week) without salary reduction and reduction of weekly hours from 44 to 40 hours weekly.

The government's proposal has constitutional status: it has 45 days to be approved in the Chamber and an equal period to pass in the Senate, otherwise it locks the voting agenda. Minister Guilherme Boulos (Secretary-General) told Valor Econômico newspaper on Wednesday, April 22, that the government is working with the prediction of approving the matter in the Chamber by the end of July. He advocates, for example, a 90-day transition for implementing the end of the current schedule, while business entities speak of at least five years.

"Now the maneuvers have ended," said deputy Sâmia Bonfim (PSOL-SP) today, who voted in the CCJ and is one of the main parliamentarians defending the PEC to end the 6x1 schedule. "If the Right tries to delay again, the government Lula's urgent bill sent last week will have to be voted on anyway in the next month. That is, there's no escape: we will approve the end of the 6x1 schedule."

The government's project already faces resistance from the business sector. In a statement, the National Union of Commerce and Services Sectors (Unecs), which represents one of the sectors most affected by the end of the 6x1 schedule, said it viewed the text with "concern," as well as other similar proposals moving through the Legislature.

"Although such propositions present themselves under the mantle of worker valorization and expansion of rest time, it is necessary to recognize that, in the form they are being outlined, they suffer from relevant legal flaws and potential adverse economic effects of systemic character," says the entity. "Finally, it is warned that measures without adequate technical and legal basis can generate counterproductive effects, compromising both the business environment and social protection itself. The country's development requires solutions that are legally sound and economically viable," the statement concludes.
