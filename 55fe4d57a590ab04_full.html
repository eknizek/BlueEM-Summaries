# BRB Racing Against the Clock with Privatization Now on the Table to Save the Bank

Brasília — With the deadline approaching at the end of May to execute the capitalization plan submitted to the Central Bank, privatization is already being discussed in the corridors of BRB as a possibility to try to save the state bank from the deep crisis it finds itself in following the revelation of the Banco Master case.

Privatizing Brasília's public bank would be the last solution and one of the most drastic in the menu of options for its financial recovery. President Nelson de Souza had been totally denying this exit in recent months. Facing the lack of market appetite to grant loans to BRB or buy its portfolios, the Central Bank has been demanding concrete capitalization measures from the bank, and privatization has already emerged as an alternative, albeit a bitter one, in an attempt to avoid a future liquidation — the same fate as Master — according to NeoFeed's findings.

The independent asset manager Quadra Capital, which operates ports in Espírito Santo, even signed an agreement to purchase R$ 15 billion in Master assets under BRB's control, with a promise to pay R$ 4 billion upfront. But while the amount is not yet accounted for in the state bank's cash, the Central Bank continues to demand concrete capitalization measures, according to sources familiar with the negotiations. There have even been recent meetings between the Central Bank president, Gabriel Galípolo, and technical staff from the monetary authority, with the president of BRB and other representatives of the Brasília public bank. One of them even took place on Labor Day, Friday, May 1st.

The short window (less than a month), in an election year, is the main factor pressuring BRB to work with more radical scenarios, unthinkable a few months ago. Internally, the leadership of the capital's bank has always treated words like "federalization," "privatization," and "liquidation" as forbidden. However, as NeoFeed showed, the risk of liquidation, once unthinkable, has grown since the last phase of the Federal Police's Operation Compliance Zero, despite having the potential to cause some systemic risk in the market.

Federalizing BRB is already an option that has been rejected repeatedly by federal government authorities. On Monday, May 5th, in an interview with TV Cultura's "Roda Viva" program, Finance Minister Dario Durigan added his voice to the chorus and reinforced that BRB's problem belongs to the GDF (Federal District Government), signaling that a Treasury guarantee would be out of the question. The GDF is BRB's controlling shareholder.

Private banks such as BTG Pactual, for example, according to information obtained by NeoFeed, even admitted confidentially that without a Treasury guarantee, they would not enter into a consortium with other private banks to grant loans to BRB. In recent weeks, this was the "Plan A" attempted by the state bank's president and the DF governor, Celina Leão, in insistent outreach to the financial sector during trips to São Paulo.

The Brasília local government and BRB are betting mainly on the DF's real estate credit portfolios, which is the fourth largest in the country, and argue that they would be healthy. A senior BRB source analyzes that the list of possible bank buyers would include these same private banks. This is because it has been identified that private financial institutions would have greater interest in buying the public bank than financing it (an operation seen as high risk at this moment).

"If the capitalization measures are not concretized by the end of May, the inevitable solution is privatization," reveals this source.

However, privatization, which is normally preceded by an intervention by the Central Bank, would involve certain costs: the possibility of dismissing, albeit voluntarily, around 5,000 employees and would still need approval from the Federal District Legislative Assembly, in the midst of an election year.

The political difficulty for the GDF to privatize its public bank could run into, for example, resistance from parliamentarians, many of them pre-candidates for reelection or applicants for other positions in October's elections, in voting to authorize a privatization project. Furthermore, liquidations of state or regional banks are not common in the market.
