# At Oncoclínicas, Votes Reveal a Split in the Board

In a strategy to downplay the repercussions of dissenting votes from two members of its board of directors, Oncoclínicas published on the eve of Easter—Thursday, April 2, between 5:25 p.m. and 5:30 p.m.—documents revealing the positions of Marcos Grodetzky and Raul Rosenthal Ladeira de Matos regarding the non-binding agreement with Porto and the departure of CFO Camille Faria. The content of the documents, relating to meetings on March 13 and 15, reveals harsh criticism of Oncoclínicas' board and company management. It reinforces the warnings made by Mak Capital in its recent offensive against Latin America's largest specialized cancer treatment group.

The hedge fund addressed, in one of its letters, the lack of transparency and omission of relevant information for investors to understand why the company would negotiate an M&A at the worst moment in its history. Now, the votes signed by independent board members Grodetzky and Ladeira de Matos, which were not public, reveal a split in the board and raise questions about the company's financial structure and operational continuity.

The main focus of the disagreements is the non-binding proposal presented by Porto, which provides for the transfer of control of Oncoclínicas' primary line of business. The board members voted against signing the proposal and advocated for resuming negotiations. Among their arguments, Grodetzky and Ladeira de Matos emphasize that the presented terms do not meet the company's needs and could compromise its future operational capacity. According to them, the operation would create a structure where the primary line of business would be under Porto's control, while Oncoclínicas would maintain only a minority stake—but with significant debt.

The resulting structure, they argue, could "leave the company without cash generation to serve its interest and principal obligations," making the operation unfeasible under current conditions. In another section, the board members are even more direct in pointing out that the company would depend on uncertain future cash flows to survive, which, in their view, is not sustainable.

The votes also raise questions about the decision-making process. For Grodetzky and Ladeira de Matos, negotiations with Porto were not conducted by the executive management (as would be expected), but by company shareholders—which, in their view, compromises the credibility of the process. "The negotiation of the operation was conducted by company shareholders, not by its management," they wrote. "Management and the board of directors cannot delegate this function to shareholders."

The change in procedure "raises serious concerns that the Board cannot defend important aspects of the conduct and terms of the operation," they add. They stress that this type of operation should be led by management, with direct participation from the CEO and the financial area, under board supervision. For this reason, they opposed Camille Faria's departure, who held the positions of CFO and investor relations director. The resignation was formalized in a board meeting on March 15, amid discussions about the company's restructuring.

"We also oppose the proposal by the Board president to replace the Investor Relations Director simply because some shareholders and a majority of the Board did not accept the draft material fact presented by her," they wrote. "It is absurd to replace an Investor Relations Director during debt renegotiation, at a critical moment for the Company, because of a disagreement over a draft material fact. But this was the proposal from the [board] president [Marcelo Gasparino]," they add.

## Absence of Recording

The document also contains direct criticism of how the process was conducted within the company. At the outset, they state that since January 15 of this year, when the new board took office, 10 meetings and numerous sporadic interactions have been held, but only two minutes have been distributed. The board members record their "discontent" with the decision not to record the board meeting—a practice that had been previously approved—and state that the change, precisely at the moment of discussing a material operation, raises "serious concerns."

One of the most sensitive sections of the votes goes beyond financial discussion and enters the operational realm. The board members criticize decisions related to cash management and state that the company should prioritize operational continuity. "The company needs to allocate every available resource to purchase medications that are lacking and are critical for operational continuity and patient treatment."

The mention reinforces recent signals of operational deterioration amid the liquidity crisis facing the company. The votes also question decisions related to creditor treatment, citing payments made to a specific financial institution while other creditors remain in negotiations. According to the board members, such measures could violate existing agreements and harm the overall restructuring process by undermining confidence in negotiations.

The publication of the votes comes days after Mak escalated its tone against the company. In a letter sent to the board, Mak had stated that shareholders have the right to know financing proposals and internal disagreements during a moment of financial stress. On one side, the fund manager pressures for changes to the board and conditions a potential investment of up to R$ 500 million on a reconfiguration of governance. On the other, the company has been advancing negotiations with Porto as an alternative to strengthen its financial structure.

The convening of the Extraordinary General Assembly (AGE), as requested by Mak, was officially set for April 30. On the AGE agenda, among other matters, are discussions about the economic-financial situation and measures adopted or underway for renegotiating due dates and protecting operations; the removal of board members; fixing the number of members, qualification of candidates, and election of independent board members; as well as the appointment of board president and vice-president.
