# Oncoclínicas governance split revealed in board votes

Oncoclínicas disclosed, on April 2, the divergent votes of independent board members Marcos Grodetzky and Raul Rosenthal Ladeira de Matos regarding an agreement with Porto and the departure of CFO Camille Faria. The documents criticize the company's management and lack of transparency, reinforcing alerts from Mak Capital about financial and operational risks. The board members opposed Porto's non-binding proposal, which would transfer control of the main business line, arguing that it would compromise the company's future capacity. They questioned how the negotiation was conducted, which was not handled by management, and criticized Faria's departure at a critical moment. Additionally, they expressed dissatisfaction with the lack of recordings of board meetings and the prioritization of payments to certain creditors, which could harm the company's restructuring.

In a strategy to reduce the repercussions of the dissenting votes from two board members, Oncoclínicas published the documents on the eve of Easter — Thursday, April 2, between 5:25 p.m. and 5:30 p.m. — revealing the positions of Marcos Grodetzky and Raul Rosenthal Ladeira de Matos on the non-binding agreement with Porto and the departure of CFO Camille Faria. The contents of the documents, relating to meetings on March 13 and 15, reveal harsh criticism of Oncoclínicas' board and company management. It reinforces alerts made by Mak Capital in its recent offensive against Latin America's largest specialized cancer treatment group. The hedge fund addressed, in one of its letters, the lack of transparency and omission of relevant information for investors to understand why an M&A negotiation would occur at the worst moment in the company's history.

Now, the votes signed by independent board members Grodetzky and Ladeira de Matos, which were not public, reveal a split in the board and raise questions about the company's financial structure and operational continuity. The main focus of disagreement is the non-binding proposal presented by Porto, which provides for the transfer of control of Oncoclínicas' main business line. The board members voted against signing the proposal and advocated for renegotiations. Among their arguments, Grodetzky and Ladeira de Matos emphasize that the presented terms do not meet the company's needs and could compromise its future operational capacity. According to them, the operation would create a structure in which the main business line would be under Porto's control, while Oncoclínicas would maintain only a minority stake — but with significant debt. The resulting structure, they assert, could "leave the company without cash generation to serve its interest and principal obligations," making the operation unviable under current conditions.

In another section, the board members are even more direct in pointing out that the company would depend on uncertain future cash flows to survive, which in their view is not sustainable.

The votes also raise a question about the decision-making process. For Grodetzky and Ladeira de Matos, the negotiation with Porto was not conducted by executive management (as would be expected), but by company shareholders — which, in their view, compromises the credibility of the process. "The negotiation of the operation was conducted by company shareholders, not by its management," they wrote. "Management and the board of directors cannot delegate this function to shareholders." The alteration of the procedure "raises serious concerns that the Board cannot defend important aspects of how the operation was conducted and its terms," they add. They emphasize that this type of operation should be led by management, with direct participation from the CEO and finance area, under board supervision. This is why they opposed the departure of Camille Faria, who held the positions of CFO and investor relations director. The resignation was formalized at a board meeting on March 15, amid discussions about the company's restructuring. "We also opposed the Board chair's proposal to replace the Investor Relations Director simply because some shareholders and the board majority did not accept the material fact memo she presented," they wrote. "It is absurd to replace an Investor Relations Director during a debt renegotiation, at a critical moment for the Company, because of a disagreement over a material fact memo. But this was the proposal from the [board] president," they add.

According to reports, Faria was "invited" to resign because she demanded transparency and full details in information about the M&A that would be disclosed to the securities regulator.

**Absence of recordings**

The document also brings direct criticism of the process conducted within the company. At the outset, they state that since January 15 of this year, when the new board took office, 10 meetings and numerous individual interactions were held, but only two minutes were distributed. The board members express "dissatisfaction" with the decision not to record board meetings — a practice that had been previously approved — and state that the change, precisely when discussing a significant operation, raises "serious concerns."

One of the most sensitive sections of the votes goes beyond financial discussion and enters operational territory. The board members criticize decisions related to cash management and state that the company should prioritize operational continuity. "The company needs to allocate all available resources to purchase medications that are lacking and are critical for operational continuity and patient treatment." The mention reinforces recent signals of operational deterioration amid the liquidity crisis facing the company. The votes also question decisions related to creditor treatment, citing payments made to a specific financial institution while other creditors remain in negotiation. According to the board members, this type of measure could violate existing agreements and harm the overall restructuring process by compromising trust in negotiations.

The publication of the votes comes days after Mak intensified its criticism of the company. In a letter sent to the board, Mak had stated that shareholders have the right to know financing proposals and internal disagreements during a period of financial stress. On one side, the fund manager pressures for board changes and conditions a potential investment of up to R$ 500 million on a governance reconfiguration. On the other, the company had been advancing negotiations with Porto as an alternative to strengthen its financial structure. The calling of an Extraordinary General Meeting (AGE), as requested by Mak, was officially set for April 30. Among the topics on the AGE agenda are discussions about the economic-financial situation and measures adopted or underway for debt restructuring and protection of operations; removal of board members; determination of the number of members, qualification of candidates and election of independent board members; and the appointment of board chair and vice-chair.
