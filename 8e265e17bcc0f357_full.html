Only 8% of Colombia's mining executives satisfied with Petro government support

16 April 2026 · 10:08 PM

Only 8% of the country's mining executives are satisfied with the support of the Petro Government

The perception of conflict around mining activity reached 66 percent, according to the latest Mining Compass report. Colombia plays a key role in satisfying global demand for high-quality coal.

Only 8 percent of the country's mining executives are satisfied with the support of Gustavo Petro's Government for the activity, one of the lowest levels along with that of 2023 (3 percent) from the 'Mining Compass', a study that has measured the perceptions, concerns and expectations of the industry since 2015.

Although in 2022 this indicator was at the level of 35 percent, the highest level recorded, the perception of government support decreased by 27 percentage points to 8 percent, which according to the study reflects the sustained deterioration in the relationship between the sector and the National Government.

"The 2026 measurement highlights the central paradox of this four-year period: an industry that has sustainably and verifiably increased the satisfaction of State officials, but faces historic levels of institutional distrust and an environment in which growing conflict is perceived," said Jaime Arteaga, director of the firm Arteaga Latam, which conducted the study together with the National Consultation Center in association with the Colombian Mining Association and the Andi.

In total, 273 mining sector executives and 269 government officials—local, mining, environmental and control authorities—were consulted between February 26 and April 4, 2026 to prepare this new report.

The report highlights the perception gaps between executives and authorities. While 32 percent of the former believe the country has advanced in management and generation of specialized information for the sector, this level reaches 65 percent for the latter. The same occurs with the question of "how much has the country advanced in territorial presence." Only 33 percent of executives answered yes while that level was 60 percent for authorities.

Similarly, on the question of "how much has the country advanced in formalization of artisanal and small-scale mining," the responses were 43 and 61 percent, respectively; and on legal certainty they were 25 and 53 percent, respectively.

"Perhaps the most worrying indicator is legal certainty as it is the foundation on which long-term investment decisions are made and its perceived deterioration can affect the country's competitiveness," said the expert, within the framework of the Minexpo 2026 mining fair held in Medellín, where the report was launched.

Perception of conflict increased

Another finding of the study is that the perception of conflict around mining increased over the past year for sector executives, going from 51 percent to 66 percent, representing an increase of 15 points. Meanwhile, for authorities the level was 42 percent, 7 points higher.

"When only 8 percent of executives are satisfied with state support and the perception of increased conflict reaches 66 percent, we are facing a sector that advances with the brakes on. Colombia needs a comprehensive mining policy, with a National Government that works alongside companies and does not leave them alone," said Arteaga.

Specifically, 49 percent of executives reported having problems due to road blockades in the last 12 months, 42 percent said they were impacted by marches, 34 percent said they were affected by land conflicts and 30 percent by armed strikes, a scourge that increased the most from one year to another.

Other problems that generated conflict situations for business owners were the takeover of facilities or properties (28 percent), opposition campaigns by NGOs (25 percent), and threats.
