# Smugglers Positioned as Winners Amid Colombia-Ecuador Tariff Crisis

The political and commercial crisis facing Colombia with Ecuador once again dominates the media agenda due to the effects both countries are experiencing from rising tariffs. The 100% tariff increase by the neighboring country and Colombia's escalating increases to Ecuador mark a new concern in the business and foreign trade sector, given the impossibility of resuming exports to the Ecuadorian nation. Although the effects are already becoming evident, according to the director of the Colombia-Ecuador Chamber, Oliva Díazgranados, the beneficiary of the diplomatic dispute is smuggling.

She explained that the borders with Ecuador are virtually nonexistent, given that the economies of those territories function jointly. However, the tariff restrictions are forcing products to be sold illegally. "What this war between the presidents is generating is a waste through smuggling. Really, what is being proposed about controlling illegality is what is giving way to all illegality and smuggling at the border," she indicated.

Regarding the effects on companies, the director reiterated that entrepreneurs currently face a very "complex" scenario and that at least 3,287 companies exporting to Ecuador are at risk. "Of that total of companies, 80% are micro, small, and medium enterprises. What worries them most is that each one has at least 10 workers, so more than 26,000 jobs are at stake," Díazgranados mentioned.

The executive also detailed that these would be direct jobs, but if indirect jobs were counted, more than 400,000 people would be affected, "which is basically the population of a city like Armenia," she said. She also pointed to the possible closure of companies due to restrictions on sales caused by the 100% tariffs imposed by Ecuador beginning May 1st of this year.

"There is an idea to replace markets, but that is not so easy. Behind it there is investment, company registration procedures, trademark registrations, certifications, health and phytosanitary registrations. There are at least ten procedures that companies must complete to export," questioned the business leader.

From a macroeconomic perspective, the measures introduce price distortions, alter intraregional trade flows, and generate a negative supply and demand shock.

## Null Negotiations

Given that stopping the presidential dispute requires the two countries to enter joint negotiations involving less risk for companies, foreign trade actors see problem resolution between the countries as null. Based on statements by Javier Díaz, president of the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex), the political tension between presidents Daniel Noboa and Gustavo Petro continues to increase, and everything suggests there will be no solution until August, according to what the Ecuadorian president has said.

"Therefore, there is a decline coming in bilateral trade exchange. With a 100% tariff, it is impossible to export to Ecuador," said Díaz. Additionally, the Analdex president noted that Colombia has already demonstrated willingness to negotiate, but the solution involves other political issues. "A complaint has already been filed before the Andean Community," he added.

"What remains for exporters is to diversify their sales to other markets, to be able to wait for a solution to be reached, without having to make mass layoffs or even close companies," he opined. Meanwhile, the director of the Colombia-Ecuador Chamber highlighted that the binational organization has made approaches to the Ecuadorian government, but these have not borne fruit.

"President Noboa has told us that as long as Gustavo Petro is president in Colombia, there is no possibility of negotiating. He has been very emphatic that there is no possibility of reaching an agreement while President Petro is in office. He has said it openly and publicly," she stated.

Similarly, the director maintains that if the situation continues this way, there would be eight months where companies have no possibility of negotiating any bilateral exchange, which would prevent the exit and entry of products from both countries.

Regarding Colombia's tariffs on Ecuador, Díazgranados reiterated that imposing a 35% tariff on products like tuna from the neighboring country, which are part of the basic family basket, affects the cost of living of households in the country. "The tuna that Colombia produces is absolutely insufficient for the quantities consumed, and it is strata 1, 2, and 3 that buy it the most. How can you tell these families the price went up 35% due to tariffs, plus VAT. It's complicated," she indicated.

Finally, the business leader insisted that tuna is also part of the School Feeding Program (PAE), so the tariff would also affect the public policy for feeding children in Colombia.
