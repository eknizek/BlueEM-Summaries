# Smugglers Emerge as Winners Amid Colombia-Ecuador Tariff Crisis

The political and commercial crisis facing Colombia with Ecuador once again dominates the media agenda due to the impact both countries are experiencing from rising tariffs. The 100% tariff increase by the neighboring country and Colombia's staged increase to Ecuador mark a new concern in the business and foreign trade sector, due to the impossibility of resuming exports to the Ecuadorian nation. And although the effects are already becoming evident, according to the director of the Colombia-Ecuador Chamber, Oliva Díazgranados, the beneficiary of the diplomatic dispute is smuggling.

According to her explanation, the borders with Ecuador are almost non-existent, considering that the economies of those territories function jointly. However, tariff restrictions would be forcing the sale of products through illegal channels.

"What this war between the presidents is generating is a proliferation of smuggling. Really, what is being proposed about controlling illegality is what is giving all the way to all illegality and smuggling at the border," she stated.

Regarding company impacts, the director reiterated that at this moment businesspeople face a very "complex" scenario and that at least 3,287 companies exporting to Ecuador are at risk. "Of that total of companies, 80% are micro, small and medium enterprises. What worries them most is that each one of them has at least 10 workers, so more than 26,000 jobs are at stake," Díazgranados mentioned.

The executive also detailed that these would be direct jobs, but if indirect jobs were counted, more than 400,000 people would be affected, "which is basically the population of a city like Armenia," she said.

Similarly, she pointed to the possible closure of companies due to restrictions on sales because of the tariffs imposed by Ecuador of 100% that begin to take effect from May first of this year.

"There is an idea that markets can be replaced, but that is not so easy. Behind it there is investment, company registration procedures, trademark registrations, certifications, health registrations, phytosanitary registrations. There are at least ten procedures that companies have to do to be able to export," questioned the industry leader.

From a macroeconomic perspective, the measures introduce distortions in relative prices, alter intra-regional trade flows and generate a negative supply and demand shock.

## Null Negotiation

Given that for the presidential dispute to stop, the two countries need to enter into joint negotiations that involve less risk for companies, foreign trade actors see no resolution of problems between the countries.

Based on what Javier Díaz, president of the National Association of Foreign Trade (Analdex), said, the political tension between presidents Daniel Noboa and Gustavo Petro continues to increase and everything suggests there will be no solution until August, according to what the Ecuadorian president has stated.

"Therefore, a fall in bilateral trade exchange is coming. With a 100% tariff, it is impossible to export to Ecuador," said Díaz.

Additionally, the Analdex president noted that Colombia has already demonstrated willingness to negotiate, but the solution involves other political issues. "A complaint has already been filed before the Andean Community," he added.

"What follows on the exporters' side is to diversify their sales to other markets, in order to wait for a solution to be reached, without having to make massive layoffs or even close the companies," he opined.

For her part, the director of the Colombia-Ecuador Chamber highlighted that the binational organization has made approaches to the Ecuadorian government, but these have not borne fruit.

"President Noboa has told us that as long as Gustavo Petro is president in Colombia, there is no possibility of negotiating. He has been very emphatic that there is no probability of reaching an agreement while President Petro is in office. He has said it openly and publicly," she stated.

Similarly, the director maintains that if the situation continues this way, there would be eight months in which companies would have no possibility of negotiating any binational trade exchange, making it impossible for products from both countries to enter and exit.

Regarding Colombia's tariffs to Ecuador, Díazgranados reiterated that placing a 35% tariff on products like tuna from the neighboring country, which are part of the basic family basket, is affecting the cost of living in households in the country.

"The tuna that Colombia produces is absolutely insufficient for the quantities consumed and where strata 1, 2 and 3 are the ones who buy the most. How can you tell these families the price went up 35% because of tariffs, plus VAT. It is complicated," she indicated.

Finally, the industry leader insisted that tuna is also part of the School Feeding Program (PAE), so the tariff would also affect the public policy of feeding children in Colombia.
