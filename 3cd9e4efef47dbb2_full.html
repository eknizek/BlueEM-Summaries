# Russian Roulette Again

By Milton Von Hesse

Everything suggests that the runoff election will once again face, as in 2021, two candidates radically opposed in their economic vision of development. The candidate who obtained the most votes is in favor of the economic principles outlined in the 1993 Constitution; that is, private sector leadership in productive activity, limited state participation in business, commercial integration with the world, equal treatment between nationals and foreigners, fiscal balance, monetary policy directed by an autonomous Central Bank, and strict respect for private property and legal stability, among other elements that characterize a social market economy.

The other candidate, conversely, promotes the opposite, starting with modifying the economic chapter of the 1993 Constitution to create more state-owned enterprises, altering the rules governing international commerce to favor local production for domestic consumption in certain productive sectors and restrict imports of specific products, distancing itself from Central Bank independence to access more discretionary use of international reserves, as well as more expansive monetary policy management, and proposing renegotiation of contracts particularly with foreign capital companies, among others.

At the level of aggregate macroeconomic results, it is clear that the social market economy has been far superior to those recorded by countries with socialism-inspired regimes. None of the South American countries that followed left-wing statist policies from the 1990s onward (Kirchner's Argentina, Evo's Bolivia, Fidel's Cuba and his heirs, or Chávez and Maduro's Venezuela) achieved a higher level of well-being for their population than the results obtained by Peru, Colombia, or Chile while governed by pro-market governments.

Peru, for example, between 1990 and today, increased its real per capita production more than 7 times, its exports more than 28 times, its annual inflation reduced from over 7,000% to just 1.5% last year, poverty cut in half between the late 1980s and today, life expectancy increased by 14 years in that period, and infant mortality reduced to almost one-sixth of what it was.

These positive effects described, however, have not permeated homogeneously throughout the territory. Design flaws in national sectoral policies, which have been dramatically exacerbated over the past ten years by political instability, within the framework of decentralization characterized by poor management of public resources by subnational governments, have not allowed populations in the poorest departments to fully benefit from the positive impacts of sound macroeconomics and international trade.

In this context, it is unsurprising that the regions registering the lowest levels of well-being—Apurímac, Cajamarca, Cusco, Madre de Dios, and Puno—measured by the Human Development Index (HDI), an indicator that summarizes population income, education, and healthy life expectancy, are those where the disruptive and anti-system candidate obtained his best results. These departments are characterized by having the largest portion of their population working in the informal sector and increasingly in illegal activities, with access to basic services provided by the State (education, health, water, and sanitation) of terrible quality.

To stop playing this kind of Russian roulette every five years, it falls to center and right-wing parties represented in the new Congress of the Republic to define an agenda for defending and deepening the social market economy model throughout the country with the firm purpose of improving the quality of life of its population.
