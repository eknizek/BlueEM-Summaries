# Russian Roulette Again

By Milton Von Hesse

Everything indicates that the electoral runoff will again face, as in 2021, two candidates radically opposed in their economic vision of development. The candidate who obtained the highest vote is in favor of the economic principles set forth in the 1993 Constitution; that is, leadership of the private sector in productive activity, limited state participation in business activity, commercial integration with the world and equal treatment between nationals and foreigners, fiscal balance, monetary policy directed by an autonomous Central Bank and unrestricted respect for private property and legal stability, among other elements that characterize a social market economy. The other candidate, in contrast, promotes the opposite, starting with the modification of the economic chapter of the 1993 Constitution to create more public enterprises, modify the rules governing international commerce to privilege local production for domestic consumption in some productive sectors and restrict the importation of certain products, move away from Central Bank independence to access more discretionary use of international reserves, as well as a more expansive handling of monetary policy, and propose the renegotiation of contracts especially with foreign capital companies, among others.

At the level of aggregate macroeconomic results, it is clear that the social market economy has been far superior to those recorded by countries with socialism-inspired regimes. None of the South American countries that followed left-wing statist policies from the nineteen-nineties onwards (Kirchner's Argentina, Evo's Bolivia, Fidel's Cuba and his heirs, or Chávez and Maduro's Venezuela) achieved a higher level of well-being for its population than the results obtained by Peru, Colombia, or Chile while they were led by pro-market governments. Peru, for example, between 1990 and today, increased its real per capita production more than 7 times, its exports more than 28 times, its annual inflation was reduced from more than 7,000% to just 1.5% last year, poverty was reduced by half between the late eighties and today, life expectancy increased by 14 years in that period and infant mortality was reduced to almost one-sixth of what it was.

These positive effects described, however, have not permeated homogeneously throughout the territory. The design failures of national sectoral policies, which were dramatically aggravated in the last ten years with political instability, within the framework of decentralization characterized by appalling management of public resources by subnational governments, have not allowed populations in the poorest departments of the country to fully benefit from the positive impacts of sound macroeconomics and international trade. In that context, it is not surprising that regions recording the lowest levels of well-being (Apurímac, Cajamarca, Cusco, Madre de Dios, Puno) measured by the Human Development Index (HDI), an indicator that summarizes population income, education, and life expectancy in healthy conditions, are those where the disruptive and anti-system candidate obtained his best results. These departments are characterized by having the largest portion of their population working in the informal sector and increasingly also in illegality, with access to basic services provided by the State (education, health, water and sanitation) of terrible quality.

To stop playing this sort of Russian roulette every five years, it is up to the center and right-wing parties represented in the new Congress of the Republic to define an agenda for defending and deepening the social market economy model throughout all territories of the country with the firm purpose of improving the quality of life of its population.
