Policy design failures limit benefits of macroeconomic growth in poorest regions

02 May 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Russian Roulette Again by Milton von Hesse

It appears that the runoff election will again face, as in 2021, two candidates with radically opposed visions of economic development. The candidate who obtained the most votes supports the economic principles enshrined in the 1993 Constitution; that is, private sector leadership in productive activity, limited state participation in business, commercial integration with the world, equal treatment of nationals and foreigners, fiscal balance, monetary policy directed by an autonomous Central Bank, and strict respect for private property and legal stability, among other elements that characterize a social market economy.

The other candidate, in contrast, promotes the opposite, starting with modifying the economic chapter of the 1993 Constitution to create more state-owned enterprises, changing the rules governing international trade to privilege local production for domestic consumption in certain productive sectors and restrict the importation of specific products, moving away from Central Bank independence to access more discretionary use of international reserves, as well as more expansive monetary policy management, and proposing the renegotiation of contracts, especially with foreign capital companies, among others.

At the level of aggregate macroeconomic results, it is clear that the social market economy has been vastly superior to those registered by countries with socialism-inspired regimes. None of the South American countries that followed left-wing statist policies from the nineties onward (Kirchner's Argentina, Evo's Bolivia, Fidel's Cuba and his heirs, or Chávez and Maduro's Venezuela) achieved a higher level of welfare for their population than the results obtained by Peru, Colombia, or Chile while led by pro-market governments.

Peru, for example, between 1990 and the present, increased its real per capita production more than 7 times, its exports more than 28 times, its annual inflation reduced from over 7,000% to just 1.5% last year, poverty was cut in half between the late eighties and now, life expectancy increased by 14 years in that period, and infant mortality was reduced to almost a sixth of what it was.

These positive effects described, however, have not permeated homogeneously throughout the territory. The design failures in national sectoral policies, which have been dramatically aggravated in the last ten years with political instability, within the framework of a decentralization characterized by appalling management of public resources by subnational governments, have not allowed populations in the poorest departments of the country to fully benefit from the positive impacts of sound macroeconomics and international trade.

In this context, it is not surprising that the regions registering the lowest levels of welfare (Apurímac, Cajamarca, Cusco, Madre de Dios, Puno), measured by the Human Development Index (HDI), an indicator that summarizes population income, education, and life expectancy in healthy conditions, are those where the disruptive and anti-system candidate obtained his best results. These departments are characterized by having the largest portion of their population working in the informal sector and increasingly in illegal activities, with access to basic services provided by the State (education, health, and water and sanitation) of poor quality.

To stop playing this kind of Russian roulette every five years, it falls to the center-right parties represented in the new Congress of the Republic to define an agenda for defending and deepening the social market economy model throughout the country with the firm purpose of improving the quality of life of its population.
