# Understand in 5 points the Copasa privatization project, which enters the final stretch

In the coming weeks, the Government of Minas Gerais should announce the final details of the privatization process of Copasa, the state-owned sanitation company. A campaign promise of former governor Romeu Zema (Novo) since 2018, the company's privatization has gone through intense political ups and downs and has accelerated in recent months with the approaching electoral calendar.

With net profit of R$ 1.42 billion in 2025, Copasa serves 636 municipalities in Minas Gerais (74% of the state), with 309 receiving water and sewage services and 327 receiving only water. Understand, in five points, the main issues involving Copasa's privatization.

## 1 - Why does the MG Government want to privatize Copasa?

The justification presented by the then Zema administration for the sale of state companies was to reduce state expenses and generate cash for investments. In the case of Copasa, the bill that authorized privatization states that the funds raised (estimated at approximately R$ 10 billion) will be used to amortize Minas Gerais' debt with the Federal Government—which currently stands at R$ 180 billion. There is also a provision to allocate part of the resources to a fund focused on infrastructure and sanitation development. The Minas Gerais government also argues that privatization will improve service provision and accelerate sanitation investments in the state.

## 2 - Is it the same as what the SP Government did with Sabesp?

The projects in Minas and São Paulo have differences in model and approach. Starting with political discourse. While the Minas Gerais government associated the project with debt relief with the Federal Government, the Tarcísio de Freitas (Republicans) administration promoted the privatization of the São Paulo sanitation company under the argument of advancing universalization goals and accelerating investments in water and sewage. The strategy did not exempt the project from criticism, but it gave the government a socially appealing discourse. Tarcísio also created a fund to mitigate tariff increases that would accompany the jump in investments due to the new universalization target. The Minas Gerais administration has not signaled that it intends to address this issue. Another difference relates to the state's participation in the company after privatization. São Paulo reduced its participation in Sabesp from 50.3% to 18%, maintaining three names on the board of directors. Minas Gerais will go from 50.3% to a stake of 5% of the capital, with a single representative on the board of directors.

## 3 - How will the Copasa privatization process work?

The chosen option was to make a follow-on offering of shares in two stages. Of the 45% stake of Copasa to be offered, 30% is intended for a reference shareholder, which is a kind of strategic partner—as happens with Equatorial in Sabesp in São Paulo. The other 15% will be offered to the market via bookbuilding, a stock exchange process in which investors indicate the quantity of shares they want to buy. The reference investor must acquire the 30% stake alone. According to the rules, interested companies must prove they have made infrastructure investments (such as sanitation, energy, or highways) worth a minimum of R$ 6.3 billion over the last 20 years.

People with knowledge of the process say that the dispute is concentrated between two groups: Aegea and Sabesp, which should participate in a consortium with Equatorial. The winning reference investor will be whoever offers the highest price per share. The minimum values will still be defined by the government, but estimates point to a check of approximately R$ 7 billion. The strategic partner will also commit to a "lock-up," a restriction that prohibits the sale of shares for a period of four years. After this period, this strategic partner must remain linked to Copasa with at least 50% of the acquired shares until 2033 (or until the universalization goals for water and sewage are met).

## 4 - Will the state have any influence in the company after the sale?

The Minas Gerais Government disclosed the model of the shareholders' agreement that will apply after the process is completed. The document formalizes the terms of the relationship between the reference investor and the state in managing Copasa. In addition to guaranteeing a seat on the board of directors and fiscal council, the agreement establishes that the government will have the right to a "golden share," a preferred share that gives the public administration veto power over specific decisions. The state will also have the power to prevent the reference investor from approving relevant matters without its prior consent. Should the state object or remain silent, the partner will be required to vote to maintain the status quo ante (the previous situation) on matters such as dividends, capital structure, corporate reorganization, and strategic business.

## 5 - What stage is the privatization at and when will it be completed?

Officially, the process is under review and still awaits the official disclosure of the conclusive stages for the share sale. Recently, the Minas Gerais Government opened the period for groups interested in becoming a reference investor to register at B3 and present qualification documents. The deadline is May 8th. Next steps include publication of the prospectus, with final rules and the timeline for all phases until the process is completed. Publication of this document will mark the launch of the share offering. The Minas Gerais Government intends to complete Copasa's privatization in the first semester, but needs to align with the Treasury.
