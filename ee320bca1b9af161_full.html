# Understand in 5 Points the Copasa Privatization Project, Which Enters the Final Stretch

In the coming weeks, the Government of Minas Gerais should announce the final details of the privatization process for Copasa, the state-owned sanitation company. A campaign promise of former governor Romeu Zema (Novo) since 2018, the company's privatization has experienced intense political ups and downs and has accelerated in recent months as the electoral calendar approaches. With net profit of R$ 1.42 billion in 2025, Copasa serves 636 municipalities in Minas Gerais (74% of the state), with 309 receiving water and sewage services and 327 receiving only water.

## 1 - Why does the MG Government want to privatize Copasa?

The justification presented by the then Zema administration for the sale of state companies was to reduce state expenses and generate cash to make investments. In the case of Copasa, the bill authorizing privatization states that the resources raised (estimated at approximately R$ 10 billion) will be used to pay down Minas Gerais' debt to the Federal Union—which currently stands at R$ 180 billion. There is also a provision to allocate part of the resources to a fund focused on infrastructure and sanitation development. The state government also argues that privatization will improve service delivery and accelerate sanitation investments in the state.

## 2 - Is it the same as what the SP Government did with Sabesp?

The Minas Gerais and São Paulo projects have differences in model and approach. Starting with the political discourse. While the Minas Gerais government associated the project with relieving debt to the Federal Union, the Tarcísio de Freitas administration (Republicans) promoted the privatization of the São Paulo sanitation company under the argument of advancing universalization targets and accelerating investments in water and sewage. The strategy did not exempt the project from criticism, but gave the government a discourse with social appeal. Tarcísio also created a fund to mitigate tariff increases that would accompany the jump in investments due to the new universalization target. The Minas Gerais administration has not signaled that it should address this issue. Another difference concerns the state's participation in the company after privatization. São Paulo reduced its stake in Sabesp from 50.3% to 18%, maintaining three names on the board of directors. Minas Gerais will go from 50.3% to a stake of 5% of the share capital, with a single representative on the board of directors.

## 3 - How will the Copasa privatization process work?

The chosen option was to make a follow-on stock offering in two stages. Of the 45% stake in Copasa to be offered, 30% is allocated to a reference shareholder, which is a type of strategic partner—as happens with Equatorial in Sabesp in São Paulo. The other 15% will be offered to the market through a bookbuilding process, a stock exchange process in which investors indicate the quantity of shares they want to buy. The reference investor must acquire the 30% stake alone. According to the rules, interested companies must prove that they have made investments in infrastructure (such as sanitation, energy, or highways) worth a minimum of R$ 6.3 billion over the last 20 years. People with knowledge of the process say that the competition is concentrated between two groups: Aegea and Sabesp, which should participate in a consortium with Equatorial. The winning reference investor will be whoever offers the highest price per share. Minimum values will still be defined by the government, but estimates point to a check of approximately R$ 7 billion. The strategic partner will also commit to a "lock-up," a restriction that prohibits the sale of shares for a period of four years. After this period, this strategic partner must remain linked to Copasa with at least 50% of the acquired shares until 2033 (or until the water and sewage universalization targets are met).

## 4 - Will the state have any influence on the company after the sale?

The Government of Minas Gerais released the model of the shareholder agreement that will be valid after the process concludes. The document formalizes the terms of the relationship between the reference investor and the state in managing Copasa. In addition to guaranteeing a seat on the board of directors and audit board, the agreement establishes that the government will have the right to a "golden share," a preferred share that gives the public administration veto power in specific decisions. The state will also have the power to prevent the reference investor from approving relevant matters without its prior consent. If the state objects or remains silent, the partner will be obligated to vote to maintain the status quo ante (the previous situation) on matters such as dividends, capital structure, corporate reorganization, and strategic business.

## 5 - What stage is the privatization in and when will it be completed?

Officially, the process is under analysis and still awaits the official disclosure of the conclusive stages for the sale of shares. Recently, the Government of Minas Gerais opened a period for groups interested in becoming a reference investor to register on B3 and submit qualifying documents. The deadline is May 8. The next steps include the publication of the prospectus, with the definitive rules and timeline for all phases until the completion of the process. The publication of this document will mark the launch of the stock offering. The Government of Minas Gerais intends to complete the Copasa privatization in the first semester, but needs to align with the Court of Accounts.
