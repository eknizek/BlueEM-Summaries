Amid record credit defaults, Argentine province launches debt relief plan for public and private sector employees

29 April 2026 · 04:10 PM

# Amid Record Credit Defaults, Argentine Province Launches Debt Relief Plan for Public and Private Sector Employees

The government of Santa Fe announced the launch of a debt relief plan aimed at public and private sector workers, in a context of increasing credit defaults and deteriorating household disposable income. The initiative, presented by the administration of Maximiliano Pullaro, seeks to limit the weight of debts on salaries and offer more accessible refinancing mechanisms.

The so-called Income Protection Plan contemplates a series of measures aimed at state employees, retirees, private sector workers, and self-employed individuals whose incomes are affected by deductions associated with loans. According to details from the provincial Executive, the objective is to "alleviate the financial burden through debt refinancing" and, in the case of the public sector, ensure that deductions do not exceed 25% of salary.

The presentation was led by government spokeswoman Virginia Coudannes, along with Economy Minister Pablo Olivares and Public Function Secretary Malena Azario. It was emphasized that the proposal combines immediate relief tools with financial education and institutional advisory services.

"This is an unprecedented experience in the country and in Santa Fe," said Coudannes, who added that the initiative aims to "recover margin in the disposable income" of families.

The launch occurs in a scenario of growing deterioration in credit indicators. According to official data cited by the province, delinquency in the traditional financial system rose from 10.6% in January to 11.2% in February, while in non-bank credit—such as fintech and digital wallets—irregularity reached 29.9%.

At the local level, 33% of state employees have credit deductions on their pay stubs, and about 12,000 exceed the 25% threshold. To this group are added 7,000 retirees and approximately 60,000 private and self-employed workers in a similar situation.

## How the Plan Works

The plan is structured around four main axes. First, progress will be made in reorganizing the discount code system through re-registration of member entities. Second, the salary deduction limit will be reduced from the current 50% to 25%. The third component aims at adjusting existing debts to this new cap. For this, refinancing with creditor entities at lower rates or transfer of balances to other institutions is foreseen. In cases where there is no agreement, the provincial State may intervene with zero-rate financial assistance to cover the difference, which will later be recovered when the installment falls below the established limit.

Finally, new credit lines will be enabled for debt consolidation, with terms of up to 60 installments and two months of grace. This mechanism will allow consolidating commitments into a single loan, although those who access it will not be able to take new credit through discount codes until canceling the operation.

The program also includes specific tools for the private sector. Among them, a line from the Bank of Santa Fe is foreseen to cancel long-term debts at rates below market average, as well as agreements with business chambers to promote sectoral debt relief schemes. Additionally, financial entities will be called upon to expand the supply of consolidation loans.

In parallel, the Solidarity Bank—dependent on the Ministry of Equality and Human Development—will launch measures aimed at workers with irregular income, a segment that usually faces greater difficulties accessing formal credit.
