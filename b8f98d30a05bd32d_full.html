Amid record loan defaults, Argentine province launches debt relief program for public and private employees

# Amid Record Loan Defaults, Argentine Province Launches Debt Relief Program for Public and Private Employees

The Santa Fe provincial government announced the launch of a debt relief plan aimed at public and private sector workers, in a context of increasing loan defaults and deteriorating household disposable income. The initiative, presented by Maximiliano Pullaro's administration, seeks to limit the weight of debt on salaries and offer more accessible refinancing mechanisms.

The so-called Income Protection Plan includes a series of measures targeting state employees, retirees, private sector workers, and self-employed individuals whose income is affected by deductions associated with loans. According to provincial officials, the objective is to "alleviate the financial burden through debt refinancing" and, in the case of the public sector, ensure that deductions do not exceed 25% of salary.

The presentation was led by government spokesperson Virginia Coudannes, together with Economy Minister Pablo Olivares and Public Function Secretary Malena Azario. They highlighted that the proposal combines immediate relief tools with financial education and institutional advisory services. "This is an unprecedented experience in the country and in Santa Fe," Coudannes stated, adding that the initiative aims to "recover margin in the disposable income" of families.

The launch comes as credit indicators continue to deteriorate. According to official data cited by the province, delinquency in the traditional financial system rose from 10.6% in January to 11.2% in February, while in non-bank loans—such as fintech and digital wallets—irregularity reached 29.9%. At the local level, 33% of state employees have loan deductions on their paychecks, with about 12,000 exceeding the 25% threshold. This is in addition to 7,000 retirees and approximately 60,000 private sector workers and self-employed individuals in similar situations.

## How the Plan Works

The plan is structured around four main pillars. First, there will be reorganization of the discount code system through re-registration of affiliated entities. Second, the salary deduction limit will be reduced from the current 50% to 25%. The third component addresses adjusting existing debts to this new cap, involving refinancing with creditor entities at lower rates or transferring balances to other institutions. In cases where agreement cannot be reached, the provincial state may intervene with zero-interest financial assistance to cover the difference, which will be recovered when the installment falls below the established limit.

Finally, new credit lines will be opened for debt consolidation, with terms of up to 60 installments and two months of grace. This mechanism will allow consolidating multiple debts into a single loan, though those who access it cannot take on new credits through discount codes until the operation is paid off.

The program also includes specific tools for the private sector. These include a Santa Fe Bank line for canceling long-term debt at rates below market average, as well as agreements with business chambers to promote sector-specific debt relief schemes. Additionally, financial entities will be convened to expand the supply of consolidation credits.

In parallel, the Solidarity Bank—dependent on the Ministry of Equality and Human Development—will implement measures targeting workers with irregular income, a segment that typically faces greater difficulty accessing formal credit.
