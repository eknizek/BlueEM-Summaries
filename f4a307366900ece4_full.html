# Cimed Achieves R$1 Billion in Quarterly Revenue; CEO Targets 'Brazilian P&G' Status with Consumer Pharma Portfolio

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "The revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold three times the number of items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years and which has recently gained momentum — investments and brands.

In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and managed to grow by reducing production volume for the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

But it's in the consumer segment that the company sees greater diversification across income levels, from low to high income, and margins — it has already entered the infantile hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorant segments and will now launch a supplement line called Urso.

"We want to be the Brazilian P&G," states the Cimed CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of its Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand — featuring hyaluronic acid — which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and targets R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the start of the year, launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted by marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing Super items but through an intermediary — owning production saves 25% in costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$600 million.

The consumer segment performance helped Cimed achieve record growth for the first quarter, with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and a 26% increase in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, greater than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer segment represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a share from pharma. Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because it's a relevant definition — gaining market share even at the expense of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with product trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if it causes an allergy? Nobody will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty through quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

The entry into recurring-use products wasn't without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where restocking happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some retail points. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock ruptures than sales," Marques recounts, and he adjusted the logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "These are completely different businesses. We're discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," the CEO says.

The company projects R$4.8 billion in revenue for 2026 and has a target to reach R$10 billion by 2030. To this end, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then — those with the potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Cimed Generic, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth, perhaps, could be Urso or Milimetric.
