# Cimed Generates R$1 Billion in Quarterly Revenue: 'We Want to Be the Brazilian P&G,' Says CEO

João Adibe Marques walks into the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "Revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold triple the number of items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years and which has gained momentum—investments and brands.

In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing production volume for the hospital channel to increase offerings in generics. It is in the consumer segment, however, where the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and of margins. It has already entered the infant hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorant segments, and will now launch a supplement line, Urso.

"We want to be the Brazilian P&G," affirms Cimed's CEO.

The company has been encouraged by the performance of the Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand—featuring hyaluronic acid—which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and targets R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at year-end, launched the Super brand for deodorant and oral hygiene, which was boosted through marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but through an intermediary. Owning production saves 25% of costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, which projects annual revenue of R$600 million.

The strong consumer performance helped Cimed achieve a record first quarter with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and a 26% increase in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharmaceuticals. Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition—gaining market share even at the cost of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps drive trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the smell, the effectiveness, if it causes allergies? No one will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty through quality," he says. "I didn't win from the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

Entering the market for recurring-use products came with challenges. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy pace, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some points of sale. "We had demand greater than our capacity, with more stock outs than sales," Marques recounts. He adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has prompted Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "They are completely different businesses. We're discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," the CEO says.

The company projects R$4.8 billion in revenue for 2026 and aims to reach R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Cimed Generic, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth could be either Urso or Milimetric.
