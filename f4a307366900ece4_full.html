# Cimed posts R$ 1 billion in quarterly revenue: 'we want to be Brazil's P&G,' says CEO

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "The revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three generated around R$ 400 million, but Cimed sold triple the number of items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years, which has been gaining momentum—investments and brands. In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing production volume aimed at the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

It is in the consumer segment that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and of margins—it has already entered the children's hygiene, oral hygiene, deodorant segments, and will now launch a supplement line, Urso.

"We want to be Brazil's P&G," says Cimed's CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of the Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with the lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand—such as hyaluronic acid—which generated R$ 140 million in revenue last year and targets R$ 300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children, and at year-end launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted with marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but charging an intermediary fee—owning production saves 25% of costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$ 150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$ 600 million. Consumer performance helped Cimed achieve record first-quarter growth, with R$ 1 billion in revenue, up 20% compared to the same period last year, and up 26% in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharmaceuticals. Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition—gaining market share even at the cost of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if you get an allergy? Nobody will risk it if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty with quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

The entry into recurrent-use products did not come without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some retail points. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock ruptures than sales," Marques recounts, explaining that he adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it is worthwhile to separate the company into two. "They are completely different businesses. We are discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," the CEO says.

The company projects R$ 4.8 billion in revenue for 2026 and has the goal of reaching R$ 10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with the potential to generate over R$ 1 billion each. Five are already defined: Genérico Cimed, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth could be Urso or Milimetric.
