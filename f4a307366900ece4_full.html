# Cimed Generates R$1 Billion Quarterly Revenue; CEO Targets "to be Brazilian P&G"

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "Revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold triple the number of items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years—investments and brands. In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing production volume for the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

It is in the consumer segment that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and margins—it has already entered the children's hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorant segments, and will now launch a supplement line called Urso. "We want to be the Brazilian P&G," states Cimed's CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of the Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with its lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand—such as hyaluronic acid—which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and aims for R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the beginning of the year, launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted through marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed completed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but charging an intermediary fee—owning production saves 25% of costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, which projects annual revenue of R$600 million. The consumer performance helped Cimed achieve a record first quarter with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and a 26% increase in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharma. Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition—gaining market share even at the expense of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the efficacy, if you get an allergy? No one will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty with quality," he says. "I didn't make money on the first sale. I made it on the repurchase."

The entry into recurrent-use products did not come without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some points of sale. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock shortages than sales," explains Marques, who adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory. The consumer expansion has made Cimed discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "These are completely different businesses. We are discussing whether a spin-off would make sense," the CEO says.

The company projects revenue of R$4.8 billion in 2026 and has a target to reach R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has set a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with the potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Cimed Generic, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth, perhaps, could be Urso or Milimetric.
