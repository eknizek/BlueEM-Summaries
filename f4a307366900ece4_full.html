# Cimed Reaches R$1bn in the Quarter: 'We Want to Be the Brazilian P&G', Says CEO

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "The revenue is similar, but look at our sales volume," the CEO points to the laptop screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold three times more items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years and which has recently gained momentum — investments and brands. In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing production volume aimed at the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

It is in the consumer segment that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and margins — it has already entered the segment of children's hygiene, oral hygiene, deodorants, and will now launch a line of supplements called Urso. "We want to be the Brazilian P&G," says Cimed's CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of Carmed's lines, which became a sales phenomenon with its lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand — such as hyaluronic acid — which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and aims for R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the turn of the year, launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted by marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but with intermediary charges — owning production saves 25% of costs, says the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$600 million. The consumer performance helped Cimed achieve a record first quarter for the period, with R$1 billion in revenue, a 20% increase compared to the same period last year, and a 26% increase in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented, from January to March, 45% of revenue, taking a slice from pharma. Marques draws attention to the sales volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition — gaining market share even at the cost of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with product trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if you get an allergy? Nobody will risk it if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty with quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

The entry into products with recurring use did not come without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some retail locations. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock breaks than sales," says Marques, who adjusted logistics dynamics and now negotiates the acquisition of an aerosol factory. The consumer expansion has made Cimed discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "They are completely different businesses. We are discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," says the CEO. The company projects R$4.8 billion in revenue for 2026 and has a goal of reaching R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then — those with potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Generic Cimed, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth, perhaps, could be Urso or Milimetric.
