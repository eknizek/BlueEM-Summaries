# Cimed posts R$1 billion quarterly revenue, CEO says company aims to become Brazilian P&G

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, which shows Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "Revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three companies generated around R$400 million, but Cimed sold three times as many items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years, which has begun to gain momentum through investments and brands. In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and has managed to grow by reducing production volume aimed at the hospital channel to increase generic offerings.

It is in the consumer segment, however, that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and margins. The company has already entered the segments of children's hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorants, and will now launch a supplement line called Urso. "We want to be the Brazilian P&G," says Cimed's CEO.

The company was encouraged by the performance of its Carmed line, which became a sales phenomenon with its lip balm, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand featuring products like hyaluronic acid, which generated R$140 million in revenue last year and targets R$300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the year's turn, launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted through marketing on the reality show BBB.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but was charging an intermediary fee. Owning production saves 25% in costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$600 million.

Consumer performance helped Cimed achieve record first-quarter growth, with R$1 billion in revenue, up 20% compared to the same period last year, and 26% growth in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a slice from pharma. Marques calls attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition: gaining market share even at the expense of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps with trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the scent, the effectiveness, if you get an allergic reaction? Nobody will take the risk if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty through quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

Entering products with recurring use came not without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy rhythm, where restocking happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some points of sale. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock ruptures than sales," says Marques, who adjusted the logistics dynamic and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "These are completely different businesses. We're discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," the CEO says.

The company projects revenue of R$4.8 billion in 2026 and has a target to reach R$10 billion by 2030. To achieve this, it has outlined a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with potential to generate more than R$1 billion each. Five are already defined: Genérico Cimed, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth could be Urso or Milimetric.
