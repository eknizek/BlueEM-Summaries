# Cimed hits R$ 1 billion in quarter: 'we want to be Brazilian P&G', says CEO

João e Maria, Super and Milimetric fill the consumer portfolio of the pharmaceutical company

By Maria Luíza Filgueiras — São Paulo

João Adibe Marques enters the room with fresh data: he had just received IQVIA's quarterly ranking, showing Cimed beating competitors P&G and Nestlé in comparable consumer categories in March. "Revenue is similar, but look at our volume," the CEO points to his notebook screen. All three made around R$ 400 million, but Cimed sold three times as many items. The data is symbolic of the strategic movement Cimed has been making over the past two years and which has been gaining momentum—investments and brands.

In the pharmaceutical ranking, the company remains in sixth place and managed to grow by reducing production volume for the hospital channel to increase generic offerings. But it is in the consumer segment that the company sees greater diversification of audiences, from low to high income, and margins—it has already entered the children's hygiene, oral hygiene, and deodorant segments, and will now launch a supplement line called Urso.

"We want to be the Brazilian P&G," says the Cimed CEO. The company was encouraged by the performance of the Carmed lines, which became a sales phenomenon with the lip moisturizer, and created Milimetric, a cosmetics brand—such as hyaluronic acid—which made R$ 140 million in revenue last year and targets R$ 300 million this year. The company also created the João e Maria brand for children and, at the turn of the year, launched the Super deodorant and oral hygiene brand, which was boosted by marketing on the BBB reality show.

In the quarter, Cimed closed the acquisition of Ice Fresh, which had been producing its Super items but with intermediary charges—owning production saves 25% in costs, according to the CEO. Super generated R$ 150 million in the period, projecting annual revenue of R$ 600 million.

Performance in consumer goods helped Cimed achieve a first quarter with record growth for the period, with R$ 1 billion in revenue, up 20% compared to the same period last year, and 26% growth in Ebitda. "Sell-out increased 25%, higher than sell-in. This means the product left the shelf, not just the warehouse," he emphasizes.

Consumer represented 45% of revenue from January to March, taking a share from pharma. Marques draws attention to volume at the beginning of the conversation because this is a relevant definition—gaining market share even at the expense of some profitability. Selling cheaper than established competitors helps drive trial. "Why would you switch your deodorant if the price is the same? And if you don't like the smell, the effectiveness, if you get an allergy? Nobody will risk it if the price isn't attractive, and then we build loyalty with quality," he says. "I didn't win on the first sale. I won on the repurchase."

Entering recurring-use products didn't come without disruption. Cimed was accustomed to the pharmacy pace, where replenishment happens every 15 days, and discovered that deodorant and toothpaste turn over every two days in some outlets. "We had greater demand than our capacity, with more stock shortages than sales," says Marques, who adjusted logistics dynamics and is now negotiating the acquisition of an aerosol factory.

The consumer expansion has led Cimed to discuss whether it's worth separating the company into two. "These are completely different businesses. We're discussing whether a spin-off would be worthwhile," the CEO says.

The company projects R$ 4.8 billion in revenue for 2026 and has a goal of reaching R$ 10 billion by 2030. To do this, it has drawn up a plan to have 10 "superbrands" by then—those with potential to generate more than R$ 1 billion each. Five are already defined: Genérico Cimed, Carmed, Lavitan, João e Maria, and Super. The sixth could be Urso or Milimetric.
