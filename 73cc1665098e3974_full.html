# New Diesel Subsidy Rules Expand Uncertainties, Says Sector

The fuel sector received the new rules for diesel subsidies announced by the government this week with concern. The changes, according to executives interviewed by Folha, expand uncertainties and may deter companies. The rules, announced on Tuesday (14), were detailed in decrees published late Wednesday evening (15) and reinforce the government's offensive against profit margins of fuel distributors and gas stations.

The changes increased the subsidy value by R$1.20 per liter in an attempt to attract more companies to the program created to reduce the effects of the war in Iran on fuel prices—which still has low private participation.

As of Thursday (16), the ANP (National Agency of Petroleum, Natural Gas and Biofuels) had approved the participation of 19 companies, including the two largest fuel producers in the country: Petrobras and Mataripe Refinery. However, two of the largest distributors remain outside the program—Raízen and Ipiranga—as well as relevant private importers, such as distributors Atem and Saara.

For importers, the main concern is the obligation to "require the distributor to prove the discount was passed on," established by decree 12,930. The text states that the distributor must provide a declaration that it will pass on the subsidy and then prove it through invoices. "There is great concern about the risk of importers being held responsible for their clients' pricing practices," says Sergio Araújo, president of Abicom (Brazilian Association of Fuel Importers). "The intention is good, to ensure that discounts are passed through the entire chain, but it's very difficult for us to assume this commitment. We are thinking about how to do it, but it's a complicated task," he continues, saying the segment will seek clarification from the government.

For distributors, the main criticism falls on the obligation to send the ANP the evolution of their gross profit margin per product on a weekly basis. The requirement applies to all companies authorized to operate as distributors, including those that choose not to join the subsidy program. The ANP may disclose this information, considered strategic by companies. Distributors with capital traded on stock exchanges disclose their margins quarterly—this is the case of the three largest in the sector: Vibra, Ipiranga, and Raízen. However, sources argue that opening the numbers weekly transfers strategic business information to competitors.

Additionally, by opening the margins of all companies in the sector, the government could practically be opening space for "cartelization" of the sector, with all companies adopting similar profit margins.

The government began the period after the war started by criticizing distributors and gas stations for what were considered abusive price increases. In response, it coordinated joint action involving the ANP, Cade (Administrative Council for Economic Defense) and consumer protection agencies to monitor and punish irregularities. In the first weeks after the conflict began, distributor and gas station margins rose, but fell in the following weeks, according to data from the MME (Ministry of Mines and Energy). The distribution and resale margin for S-10 diesel sales, for example, reached R$1.17 per liter in the last week of March, but later fell to around R$1.00, the same level observed for most of 2025.

It is not possible to assert that the decline is a result of the government's offensive, but the sector has made public criticism of the pressure to control prices that emerged after the Iran war began. In a statement of "vehement repudiation" of the government campaign for the population to report gas stations, Sincopetro, which represents gas stations in São Paulo, says the initiative is "serious, irresponsible and technically unsustainable" and generates "undue distrust" of gas station owners.

"Gas stations are the final link in the chain and have no influence whatsoever on price formation in previous stages, especially in production, importation and distribution—where the main factors that impact the final value to the consumer are concentrated," it states.

Sindicom (National Union of Fuel and Lubricant Distribution Companies), in turn, released a statement Wednesday expressing "concern about the dissemination of content that could lead to a misunderstanding about fuel price formation." The entity argues that the historical average share of the distribution margin represents only 5 percent of the final price composition.

"In practice, it is a relatively stable slice with low participation in the formation of the final price to the consumer," it says.
