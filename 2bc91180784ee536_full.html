# Dollar Maneuvers: Central Bank Fined Currency Exchange Firm Linked to "Chiqui" Tapia $18 Billion and Revoked Operating License

The Central Bank decided to sanction the currency exchange house of Ariel Vallejo and revoked its authorization to operate in the market for manipulations with the official dollar in 2023 while exchange controls were in effect. It also imposed fines of $5.4 billion on Vallejo himself and $7.2 billion on his mother, a shareholder of the financial firm.

The board of the monetary authority approved last Thursday a sanction with operating disqualification to ARS Cambios, the currency exchange house that operated for Sur Finanzas. This is the financial firm under investigation by the courts for alleged money laundering and is pointed out as being linked to AFA president Claudio "Chiqui" Tapia.

The monetary authority, through the Superintendency of Financial Entities, investigated ARS Cambios for conducting suspicious operations with official dollars totaling 25 million dollars between January and December 2023, while exchange controls and reserve management were in effect. According to the Central Bank's hypothesis, these were used to supply the illegal market and thus take advantage of the exchange rate spread.

According to the investigation file, during that year the entity concentrated its activity on the buying and selling of foreign currency with other currency operators, for amounts exceeding $25 million USD, while sales to end customers represented only a marginal portion of the total. This dynamic led the agency to consider that the firm did not act as an intermediary for genuine demand, but as a cog within a broader circuit among entities.

In this context, the investigation argues that the operations configured a mechanism of "passing hands" of pesos and foreign currency without apparent economic justification. For the BCRA, the currency exchange house would have acted as a vehicle to channel funds within a circuit in which foreign currency could be withdrawn from the official sphere.

99.1% of ARS Cambios' operations with official dollars were directed to other currency exchange houses—Cambio Posadas, Fenus, Lagriet, Dibehim Gestiones San Miguel, Areco Cambios, Dos Arroyos, and Cambio Bacarat—"whose operations did not comply with applicable regulations for their activity," the Central Bank reported.

In this regard, the document indicates that the operations "would be aimed at acquiring foreign currency at official rates to later be destined to supply the parallel market."

According to the BCRA investigation file, the average spread at the time these operations occurred was 102%, so the presumed profit from the maneuver would have been 25.8 million dollars.

Those sanctioned with disqualification "will not be able to serve as partners or shareholders, promoters, founders, directors, administrators," and other relevant positions in financial or non-financial credit entities. That is, neither in traditional banks nor in virtual wallets or fintech companies.

For Ariel Vallejo, the disqualification is for 5 years and for Graciela Vallejo, for 6 years.

ARS Cambios was, strictly speaking, the name of the entity authorized by the BCRA to function as a currency exchange house. Sur Finanzas was how that financial firm presented itself in the market. It had registered offices with the Central Bank in downtown Buenos Aires, Monte Grande, Ezeiza, and Canning.

In recent months, the Central Bank conducted a broad investigation into manipulations with the official dollar in various currency exchange houses during the time of exchange controls in 2023. Illegal manipulations with official dollars could reach up to $1.5 billion USD, according to a calculation made by the national government.

The last investigation report published by the superintendency, before the currency exchange house linked to Sur Finanzas, was Stema Cambios, for an amount of $180 million USD. Before that, Mega Latina, Gallo Cambios, and Concordia also appeared.

It is a mechanism that from an important official office has been called "the little ladder." It is a circuit that, they believe, functioned very smoothly during those two years.

That "ladder" descended from a commercial bank that obtained dollars at official rates, sold them to currency exchange houses, and these, in turn, sold them to others. That passing of hands could be repeated again, and at a certain point in the circuit, cash withdrawals were made, which obscured the traceability of those dollars.

Estimates by investigators within the government calculated that in total, this scheme involved between 1.4 and 1.5 billion dollars. Of that total, traceability was lost for 84%, they calculate.

Toward the end of 2023, they assure from the Executive Branch, the maneuver stopped.
