# Milei's deterioration pressures bonds and amplifies market concern over Argentina

Concern is growing again in Argentina's bond market as the population shows signs of fatigue with President Javier Milei and his radical economic reform agenda. Just six months after Milei's party's surprising victory in midterm elections, concerns about his future have been reignited by polls showing his approval rating at its lowest level since he took office nearly two and a half years ago.

The libertarian president's approval fell to 35.5% in April, while disapproval rose to 63%, according to the LatAm Pulse, a survey conducted by AtlasIntel for Bloomberg News. These figures were 44% and 51.6%, respectively, at the beginning of the year.

Sovereign dollar bonds issued under local legislation and maturing in October 2028 — 12 months after Argentina's next presidential election — now yield 8.3%, a striking increase of 360 basis points compared to similar bonds maturing in October 2027.

The cost of default insurance on Argentina shows a similar pattern, increasing the further from the next election the maturity date. For investors, the risk represents a shift back toward policies considered less favorable to fiscal discipline, market access, and private investment.

"Electoral risk is evident across the entire yield curve, and the 2028 bond in particular is being affected by it," said Alejo Costa, head of economics and strategy at Buenos Aires-based brokerage Max Capital.

This risk has a name in local markets: Kuka, a combination of Kirchner, the surname of two former presidents, and the Spanish word for cockroach, cucaracha. It refers to the interventionist and populist policies that shaped much of the past two decades, including stricter currency and capital controls, regulated prices, higher public spending, and a more unpredictable political framework.

"I believe the Kuka risk is zero," Economy Minister Luis Caputo told investors on Tuesday. "There is no danger of returning to the past."

But the market disagrees. The higher premium investors demand to maintain exposure beyond Milei's current term is also visible in inflation-indexed bonds. In this segment, the yield curve has steepened sharply this month, with inflation-indexed bonds maturing in June 2028 rising over the last 30 days from 5.5% to 7.9%, while bonds maturing in June 2027 yield a negative real rate of 1.2%.

"Surveys showing a decline in Milei's popularity began to emerge, on average, and consensus began to shift in the direction that Milei's approval is deteriorating," said Costa.

Political and electoral factors are not solely responsible for this risk, given that sovereign debt payments accumulate between 2027 and 2033 and electoral uncertainty is increasing in the U.S., which contributes to the current support for the Argentine government and its bonds.

Public support for Milei has declined in the early months of the year as major economic sectors, including manufacturing, retail, and construction, have failed to keep pace with the recovery in exports following the 2023 and 2024 recession.

"People began to focus on elections because they are concerned about the fact that the economy is not growing," said Miguel Kiguel, director of local consulting firm Econviews and former treasury secretary. "Things may improve, but the main question is whether the pace of that improvement will be sufficient to change the mood of the population."

Argentina's economic activity fell 2.6% in February, the last month for which data is available, recording the largest decline in nearly five years. Inflation also remains high, with consumer prices up 32.6% in March compared to the same period last year.

Meanwhile, an index of public confidence in the government, compiled by Torcuato Di Tella University, fell for the fifth consecutive month in April, reaching 2.02 points on a five-point scale.

"We know the last few months have been difficult," Milei posted on X in early April. "That's why we ask for patience. This is the right path. Changing it would mean throwing away everything that has been achieved."

Credit Default Swaps (CDS) indicate a default risk of 5% for the next year, but this risk rises to 22% in three years and to almost 60% in the next decade, according to data calculated by Max Capital and Portfolio Personal Inversiones.

"All of this is being driven, in large part, by the side effects of the economic transition," said Pedro Siaba Serrate, head of research and strategy at PPI. "We are seeing a very unequal economy, with strong performance in primary product exports, which has a positive impact at the federal level, and at the same time, very little activity in sectors concentrated in the Greater Buenos Aires area, which are more labor-intensive."
