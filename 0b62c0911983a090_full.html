# Comfenalco Quindío contests SIC sanction and denies overpricing in Ferinject medication

Comfenalco Quindío responded to the sanction announced by the Superintendence of Industry and Commerce (SIC) and denied the existence of a 400% markup on the Ferinject medication, claiming it was an administrative error with no economic impact.

The entity stated that the irregularity pointed out by the authority originated from a typing error in its electronic billing system in 2023. "The alleged 400% markup did not occur," according to the fund.

According to the box, the inconsistency was not due to a pricing policy but rather a specific error in the coding of the product billed during that period. The core of the case centers on the incorrect assignment of the Ferinject medication code, which led to a distorted comparison during the investigation. A 2ml presentation that was no longer valid was recorded, instead of the correct 10ml reference that was actually sold to customers in the transactions.

This coding discrepancy caused the price charged to be compared with a regulatory ceiling corresponding to a product that had been off the market since 2014. "This coding confusion generated an incorrect comparison during the investigation."

The entity maintains that the 400% figure does not reflect the economic reality of the operations performed.

In regulatory terms, Comfenalco Quindío defended that its prices remained within the limits established by the National Commission for Prices of Medications and Medical Devices. The charging range for the medication oscillated between $407,554 and $413,760, aligned with the maximum cap set by the authority in Circular 13 of 2022. The fund emphasized that there was no breach of the price control regime, as the maximum permitted value was not exceeded. "Comfenalco Quindío operated within that limit at all times," they stated.

To support its defense, the entity indicated that it provided the SIC with a set of documentary and testimonial evidence supporting its version of events. These include certifications from the supplier, customer statements, and internal records seeking to demonstrate the correct traceability of the medication delivered during the analyzed period. The certifications submitted indicate that the reference associated with the incorrect code had no active record since 2014. "Customer statements attest to having received the correct medication at the corresponding price," they noted.

With this, the entity attempts to demonstrate that there was no economic impact for clinics, healthcare providers, or the health system in general.

Another key element of the case is the procedural status of the decision adopted by the authority. Comfenalco Quindío emphasized that the sanction is not final and that the process remains active under review within the SIC itself following the appeals filed within legal timeframes. This point keeps the controversy open.

From an institutional perspective, the fund expressed concern about the reputational impact resulting from the disclosure of the case at this stage of the process. "The public disclosure of a decision that is still being reviewed unfairly affects the reputation of an institution."

In that context, the entity reiterated that its actions have been aligned with principles of transparency and regulatory compliance in medication management. It also insisted that no actor in the health system, including patients or providers, suffered economic harm from the situation investigated by the authority.

The case, in addition to its legal outcome, raises questions about the robustness of billing systems and precision in medication coding. "There was no harm to the health system," they indicated.

Comfenalco Quindío concluded that it is confident that a comprehensive review of the evidence will clarify the facts as they occurred.
