# Peter Thiel's Visit: Argentina at a Manhattan Moment

The arrival of this magnate and one of Silicon Valley's leading symbols is a signal of how Argentina's insertion into artificial intelligence, technological defense, and food security is being viewed.

When Peter Thiel rents a mansion in Barrio Parque and lunches alone with a prominent political strategist, it is a signal of geopolitical intelligence: someone with $29 billion and direct access to the centers of power in Washington, Silicon Valley, and Israel has decided that Milei's Argentina deserves careful, in-person, and prolonged examination.

The name "Manhattan Moment" is revealing in itself: the visit by one of Palantir's co-founders occurs at the very moment when Javier Milei signs the "Isaac Accords" with Netanyahu in Jerusalem, while his government calls on companies to attend a Congress on Science, Technology, and Innovation in Defense. The threads connect with unusual clarity. All that remains is for Argentina to align with the Abraham Accords to attract potential trillions of dollars in investment from Saudi Arabia and the UAE.

## The 21st Century Manhattan Project

The metaphor of the "Manhattan Moment" is not gratuitous. In 1942, the United States concentrated in a single geographic point—Los Alamos, in the New Mexico desert—the intellectual capital, financing, and political and technological will that changed history. Today, the race is not for the atom but for data, algorithms, and the artificial intelligence supply chain. Thiel understands this better than anyone.

His company Palantir was built on the premise that 21st-century power does not reside in traditional military hardware but in the capacity to process, correlate, and act on large volumes of information. In a manifesto recently published by CEO Alex Karp—significantly reposted by Santiago Caputo—it is expressed unambiguously: "The hard power of this century will be based on software. The elite of engineers has the positive obligation to participate in the defense of the Nation."

Argentina enters this board with a combination of assets that few countries in the Southern Hemisphere can simultaneously exhibit: high-level human capital in exact sciences and engineering, competitive operational costs for talent, a privileged position in the Atlantic, and now a government ideologically aligned with the libertarian and techno-optimist currents that dominate the Silicon Valley ecosystem.

## The Strategic Triad

The positioning that the Milei government is building, with deliberate patience and sometimes with more intuition than formal planning, rests on three interdependent pillars that form a system of comparative advantages difficult to replicate in other latitudes.

The first is applied artificial intelligence. Argentina possesses one of the most sophisticated software developer communities in the Americas, with an academic tradition in mathematics and computer science demonstrated by companies like Satellogic, Invap, and the academic infrastructure of the Balseiro Institute and its nuclear development. The National Universities of Buenos Aires, Rosario, and Córdoba, among others, have converted Argentina into a potential platform of global scale. Also centers of excellence like San Andrés and ITBA.

Milei, on the anniversary of his government, explicitly spoke of a "perfect storm" to convert the country into an AI hub: available talent, low comparative costs, and regulatory openness. The presence of Thiel—whose view of the country, according to sources in his circle, includes the evaluation of "the entrepreneurial ecosystem and the potential for technological development"—suggests that this narrative has found heavyweight receptors.

The second pillar is technological defense and security. The Argentine government has taken steps that, viewed in isolation, seem disconnected, but that form an architecture: the signing of the "Isaac Accords" with Israel.

The third pillar is the least discussed in conventional geopolitical analysis but perhaps the most structurally relevant for Argentina's federal development: agrobioindustry as a platform for global food security. Argentina is one of the few countries in the world capable of feeding ten times its population. With the convergence of artificial intelligence, agricultural biotechnology, and data chains applied to agriculture—what the Anglo-Saxon world already calls "agri-tech" or "precision farming"—Argentina can convert its historical comparative advantage into a new generation competitive advantage.

## What Thiel Comes to See

Thiel's intellectual profile is well known: he does not invest in markets, he invests in disruptions. His central thesis, articulated in his book *Zero to One*, is that genuine innovation has no competition because it creates new categories. What he seeks in Argentina is not to participate in a cyclically recovering economy: he seeks to detect whether the conditions exist here for a structural leap.

Those conditions are, in his view, three: human capital capable of building transformational technology, a regulatory and political environment that does not slow the speed of innovation, and costs that make the bet competitive against other geographies. The Argentina of 2026 offers all three, although in a fragile way and conditioned to the stabilization underway.

His curiosity about the Superclásico at the Monumental is not anecdotal. According to those who know him, Thiel has a documented fascination with phenomena of massive scale.

The atoms of the Manhattan Project produced light and shadow in equal proportions. Argentina has today the opportunity and the ethical responsibility to choose with greater wisdom in what type of disruption it wants to participate and on what terms.
