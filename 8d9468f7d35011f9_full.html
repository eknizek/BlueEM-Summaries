# U.S. Corporate Earnings Push Back Wall Street's Wall of Worry

U.S. corporate earnings are offering Wall Street better-than-expected results, driving American equities to record after record. As earnings wrap up for two-thirds of S&P 500 stocks, the proportion of companies missing analyst estimates sits at its lowest level since 2021. This strength is not solely due to outsized profits from technology giants, which were expected to lead the charge. Non-technology S&P 500 companies have posted their largest positive earnings surprises since the fourth quarter of 2024, according to Seaport Research Partners.

For Wall Street investors, this represents a vote of confidence in the U.S. corporate profit machine, which continues humming despite oil price turmoil, tariff disruptions, and growing concerns about American consumer health.

"Looking at how companies have communicated their results, I would say resilient is almost too modest a word. There is real and evident strength," said Marta Norton, chief market strategist at Empower. "The foundations of the economy are proving to be very, very strong."

Strength is manifesting across all sectors. Small-cap stocks are on a roll, bank profits are booming, and companies continue navigating macroeconomic obstacles, though some concerns persist.

## Tech Spending Spree

Microsoft Corp., Amazon.com Inc., Alphabet Inc., Meta Platforms Inc., and Apple Inc.—which represent approximately one-quarter of total S&P 500 market capitalization—dominated headlines this week. Their earnings were generally better than expected, though Meta and Microsoft fell amid concerns about corporate capital spending plans.

Meanwhile, semiconductor stocks extended their rally. Intel Corp. surged 114% in April, aided by a revenue forecast that beat estimates. Texas Instruments Inc. was also a notable winner driven by earnings. After rising nearly 50% during an 18-session winning streak last month, the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index closed Friday at record highs.

More broadly, technology information services earnings within the S&P 500 grew nearly 50% per share, outpacing the 30% gain for the broader index. This is pushing analysts to raise price targets.

## Russell Roars

U.S. economic resilience and earnings growth are finally propelling riskier market stocks forward, after ups and downs in recent years. According to Keith Lerner, chief investment officer and chief market strategist at Truist Advisory Services, small-cap stocks are showing the kind of long-term momentum and rapid earnings growth that could set the sector up for sustained gains.

"We're seeing forward earnings estimates hit new highs every week," he said.

The Russell 2000 index is up year-to-date 2026, well ahead of the S&P 500's gains. Small-caps "do most of their business domestically and are therefore disproportionately benefiting from the strong U.S. economic environment," said Mike Dickson, head of research at Horizon Investments.

## Banking Boom

Major U.S. banks posted their most profitable quarter in history, with executives projecting confidence in the U.S. economy and record loan origination expectations. The KBW banking index rose 10% in April, the largest monthly gain since November 2024.

"This sector is ripe to capitalize on AI," according to Barclays analyst Jason Goldberg, who expects increased bank profits, including from Bank of America Corp. and KeyCorp.

At Wells Fargo & Co., CEO Charlie Scharf told investors that "the financial health of consumers and businesses remains strong." Consumers are spending more than last year, he said, and not just on gasoline.

Still, Bloomberg Intelligence's senior analyst Herman Chan cautioned about net interest margin outlook for the rest of the year, especially if the Federal Reserve refrains from cutting interest rates. "Banks are seeing decent credit activity, but not cutting rates means harder competition for deposits to fund loan growth."

JPMorgan Chase & Co.'s Jamie Dimon also sounded a cautionary note, warning that a credit market decline could be worse than expected.

## Oil Shock

Other concerns persist. Oil price swings are complicating guidance and shaking energy producers and oil-dependent companies. The energy sector plummeted in April after leading in March.

More than 70% of S&P companies that reported in April mentioned "Iran" or "oil," double the proportion that mentioned "tariffs," according to BofA. Corporate sentiment measures show "a glimmer of caution" creeping into earnings reports, even as fundamentals held, bank strategists said.

Exxon Mobil Corp.'s CFO Neil Hansen said that "part of the challenge in providing guidance is, as you might imagine, that we really don't know how long the Strait of Hormuz will remain closed." The oil giant beat expectations as crude prices surged, as did Chevron Corp.
