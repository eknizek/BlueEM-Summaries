One in four Colombians start businesses but most fail to sustain operations

17 April 2026 · 01:12 AM

# One in four Colombians start businesses, but most fail to sustain operations. What are the reasons?

In Colombia, entrepreneurship has ceased to be an exception and has become a constant. In the midst of a challenging economic environment and a transforming labor market, more and more people are choosing to forge their own path. According to the Global Entrepreneurship Monitor, cited in an Epson report—on the occasion of Global Entrepreneurship Day (April 16)—it is noted that one in four adults in the country is involved in creating or managing a business, one of the highest rates in Latin America. However, this dynamic coexists with a less encouraging reality. According to figures from Confecámaras and analysis of the business sector, only between 30 and 33.5 percent of companies manage to survive beyond five years of operation, which shows that the main challenge is not in starting a business, but in sustaining it.

In this scenario, access to financing appears as one of the main critical points. This is demonstrated by the case of ALA Global Colombia, a company in the foreign trade sector, whose executives explain that lack of liquidity directly impacts operations: "Not being able to respond with liquidity directly affected sales and put sustainability at risk. You have to pay suppliers abroad immediately and sometimes wait weeks or months for customer income," they comment. This type of situation is not isolated. In sectors with high working capital requirements, liquidity defines the ability to grow, compete, and even survive. But when that access is provided—and complemented with training—the results change significantly.

According to data from Bancamía, an entity of the BBVA Microfinance Foundation, microentrepreneurs who receive comprehensive support register growth of 15 percent in sales and 18 percent in surpluses, showing improvements in financial and operational management. The impact transcends the business realm. According to the same entity, 66 percent of entrepreneurs improve their income, 50 percent manage to exit the poverty line in two years, and one in four generates employment, positioning entrepreneurship as an engine of social transformation.

It's not pocket money

In this process, financial education becomes decisive. This is evidenced by Angélica Villanueva, an entrepreneur in the sustainable fashion sector, who after participating in training processes states that: "We realized there are correct ways to calculate profitability. The most important decision was to establish a fixed salary and formally separate accounts: to stop seeing business results as 'pocket money' and see them as a growth foundation." This change in mindset reflects a transition that forces a move from intuition to strategic management.

The way entrepreneurship is conducted is also changing. The rise of e-commerce has redefined the rules of the game. According to the NubeCommerce 2025/2026 report from Tiendanube, 73.5 percent of digital ventures in Colombia operate without a physical store, consolidating a "digital-first" model that reduces costs and barriers to entry. The same report reveals that 68 percent of these businesses are managed by a single person, which shows both the agility and fragility of the model. Still, optimism is maintained, as 85.1 percent of digital entrepreneurs project a positive outlook for 2026. Today, digital entrepreneurship is not just about selling more, but about being more efficient.
