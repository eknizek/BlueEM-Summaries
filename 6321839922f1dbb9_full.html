# Employment Crisis in Buenos Aires Suburbs: 3,000 Applicants for 60 Positions

The Buenos Aires suburbs are a faithful reflection of the lack of employment opportunities. The causes are varied, but the crisis in the industrial sector is the leading factor when discussing the new reality of unemployment and precarious employment.

"Join our team. At Cabaña Don Theo, our Moreno branch will be hiring personnel for all sectors. Apply with CV in hand on Wednesday the 6th at 11 a.m.," stated a notice distributed by a meatpacking plant that needed to fill 60 positions. The result surprised the plant owners, who never imagined receiving so many applicants, and also the potential future employees who arrived at the location only to find endless lines that stretched for up to 10 blocks.

The employment problem in the suburbs is not minor. However, there is data that supports what is happening. Fifty percent of industrial jobs are concentrated in Buenos Aires province, with a large portion belonging to the manufacturing corridors that flourished starting in the 1950s and 1960s. Today the reality is very different—for the country and the province in particular.

For example, at the end of the 1960s and early 1970s, Argentine industry was larger than China's industry. Argentina's Gross Domestic Product (GDP) was greater than Brazil's GDP. Today Argentina's GDP is smaller than Brazil's GDP and also smaller than the state of São Paulo's GDP.

Added to the collapse of much of the local industry over the last 50 years are government measures such as the removal of import tariffs, which expose industries to global competition that many sectors may no longer be able to withstand for various reasons.

The results are reflected in the numbers. According to the latest report from the Argentine Industrial Union (UIA), textiles, clothing, leather, and footwear today employ 12,923 fewer people than a year ago, representing a decline of 11.5% in those sectors. If the comparison is made from the previous peak of August 2023—just a few months before Javier Milei took office—the drop in employees in those sectors reaches 22,382.

During the same period, other sectors are also employing fewer people. In metalworking there are 18,931 fewer employees; in automotive and tires 6,950; in wood and paper 6,733; in chemicals and petrochemicals 5,416; in food and tobacco 558 fewer; and in other manufacturing 12,878. In total, across all these sectors, 72,731 jobs were lost.

If 50% of industry is concentrated in Buenos Aires province, it should come as no surprise that more than 3,000 people applied for 60 jobs. There's more: to the laid-off industrial workers must be added new generations who, by age, are seeking their first job.

The government has always been aware that the current situation could occur. First, because Milei has always said that he "takes care of the macro while entrepreneurs take care of the micro." Second, because Milei embodies a government that prefers competition and deregulation and is opposed to providing industrial subsidies. A fiscal surplus is part of the plan, and subsidies are counted as part of the deficit. In other words, the surplus is not a banner that Milei is willing to give up.

Added to this is another conviction: the government is betting that industrial jobs that are lost will be replaced by jobs in the services sector. The dilemma is how to ensure that jobs lost in industry are converted into quality jobs in the services sector.
