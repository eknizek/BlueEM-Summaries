# Fourth Transformation Government Resumes Fracking Development

In a reversal of the Fourth Transformation's energy policy, President Claudia Sheinbaum announced yesterday a project to exploit gas through "fracking" or hydraulic fracturing beginning next year. The goal is to increase natural gas production from 2.3 billion cubic feet per day to 8.31 billion by 2035, representing a 261 percent increase.

Pemex, which has never engaged in this type of exploration, will be the responsible company in an attempt to reduce Mexico's dependency on imported natural gas, which currently accounts for 75 percent of national consumption and comes from the United States. During her morning press conference, the President stated that this technique must be used under environmental criteria that guarantee environmental protection and responsible use of resources such as water. An expert commission will validate the process, she said.

Additionally, she acknowledged that Mexico would not stop importing gas, as the country currently requires significant quantities from abroad, specifically from Texas, which provides 80 percent of gas imports, and from California, which provides the rest. With this plan, the federal government aims to increase natural gas production from 2.3 billion cubic feet per day to 5.8 billion by the end of her administration and 8.31 billion by 2035.

According to Pemex, the country has 141.5 trillion cubic feet of gas in unconventional deposits that require fracking. Additionally, an estimated 83 trillion cubic feet could be obtained from conventional deposits.

The three zones where fracking could be used are the Sabinas-Burro-Picachos and Burgos basins located in Coahuila, Nuevo León, and Tamaulipas, and the Tampico-Misantla basin in Veracruz, San Luis Potosí, Hidalgo, and Puebla.

The previous government of Andrés Manuel López Obrador questioned hydraulic fracturing projects, arguing they pose environmental risks and made opposition to fracking a central theme of its energy policy propaganda. Now, to decide whether the country will use fracking to exploit unconventional gas, a scientific committee will be formed to advise the government on whether this is possible without causing environmental damage.

"The question is: do we exploit unconventional gas, yes or no? Why yes? Because it gives us more energy sovereignty. Why no? Because it has environmental impacts," Sheinbaum explained.

She added that for the past four months a team has been working on the issue and has traveled to Texas, California, and Canada to learn about new technologies for unconventional gas exploitation. She stated that the decision will be submitted to the opinion of a specialist committee.
