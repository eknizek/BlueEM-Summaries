# Azul Hires Embraer CFO for Executive Role: Life After Chapter 11

Antonio Garcia is leaving Embraer to assume the role of CFO at Azul, replacing Alex Malfitani, at a time when the airline is working to execute its restructuring plan after exiting Chapter 11.

Malfitani, one of the company's founders and CFO since operations began, will step down on the 20th after 18 years at Azul. According to a source close to the airline, Malfitani had already signaled his interest in departing after the restructuring was complete, and conversations to hire Garcia took place last month.

"The transition was coordinated and there was an agreement that the departure would only occur after the company's stabilization," the source told Brazil Journal. Azul's stock was up 1% in early afternoon trading.

Garcia — who joined Embraer in 2020 after ten years as CFO of Thyssenkrupp — led the financial restructuring of the São José dos Campos aircraft manufacturer during the pandemic and the fallout from the failed Boeing deal.

"I believe he could play a similar role at Azul, in the context of the company's financial restructuring," said Lucas Laghi, who covers both companies at XP.

The new CFO assumes leadership of an Azul that exited Chapter 11 in February, nine months after the process began. During negotiations, the company founded by David Neeleman reduced its financial debt by $6.7 billion and cut nearly R$10 billion in aircraft lease liabilities compared to 2024. Additionally, it completed a R$5 billion capital increase in February anchored by United Airlines, along with subscriptions from bond holders with maturities through 2030. As a result, Azul's leverage fell from 4.8x in the fourth quarter to 2.5x in February.

One of the renegotiations conducted by Malfitani was precisely with Embraer: the reworking of a firm order for E195-E2 jets made between 2014 and 2018. The order was reduced from 51 to 25 units. Now, Garcia is switching sides.

"Antonio's experience at Embraer, one of our most important partners, provides him with a unique perspective on our business," said John Rodgerson, Azul's CEO.

For the market, the CFO change occurs at a delicate moment — with distinct readings between Azul and Embraer. On the manufacturer's side, Garcia's departure does not change the investment thesis. The company is experiencing a strong demand cycle, with a backlog practically sold out through the end of the decade, and the succession should be absorbed internally without major impacts.

For Azul, however, the challenge is more structural. The airline industry remains one of the most difficult to operate — and the company's recovery is not yet consensus. According to one analyst's assessment, Azul's chances of resuming a sustainable trajectory of growth and profitability are "between 6 and 7 on a scale of 0 to 10."

At the same time, there is a more constructive medium-term outlook — including for Embraer itself. If Azul can recover, this tends to boost demand for regional aviation in Brazil — a segment where the Brazilian manufacturer is dominant.

According to a source close to Azul, the company's plan after Chapter 11 is to prioritize cash generation and capital discipline, slowing the pace of expansion and focusing on more profitable routes.

"I think Antonio can take advantage of this good moment, with lower leverage, and focus on delivering cash generation," said the source.

Azul is valued at R$12 billion on the stock exchange.
