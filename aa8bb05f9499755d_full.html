Imports Grow 7.8% in February Driven by Manufacturing and Machinery & Transport Purchases

21 April 2026 · 03:36 PM

# Imports Grow 7.8% in February Driven by Manufactures and Machinery & Transport Purchases

Imports in Colombia regained momentum in February 2026, growing 7.8% and reaching US$5,741.4 million, driven primarily by the performance of manufactures, according to official data from Dane. This evidences a reactivation of internal demand with direct effects on economic activity.

In this context, the monthly result not only confirms a rebound compared to the same month in 2025, but also reveals a change in the composition of foreign trade. Growth was explained primarily by an increase of 13.2% in the manufactures group, which continues to be the main driver of external purchases. Based on Dane records, manufactures concentrated 75.6% of total imports, consolidating their role as the axis of Colombian foreign trade, well above other sectors such as agriculture and fuels, which had significantly smaller shares.

Indeed, the agricultural, food and beverage group represented 13.7%, while fuels and extractive products reached 10.7%. This reflects an economy more dependent on imported industrial goods than on external raw materials, in line with recent consumption and investment patterns.

The performance of manufactures, with purchases of US$4,339.8 million, was particularly driven by key segments. The 28.6% growth in machinery and transport equipment was decisive in the aggregate result, accompanied by a 7.7% increase in miscellaneous manufactured articles, which also contributed significantly. This behavior suggests strengthening demand for capital goods and transport.

The increase in machinery imports is usually associated with investment and productive expansion processes, while vehicles reflect both consumption and business activity. However, not all sectors followed this dynamic. The fuels and extractive industries products group registered a decline of 10.8%, with US$611.6 million. "The behavior resulted from lower purchases of petroleum, petroleum derivatives and related products (-25.3%)," which reduced 18.9 percentage points from the group's variation. In the agricultural sector, behavior was also contractionary in February. Food and beverage imports fell 2.4%, with US$785.2 million, affected by the decline in oils and fats of animal and vegetable origin, a key segment within this group.

## Year-to-Date Performance

In the accumulated January and February 2026 period, the trend continues. Imports reached US$11,644.4 million, with 8.8% growth compared to the same period in 2025, consolidating a strong start to the year with greater commercial activity. The protagonism of manufactures continues in the accumulated figures. This group totaled US$8,924.7 million and grew 13.6%, driven again by machinery and transport equipment, which provided the bulk of sectoral growth. In contrast, the agricultural sector shows more favorable behavior in the accumulated period. Food imports grew 5.3% between January and February, driven by greater purchases of food products and live animals, "which contributed 8.9 percentage points to the group's variation."

Meanwhile, the fuels group deepened its decline in the accumulated period. Imports from this segment decreased 17.4%, reflecting lower dependence on external purchases of energy, particularly in mineral fuels and lubricants. "It contributed 19.8 negative percentage points to the total variation of the group," the document adds.

## Main Origins

In this scenario, analysis by origin of imports introduces a second relevant angle. China consolidates itself as Colombia's main supplier, with a 30.5% share in February, well above other trading partners. It is followed by the United States, Mexico, Brazil, Germany, Japan, and India, although with significantly smaller shares. This concentration evidences high dependence on a single country in the supply of imported goods, especially manufactures.

Growth in purchases from China was decisive in the monthly result. Imports from that country increased 24.5% and, together with Vietnam, contributed 7.4 percentage points to total growth, consolidating Asia's role in Colombian trade. "The increase in imports originating from China was explained by greater purchases of vehicles for the transport of persons, motorcycles and cellular telephones," the report specifies, highlighting key sectors. Vietnam also showed notable growth, with an increase of 60.3%. This suggests diversification within Asia as a strategic supplier, in a context of global reconfiguration of supply chains.

In contrast, some countries showed relevant declines. Imports from Canada decreased 29%, contributing negatively to the month's total growth, reflecting changes in the supplier structure. In the January-February accumulated period, the trend deepens. China maintains a 30.8% share and continues to be the main driver of import growth, followed by Mexico as another relevant actor. The behavior of the United States contrasts with this dynamic. Imports from that country fell 4.7% in the accumulated period, which reduced its contribution to growth and evidences a change in the trade relationship. "It was explained by lower external purchases of Gas oils (-68.8%)," the document states.
