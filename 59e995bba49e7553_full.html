US trade representative accuses Mexico of commercial barriers ahead of USMCA review

# US Trade Representative Accuses Mexico of Commercial Barriers Ahead of USMCA Review

Within the framework of the T-MEC review, the Office of the U.S. Trade Representative (USTR) identified a series of commercial and business barriers that exist with Mexico.

In the 2026 National Trade Estimate Report on Foreign Trade Barriers from the USTR, the U.S. agency stated that Mexico's regulatory processes are typically "opaque and inconsistent."

"Not all stakeholders have the opportunity to provide comments, and when input is provided, its acceptance and use is unequal," the document highlighted.

## On Customs

The report noted that in customs matters there is a more restrictive and punitive system. The United States emphasized that "significantly more information is required for each import transaction, while liability for penalties has increased drastically," which increases compliance costs.

It also indicated that "customs authorities have discretionary capacity to stop and seize shipments," which introduces logistical uncertainty and financial risks for exporters.

## On Telecommunications

The disappearance of autonomous agencies is another U.S. complaint. "It is important that the new CRT (Telecommunications Regulatory Commission) continues to apply existing asymmetric measures and takes additional necessary steps to address the dominant provider status as a preponderant economic agent and promote competition in Mexico's telecommunications sector," it stated.

## On Energy

In energy, the report noted that recent reforms consolidate a model that privileges state-owned enterprises and limits private participation. Among the most relevant changes, there is mention of reduced permit validity periods and new logistical restrictions that "increase operating costs and unfairly favor Pemex."

Additionally, companies in the country report debts exceeding 2.5 billion dollars from Pemex, which adds financial pressure and raises country risk in the sector.

## Technical Barriers

The U.S. government maintains its concerns about the harmonization of official standards because the regulation related to this process has not been approved, which creates a regulatory gap, so standards harmonized with the rest of North America cannot be officially adopted as Mexican Official Standards (NOMs).

Regarding railway transport, the USTR is concerned that Mexico requires the use of European standards instead of international or North American standards for signaling, dispatch systems, and electrical subsystems of passenger railways, as obstacles may arise for future integration of new and existing railway lines.

Finally, the report emphasizes that the United States monitors Mexico's compliance with its USMCA commitments to ensure that the country's agricultural biotechnology measures are based on scientific evidence and provide U.S. corn producers the market access that Mexico agreed to grant in the Treaty.
