# US Trade Representative Accuses Mexico of Commercial Barriers in T-MEC Review

In the framework of the T-MEC review, the Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR) pointed out a series of commercial and business barriers that exist with Mexico.

In the 2026 National Trade Estimation Report on barriers to foreign trade, the US agency stated that Mexico's regulatory processes tend to be "opaque and inconsistent."

"Not all stakeholders have the opportunity to provide comments, and when input is provided, its acceptance and use is unequal," the document highlighted.

## Customs

The report emphasized that customs matters involve a more restrictive and punitive system. The United States stressed that "significantly more information is required for each import transaction, while responsibility for sanctions has increased drastically," which increases compliance costs.

It also indicated that "customs authority has discretionary capacity to stop and seize shipments," which introduces logistical uncertainty and financial risks for exporters.

## Telecommunications

The disappearance of autonomous agencies is another US complaint. "It is important that the new CRT (Telecommunications Regulatory Commission) continues to apply existing asymmetrical measures and takes additional necessary measures to address the status of the dominant provider as a preponderant economic agent and promote competition in Mexico's telecommunications sector," it stated.

## Energy

In energy, the report noted that recent reforms consolidate a model that privileges state-owned enterprises and limits private participation. Among the most relevant changes, the reduction in permit validity and new logistical restrictions are mentioned, which "increase operational costs and unfairly favor Pemex."

Additionally, companies in the country report debts exceeding 2.5 billion dollars from Pemex, which adds financial pressure and raises country risk in the sector.

## Technical Barriers

The US government maintains its concerns about the harmonization of official standards because the regulation related to this process is not approved, creating a regulatory gap, so standards harmonized with the rest of North America cannot be officially adopted as Mexican Official Standards (NOMs).

In railway transportation, the USTR is concerned that Mexico requires the use of European standards instead of international or North American standards for signaling, dispatch systems, and electrical subsystems of passenger railways, as obstacles may arise for the future integration of new and existing railway lines.

Finally, the report highlights that the United States monitors Mexico's compliance with its T-MEC commitments to ensure that the country's agricultural biotechnology measures are based on scientific evidence and provide US corn producers with the market access that Mexico agreed to grant in the Treaty.
