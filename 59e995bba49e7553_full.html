US trade representative accuses Mexico of commercial barriers

19 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# US Trade Representative Accuses Mexico of Commercial Barriers

As part of the T-MEC review, the Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR) identified a series of commercial and business barriers that exist with Mexico.

In the 2026 National Trade Estimation Report on barriers to foreign trade by the USTR, the U.S. agency stated that Mexico's regulatory processes are typically "opaque and inconsistent."

"Not all stakeholders have the opportunity to provide comments, and when input is provided, its acceptance and use is unequal," the document stated.

## Customs

The report highlighted that customs procedures are more restrictive and punitive. The United States emphasized that "significantly more information is required for each import transaction, while liability for penalties has increased dramatically," which increases compliance costs.

It also noted that "customs authorities have discretionary capacity to stop and seize shipments," which introduces logistical uncertainty and financial risks for exporters.

## Telecommunications

The disappearance of autonomous agencies is another U.S. complaint. "It is important that the new CRT (Telecommunications Regulatory Commission) continue to apply existing asymmetric measures and take additional necessary steps to address the status of the dominant provider as a preponderant economic agent and promote competition in Mexico's telecommunications sector," it stated.

## Energy

In energy, the report noted that recent reforms consolidate a model that favors state-owned enterprises and limits private participation. Among the most relevant changes is the reduction in permit validity and new logistical restrictions that "increase operational costs and unfairly favor Pemex."

Additionally, companies report debts exceeding 2.5 billion dollars from Pemex, which adds financial pressure and increases country risk in the sector.

## Technical Barriers

The U.S. government maintains concerns about harmonizing official standards since regulations related to this process are not approved, creating a regulatory vacuum so that standards harmonized with the rest of North America cannot be officially adopted as Mexican Official Standards (NOMs).

Regarding rail transport, the USTR is concerned that Mexico requires European standards instead of international or North American standards for signaling, dispatch systems, and electrical subsystems of passenger rail, as this could create obstacles for future integration of new and existing rail lines.

Finally, the report highlights that the United States monitors Mexico's compliance with its T-MEC commitments to ensure that the country's agricultural biotechnology measures are based on scientific evidence and provide U.S. corn producers access to the market that Mexico agreed to grant in the Treaty.
