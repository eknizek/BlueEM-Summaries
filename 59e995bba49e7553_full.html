US commercial representative accuses Mexico of trade barriers

19 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# US Commercial Representative Accuses Mexico of Trade Barriers

Within the framework of the T-MEC review, the Office of the US Trade Representative (USTR) identified a series of trade and business barriers that exist with Mexico.

In the 2026 National Trade Estimate Report on Foreign Trade Barriers from the USTR, the US agency asserted that Mexico's regulatory processes tend to be "opaque and inconsistent."

"Not all stakeholders have the opportunity to provide comments, and when input is provided, its acceptance and use is unequal," the document stated.

## On Customs

The report highlighted that in customs matters, there is a more restrictive and punitive system. The United States emphasized that "significantly more information is required for each import transaction, while liability for penalties has increased drastically," which increases compliance costs.

It also indicated that "the customs authority has discretionary capacity to detain and seize shipments," which introduces logistical uncertainty and financial risks for exporters.

## On Telecommunications

Additionally, the disappearance of autonomous agencies is another US complaint. "It is important that the new CRT (Telecommunications Regulatory Commission) continues to apply existing asymmetric measures and takes additional necessary measures to address the status of the dominant provider as a preponderant economic agent and promote competition in the telecommunications sector in Mexico," it stated.

## On Energy

In the energy sector, the report noted that recent reforms consolidate a model that privileges state-owned enterprises and limits private participation. Among the most relevant changes, the report mentions the reduction in permit validity and new logistical restrictions that "increase operational costs and unfairly favor Pemex."

Additionally, companies report debts of more than 2.5 billion dollars from Pemex, which adds financial pressure and raises country risk in the sector.

## Technical Barriers

The US government maintains its concerns about the harmonization of official standards because regulations related to this process are not approved, which creates a regulatory gap, so standards harmonized with the rest of North America cannot be officially adopted as Mexican Official Standards (NOMs).

In rail transport matters, the USTR is concerned that Mexico requires the use of European standards, rather than international or North American standards, for signaling, dispatch systems, and electrical subsystems of passenger rail, as obstacles may arise for future integration of new and existing rail lines.

Finally, the report highlights that the United States monitors Mexico's compliance with its T-MEC commitments to ensure that the country's agricultural biotechnology measures are based on scientific evidence and provide US corn producers with market access that Mexico agreed to grant in the Treaty.
