Opinion: Platforms want to sell everything and take responsibility for nothing

29 April 2026 · 07:37 PM

# Opinion: Platforms want to sell everything and take responsibility for nothing

Medicine is not a t-shirt or a cell phone charger. It involves an active ingredient, dosage, contraindications, drug interactions, storage requirements, traceability, package inserts, sanitary registration, and the need for prescription and pharmaceutical guidance. When something goes wrong, it is not mere consumer dissatisfaction. It is harm to health. In extreme situations, it is a matter of life and death. Therefore, the central question is simple: who is responsible for what is sold?

In an article yesterday here on Brazil Journal, Deputy Leonardo Siqueira throws a lot of confetti at what he calls the "free market," but seems to disregard the risks to families, citizens, and public health with the "anything goes" sale of medicines on marketplaces. These platforms want to operate with scale, advertising, traffic, payment, logistics, and commissions. But they refuse to assume sanitary, legal, tax, and civil responsibility for what is commercialized in their environments. They want the bonus of intermediation without the burden of responsibility. That is the difference between a regulated market and a digital no-man's-land, as proposed by the deputy.

A simple search on digital platforms reveals the problem. Products with therapeutic appeal are sold as solutions for sexual dysfunction or weight loss, without clarity on composition, origin, or sanitary registration. Who manufactured it? What does it contain? Is there Anvisa authorization? Who guarantees safety? If someone takes this illegally offered medicine as a "deal" and has an adverse reaction, is hospitalized, or suffers consequences, the marketplace claims it has no responsibility whatsoever for it. In other words, the bill will fall to the consumer, often costing them their very life.

Recent cases involving weight-loss pens make this risk clear. These are medications with high clinical impact that require prescription, monitoring, and rigorous control. Yet counterfeit and irregular versions circulate widely in these environments. The increase in thefts of GLP-1 class medications, precisely because of their high value, mobilizes manufacturers, distributors, and authorities. It is not an isolated case, but an entire chain impacted.

The pharmaceutical industry itself is directly impacted by this illicit market. Without control of origin and traceability, digital platforms open an avenue for distribution of these products, aggravating a problem that is simultaneously sanitary and one of public safety.

Given this, responsibility cannot be diffuse. In a pharmacy, there is a CNPJ, sanitary license, responsible pharmacist, inspection, traceability, technical responsibility, and real possibility of closure. The same principle needs to apply in the digital environment. If a marketplace sells irregular, counterfeit, stolen, unregistered medicine, or with improper therapeutic appeal (as already happens), the sanitary authority should have the means to take it down immediately and hold all those who profited from the transaction accountable.

The comparison with the United States is frequently used in this debate dishonestly. Amazon Pharmacy is not an open marketplace where any seller advertises medications subject to sanitary surveillance. It is its own operation, with prescription validation, review by licensed pharmacists, and full responsibility for dispensation. There is no diffuse intermediation. There is control.

The European Union follows the same logic. There, illegal sale of medicines over the internet is treated as a serious threat to public health, and legal online pharmacies must be identifiable and verifiable through registration with national authorities. In several European countries, the online sale of prescription medicines can even be limited or prohibited according to local regulatory decisions.

Therefore, it is not true that the developed world simply "let everything go." In Brazil, recent legislation has already delimited this field. Law No. 15,357/2026, supported by Abrafarma, regulated the sale of medicines in supermarkets but established adequate sanitary conditions. This same law authorized the use of digital platforms by pharmacies for logistics and delivery, fully preserving sanitary responsibility in dispensation. And Abrafarma has already requested that Anvisa immediately regulate this logistics process. That is: the problem was never the supermarket, the app, or the internet. It is not about protectionism, but about guaranteeing population safety.

Brazil cannot accept irresponsibility disguised as innovation. Innovation is using technology to expand access, reduce costs, improve patient experience, and preserve safety. Irresponsibility is creating an environment where anything is sold by anyone with any promise, and then claiming the platform has nothing to do with it.

Brazil has 93,000 pharmacies that cover 99% of the country's cities and guarantee access not only to medicines but often to first healthcare, in municipalities that sometimes do not even have a Basic Health Unit. They respect sanitary rules, legislation, and inspections at all federative levels, and generate employment and income for cities. And marketplaces? They sell counterfeit, irregular products without Anvisa registration and refuse to even answer for this illegality. Yet they profit from advertising, ranking, payment, and logistics. There is concrete evidence of this. Private-label products from pharmacies already appear being sold by third parties in these environments without any link to manufacturers, indicating diversion or illicit origin. Anvisa itself recently seized irregular items in logistics centers of major platforms, including products without sanitary registration.

Free market is not a safe conduct for absence of responsibility and insecurity.

*Sérgio Mena Barreto is CEO of Abrafarma – Brazilian Association of Pharmacies and Drugstores.*
