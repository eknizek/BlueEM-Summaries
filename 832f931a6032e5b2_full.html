# Government Backs Down on Holding Down Electricity Bills. For Now

Yes, there will be an increase in electricity bills. Eight energy distributors received authorization today to raise their rates in the coming days, after the Government backed down—at least for now—from measures under study to ease consumer costs during an election year. The board of the National Electric Energy Agency (Aneel) approved tariff reviews and adjustments for concessionaires from the Energisa, Enel, CPFL Energia, and Neoenergia groups, with impacts of up to double digits. Last week, the agency had removed all tariff-related processes from the agenda, with some companies agreeing to postpone increases after a request from the Ministry of Mines and Energy—which said it was evaluating alternatives to mitigate the effects. The Government even discussed a Provisional Measure to enable a loan to distributors to allow smaller increases, but the plan fell through and Aneel decided to proceed with the adjustments. "The Provisional Measure was already being drafted, but there was considerable opposing pressure and they backed down, although it's not definitive," a source following the conversations told Brazil Journal. At today's Aneel meeting, Director-General Sandoval Feitosa said that tariff processes need to move forward given "contractual obligations." Without directly citing the Government's previous request for postponing the processes, Feitosa said that "there are recurrently conjunctural actions" to relieve tariffs, but "they don't solve the problem structurally." "At this moment Aneel fulfills its role of following contracts, ratifying tariff processes provided for in contracts."

With the processes reviewed today by Aneel, rates for Energisa Mato Grosso do Sul and CPFL Paulista will rise an average of 12.1%, while CPFL Santa Cruz will see an average increase of 15.12%. Other concessionaires like Neoenergia Cosern, Neoenergia Coelba, Energisa Sergipe, and Enel Ceará will have increases of around 5% for residential customers and 10% for high-voltage clients, such as industries. The Government's plans to contain the adjustments, possibly through a loan, had been criticized by experts because they would only postpone charges, which would later return to consumer bills with (high) interest. Following logic and mathematics, the most correct way to relieve electricity bills would be to change taxation or surcharges charged to consumers to fund public policies such as the Luz Para Todos program, Luz do Povo (free energy for low-income), and incentives for renewable energy. This path, however, would require legislative changes and would face resistance from the various segments currently benefiting from subsidies. The Government has already approved a law that sets a cap on the cost of these programs funded by the tariff, but this measure would be sufficient at most to maintain their weight on bills, not reduce it. Currently, surcharges that fund these public policies account for about 15% of the electricity bill, while nearly 20% comes from federal and state taxes.
