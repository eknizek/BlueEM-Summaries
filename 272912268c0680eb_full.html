# Rio is Negotiating with Turkish and Chinese Partners for Direct Flights at Galeão Airport

Istanbul, China, and San Francisco. These are, in order of probability, the three international routes that could be added to Galeão's schedule in the coming months. The negotiations are described as complex, but expanding Rio's air network is being treated as a priority for Invest.Rio – the city government company seeking to attract investments to the city.

"Tourism is a major driver of development for Rio and air connectivity is essential for the city to grow," Sidney Levy, president of Invest.Rio, told Brazil Journal. Rio received 2 million tourists last year, while Cancún received 10 million, which, according to Levy, leaves ample room for growth for the Marvelous City.

According to him, focus needs to be on three pillars: accommodation, events calendar, and air network, the third of which is currently the least developed in Rio. After more than three decades in the private sector, working for companies like De La Rue and Valid, Levy headed the organization of the 2016 Olympics in Rio – and says today's scenario is completely different from that time.

"Back then we made a herculean effort to increase the city's hotel room supply from 20,000 to 40,000, which was what the International Olympic Committee (IOC) required. Today we have 60,000 rooms and 20,000 Airbnbs, plus a much fuller events calendar," he said.

Meanwhile, Galeão was affected by the pandemic and political attempts to reduce its flight offerings in favor of Santos Dumont, at one point receiving only 6 million passengers per year compared to the 17.5 million recorded last year (when Guarulhos received 47 million).

"That's why we launched the New Routes program, which aims to support airlines willing to operate destinations not yet served in the city," Levy said. Designed to attract foreign airlines, the plan includes discounts on airport fees during the first months of route operation and short-term subsidies if the route takes time to take off – which initially caught the attention of Gol.

The Constantino family's airline has transformed Galeão into its hub in the country and announced routes to New York (debut July 8), Lisbon (September 16), Orlando (no date), and Paris (no date) in recent months.

But Invest.Rio hasn't given up on international airlines, and held the Routes event last month, which brought together over a thousand network planners in the city – the professionals responsible for mapping prices and passenger flows to define airlines' priority routes.

"There is great enthusiasm from foreigners regarding Brazil, but route development is a complex process involving logistics, maintenance, hub positioning, pilot training, and advertising," Sidney said. "These are decisions that carry a lot of risk."

The negotiation with Turkish Airlines to create a Rio-Istanbul route is the most advanced at the moment, which would give Rio a direct connection to Europe's second busiest airport and a new gateway to the East beyond the Rio-Dubai route already operated by Emirates.

After Levy's visit to China, Invest.Rio also started conversations to establish a route between Rio and the Asian giant, but negotiations have not yet reached a specific airline or destination.

The connection between Rio and the American West Coast, specifically San Francisco, appears to be the most difficult to achieve at the moment. "This route would be ideal to support the development of our AI City (the data center park under construction in Barra), but American airlines are reluctant," Sidney said. "There are issues that weigh on it, such as seasonality. In Northern Hemisphere summer, for example, it's unthinkable for them to take planes off routes to Europe, where Americans vacation."

Beyond the incentives, Invest.Rio is betting on Aena's arrival at Galeão as a new trump card at the negotiation table, since the Spanish operator maintains agreements with major airlines worldwide at airports like Madrid-Barajas and Barcelona's El-Prat.

Galeão currently operates direct flights to New York, Atlanta, Miami, Houston, and Dallas in the U.S., in addition to connecting Rio non-stop with major European and South American capitals, Panama City, Toronto, Montreal, and Dubai.
