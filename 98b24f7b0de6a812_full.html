# Nobel Economics Laureate: "Brazil's Political Debate Must Focus on Growth"

James Robinson, political science professor at the University of Chicago and co-author of "Why Nations Fail," a reference work on the role of institutions in economic development, and co-author of "The Narrow Corridor," visited São Paulo this month to participate in the healthtech conference Mevo. In an interview with Pipeline, he analyzes institutional erosion in the United States, Brazil's potential and obstacles, the fragility of the Chinese model, and the risks artificial intelligence poses to democracy — through the same lens that earned him the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2024.

**Pipeline: Considering today's world — Trump, war in the Middle East, China — what confirms, challenges, or surprises you about your thesis on why nations fail?**

Robinson: Three things. First: we are frequently accused of neglecting the global balance. We talk a lot about inclusive and extractive institutions and about the internal challenges of each country, but we talk very little about how one nation's institutions affect others. This is a moment when people are very aware of this — how less inclusive institutions in the United States can spread to Latin America, for example. Second: what we learned in the last year and a half is that there is far less commitment to American institutions than we would have thought. The decline of inclusion, the slowdown in social mobility, extreme polarization — people don't trust institutions, they're willing to believe lies and misinformation. This created space for Trump's political project. What surprises me is how much we underestimated the severity of this problem. Third: China's continued rise, which flourished because its economic institutions became much more inclusive from the late 1970s onward, but whose political institutions remain extractive. This combination cannot work, except temporarily. I keep saying it's going to collapse, but it's lasted 50 years. Are the United States experiencing institutional erosion or a transition to a new role for institutions?

Robinson: I think it's a moment of reconfiguration. Gunnar Myrdal, Nobel Prize-winning economist, wrote in the 1940s that Americans don't have an ideology — they have a creed. A package of things they believe in: liberal, democratic, anti-state, egalitarian. The problem is that some of these things are mutually inconsistent. Think of the Declaration of Independence: "all men are created equal." And the slaves? The central incoherence today is meritocracy. It was a philosophy that was supposed to combine equality with innovation and individualism, but it didn't. It led to a massive increase in inequality. Trump didn't cause this; he's a symptom of structural problems. The optimistic scenario, which I consider most likely, is that the United States will turn around, find a new narrative, reconcile these elements, and move forward for another 50 or 60 years. It's happened before.

**Is Brazil still the country of the future? Are we condemned to low growth or can we change without major disruption?**

Robinson: In "Why Nations Fail," we were optimistic about Brazil. We told the story of the rise of the PT, of participatory budgeting, of initiatives that seemed to make society more inclusive. But the problem is that once in power, the PT was absorbed by the system. The bigger project fell apart. Today there is this polarization, pro-Bolsonaro, anti-Bolsonaro, which won't help at all. The PT did many good things in terms of social protection — the universal health system, for me, is an extraordinary foundation. But you need a model of growth, of economic dynamism, and recognition that the private sector must play a critical role. In the last 250 years of world economic history, there is not a single example of an economically successful society without a dynamic private sector. This is true in 18th-century Britain, in the United States, in South Korea — the Chaebols, Samsung. In China, what happened? The socialists stopped trying to control everything and the private sector took off.

**What could Brazil learn from South Korea, for example?**

Robinson: An absolutely focused effort on making the economy work. If you go to Seoul, go to President Park's museum. That was his legacy — he was obsessed with economic growth and development, nothing else. Lee Kuan Yew in Singapore, Deng Xiaoping in China, all had this razor-sharp focus. And the message that South Korea absorbed from Japan was: there is nothing they do that others cannot do. It's possible to do it here. Brazil's political debate must focus on economic growth and on showing people what this country is capable of — it shouldn't be about identity questions, about abortion, about ideological themes that will certainly dominate electoral debate. From my perspective, there is no reason Brazil or any other country in Latin America couldn't have the same per capita income as the United States. It's about the history of institutions and political syndromes.

**Were you pessimistic about the Chinese economic model? Have you changed your mind?**

Robinson: My reading is somewhat controversial. Power tends to corrupt — absolute power corrupts absolutely, to quote Lord Acton. There is no precedent in world history of a truly successful economy with a totalitarian political system. China has been much better than the Soviet Union because it opened its economy and engaged with the world market. You have to produce things that people want to buy. But what you see in history is that concentrated and personalized power is always abusive, to the detriment of everyone and the economy. Xi Jinping is concentrating everything, turning his government into a cult of personality. He purged Politburo members, purged the head of the Armed Forces, wants to be surrounded by loyalists who say he's a genius. I've seen this hundreds of times in world history. China has unique cultural characteristics, like this idea of a common project, but still I think the same fate that befell the Mao era will befall the Xi era. I don't know how, but it will happen.

**What about artificial intelligence — does it strengthen or weaken the economy and democracy?**

Robinson: AI is obviously going to increase productivity and economic growth; you see it all the time. But it will have a tectonic effect on the labor market. Unlike the diffusion of computers, which was very complementary to skilled workers, AI will replace skilled workers. Not plumbers, not electricians, not those who repair gutters — they're not threatened. But people who work in law offices, at McKinsey, many skilled workers will have their jobs challenged. And this will lead to an enormous increase in inequality. The ownership of AI is extremely concentrated. All the benefits will flow to a very small number of people. It's like the Gilded Age, like the robber barons of the 19th century, Vanderbilt, Rockefeller. And institutions were strong enough to bring them under control. Will it happen again? I don't know. Mr. Musk giving hundreds of millions to influence elections cannot be good for democracy.

**Are big tech companies the new form of extractive elite?**

Robinson: Yes. And the problem is they don't understand that.
