# From Brazil to Peru: How El Niño Threatens Agriculture and Economy of Latin America

Latin America could face unequal impacts if a strong El Niño phenomenon occurs in the second half of 2026, with risks of inflation and slower economic growth due to impacts on agriculture and the energy sector. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration of the United States states that there is a 61% probability that the El Niño phenomenon will occur in the May to July quarter, above the previous forecast of 50%.

The World Meteorological Organization (WMO) informed Bloomberg Línea that, with the information available so far from global climate prediction centers, it is still not possible to confirm whether the current scenario will evolve into a strong El Niño event. However, the scientific community has warned of the importance of taking measures to prevent impacts if a large-magnitude event occurs, given "an increasing risk."

"In other words, we must continue to closely monitor the evolution of oceanic and atmospheric conditions, but it is still too early to affirm that a strong El Niño will develop," said Bárbara Tapia Cortés, meteorologist and technical coordinator of services at the WMO. "The most responsible approach for now is to speak of an increasing risk, not a certainty."

The standard classification distinguishes between weak, moderate, and strong events, according to the magnitude of temperature anomalies in the Niño 3.4 region of the central equatorial Pacific. "What official forecasts show is a growing probability that an El Niño event will develop during the second half of 2026, but there is still insufficient evidence to guarantee an extreme episode," said Tapia Cortés.

"If El Niño consolidates, its impacts on Latin America would not be uniform. Historically, and also in recent episodes, the most probable patterns vary according to the region," she stated.

If the El Niño phenomenon occurs this year with intensity similar to that of 2016, the main expected effect for Latin America is a deceleration of economic growth. "In countries in the Andean region, GDP could suffer a reduction of between 0.6 and 1.7 percentage points, as estimated in previous studies," according to analyst Brigitte Castañeda, researcher in the area of energy and climate risks and professor at the Department of Industrial Engineering at the University of the Andes in Colombia.

Depending on the intensity of El Niño, there are also forecasts of fiscal impacts, due to the need to allocate resources to respond to disasters that may occur and their effects in subsequent years, in a context of lower tax collection due to negative effects on the local economy. This is coupled with a possible increase in inflation, driven mainly by food prices, due to the impact on important sectors such as agriculture, livestock, fishing, energy, and also on infrastructure.

Dante Romano, professor and researcher at the Center for Agribusiness and Food at Austral University in Argentina, states that the impact of the El Niño phenomenon in Latin America would manifest through several channels. He mentions an increase in energy costs in the region due to lower hydroelectric generation (cheaper) and greater use of thermal energy at higher costs, higher logistics costs associated with possible transit difficulties through the Panama Canal, and lower food production.

According to the World Meteorological Organization, on the coasts of Ecuador and Peru, the El Niño phenomenon usually increases the risk of intense rainfall, flooding, and landslides. "The problem with this effect is that in regions where it rains, precipitation tends to be excessive, causing damage to infrastructure and having a negative impact on agriculture and fishing," said analyst Dante Romano.

In the Dry Corridor of Central America and parts of northern South America, El Niño is frequently associated with rainfall deficits, agricultural drought, water stress, and heat waves.

In the Amazon, northern Brazil, and the Pantanal, one of the greatest risks is the combination of scarce rainfall, high temperatures, fires, and low river levels, a situation that has already been observed with intensity in recent years.

Conversely, in regions of southern Brazil, Uruguay, Paraguay, and northeastern Argentina, El Niño usually brings above-average rainfall, increasing the probability of floods and inundations. "Brazil emerges as one of the most vulnerable countries, due to its extension and agricultural weight, with greater risk of drought, heat, and rainfall irregularity in the north and part of the northeast, while the south would be more exposed to above-normal precipitation, floods, excess humidity, and logistics problems," states Yedda Monteiro, intelligence and strategy analyst at agricultural consulting firm Biond Agro.

"In the Southeast and Center-West, there should be more heat and planting delays due to rainfall irregularity," said Monteiro, from Brazil.

## Agricultural Impacts

According to information from the World Meteorological Organization, one of the main effects of El Niño on agriculture is the alteration of agricultural calendars. El Niño can alter the start of planting, reduce soil moisture in some regions, cause flooding in others (water saturation), and increase thermal stress on crops and livestock.

It is estimated that Latin America's agricultural sector suffers approximately 26% of the damage caused by climate disasters and up to 82% in cases of droughts. The most vulnerable crops are usually corn and beans in Central America, especially in family agriculture in the Dry Corridor, as well as soybeans in various regions of South America, particularly when droughts or excessive rainfall occur during critical phases of the production cycle. Rice, coffee, cocoa, sugarcane, pastures, and livestock can also be affected, depending on the sub-region and the type of predominant threat.

For Bárbara Tapia Cortés, "what is most delicate is that the impact is not limited to agricultural yield." This can also translate into higher production costs, lower water availability for irrigation, damage to rural infrastructure, increased pests and diseases, and greater pressure on food security and the income of rural families.

Biond Agro indicates that in agricultural terms, impacts would translate into lower productivity due to water stress and excessive rainfall or abnormal heat. Additionally, deterioration of crop quality due to moisture in crucial phases, such as filling or harvesting, and operational disruptions caused by field delays, increased costs, and logistics problems.

## Energy Pressures

In Latin America, approximately 50% of energy comes from hydroelectric plants, implying vulnerability during droughts.
