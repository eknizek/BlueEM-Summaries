# Agrishow Reports 22% Decline in Deal Volume: 'We Are Living Through an Agricultural Storm,' Says Fair President

The organization of Agrishow, the international agricultural technology fair that ended this Friday, May 1st, in Ribeirão Preto (SP), announced that the five-day event generated R$ 11.4 billion in business deals, a 22% decline compared to last year's edition, when R$ 14.6 billion was recorded. This amount includes agricultural machinery, irrigation equipment, and storage equipment.

The recorded attendance was 197,000 people, the same as in 2025, despite many exhibitors' perception that there were fewer visits to their stands. The numbers were announced at a press conference attended by fair president João Carlos Marchesan, honorary president Maurílio Biagi, and Pedro Estevão, president of the Agricultural Machinery Sectoral Chamber of the Brazilian Machinery Association (Abimaq), one of the organizers.

For Marchesan, the results reflect market conditions. According to him, factors such as high interest rates, lower commodity prices due to dollar depreciation, and producer defaults limit credit capacity for investments in new technologies. "We are living through a storm for agriculture," he argued. He noted that conflicts in the Middle East also generate concern, prompting farmers to exercise caution.

The fair president, who was confirmed in his position for Agrishow 2027, also mentioned the R$ 10 billion program announced by Vice-President Geraldo Alckmin on the previous Sunday, May 26th, during the event's opening. Marchesan explained that there was a request to the federal government to release a special credit line before Agrishow took place, so producers could already use it. "Since operations haven't begun yet, this hindered things." The line announced by Alckmin should take effect within two weeks, and the interest rate has not yet been defined.

Pedro Estevão from Abimaq said that this year's fair decline is very close to the 19.9% decline recorded in the Brazilian agricultural machinery market in the first quarter of this year compared to the same period last year. Biagi declared that despite the reduction, he was positively surprised. "I expected even less."

For next year, the Agrishow dates have already been announced: May 26-30.

## Among Exhibitors

Among exhibitors, there was a perception of reduced foot traffic. However, according to them, visitors had a more qualified profile for investment and clear objectives.

At Prix/Toledo do Brasil, a traditional scale company, the expectation was for positive business closure, driven by buyers with whom negotiations were already underway. "We offered special conditions during the fair and brought clients here to close deals started earlier," said César Cabrera, regional manager of Toledo. Even if the final balance shows achievement of sales targets, Cabrera states that margins will be smaller. "We had to reduce profits because agribusiness, as a whole, is in a very tight reality."

Tritucap, a manufacturer of high-performance grinders, celebrated the results. The company returned to the fair after seven years and sold 31 machines, plus another 27 in advanced negotiation stages. Out of the fair since 2019 due to impacts from the COVID-19 pandemic, the company decided to resume participation, taking advantage of a scenario it considers favorable for the coffee sector. According to director Eduardo Alves Ferreira, sales exceeded expectations and already drive growth plans for the next edition. "We had a positive projection with the launch aimed at coffee, which is going through a good time, but the volume sold exceeded expectations by three times."

Kawashima, a Brazilian company of Japanese origin, which launched at the fair a grass cutter equipped with a 1.0 car engine aimed at family farming, is not yet discussing financial results, but Ivanildo Diniz, supervisor of the CCM Brasil brand (which owns Kawashima), evaluates that the contacts established at the fair open good perspectives for the rest of the year. "They were qualified contacts. We received visitors from various Brazilian states, such as Mato Grosso, Goiás, Pará, Piauí, Santa Catarina, and mainly Paraná, Minas Gerais, and São Paulo, regions where family farming is strong."
