# Levi's wants to double down in Brazil – and recruited a former Nike executive for it

Brazil is more on Levi's radar than ever. The iconic jeans brand that was born with the conquest of the American West has decided to accelerate its growth strategy in the country – just as it has been doing in other nations. With a global strategy focused on direct-to-consumer (DTC) – reducing the weight of department stores in sales – and expanding its product range beyond denim, Levi's has been showing growth that pleases the market. Proof of this: its stock has risen 37% in the last twelve months, above the 28% of the S&P 500.

Even without breaking down numbers by country, Levi's says Brazil is one of the markets showing the highest growth rates within its global operations. But the company realized it could boost this momentum even further. To lead this moment, the company recruited German executive Karsten Koehler, who spent 15 years at Nike commanding operations in Europe and Africa, and who has just taken over as general manager in Brazil. Over the last four years, Koehler led Fisia, Nike's distributor in Brazil, which belongs to the SBF Group, owner of Centauro.

"Brazil is growing at high double digits and, even so, we see room to double in size over the next three years," Koehler told Brazil Journal.

The strategy follows three clear fronts, aligned with global operations: physical store expansion, increased digital penetration, and product line expansion. Levi's opened eight new stores in Brazil in the last year – and promises to accelerate going forward. The company sees significant room to grow, mainly through franchises and in regions with little penetration, such as the North and Northeast. Koehler also wants to increase participation in shopping malls and outlets – its largest channels here and abroad.

"We have just over 100 stores today, and the potential is much greater, since there are over 600 shopping malls in Brazil," Koehler said. "We won't be in all of them, but this shows there is enormous potential."

The general manager said that outlets are the best way to reach consumers who like the brand but seek more accessible prices, in line with what is seen abroad. Koehler also sees room to increase digital participation: in addition to its own website, the company has only one official store on Mercado Livre – something the executive wants to change quickly.

To capture more sales, the company has been expanding its product mix beyond jeans: t-shirts, polos, dresses and accessories have become more frequent in its collections – all to increase average ticket size. "We want consumers to buy the complete look at Levi's," Koehler said.

To convince customers – especially women – Levi's has been making a move to stop being associated with basic fashion – 153 years after its founding. (For those unfamiliar with the company's history: Levi's was born in 1873, when German immigrant Levi Strauss began selling reinforced pants for gold mining in California. Jeans were born.)

The company recently hired Beyoncé to be the face of its new phase, with an exclusive collection called "The Denim Cowboy." The campaign was called Reimagine. In Brazil, they did something similar, choosing singer Ludmilla (who was called MC Beyoncé at the beginning of her career). Additionally, Levi's has been betting on "blue tab" products: more refined items, some made in collaboration with other brands, like Air Jordan. According to Koehler, products from the partnership with Michael Jordan's brand "sold out in minutes" on Levi's Brazil website.

"Levi's doesn't want to be seen as a basic brand, but as the brand where you find the greatest possible variety for your casual lifestyle," Koehler said.

The results of CEO Michelle Gass's strategy are showing in the numbers. In the first quarter, the company reported revenue of $1.74 billion, up 14%, while the bottom line advanced 26% to $177 million. The investment in DTC is also paying off: own channels now account for 52% of global sales. Three years ago, it was 42%.

The results were praised by the market. For UBS, the quarter was a typical "beat and raise," which is validating the company's strategy. Levi's is worth $8.5 billion on the NYSE.
