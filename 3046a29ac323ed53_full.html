# Bolivia evaluates return to global bond markets after four-year pause

Bolivia is sounding out investor interest to carry out its first dollar bond issuance in four years, after the new market-friendly government avoided external debt default in March. The South American country hired Deutsche Bank Securities and Santander to hold meetings with investors starting Monday to gauge interest in a reference-sized dollar bond, according to a person familiar with the matter cited by Bloomberg who requested anonymity because they are not authorized to discuss it.

The sale comes at a time when emerging market bond spreads have narrowed to near their lowest levels since 2013, despite the prolonged conflict with Iran causing economic damage globally. Meanwhile, the risk premium that investors demand for holding Bolivia's sovereign bonds fell to 378 basis points above U.S. Treasury bonds, its lowest level since 2020, according to JPMorgan Chase & Co. data.

"Bolivia is advancing in the implementation of constructive economic reforms and spreads have narrowed," said Sebastián Vargas, strategist at Seaport. "It is positive that Bolivia builds a liquidity cushion in dollars, demonstrates market access and perhaps repurchases shorter-maturity bonds."

Since taking office in November, President Rodrigo Paz's administration has cut fuel subsidies and met external debt payments, which led Moody's Ratings to upgrade the country's credit rating one notch and assign it a positive outlook. Still, the country faces liquidity problems, as it must face debt obligations of around $2.3 billion due this year and a decline in natural gas revenues. The government also requested up to $3.3 billion in financing from the International Monetary Fund.

"Undoubtedly, it is a timely moment to issue eurobonds," said Jason Keene, strategist at Barclays. "However, investors may need reassurances that negotiations on the IMF program are proceeding well."

Bolivian bonds maturing in 2030 rose half a cent on Monday to 97.5 cents per dollar, outperforming most comparable sovereign bonds, according to indicative price data compiled by Bloomberg.
