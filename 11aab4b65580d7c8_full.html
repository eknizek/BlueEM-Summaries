Global changes that will shape the pace of Chile's investment plans

24 April 2026 · 08:48 PM

# Global changes that will shape the pace of Chile's investment plans

When Señal DF celebrated its first year, we already anticipated important changes in the global economy. Unfortunately, the scenario of slower growth, greater protectionism, and weaker international institutions has come to pass. To this must now be added the changes unleashed by the war in Iran, which began on February 28th. Regardless of the speed or manner in which it is resolved, the conflict between the United States—in alliance with Israel—and Iran unleashes a series of changes that are relevant to the government's plans to promote the country's development.

## Greater competition for energy independence

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz—through which approximately 20% of global crude oil, liquefied natural gas, and fertilizers pass—exposed the fragility of the global energy system. Even with the fragile ceasefire in place, traffic through the strait remains well below pre-war levels. While several Asian countries adopted emergency measures to reduce demand—from four-day work weeks in the Philippines to fuel rationing in Southeast Asia—and Europeans study subsidies to contain fuel price increases, China's ample reserves position it as one of the winners, or at least among the least affected, by the crisis.

With approximately 1.4 billion barrels of strategic crude oil, accumulated at a rate of 1.1 million barrels per day during 2025, Beijing has the world's largest energy reserve: more than triple that of the United States. And although its reserves of critical minerals are a state secret, analysts calculate that China's inventories of copper, nickel, cobalt, and rare earths cover between 35% and 133% of the country's annual demand. In comparison, U.S. reserves cover less than 10% of essential civilian requirements.

The International Energy Agency (IEA) projects that China will maintain its crude oil purchase pace to continue building its reserves during 2026. Meanwhile, in Europe, experts are calling for accelerating the energy transition to reduce dependence on fossil fuels. "To reduce Europe's vulnerability to recurring geopolitical crises, it is essential to have clean and reliable energy of national origin. This has already been demonstrated in countries like Spain or Greece, where the increase in renewable energy's share has helped cushion the impact on electricity prices," said Zissis Marmarelis, senior researcher at the Chatham House think tank.

The security argument could even accelerate the energy transition in non-oil-producing countries. This movement will translate into renewed interest in nuclear energy, but also greater demand for copper and lithium. For Chile, the message is twofold. On one hand, the expected increase in demand for its two main mining commodities. On the other, a signal to follow similar steps: reduce its own dependence on imported fossil fuels and, with it, its vulnerability to geopolitical events that destabilize global prices.

## The race for copper and rare earths

The vulnerability of supply chains coincides with accelerated artificial intelligence (AI) expansion, defense investments (due to geopolitical conflicts), and a renewed space race. All are industries requiring critical minerals. Schneider Electric calculates that a 1GW data center consumes approximately 65,800 tons of copper. If we add the growing demand from expanding emerging economies, S&P Global projects that copper demand will increase 50% to 42 million tons by 2040. According to BloombergNEF, by 2035 the accumulated copper deficit will be 6 million tons. S&P Global raises the expected deficit to 10 million tons by 2040.

Analysts at Canaccord Genuity warn that the lithium industry would face a supply deficit already this year and could extend through 2035 due to delays suffered by new investments during the price downturn. In rare earths, the IEA warns that at least $60 billion in investments are required "now" to begin building a rare earth supply chain less dependent on China. Currently, that country controls 60% of rare earth mining production, 90% of its refining, and 95% of magnet production.

Of recent investment projects, such as USA Rare Earths' acquisition of Brazil's Serra Verde, almost all concentrate on mining extraction. Meanwhile, countries are launching a race to accumulate inventories. The U.S. announced "Project Vault," a $12 billion plan to accumulate critical minerals; Australia allocated almost $1 billion in a reserve of rare earths, antimony, and gallium; the EU advances in its own joint reserve under the RESourceEU strategy. Congo and Zimbabwe recently announced limits on their lithium exports and other key minerals like cobalt and germanium to build strategic reserves and force the development of material processing industries.

## Competition is closer

While greater mineral demand favors Chile's plans, it also encourages competition. Peru is experiencing its biggest mining investment boom in decades, and while Chile saw its production fall in 2025, its neighbor's increased moderately (1.2%). Going forward, Argentina emerges as another rival. Javier Milei's government has captured investment commitments of $33 billion since introducing the special investment regime (RIGI) in mid-2024, concentrated in copper and lithium, with 12 mining projects already approved for over $26 billion.

Meanwhile, Brazil has rushed to capitalize on the U.S. and European interest in reducing dependence on China to boost its mining industry. Vale has pushed its nickel production. The Sigma Lithium complex is positioning itself as the world's fifth largest in lithium oxide concentrate production after starting commercial production just in 2023. Additionally, the country already has 27 rare earth projects in development, having ignored exploitation of its reserves—the world's second largest—for years.

This more intense regional competition forces Chile to rediscover what was once its natural advantage. Political stability, legal security, and fiscal discipline—the three attributes that for decades made Chile the default mining jurisdiction in Latin America—have eroded over the past 10 years. Recovering them is no longer enough: they must be accompanied by modernization and acceleration of permitting processes.

## China advances while the U.S. is distracted

While Washington concentrates diplomatic, military, and political resources in the Middle East, Beijing quietly accelerates a long-term strategy: technological self-sufficiency, mineral accumulation, consolidation of its own supply chains, and expansion of influence in other emerging countries. Last January, Beijing published its third official roadmap for Latin America and the Caribbean since 2008, identifying artificial intelligence, telecommunications, renewable energy, hydrogen, mining, and mineral processing as priority sectors.
