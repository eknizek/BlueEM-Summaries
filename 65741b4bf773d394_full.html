# Brazil Can Be a Rare Earths Powerhouse—If It Avoids Creating a State-Owned Company

Brazil has the potential to become a rare earths mining hub and develop a local refining chain, replacing China in this market for much of the Western world—but the idea of creating a state-owned company to manage the sector, TerraBrás, could jeopardize everything.

That's the view of Rafael Moreno, CEO of Viridis Mining, a company listed on the Australian stock exchange that is betting heavily on a project in Poços de Caldas, Minas Gerais, with billion-dollar investments and projected production start in 2028.

"If Brazil wants to become a strong player in rare earths, technology needs to come here. And to build this value chain in the country, you need to bring foreign investment," Moreno told Brazil Journal. "You cannot rely on a state-owned company, otherwise capital won't come. And not just for rare earths. This applies to lithium, nickel, all other critical minerals."

Beyond mining, Viridis's plan involves attracting the sector's processing chain to the country. The company is already in talks with industries operating in Brazil to supply them in the future with rare earths that will be extracted and refined locally.

"This is the opportunity for Brazil to supply not just the domestic market, but South America, North America, everywhere. And become like China," Moreno said.

Among potential customers with whom Viridis is in contact are electric vehicle manufacturers with operations in Brazil and equipment manufacturers such as wind turbine producers.

"I have 120% confidence that this local industry will buy this locally once the chain is here. But this won't happen if we start with TerraBrás and other things."

Currently, Congress and the Government are discussing the creation of a National Policy on Critical and Strategic Minerals. Deputy Arnaldo Jardim is the rapporteur on the issue in the Chamber, and he intends to present his proposal for discussion on May 4. The PT, President Lula's party, supports the creation of TerraBrás to manage the sector—although recent reports suggest the idea is unlikely to move forward. The PT also supports a production-sharing regime for these minerals, similar to the model adopted in the pre-salt oil sector.

For Viridis's CEO, attracting investments in the rare metals chain requires first ensuring confidence that mining will occur. State control of the sector would generate uncertainty.

"I don't believe that will happen. I'm very confident that there will be a consensus that it's not the right thing to do."

The decision also needs to be made soon, as Brazil competes with other geographies that are racing to develop the segment, including Australia and Africa. And there's no room for everyone.

"The rare earths market is small. It's not like iron ore. Oil is a market worth trillions of dollars. In rare earths, we're talking about something in the billions," Moreno said.

For now, Viridis has already invested about $50 million in its operations in Brazil, for which it expects to obtain the environmental installation license in the second half of this year. Until operations, estimated to begin in the first half of 2028, reaching full capacity by year-end, the company expects to spend another $370 million.

According to the current schedule, Viridis understands that its project will be the next rare earths operation to begin the implementation phase not just in Brazil, but in the world.

To finance the work, Viridis is in talks with BNDES, while also negotiating potential loans linked to production sales contracts. Brazil's pioneering rare earths project, from Serra Verde—which belonged to private equity firms Denham Capital, Energy & Minerals Group, and Vision Blue and was sold to USA Rare Earth—obtained financing from agencies linked to the American government associated with mineral supply (offtake). Until then, production was being sold to China.

In Viridis's case, the plan is to pursue supply contracts outside the Asian giant from the start, which today controls the sector's exploration and refining chain.

"It's not that we have anything against China. But China said, because of Trump, that it will stop supplying rare earths to the rest of the world, not just the US. Now everyone is terrified of this dependence on them. The West realized it needs to dissociate itself and diversify this chain away from China."

Viridis signed the acquisition of rights to reserves in Poços de Caldas in August 2023. The so-called "Colosso Project" has ionic clay reserves, with simpler and cheaper processing than hard rock rare earths. Additionally, the region is known for mining, meaning there is local labor and suppliers, plus access to infrastructure like roads.

"The project has one of the largest ionic clay reserves and highest grade in the industry, and with low operational costs, positioning it to be one of the most profitable in the world," said Guilherme Lopes, head of Capital Markets and Investor Relations at Viridis.

Listed on the Australian stock exchange since 2022, the company has a market value of approximately 350 million Australian dollars ($250 million). Among shareholders, however, about 30% of investors are Brazilian, as are several directors.
