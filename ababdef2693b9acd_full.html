Pescado Raúl alfajor brand gains traction amid Argentina's economic crisis, with stocks depleted at retail outlets

18 April 2026 · 07:36 PM

# Pescado Raúl, the alfajor that depletes stocks in kiosks amid the crisis

When an Italian machine arrived around 1976 with the inscription Fantoche, the Diéguez and López families finally found the name for their brands, including the triple alfajor. Unlike many sectors, the Dielo firm, a union of the Diéguez and López surnames, operates at its Buenos Aires plant in Lugano with 400 employees 24 hours a day, seven days a week. At the 26,000 square meter facility, demand grows, curiously in step with tight pockets. A triple alfajor is available in kiosks for $1,300 and replaces lunch for many.

The López family sold their share, and the second and third generation of the Diéguez family continues, having started in a general store when their family fleet of 60 trucks distributed everything from food to sandals throughout the country. The president is Antonio Diéguez. They process 1.5 million alfajores daily. CEO Claudio Messina has just incorporated another innovation, this time with Joaquín Levinton. The leader of the band Turf wanted to produce his own alfajor and partnered under the brand "Pescado Raúl," the alter ego that made him a phenomenon during his time on the cooking reality show MasterChef. "We deplete stock every day with sales reaching 70,000 units," Messina says. The next leap is exportation. They are already in Uruguay and Paraguay, and ship via distributors to Europe and Brazil.

Martín Soto, a finance expert, and his wife Brenda Pietraccone, who holds a degree in Public Policy, met when they were officials at the IDB. After the pandemic, they launched their own consulting firm, which they named Yinyang in obvious reference to the harmony and complementation between the feminine and masculine. Their credentials, after many years of experience in a multilateral organization, allow them to advise on preparing projects to access looser financing and longer terms from international organizations. In Argentina they assist Mendoza in education, health, digital transformation, and water and sanitation. In Chaco, in logistics for improving its ports. They also work for Santa Fe on a product development program and on roads leading to ports. They have more work in Río Negro, Córdoba, Jujuy, and Formosa. Yinyang opened offices in El Salvador and another in Mexico. In Bukele's country, via CAF (Andean Development Corporation), a unit was created to structure various programs, from digitization and telemedicine to early childhood literacy.

The River case is one of the most striking and has become a spearhead for other football clubs. They were responsible for managing and structuring an unprecedented $100 million financing with BID Invest and CAF for the expansion and roofing of the Más Monumental Stadium. The project incorporated a social component: the club committed that 25% of additional revenue would go to scholarships, expansion of the River Plate Institute, and the youth residence Casa River.

Gonzalo Rioja, 33 years old and a civil engineer from the National University of La Plata, joined his friends Agustín Albiero and Santiago Smoris to create a solution for the hotel business. They named it We Speak after discovering the lack of response speed from establishments facing customer requests in a highly competitive business. In 2024 they created a tool with Artificial Intelligence agents that allows them to become independent from Booking and in a short time they added a clientele of 700 hotels in 20 countries. 43% are in Argentina, including the Howard Johnson and Days Inn chains from the Albamonte family. The business model is based on a commission of $1.47 per reservation. Rioja says they are profitable and self-financed.
