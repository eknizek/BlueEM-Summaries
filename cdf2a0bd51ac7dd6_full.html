26 April 2026 · 11:00 PM

# Peruvian Household Consumption Loses Momentum: What Are Peruvians Spending with Their Credit Cards?

The rise in prices has caused household spending to enter a moderation phase, with less momentum than in previous months. Here are all the details.

Household consumption in Peru continues to grow at double-digit rates, although it has lost some momentum in March. Last month, BBVA Research's Big Data Consumption Index—which measures card usage and cash withdrawals, adjusted for inflation—grew 13.2%, a figure lower than those recorded in previous months. While the acceleration in consumption in previous months was mainly associated with withdrawals of pension funds, this boost has begun to fade. This is compounded by the increase in annual inflation in March (3.8%), surpassing the target range of the Central Reserve Bank of Peru (BCRP), which is between 1% and 3%.

The rise in prices last month had a direct impact on household purchasing power, affecting consumption in categories linked to transportation and food, explained Vanessa Belapatiño, senior economist at BBVA Research. "This price increase occurred due to pressure from the El Niño phenomenon on food and the rise in fuel prices caused by the rupture of the natural gas pipeline in the country, as well as geopolitical conflicts that led to increased global oil prices," she commented.

Despite these setbacks, the BBVA Research report indicates that family spending continues to be supported by consumer credit, which maintained growth above 10%, as well as by the strength of the labor market and income in the first months of the year. In the first quarter of 2026, the Big Data Consumption Index grew 17.4%, well above previous quarters. "While the rates of this indicator are much higher and more volatile than the private consumption indicator from the BCRP's national accounts, we are also expecting a small acceleration compared to the fourth quarter of 2025 for private consumption," Belapatiño added.

## What Did Households Spend More on in March?

Looking only at purchases with credit and/or debit cards, increases were recorded in nearly all components (except residential public services), but with nuances. According to the BBVA Research report, only two categories remained dynamic: education (6.5%) and entertainment (35%).

In the case of education, higher spending was mainly associated with payments to higher education institutions amid the start of the academic year. In fact, 7 out of every 10 soles spent in this category correspond to universities and institutes.

On the entertainment side, spending was sustained by purchases of cinema, theater, and event tickets, as well as spending related to casinos and lotteries. These services represent more than half of what is spent in entertainment businesses. This behavior suggests that, despite pressure from rising prices, households still maintain spending capacity for non-essential activities.

"In entertainment, particularly event tickets, this is what has been improving. The supply of concerts doesn't stop, and if you're reducing other expenses like tourism, you can redirect that spending to occasional outings," explained Belapatiño.

Beyond these categories, one sector that stood out by advancing at the same pace as the previous month was "other purchases." This category grew 14.7%, driven by purchases of electronic products.

## Categories That Grew Less

Spending in other businesses—both essential and non-essential—continued to grow, although at a slower rate. One of the most affected sectors was food, which went from growing 10.1% in previous months to 6.2% in March due to a moderation in purchases at supermarkets and restaurants. In line with the increase in fuel prices, there was also a slowdown in transportation consumption, which grew 15.6% in March when it had previously reached 19%. It's worth noting that fuel consumption represents 6 out of every 10 soles spent in this category.

Beyond these categories, sales also lost momentum in tourism (6.3%), health (14.3%), fashion and beauty (20.5%), and home (23%).

## Will Spending Continue to Adjust in April?

For the coming months, a normalization phase is expected in spending by Peruvian households, which would be reflected in the Big Data Index, which could stop growing at double-digit rates. After the peaks recorded due to factors such as pension fund withdrawals, Belapatiño believes that between April and May household consumption "could be returning to growth rates of between 5% and 10%."

One of the factors explaining the estimated moderation is the depletion of pension fund withdrawals. Although the disbursement process concludes in May, the strongest deposits were made between November and February, leaving households with less cash availability for the second quarter of the year.

Additionally, consumption behavior would continue to be impacted by fuel prices, which drive inflation. "Tracking fuel prices for consumers in the first week of April, we have not yet seen declines, especially those related to oil. We would expect moderation in April," Belapatiño stated.

Higher fuel prices would continue to affect the transportation sector. Meanwhile, climatic factors linked to the El Niño phenomenon could continue to pressure food prices and affect tourism performance if conditions worsen.

Looking ahead, analysts will continue to monitor the evolution of formal employment, as—although it remains in positive territory—recent data from Metropolitan Lima shows a slight moderation and could imply changes in consumption in the medium term.
