Volatility plummets and 'carry trade' gains additional momentum

24 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Volatility Plummets and 'Carry Trade' Gains Additional Momentum

DoubleLine Capital and VanEck are among investors who see renewed interest in a strategy gaining momentum as a ceasefire in the Middle East helps stabilize markets and reignite appetite for risk. The carry trade — borrowing in countries where interest rates are low and investing in those offering higher rates — had already been thriving while war drove oil prices soaring, strengthening commodity currencies like the Brazilian real and Colombian peso.

Now, however, the strategy is being driven by the relative reduction in international tensions, which has led to a sharp drop in volatility across currencies, bonds, and equities.

This environment gives investors more confidence that exchange rates won't suffer abrupt swings against their positions, as occurred in 2024, when the strategy collapsed and shook markets widely.

Banks like Citi and Goldman Sachs, which have been recommending different versions of the strategy for weeks, maintain their bets. A common version of the trade — borrowing in yen to buy a basket composed of the real, Colombian peso, and Turkish lira — has accumulated gains of about 12% in 2026, the best start to a year in three years.

"Carry has returned to dominate," said Luis Estrada, strategist at RBC Capital Markets. "Markets recovered losses too quickly for most investors to keep up, so they're migrating from hedging strategies to a search for yield, while volatility continues to decline."

The reduction in market turbulence has driven operations across various asset classes. In the bond market, bets that Treasuries will outperform interest rate swaps — a strategy that tends to perform better in low-volatility environments — gained strength with the ceasefire, while the S&P 500 index hit a historic record this week.

Rising Optimism

"The perception that the war is coming to an end has become the base case, though it may be excessively optimistic," said Anna Wu, multi-asset strategist at VanEck. "In particular, South American currencies and interest rates are very strong right now thanks to their link with commodities, which makes them attractive for carry trade strategies."

At RBC, Estrada suggests betting on the appreciation of the Brazilian real against the yen and Chilean peso. Citigroup's recommendations include the Mexican peso, the real, and the Turkish lira, while Goldman Sachs strategists have advised clients to bet on an equally weighted basket of currencies — including the lira and Nigerian naira — against the dollar.

At Bank of America, John Locascio, head of currency options trading for Latin America, stated that hedge funds have focused on short-term operations betting on the appreciation of the Brazilian real, while asset managers have been entering longer-term operations through digital options. These contracts offer a fixed payout if the real reaches a predetermined higher level, functioning, in practice, as an all-or-nothing bet.

Locascio also stated that the Colombian peso has gained "significant traction" in carry trade strategies in recent weeks, despite higher volatility and lower liquidity in the currency. The peso is among the currencies benefiting from expectations that higher oil prices will support energy-producing economies, at the expense of countries dependent on imported fuels. And although oil prices have pulled back from their peak during the conflict, they remain well above pre-conflict levels, preserving this dynamic.

"Emerging market currencies of energy exporters outside the Middle East, especially those with high real interest rates, may continue to stand out," said Valerie Ho, portfolio manager at DoubleLine. "The real has established itself as one of the market's favorites."

Fragile Situation

Of course, the volatility scenario could change in an instant, given the fragile nature of the situation in the Middle East. U.S. stocks fell on Thursday and oil rose as tensions increased with the stalemate in peace negotiations. On Friday, signs emerged of a possible second round of talks between Tehran and Washington.

For Jamie Patton of TCW Group, all of this suggests that investors are being excessively complacent.

"People are loading up on risk in shallow waters," said Patton, co-head of global rates at TCW. "Regardless of where you look, risk is being priced too low."

Another concern is that the popularity of the strategy only increases risk for both traders and the economies benefiting from these flows. Concentrated bets in emerging markets — and, on the other side, short positions in currencies like the yen — could trigger a rush for the exit depending on global events or changes in macroeconomic expectations.

A striking example was August 2024, when a policy change from the Bank of Japan penalized carry trade operations, causing sharp yen appreciation and shaking global markets. A report from the International Monetary Fund released this month highlighted the growing sensitivity of hedge fund performance to carry factors in emerging markets in recent years. This suggests that unwinding these positions — normally associated with speculative capital that can exit quickly in times of stress — could pressure developing economies.

For now, however, investors seem to be betting that the peace initiative will prevail. In the currency market, a JPMorgan volatility indicator declined after reaching its highest level in several months at the end of March, and is now near lows since January. This reversal is positive for carry trade because investors using the strategy depend on capturing small yield gains over time — returns that can be quickly wiped out by exchange rate swings. Thus, the calmer the markets, the more attractive the strategy becomes in risk-adjusted terms.

"Markets always look ahead to the outcome, and carry trade is an opportunity to make money in an environment of greater risk appetite," said George Boubouras, head of research at hedge fund K2 Asset Management. "But we could see some reversals in the next 30 to 90 days, possibly because the strategy has already performed very strongly."
