# Volatility collapses and 'carry trade' gains additional momentum

DoubleLine Capital and VanEck are among investors who see renewed interest in a strategy that is gaining momentum as the ceasefire in the Middle East helps stabilize markets and reignite risk appetite. The carry trade — borrowing in countries where interest rates are low and investing in those offering higher rates — had already been thriving while the war caused oil prices to spike, strengthening commodity currencies like the Brazilian real and Colombian peso.

Now, however, the strategy is being driven by the relative reduction in international tensions, which has led to a sharp drop in volatility across currencies, bonds, and stocks.

This environment gives investors more confidence that exchange rates will not suffer abrupt swings against their positions, as happened in 2024, when the strategy collapsed and shook markets broadly.

Banks like Citi and Goldman Sachs, which have been recommending different versions of the strategy for weeks, are maintaining their bets. A common version of the operation — borrowing in yen to buy a basket composed of the real, Colombian peso, and Turkish lira — has accumulated gains of around 12% in 2026, the best start to a year in three years.

"Carry has returned to dominate," said Luis Estrada, strategist at RBC Capital Markets. "Markets recovered losses too quickly for most investors to follow, so they are migrating from hedging strategies to a search for yield, while volatility continues to decline."

The reduction in market turbulence has driven operations across various asset classes. In the bond market, bets that Treasuries will outperform interest rate swaps — a strategy that tends to perform better in low-volatility environments — gained strength with the ceasefire, while the S&P 500 index hit a historic record this week.

**Rising optimism**

"The perception that the war is coming to an end has become the base scenario, even though it may be overly optimistic," said Anna Wu, multi-asset strategist at VanEck. "In particular, South American currencies and interest rates are very strong right now thanks to their connection to commodities, which makes them attractive for carry trade strategies."

At RBC, Estrada suggests betting on the Brazilian real appreciating against the yen and Chilean peso. Citigroup's recommendations include the Mexican peso, the real, and the Turkish lira, while strategists at Goldman Sachs have advised clients to bet on an equally weighted basket of currencies — including the lira and Nigerian naira — against the dollar.

At Bank of America, John Locascio, responsible for currency options trading in Latin America, said hedge funds have focused on short-term operations betting on the appreciation of the Brazilian real, while asset managers have been entering longer-term operations through digital options. These contracts offer a fixed payment if the real reaches a previously determined higher level, functioning in practice as an all-or-nothing bet.

Locascio also said the Colombian peso gained "significant traction" in carry trade strategies in recent weeks, despite the currency's higher volatility and lower liquidity. The peso is among the currencies benefiting from expectations that higher oil prices will support energy-producing economies, to the detriment of countries dependent on imported fuels. And although oil prices have declined from their peak during the conflict, they still remain well above pre-conflict levels, preserving this dynamic.

"Emerging market currencies of energy exporters outside the Middle East, especially those with high real interest rates, may continue to stand out," said Valerie Ho, portfolio manager at DoubleLine. "The real has established itself as one of the market's favorites."

**Fragile situation**

Of course, the volatility scenario could change in an instant, given the fragile nature of the situation in the Middle East. U.S. stocks fell on Thursday and oil rose as tensions increased with the stalemate in peace negotiations. On Friday, signs emerged of a possible second round of negotiations between Tehran and Washington.

For Jamie Patton of TCW Group, this all suggests investors are being overly complacent. "People are loading the boat with risk in shallow waters," said Patton, co-head of global interest rates at TCW. "Regardless of where you look, risk is being priced at levels that are too low."

Another concern is that the popularity of the strategy merely increases risk for both traders and the economies that benefit from these flows. Concentrated bets in emerging markets — and, on the other side, short positions in currencies like the yen — could trigger a rush for the exit depending on global events or changes in macroeconomic expectations.

A striking example was August 2024, when a policy change by the Bank of Japan penalized carry trade operations, causing sharp yen appreciation and shaking global markets. A report from the International Monetary Fund released this month highlighted the growing sensitivity of hedge fund performance to carry factors in emerging markets in recent years. This suggests that the unwinding of these positions — typically associated with speculative capital that can exit quickly in times of stress — could pressure developing economies.

For now, however, investors seem to be betting that the peace initiative will prevail. In the foreign exchange market, a JPMorgan volatility indicator declined after reaching its highest level in several months at the end of March, and is now near the lowest levels since January. This reversal is positive for the carry trade because investors using the strategy depend on capturing small yield gains over time — returns that can be quickly wiped out by fluctuations in exchange rates. Thus, the calmer the markets, the more attractive the strategy becomes in risk-adjusted terms.

"Markets always look to the outcome ahead, and carry trade is an opportunity to make money in an environment of greater risk appetite," said George Boubouras, head of research at hedge fund K2 Asset Management. "But we could see some reversals in the next 30 to 90 days, possibly because the strategy has already performed very strongly."
