# Venezuela Bonds Surge Following U.S. Measure to Facilitate Debt Negotiations

Venezuelan sovereign and Petróleos de Venezuela (Pdvsa) bonds surged after the U.S. Department of Treasury authorized the South American country to hire specialized advisory services for a potential restructuring.

The license enables legal and financial services for both the government and Pdvsa, including the possibility of designing concrete proposals to renegotiate liabilities. The scope of the measure includes "the evaluation, development or preparation of debt restructuring options, proposals and related support materials," which opens a formal channel to structure offers to creditors.

In the secondary market, sovereign bonds maturing in 2027 advanced 5.41% to 55.53 cents per dollar, their highest level in nine years, according to indicative prices compiled by Bloomberg, while Pdvsa bonds maturing in 2037 rose 4.1% to 40.1 cents per dollar.

The price movement has also been accompanied by a shift in expectations about the future of Venezuelan debt following Delcy Rodríguez assuming as Acting President of Venezuela after Nicolás Maduro's capture.

Despite the measure, a possible restructuring of Venezuelan debt faces challenges. S&P Global Ratings noted that "a credible economic plan linked to the recovery of oil production, along with solid fiscal and monetary policies, is key to the success of restructuring negotiations."

The magnitude of external liabilities remains a central factor in the valuation of assets, including sovereign and quasi-sovereign obligations estimated between US$150 billion and US$170 billion.

Production expectations are also central to the analysis. The sector aspires to reach 1.5 million barrels per day by 2027 and 4 million by 2035, although S&P Global estimates that "it is more likely that Venezuela will achieve production increases of 500,000 to 700,000 bpd in the next two to three years, reaching a potential of 2.5 million to 3.0 million bpd in a decade."

The restoration of relations between Venezuela and the United States has involved a progressive reopening of diplomatic and economic channels, including the reactivation of the U.S. embassy in Caracas and direct contacts with the Venezuelan government. Additionally, contacts with the International Monetary Fund have also opened the door to a possible evaluation of the debt, although access to international financing will not be automatic.
