# Do Central Bank Rate Hikes Justify Colombia's Economic Emergency Declaration?

The increase in interest rates by the Bank of the Republic has opened a new front of tension between the Government and the Central Bank. In an address, President Gustavo Petro questioned the central bank's decision and warned that its effects are already reflected in a lower growth projection for 2026. According to the president, the economic growth estimate fell from 2.8% to 2.6% following the recent rate increase.

In his intervention, he argued that the monetary policy adopted by the Bank does not adequately address the origin of current inflation. "We know, and the data shows it, that mediating inflation with the monetary rate, when it is due to supply reasons like war, is not reason to raise the interest rate," he stated.

In response to what he considers negative effects of monetary policy, the president announced a package of measures aimed at mitigating the impact on productive sectors. Among these, he highlighted the implementation of fertilizer subsidies with the objective of reducing costs in agriculture and facilitating food production. He also announced the expansion of subsidized credit lines for the agricultural sector, with compensated rates and greater availability of resources.

The third axis of the measures focuses on access to credit for small and medium-sized enterprises. Through Bancóldex, the Government seeks to offer preferential conditions with subsidized rates, with the purpose of sustaining productive activity amid a more restrictive financial environment.

The president also warned that, if these actions are insufficient, the Executive could resort to extraordinary tools. "If the measures are not enough, we will go to another economic emergency," he stated. He also announced the presentation of a new financing bill that will be attempted to process "with this or with the new Congress."

## Is It Justified?

The Government's questioning occurs in a context where inflation continues above the Bank of the Republic's target. Although the Colombian economy does not present severe macroeconomic imbalances, inflationary expectations remain above 6%, far from the 3% objective. In this scenario, the rate increase primarily responds to the anchoring of these expectations, in an environment where factors such as recent adjustments in the minimum wage have contributed to maintaining price pressures. Monetary policy seeks, in this context, to contain inflation and prevent it from consolidating at high levels.

From the private sector, various voices have defended the central bank's technical role. The president of Asofondos, Andrés Velasco, explained that the Issuer's decisions do not respond to political interests, but to rigorous analysis of multiple variables. "This decision has been made for more than 30 years in Colombia based on very serious analysis by the technical team of the Bank of the Republic," he stated.

Velasco noted that the Board of Directors evaluates the state of the economy, expectations, and external shocks before defining the rate policy. "The state of the economy, expectations, and the shocks the economy receives are analyzed," he explained.

He also warned about inflation's impact on households, especially those with lower incomes. "There is no worse tax than inflation because it is a regressive tax that affects all low-income people," he said.

The debate has also extended to the fiscal front. Juan David Ballén, director of Economics and Markets at Aval Asset Management, questioned the possibility of declaring an economic emergency in response to the current situation. "As we have been warning, the economic emergency is not only unnecessary, but also inconvenient in the current context of the country," he stated.
