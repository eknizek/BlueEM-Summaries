# Opinion: The Credit Card That Everyone Blames — and Almost No One Understands

I read several articles this week about the indebtedness of Brazilian families, almost all pointing to the credit card as the main culprit. I decided to delve into the data and conduct my own analysis. The result surprised me — not for what the numbers confirm, but for what they reveal when read carefully. There is a contradiction embedded at the center of this debate that is rarely named with precision.

The instrument with the highest interest rate in the national financial system — the credit card revolving balance, which reached 436% per year in February 2026 — is also the instrument that the absolute majority of its users utilize without paying a single cent in interest.

I must say upfront: as a citizen, I do not accept that such disproportionate interest rates exist and burden those already in financial difficulty. But this legitimate indignation cannot replace analysis. And it reveals a picture far more complex than public debate has acknowledged.

The credit card is not one product. It is three distinct products that coexist under the same plastic.

The first is the spot transaction: the consumer buys, the bill comes due, and is paid in full. Zero financial cost.

The second is interest-free installment transactions, a modality practically exclusive to Brazil among G20 economies: the consumer divides a purchase into three, ten, or twelve installments with no additional charge. The credit is real, but the cost is zero for the cardholder.

According to Abecs data, 57% of the value transacted on credit cards in Brazil is spot and 43% is interest-free installments. Together, these two modalities represent 12.4% of the total debt balance of families with the financial system — and contribute zero to income commitment.

The third product is the revolving balance and bill installments with interest — the one activated when the consumer cannot pay the full amount at maturity. This is where the 436% per year lives, the bill installment with an average rate of 189% per year according to the Central Bank — although the total accumulated charges are capped at 100% of the original debt value by Law 14.690/2023, as I explain below — and the delinquency of 63.5%, according to the Central Bank in February 2026.

A delinquency at this level is not an indicator of normal credit risk: it reveals that for a relevant portion of users, the revolving balance functions not as a transitional cash relief, but as a difficult-to-escape trap — which reinforces, rather than contradicts, the urgency of more effective regulation.

It is also here that analytical confusion begins: by citing the revolving rate as the reference for the credit card, the debate treats as representative what is, in practice, a minority segment of instrument use. The numbers from the Central Bank and Abecs speak for themselves.

In February, revolving credit represented 2.7% of the total debt balance of families with the financial system — a figure confirmed by the Abecs president at the Electronic Payment Methods Congress in April this year. The bill installment with interest adds another 2.0%. Together, the two products that dominate journalistic coverage of indebtedness total 4.7% of families' debt balance.

The BC publishes monthly the income commitment of families: the relationship between debt service and Families' Gross Available National Income. In January, this indicator reached 29.3%, the highest level in the historical series that began in 2005.

Reading the table dispels the most frequent misconception in the debate. The revolving balance, with the highest interest rate in the system — 436% per year — represents 2.7% of the debt balance and compromises 0.3% of families' income. The installment with interest, at 189% per year nominal rate, represents 2.0% of the balance and compromises another 0.3%. Together, 0.6%.

Housing financing, with a rate of 9.5% per year, compromises 2.2% of income. And unsecured personal credit — which does not dominate headlines — represents 8.3% of the balance and compromises, according to my estimates, about 4.5% of income. That is seven times more than the revolving and interest-bearing installment combined.

The block of payroll loans, vehicles, and other modalities, despite operating at an estimated weighted average rate of 25% per year, accounts for the remaining 22% of income commitment — spread over much longer terms.

The contradiction is structural and has a precise explanation. The revolving balance has a maximum term of 30 days by regulatory determination — which limits its weight in the debt stock. The brake introduced by Law 14.690/2023 and regulated by the CMN in December of the same year adds an element that public debate has not yet incorporated with due attention: the total interest and charges accumulated in the revolving and bill installment cannot exceed the original debt value.

Before the law, the free capitalization regime prevailed — debt could grow indefinitely as long as there was a rate. After it, there is a wall. An important caveat: the rule applies to operations contracted from January 2024 onwards, and the stock of previous debts remains under the old regime — the full transition is still underway. This does not diminish the progress. It makes it, however, incomplete.

What the debate should focus on with greater precision is unsecured personal credit — a modality that operates at 117% per year, with an average term of 27 months, represents 8.3% of the total debt balance of families, and compromises, according to my estimates, about 4.5% of income.

It does not appear in the headlines. It does not have a 436% rate. But it drains income silently and durably, over a period that can be twenty times longer than the revolving balance.

However, there is an argument that cannot be omitted from any honest analysis of the credit card: there is no such thing as a free lunch.

Spot credit and interest-free installments are free for the cardholder — but have real cost in the system. In Brazil, it is the retailer who provides the funding for this mechanism: receives payment in 30 days, or every 30 days in the case of installments over several periods, while the consumer has already taken the product. When needing liquidity before that deadline, they pay an additional discount to advance the receivables to financial institutions.

All of this cost — the discount rate charged by the acquirer and the financial cost of the term — tends to be passed on, to varying degrees, to product and service prices. Part is absorbed in retailers' margins; part is transferred to the end consumer.

Law 13.455/2017 created the instrument of the discount for spot payment precisely to mitigate this effect — but its adoption is still limited in Brazilian retail.

The result is a mechanism of cross-subsidy: those who pay upfront in cash or via Pix tend to finance, via pricing, those who installment in twelve interest-free times on the card.
