# Opinion: The Credit Card That Everyone Blames — And Almost No One Understands

I read several articles this week about the indebtedness of Brazilian families, almost all pointing to the credit card as the main culprit. I decided to delve into the data and conduct my own analysis. The result surprised me — not for what the numbers confirmed, but for what they reveal when read carefully. There is a contradiction embedded at the center of this debate that is rarely named with precision.

The instrument with the highest interest rate in the national financial system — the credit card revolving credit, which reached 436% per year in February 2026 — is also the instrument that the vast majority of its users utilize without paying a single cent in interest.

I must say from the outset: as a citizen, I do not accept that such disproportionate interest rates exist and burden those already in financial difficulty. But this legitimate indignation cannot substitute analysis. And it reveals a more complex picture than public debate has acknowledged.

The credit card is not one product. It is three distinct products that coexist under the same plastic.

The first is the cash transaction: the consumer buys, the bill comes due and is paid in full. Zero financial cost. The second is installment transactions without interest, a modality practically exclusive to Brazil among G20 economies: the consumer divides a purchase into three, ten, or twelve installments with no additional charge. The credit is real, but the cost is zero for the cardholder. According to Abecs data, 57% of the value transacted on credit cards in Brazil is cash and 43% is interest-free installments. Together, these two modalities represent 12.4% of the total debt balance of families with the financial system — and contribute zero to income burden.

The third product is the revolving credit and bill installments with interest — the one activated when the consumer cannot pay the full amount by the due date. This is where the 436% per year lives, the bill installment with an average rate of 189% per year according to the Central Bank — although the total accumulated fees are limited to 100% of the original debt value by Law 14.690/2023, as I explain below — and delinquency of 63.5%, according to the Central Bank in February 2026.

Delinquency at this level is not an indicator of normal credit risk: it reveals that for a relevant portion of users, the revolving credit functions not as a transitory cash relief, but as a difficult-to-escape trap — which reinforces, rather than contradicts, the urgency of more effective regulation.

It is also here that analytical confusion begins: by citing the revolving rate as a reference for the credit card, the debate treats as representative what is, in practice, a minority segment of the instrument's use. The numbers from the Central Bank and Abecs speak for themselves. In February, revolving credit represented 2.7% of the total debt balance of families with the financial system — data confirmed by the Abecs president at the Electronic Payment Methods Congress in April of this year. Interest-bearing bill installments add another 2.0%. Together, the two products that dominate journalistic coverage of indebtedness account for 4.7% of families' debt balance.

The BC publishes monthly the income burden of families: the relationship between debt service and the Gross Available National Income of Families. In January, this indicator reached 29.3%, the highest level in the historical series that began in 2005. Reading the table clarifies the most frequent misconception in the debate. Revolving credit, with the highest interest rate in the system — 436% per year — represents 2.7% of the debt balance and burdens 0.3% of families' income. Interest-bearing installments, at 189% per year nominal rate, represent 2.0% of the balance and burden another 0.3%. Together, 0.6%. Housing financing, at 9.5% per year, burdens 2.2% of income. And non-consigned personal credit — which does not dominate headlines — represents 8.3% of the balance and burdens, by my estimates, approximately 4.5% of income. That is seven times more than revolving and interest-bearing installments combined. The consigned, vehicle, and other modalities block, despite operating at an estimated weighted average rate of 25% per year, accounts for the remaining 22% of income burden — spread over much longer periods.

The contradiction is structural and has a precise explanation. Revolving credit has a maximum term of 30 days by regulatory determination — which limits its weight in the debt stock. The lock introduced by Law 14.690/2023 and regulated by the CMN in December of that year adds an element that public debate has yet to incorporate with due attention: the total interest and fees accumulated in revolving and bill installments cannot exceed the original debt value. Before the law, a free capitalization regime prevailed — the debt could grow indefinitely as long as there was a rate. After it, there is a wall.

An important clarification: the rule applies to operations contracted from January 2024 onward, and the stock of prior debts remains under the old regime — full transition is still underway. This does not diminish the advance. It makes it, however, incomplete.

What the debate should focus on with more precision is non-consigned personal credit — a modality that operates at 117% per year, with an average term of 27 months, represents 8.3% of the total debt balance of families, and burdens, by my estimates, approximately 4.5% of income. It does not appear in headlines. It does not have a rate of 436%. But it drains income silently and durably, over a period that can be twenty times longer than that of revolving credit.

There is, however, an argument that cannot be omitted in any honest analysis of the credit card: there is no such thing as a free lunch. Cash credit and interest-free installments are free for the cardholder — but have real costs in the system. In Brazil, it is the merchant who provides the funding for this mechanism: receives payment in 30 days, or every 30 days in the case of installments, while the consumer already took the product. When needing liquidity before that period, they pay an additional discount to advance receivables from financial institutions. All this cost — the discount rate charged by the acquirer and the financial cost of the period — tends to be passed on, in varying degrees, to the prices of products and services. Part is absorbed in merchants' margins; part is transferred to the final consumer.

Law 13.455/2017 created the cash discount instrument precisely to mitigate this effect — but its adoption is still limited in Brazilian retail. The result is a cross-subsidy mechanism: whoever pays cash upfront or via Pix tends to finance, through pricing, whoever installments in twelve interest-free times on the credit card. The benefit is real — but it is not free for the economy as a whole.
