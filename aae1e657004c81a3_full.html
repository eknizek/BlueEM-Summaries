# Import pressures weigh on industrial and consumer company margins while energy sector profitability surges

The divided economic activity with winning and losing sectors has begun to be reflected in one of the critical indicators for the business landscape: the profit margins companies earn from their operations. For oil companies, profitability has grown due to the maturation of projects in Vaca Muerta, but for non-energy firms, the reality is the opposite, partly due to import liberalization.

A study by the economic research department of Banco Provincia analyzed the financial statements of large companies from different sectors listed on the stock exchange to measure the profitability rate across industries. The conclusion was that revenues from construction, consumer goods, and distributed inputs fell 12% in 2025 compared to the average level recorded in the 2021-2023 three-year period. Construction dragged down this segment with a collapse of 34.4%. In contrast, the oil and energy sector "managed to more than double its sales in the same period," the public Buenos Aires institution stated.

For the losing sectors, the bank determined that import liberalization had a decisive influence. "The fall in domestic demand in a context of local import flexibility and increased protectionism internationally complicates the scenario for sectors not benefited by natural factors," they assured in a report.

One way to prevent a sales decline from implying a profitability drop would be if the costs companies face also decreased, but the study showed this did not happen. According to Bapro's calculations, non-energy sector companies compressed their profitability: it fell from 18.5% to 11% between 2023 and 2025, representing the lowest point since the pandemic. "In contrast, the average for Pampa and YPF went from 22.8% to 28.2%," the report noted.

"The combination of currency appreciation and import liberalization accelerated the fall of tradable sectors: thus, the profitability of consumer goods and distributed inputs is at the lowest levels in the last ten years," was one of the conclusions reached by the economists.

On the other extreme, the maturation of Vaca Muerta projects, the increase in utility service rates measured in dollars in a context of falling global prices (the calculation cutoff date is end of 2025, before the Middle East war), explained the improvement in energy sector earnings.

To conduct the analysis, bank economists divided large companies into four sectors: construction (Ferrum, Holcim, and Loma Negra), consumer goods (Arcor, Grimoldi, Havanna, Mastellone, Molinos Rio de la Plata), distributed inputs (Aluar and Siderar), and oil and energy (Pampa and YPF).

The four months of 2026 elapsed could have indicated, according to Matías Rajnerman, head of Macroeconomics at the Buenos Aires bank, at least an initial change in trend. Consumer goods companies may have recovered some profitability due to accelerated inflation, which exceeded the pace of wage increases, exchange rates, and utility rates. For winning sectors, by contrast, their profit rate would be reduced by "the oil price increase in March and April, which was not fully passed on to gas stations." This could have "slowed the energy sector's recovery."

This year the Central Bank lifted restrictions on dividend transfers to parent companies on profits reflected in 2025 balances. Energy and mining sector companies were the first to send profits abroad. Central Bank President Santiago Bausili mentioned last week that the most recent figure for foreign currency sent by multinationals was US$1,300 million. Through March, according to official data, oil and mining companies sent nearly US$600 million.
