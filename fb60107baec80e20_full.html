# Future Interest Rates Decline as Global Risk Perception Improves

Future interest rates closed the trading session this Friday (24) in decline, with the movement concentrated in long-term rates of the forward curve, as domestic fixed income reflected improved global risk perception linked to developments in the Middle East war. Signals that the United States and Iran will return to the negotiating table reduced investor tension and supported a reduction in the risk premium added to rates in recent days.

At close of business, the Interbank Deposit (DI) contract rate maturing in January 2027 fell from 14.13%, from the previous adjustment, to 14.095%; the DI maturing in January 2028 declined from 13.695% to 13.63%; the contract maturing in January 2029 fell from 13.545% to 13.47%; and the DI maturing in January 2031 declined from 13.59% to 13.495%.

After a more timid start to trading, future interest rates consolidated a firmer pace of decline following news—first reported by American CNN and later confirmed by the White House—that the United States will send two representatives from Donald Trump's government to resume negotiations with Iran in Pakistan. With this, oil gave back part of its recent gains and U.S. Treasury rates declined, contributing to relief in local fixed income.

In New York, the two-year T-note rate—more sensitive to inflationary prospects and therefore oil prices—fell from 3.846% to 3.791%, after touching a low of 3.787%, while the ten-year U.S. Treasury rate declined from 4.329% to 4.307%.

With the favorable external environment, local investors reduced part of the risk premiums that had pushed rates up more than 40 basis points in intermediate sections of the curve between Monday and Thursday. Thus, interest rate markets are heading into a crucial week, with monetary policy decisions in Brazil and the United States, on a more positive note.

For the Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) decision on Wednesday (29), it is almost unanimous that the board will cut the Selic rate by 0.25 percentage points, to 14.50%, repeating the March decision in a context of high uncertainty about the inflationary situation, but with a basic rate still quite high relative to current inflation levels.

In the Copom digital options market, the probability priced in for a 0.25-point cut is 87%, 3 percentage points above the previous close. Meanwhile, maintaining the Selic at 14.75% has only a 10% chance of occurring, and a larger cut of 0.75 points has a marginal probability of 4%, according to the market.

"The backdrop is less benign, but there is still room to ease monetary policy," according to the team of professionals at Bank of America (BofA) led by David Beker, head of economics for Brazil and strategy for Latin America. Although the inflationary outlook has worsened, BofA's report notes that activity data shows some deceleration, while a stronger real helps contain the impact of higher oil prices on prices.

"In our view, the main change in the Copom statement should be a less favorable characterization of inflation dynamics and expectations, which have moved further away from the target. With the relevant monetary policy horizon shifting to the fourth quarter of 2027 (likely indicating an IPCA of 3.5%, versus 3.3% in the previous report), Copom should keep the door open for further gradual cuts, depending on data releases and containment of second-order effects," evaluates BofA's team of economists, which projects a Selic rate of 13.25% by the end of 2026.
