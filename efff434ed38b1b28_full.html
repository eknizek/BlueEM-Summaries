Article: The Master case took CDB out of 'autopilot'

24 April 2026 · 07:41 PM

# The Master case took CDB out of "autopilot"

Several months after the liquidation of Banco Master, the most recent data confirm that the CDB market has indeed entered a new regime, but not exactly in the direction imagined at the height of the stress.

Until the end of March, the daily number of CDB emissions from major banks (S1 to S3) recorded a 31% decline, while among smaller institutions (S4 and S5), the contraction was more moderate, at 15%. At first glance, the result seems counterintuitive. In a confidence shock, one would naturally expect greater retraction precisely among emitters most dependent on the market. To understand why this did not happen, we must return to the moment of shock.

In the second half of November 2025, the CDB market exited repeat mode. The extrajudicial liquidation of Banco Master functioned as a stress test for the specialization most used by medium and small banks: financing the portfolio with recurring rollover of funding. What appeared to be a circumscribed case revealed where the monetary escape occurred first: at the distribution adhesion point. In our estimates, the event triggered a rapid repricing of liquidity, causing a drop of nearly 45% in the daily number of emissions.

At the same time, the impact on the Credit Guarantee Fund (FGC), estimated at around R$ 40 billion in this case alone, added to other recent liquidations, raising demand for fund resources to over R$ 50 billion in a short time interval.

Before Master, CDB was a routine product: it entered the shelf daily, in predictable volume, with something like 58 daily emissions, and the investor decided more by rate and maturity than by the issuer's balance sheet. The bank's liquidation awakened a point that the market usually sweeps under the rug while everything is functioning: when the Central Bank intervenes, does the system reprice risk in the coupon or ration credit in the channel, leaving some emitters without access to investors?

This question led us to use econometric models to separate daily noise from the shock effect. We applied an Interrupted Time Series (ITS) model to estimate the trajectory the CDB market would tend to follow without the liquidation and compare it with what was observed at the end of 2025 and throughout 2026.

What appears in the data contradicts the expectation of an immediate rate increase: the adjustment came first by quantity, with a drop of close to 26 emissions per day shortly after November 18, while average rates practically did not move the next day, as if the market had detected the barrier before discussing price. In other words, the adjustment occurred by quantity, not by price.

When a market adjusts volume, the implicit information is that the restriction is not "how much I pay," but rather "whether I can distribute and roll over." This resets CDB to its real nature, as an instrument that depends on channel and recurring confidence, especially for emitters that need to return to the market frequently.

With fewer products available, the banking system exposes the position that already exists by design in the S1 to S5 classification, which in practice separates those with recurring financing from those dependent on returning to the market to renew their cash.

To measure shock effects across different bank profiles, we used a Differences-in-Differences (DiD) model, comparing group behavior over the period. This is where the apparent initial paradox resolves itself. The results point to persistent contraction in the daily number of emissions, more intense among major banks. But contrary to a more immediate reading, this does not necessarily indicate greater restriction, but rather greater flexibility.

With access to other funding sources, often at lower costs, these institutions apparently have reduced the use of CDB as a marginal financing instrument. Smaller banks, with less substitution available, remain more dependent on the channel, which limits the intensity of emission contraction.

We also investigated impacts on other banking funding instruments and, at least initially, there was no atmospheric contamination. When we look at LCIs and LCAs, we did not identify a statistically significant effect linked to the liquidation, suggesting that the market did not "exit the bank" en bloc; it rather opened precisely the instrument most similar to short-term plumbing: the CDB.

Based on data through March this year, the market appears to have accepted a new rhythm, slower and less automatic. Recovery in the daily number of emissions is weak and statistically unimportant, a sign that confidence has not returned to functioning as default.

For the investor, this appears in practice as longer tracking of issuer and shortening of maturity, because the cost of error ceased to be just a number on the screen and became waiting time to have liquidity. When the predictability of funding falls, especially for smaller banks, the effect is not restricted to financing.

These institutions operate in niches where credit depends on relationships and regional presence, and a more frightening channel for force capture leads to reduction in origination, portfolio shortening, and cost transfer to the end. Money becomes more expensive not because of a one-time spike in rates, but because the autopilot of distribution has been turned off and each cash renewal now requires explanation.

*Guilherme Almeida is fixed income chief at Suno Research
