# Master Bank Case Takes CDB Market Out of 'Autopilot'

Several months after the liquidation of Banco Master, the latest data confirms that the CDB market has indeed entered a new regime, though not quite in the direction imagined at the height of the stress. Until the end of March, the daily number of CDB emissions from large banks (S1 to S3) recorded a 31% decline, while among smaller institutions (S4 and S5), the contraction was more moderate at 15%.

At first glance, the result seems counterintuitive. In a confidence shock, one would naturally expect greater retraction precisely among issuers most dependent on the market. To understand why this didn't happen, we must return to the moment of the shock.

In the second half of November 2025, the CDB market came out of autopilot mode. The extrajudicial liquidation of Banco Master functioned as a stress test for the funding model most used by medium and small banks: financing their portfolio with recurring rollover of deposits. What appeared to be a circumscribed case revealed itself where monetary panic first struck: at the distribution access point. By our estimates, the event triggered rapid repricing of liquidity, causing a nearly 45% drop in daily emissions.

Simultaneously, the impact on the Credit Guarantee Fund (FGC), estimated at approximately R$ 40 billion in this case alone, combined with other recent liquidations, raised the fund's resource demand to over R$ 50 billion in a short time interval.

Before Master, CDB was a routine product: it entered the shelf every day in predictable volume, with roughly 58 daily emissions, and investors decided more by rate and term than by the issuer's financial statements. The bank's liquidation awakened a point that the market typically sweeps under the rug while everything is functioning: when the Central Bank intervenes, does the system reprice risk in the coupon or ration credit through the channel, leaving some issuers without investor access?

This question led us to use econometric models to separate daily noise from the shock effect. We applied an Interrupted Time Series (ITS) model to estimate the trajectory the CDB market would have followed without the liquidation and compare it with what was observed at the end of 2025 and throughout 2026.

What appears in the data contradicts the expectation of an immediate rate spike: the adjustment came first through quantity, with a drop of nearly 26 emissions per day right after November 18, while average rates barely moved the next day, as if the market had collected the barrier before discussing price. In other words, the adjustment occurred through quantity, not price.

When a market adjusts by volume, the implicit information is that the restriction isn't "how much I pay," but rather "whether I can distribute and roll." This reframes CDB in its true nature: an instrument that depends on channel and recurring confidence, especially for issuers that need to return to the market frequently.

With fewer products available, the banking system exposes a position that already exists by design in the S1 to S5 classification, which in practice separates those with recurring financing from those dependent on returning to the market to renew cash.

To measure the shock's effects across different bank profiles, we used a Differences-in-Differences (DiD) model, comparing group behavior over the period. This is where the apparent initial paradox resolves.

The results point to persistent contraction in the daily number of emissions, more intense among large banks. But contrary to a more immediate reading, this doesn't necessarily indicate greater restriction, but rather greater flexibility. With access to other funding sources, often at lower costs, these institutions apparently have reduced CDB use as a marginal financing instrument. Smaller banks, with fewer substitution options available, remain more dependent on the channel, limiting the intensity of emission reductions.

We also investigated impacts on other banking funding instruments, and at least initially, there was no spillover effect. When we examined LCIs and LCAs, we identified no statistically significant effect linked to the liquidation, suggesting the market didn't "exit banks" as a bloc—it specifically pressured the instrument most similar to short-term funding: the CDB.

Based on data through March this year, the market appears to have accepted a new rhythm—slower and less automatic. Recovery in the daily number of emissions is weak and statistically insignificant, a sign that confidence hasn't returned to functioning as default.

For investors, this appears in practice as stricter issuer screening and shortened terms, because the cost of being wrong is no longer just a number on the screen—it's waiting time to get liquidity.

When the predictability of funding falls, especially for smaller banks, the effect isn't restricted to financing. These institutions operate in niches where credit depends on relationships and regional presence, and a more difficult funding channel triggers lower origination, shorter portfolios, and cost transfer to the end customer.

Money becomes more expensive not through a specific tax rate hike, but because the distribution autopilot was switched off and each cash renewal now requires explanation.
