Why Bank of America Upgraded Petrobras to Buy

21 April 2026 · 12:00 PM

# Why Bank of America Decided to Upgrade Petrobras to Buy

The rise in oil prices amid the global energy shock caused by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which is fundamental to approximately 20% of global supply, has rekindled interest in the oil sector. Analysts at Bank of America (BAC) have upgraded Petrobras (PETR4) and believe that "it is not yet too late to jump on the wave," despite the strong rally in shares.

Analysts Caio Ribeiro, Leonardo Marcondes, and Nicolás Barros upgraded the recommendation from "neutral" to "buy" and set a price target of R$ 65 per share, compared to the previous R$ 49. For the ADR, the price target is US$ 24.80. The bank's team incorporates a scenario with a new estimate for Brent oil of US$ 93 per barrel for 2026 and US$ 78 for 2027.

The revision comes after a rise of approximately 75% in ADRs in New York since the beginning of the year, well above comparable global indicators, but the bank argues that the new energy price scenario changes the capacity for cash generation. In this sense, the bank projects free cash flow returns to shareholders (FCFE) of 18% in 2026 and 16% in 2027, "5% above peer average," in an environment of greater investment.

## Oil Prices Drive Revenue

The adjustment in estimates is mainly due to the change in the oil scenario. BofA incorporates a 20% increase in its Brent hypothesis for 2026 and 2027, which raises estimated Ebitda by approximately 26% in both years and net income by up to 58% in 2027.

In this new context, the bank highlights that the oil recovery "is fundamental to ease concerns about an increase in debt resulting from an imbalance between free cash flow and dividends." The level identified by analysts places this balance at around US$ 68 per barrel for 2026.

The report also highlights Petrobras's greater sensitivity to oil prices. According to BofA, each US$ 1 increase per barrel in Brent adds 0.6% to FCFE return, compared to an average of 0.2% among comparable European companies. This amplifying effect becomes one of the main catalysts for valuation.

This is added to the dynamics of capital flows toward emerging markets. Brazil attracted US$ 3.2 billion so far this year, within a total of US$ 6.6 billion directed to Latin America, with foreign investors concentrating in large-capitalization sectors, such as energy. Petrobras's weighting in indices such as the Ibovespa and MSCI LatAm amplifies this impact.

## Growing Production

Beyond the oil cycle, the report highlights the company's operational performance. BofA projects an annual compound growth in oil and gas production of 5.2% through 2028, above most competitors. The majority of this expansion comes from the Búzios field, described as the most productive asset in the portfolio, where the company will install new platforms between 2026 and 2027.

In this context, the bank highlights that "the majority of this growth is expected to come from Búzios, PBR's most prolific pre-sal field," with direct implications for profitability. The quality of pre-sal reservoirs also translates into superior operational indicators. Wells show initial production rates between 20,000 and 40,000 barrels per day, with some exceeding 50,000, significantly higher levels than comparable projects.

This profile allows the company to maintain economies of scale and reduce development costs, reinforcing cash generation even in intensive investment scenarios. BofA describes the period from 2026 to 2028 as a "sowing" phase, characterized by higher capex, before a subsequent "harvest" stage with cash flow returns between 18% and 20%.

## Governance and Pricing Policy

The report identifies risks related to fuel pricing policy and capital allocation decisions, although it downplays the impact compared with previous episodes. In this sense, BofA highlights that the company "has achieved significant advances both in operational execution and in corporate governance in recent years."

Pricing policy remains a point of attention, especially in a scenario where the government has implemented diesel subsidies and is considering measures to contain gasoline prices. Currently, Petrobras sells gasoline at a 35% discount relative to import prices, in a context where oil is around US$ 100 per barrel.

Even so, the bank maintains that, even under conservative assumptions, the company maintains attractive metrics. In a scenario without additional price adjustments, BofA estimates FCFE returns of 15% and dividends of 11%, which "corroborates our upgrade to 'buy'."

The analysis also highlights the structural change in the company following the governance reforms implemented since 2016, which limit the possibility of decisions that would harm shareholder value. This framework reduces the risk of direct political interference compared with previous cycles.
