# Chilean Stock Market Resumes Gains as IPSA Closes Near 11,500 Points on Strong SQM Demand

The Santiago Stock Exchange rally returned with energy on Thursday, mainly thanks to SQM, in a context where lithium stocks have benefited from the Middle East conflict. The S&P IPSA rose 1.5% to 11,478.47 points at close, with visible support from SQM-B (9.2%), which was part of a buying frenzy for lithium stocks—the American company Albemarle (16%) rose even more—after the spot price of "white gold" reached highs since September 2023. Thus, the IPSA closed at highs since February 3.

The Chilean stock market had taken a breather from the strong rally that spanned five consecutive sessions, mainly due to expectations of diplomacy between Iran and the United States. With an advance of 26.7% since the military crisis began, SQM-B is by far the winning stock of the IPSA in this situation.

"The rally of the last two months is possibly linked to the logic that demand for electric vehicles accelerates in the North American market, which had been somewhat lagging and represents an important part of demand," said Aldo Morales, sub-manager of Equity Research at BICE Inversiones, to DF.

"SQM has become a defensive stock, in the sense that with this war there has been much pressure from fears about inflation and the decrease in GDP growth," commented Jorge Tolosa, equity operator at Vector Capital. He added that SQM has a cash cost "among the most efficient in the world," and that its dividend policy went from 30% to 50% of profits.

## International Stock Markets

At the Latin American level, Peru Select (3.7%) outperformed the IPSA. On Wall Street, the Nasdaq advanced 0.4%, the S&P 500 grew 0.3%, and the Dow Jones added 0.2%; leaving the first two with a second consecutive historic high. Previously, in Europe, the continental Euro Stoxx 50 fell 0.1% and London's FTSE 100 advanced 0.3%. It was a session where, in any case, there was an important degree of caution.

The global dollar strengthened and Brent oil rebounded 3.4% to $98.2 per barrel, at a time when leaders from Europe and the Persian Gulf have warned that it could take up to six months to see an effective agreement between Washington and Tehran.

Donald Trump confirmed market expectations by announcing that there could be a second round of diplomatic conversations with Iran this weekend. The parties are considering extending by two weeks the ceasefire that currently expires on April 22, although Trump asserted that this probably won't be necessary. The main matters being discussed include transit through Hormuz, Iran's nuclear program, and the economic sanctions weighing on the Islamic Republic. Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth warned that the Pentagon is ready to resume military operations if no agreement is reached.

There has also been an effort by Israeli and Lebanese authorities to halt hostilities focused on the militant group Hezbollah, an ally of Iran. Both sides agreed to a 10-day ceasefire, according to Trump.

In the Asian session, Japan's Nikkei jumped 2.4%, Hong Kong's Hang Seng rose 1.7%, and mainland China's CSI 300 gained 1.1%. China's GDP grew 5% in the first quarter, two tenths above expectations, although heavily influenced by the industrial sector, to the detriment of internal consumption.
