# Brazil's Renewable Energy Market Faces Price Distortion Crisis in Deregulated Sector

The Brazilian electricity sector faces a crisis due to price distortions and cuts to renewable energy generation, resulting in an almost 40% drop in energy market liquidity. Grupo Bolt and Voltera are adopting different strategies to deal with volatility. Bolt, with revenue of R$ 1.5 billion, plans to expand its operations in the free market until 2027, investing in self-generation and a wholesale purchasing and retail sales model. Voltera, with a conservative approach, prioritizes contract management and supplier diversification, aiming for stability and predictability. Both companies recognize the need to adapt to new market conditions, with expectations of growth and full opening of the free market in 2027, which could move up to R$ 250 billion.

In addition to curtailment—cuts to centralized renewable energy generation that led the ONS, the system's regulatory body, to waste 20% of solar and wind energy generated in the country last year—the electricity sector faces a new crisis, this time resulting from price distortions in the energy market, a business model that moves approximately R$ 150 billion per year.

A series of factors that began aligning two years ago created a perfect storm now hitting the sector, causing a wave of failures among energy trading companies. This scorched-earth scenario, which led to an almost 40% drop in energy market liquidity in 2025, is forcing the entire sector chain to seek alternatives. With different strategies, a large energy company, Grupo Bolt, and a medium-sized trading company, Voltera, revealed to NeoFeed what they are doing to prevent price volatility from making business unviable.

The current crisis began in early 2024, ironically amid expectations of big business with the opening of the free energy market to medium and high-voltage consumers. The opening occurred at a time of historically low energy prices, driving mass migration and high discounts. Typical electricity bills for this new layer of consumers—medium-sized companies and commercial establishments such as retail chains, supermarkets, and shopping centers—fell between 15% and 30%, expanding not only the customer base but also the complexity of contract management for generators and traders.

The phase of minimum prices was left behind last year, with a sharp adjustment in energy pricing to above historical levels. This is because, as a result of curtailment, the ONS made the price formation model more conservative, prioritizing greater water preservation and more frequent activation of thermal plants, aiming for energy security in the face of renewable intermittency and transmission limitations.

These models, managed by electronic platforms, consider variables such as hydrology, load, thermal dispatch, risk aversion—the possibility of reservoirs drying up due to lack of rain, for example—and operational constraints. The result was an increase in operating costs and greater volatility, especially in the short term.

"In practice, energy prices are not determined by negotiations between buyers and sellers, that is, by supply and demand, but by mathematical models and climatic conditions, which in turn are determinants for free market price formation," says Victor Hugo Iocca, director of electrical energy at Abrace, an association that brings together large commercial and industrial energy consumers.

To get an idea of the increase in this price volatility, a survey by the Brazilian Association of Energy Traders (Abraceel) shows that from 2024 to March 2026, the long-term price in the free market rose 59%, the quarterly price advanced 121%, and the average Price of Difference Settlement (PLD)—which is used as the reference price for the Brazilian electricity sector—increased 84%, while the IPCA varied 5%.

The increase in price volatility, with spikes even in scenarios of full reservoirs, raised the need for financial guarantees, pressuring traders with fragile cash flows, making it difficult to roll over positions and sell energy. Large aggressive trading companies such as Gold Energia accumulated losses exceeding R$ 1 billion and collapsed, while 2W Energia and América Energia suffered strong impacts and broke contracts. At the beginning of 2026, the Elétron Energia group, also with debts exceeding R$ 1 billion, entered judicial recovery, followed by IBS Energy, which sought protection after seeing its liabilities reach R$ 57 million.

The most notorious case involves Tradener, the country's first trading company: the Paraná courts suspended part of an injunction that allowed Tradener to alter the way it passed energy on to customers, who would now bear additional costs for price-hour differences—ignored by the trader. With this, the court maintained only Tradener's 60-day protection against executions while the trading company negotiates with creditors over a liability that could reach R$ 5 billion, according to market estimates. In a statement, the company says it is taking measures to "ensure the continuity of its operations and protect customer service while it negotiates, in an orderly manner, a solution with its counterparts."

Betting on the Free Market

Grupo Bolt—which operates in retail, trading, distributed generation, structured operations, and international exchange—maintained its expansion despite volatility. With presence in eight states and revenue of R$ 1.5 billion, the company is eyeing the full opening of the free market in 2027, when up to 100 million consumers could potentially migrate—far beyond the 30,000 new units of the last two years, restricted to large-load consumers.

"The energy sector has been waiting 30 years for the full opening of the free market," Grupo Bolt's CEO Gustavo Ayala tells NeoFeed, referring to the potential estimated by the market—which could move up to R$ 250 billion per year in the long term.

The strategy aiming at this opening combines three verticals and strong automation with AI: "This opening will require a new operational model, with artificial intelligence at its center," says Ayala.

Bolt's platform already operates with 30,000 customers in low voltage—customers of "subscription energy" from distributed generation (DG) vertical plants, with revenue of R$ 120 million—and about 300 customers in the retail free market. Energy self-generation is also pointed out as a pillar to mitigate volatility and reduce costs, with 21 projects totaling 220 medium megawatts (MWm). Among them stands out the partnership with Rede D'Or, which receives clean energy from 337,000 solar panels to over 70 hospitals in 13 states at the Lagoinha Complex in Russas (CE).

The focus on opening the free market, however, required Bolt to devise a model that is both simple and aggressive: buying energy wholesale and selling at retail. According to Ayala, by 2030, the reference for consumers tends to be the captive market tariff—with adjustments and an upward trend—gradually migrating to free market prices in the following years.

"In wholesale, the future curve presents levels by year: 2027 is the most expensive; there are expected declines for the following years and even lower prices in the very long term, such as in 2[...]"
