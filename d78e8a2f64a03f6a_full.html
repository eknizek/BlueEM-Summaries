# Kokoriko Changes Hands: Colombian Fund KKO Takes Control of the Fast-Food Chain

The Kokoriko restaurant chain has changed ownership and passed into the hands of the Colombian investment fund KKO, in an operation that, according to reports from radio station 'Caracol Radio', was finalized several months ago. With this move, the brand is no longer linked to the IGA group, which until now had a stake in the company and maintains holdings in Andrés Carne de Res.

The KKO fund, composed of Colombian investors, assured that it is betting on the country and on the growth of brands with a track record in the national market. Although official figures for the transaction have not been revealed, the change of ownership represents a significant shift in the gastronomic sector, especially given Kokoriko's historical weight in mass consumption of chicken in Colombia.

## A New Business Chapter

The arrival of the KKO fund will not substantially change the operation of the chain, which will maintain its operations normally. While no concrete changes in operations have been announced, this type of acquisition is usually associated with expansion processes, modernization, or restructuring. The purchasing fund has stated that it "believes in the country, in its people, and in the potential of its brands," suggesting a commitment to strengthening Kokoriko's presence in the local market and eventually exploring new growth opportunities.

The change also occurs at a time of transformation in the restaurant sector, marked by competition from new brands, the boom in delivery platforms, and the need to innovate in formats and customer experience.

## A Brand with History in Colombia

Founded in 1969, Kokoriko consolidated itself as one of the country's most representative chains in the roasted chicken segment, with presence in multiple cities across the country and strong brand recognition among consumers. In recent weeks, the company had also made news following the death of its founder, Eduardo Robayo Ferro, a key figure in the construction and positioning of the company in the national market.

The change of ownership, in this context, marks the beginning of a new stage for the chain, which now falls into the hands of an investment fund with the challenge of maintaining its legacy and adapting it to current consumption dynamics. For now, the operation revealed by 'Caracol Radio' confirms that even the country's most traditional brands are entering transformation processes, in an increasingly dynamic and competitive business environment.
