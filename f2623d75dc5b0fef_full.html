Brazil needs R$148bn to close grain storage gap, Kepler says

30 April 2026 · 06:11 PM

Brazil's grain output has expanded rapidly in recent years, but storage capacity has failed to keep pace. Kepler Weber, the largest company in the sector in Latin America, estimates the country would need R$148 billion in investment to accommodate this season's harvest and eliminate a storage shortfall of 135 million tonnes.

"Brazil already has around 19,000 storage units and builds between 1,500 and 2,000 per year, depending on size and capacity. We would need an additional 5,000 to 7,000 [to close the gap]," Kepler CEO Bernardo Nogueira told Valor.

That would require compressing roughly five years of expansion into a single year to address existing bottlenecks.

The company's investment estimate is based on data from consultancy Cogo, which projects Brazil's 2025/26 grain crop at 357 million tonnes. However, the country's static storage capacity stands at 223 million tonnes. The remainder is either stored in open-air conditions or sold immediately to free up silo space, limiting price arbitrage opportunities and increasing transportation costs during peak shipping periods.

Nogueira noted that annual growth in static storage capacity is about 2.4%, below what is needed to meet demand, as grain production has been expanding at an average rate of 4.4% per year.

Mato Grosso, Brazil's leading producing state, accounts for the largest share of storage facilities.

Another factor driving costs across the supply chain is the low level of on-farm storage. Citing data from the National Supply Company (CONAB), Nogueira said only 16% of storage units are located on farms. In the United States, that figure reaches 65%, while in Argentina on-farm capacity stands at 40%.

"I believe this will change over the next decade [in Brazil], because our agriculture is still relatively young. Much of the Cerrado region is operated by first- or second-generation farmers. In the U.S., farms are often in their fourth generation, with more than 100 years of production on the same land," Nogueira said.

"Storing grain away from farms increases the cost of moving production to grain handlers, trading companies and cooperatives, where most of Brazil's static capacity is located," he added.

Paulo Bertolini, head of Abimaq's Grain Storage Equipment Committee, shares a similar view. He noted that Brazil's grain output has grown by an average of 10 million tonnes per year over the past decade.
