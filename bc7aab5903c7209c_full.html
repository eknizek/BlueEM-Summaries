Guide to Paloma Valencia's proposal to eliminate motorcycle insurance payments for bikes up to 250cc

04 May 2026 · 04:55 PM

# ABC of Paloma Valencia's Proposal to Eliminate SOAT Payments for Motorcycles up to 250cc

Senator and presidential candidate Paloma Valencia's proposal to eliminate the mandatory Traffic Accident Insurance (SOAT) payment for motorcycles of up to 250 cubic centimeters has opened a new debate about mobility, public spending, and the sustainability of Colombia's health system. Although the initiative has not yet been officially filed in Congress, the Democratic Center leader has already explained how the scheme would function and who would be the main beneficiaries if the project becomes law.

The proposal does not seek to eliminate SOAT entirely. Rather, it proposes changing who would bear the cost for millions of motorcyclists who currently must purchase the policy to legally circulate in the country. The announcement was made during a campaign visit in Pereira, where Valencia stated that the State would assume the insurance payment for low-displacement motorcycles, especially those used by citizens in socioeconomic strata 1, 2, and 3.

## Who Would Benefit from Eliminating SOAT Payments?

According to the congresswoman's explanation, the benefit would apply only to motorcycles with displacement equal to or less than 250 cubic centimeters, which are precisely the most used for daily work and transportation in Colombia.

The initiative also establishes that motorcycles would need to be formally registered with the National Traffic Registry (RUNT) and legally classified as motorcycles in the traffic license to access State-financed coverage. According to figures cited in the proposal, Colombia already exceeds 12 million motorcycles, and approximately 93.5% correspond precisely to vehicles of up to 250cc, which would make this measure one of the broadest on popular mobility.

The project would also have a social focus. Valencia argues that approximately 92% of motorcycle owners belong to strata 1, 2, and 3, while a large portion of drivers earn less than two minimum monthly salaries.

In practice, this would mean that millions of motorcyclists would stop paying SOAT directly each year, although they would continue to have medical coverage in case of traffic accidents, according to the presidential candidate's explanation.

## How Would SOAT Function if the State Assumes Payment?

One of the most important points of the proposal is that insurance would not disappear. Medical and hospital coverage would continue to exist, but the money to finance it would come directly from public resources and not from motorcyclists' pockets. This means that if an accident occurs, victims would continue to have access to medical care, surgeries, medications, disability benefits, and medical transportation, just as currently occurs under the traditional SOAT scheme.

The difference would be in the financing mechanism. Instead of each driver individually purchasing the policy, the State would collectively assume the cost for motorcycles covered by the new law, according to Valencia's statements during public interventions.

The proposal also emerges amid high evasion rates of mandatory insurance. Various analyses indicate that more than 60% of motorcycles in Colombia currently circulate without valid SOAT due to policy costs and existing informality. Some sectors believe that transferring payment to the State could reduce evasion and increase actual medical coverage for motorcyclists. Others warn that the change could represent a multibillion-dollar fiscal pressure on national public finances.

## What Would Happen with Traffic Fines and Controls?

If the proposal is approved, motorcycles up to 250cc that qualify for the benefit would no longer be sanctioned for not carrying SOAT, as long as they were registered within the state system contemplated by the new law. Currently, driving without valid SOAT results in vehicle immobilization and financial fines. With the new model, control would shift to verifying whether the motorcycle meets the conditions to access State-financed coverage.

However, other documents would remain mandatory. The driver's license, technical-mechanical inspection, and vehicle registration would continue to be required by traffic authorities throughout the country, even if individual SOAT payment disappears.

## Would High-Displacement Motorcycles Continue Paying SOAT?

Yes. The proposal presented by Paloma Valencia would only benefit motorcycles up to 250 cubic centimeters, so higher-capacity motorcycles would have to continue purchasing mandatory insurance in the traditional way. This means that sports, touring, or high-performance motorcycles would continue directly financing their policies with private insurers under the current scheme that has operated in Colombia for several years.

The initiative aims to focus especially on work and daily transportation motorcycles used by delivery workers, informal workers, and medium and low-income families who depend daily on this vehicle for mobility and generating income.
