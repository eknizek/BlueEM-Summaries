# Federico Sturzenegger Confirms Real Estate Deregulation Bill for Congress in June

Minister of Deregulation and Transformation Federico Sturzenegger made clear that deregulation of professional colleges and the real estate sector is a priority on the Government's official agenda. "For a Professional College to set a minimum price is a social aberration," Sturzenegger stated during his presentation to developers and real estate professionals at the "Real estate 2026: expectations and reality" event, organized by Reporte Inmobiliario, held this Monday at the Juan Pablo II Auditorium of the Argentine Catholic University (UCA) in Puerto Madero.

"The problem is not the existence of colleges themselves, but when they use state power to fix conditions that don't respond to the market," he argued. Along these lines, he questioned the imposition of minimum fees, mandatory registration requirements, and the existence of entry barriers that limit competition.

For Sturzenegger, this scheme ultimately affects the end user. "When a rule sets prices or restricts who can offer a service, it is actually transferring income from society to a specific group," he stated.

With direct criticism of what he defined as "privileges created by law," he affirmed that they will send a legislative package with deregulation issues next month to "seek to eliminate the privileges that today make construction and commercial intermediation more expensive." The official intention is to move toward a "freer, more dynamic and competitive market," where "services are valued for their quality and not by legal impositions," he emphasized.

"The idea is that people can innovate and create without having to be part of a club to have a privilege," he concluded. Thus, he insisted, the intention is to eliminate what he called "unnecessary intermediation costs," in a context where the Government seeks to reduce regulatory burden on economic activity.

This was one of the axes the minister addressed, but not the only one. In a presentation titled "Challenges of the Real Estate Market in Argentina," he divided the talk into three important focuses: the importance of real estate assets in the Argentina to come; the mortgage boom in the next 10 years; and what the sector needs to reconfigure itself.

## Rental Deregulation: "The Experiment That Worked"

Another chapter Sturzenegger defended was the elimination of the rental law through DNU 70/2023 just days after President Javier Milei took office. For Sturzenegger, the decision to completely deregulate the market was not only necessary but already shows concrete results.

"The current framework is completely deregulated and that allowed the market to function again," he stated.

According to his explanation, for years restrictions (such as mandatory minimum terms or rigid update schemes) had reduced supply and distorted prices. The change in rules, on the other hand, enabled greater contractual flexibility: shorter agreements, conditions adapted to each case, and even hybrid formats, such as rentals tied to a possible sale of the property.

According to data he himself mentioned, the supply of rental properties increased by 300%, while real prices recorded a drop of nearly 30%.

"The new scheme enabled more flexible contracts, shorter terms, and agreements adapted to each case, including temporary rentals or clauses linked to the sale of the property," he assured.

## "Property Prices Are Going to Rise"

Beyond deregulations, the presentation offered an optimistic vision for the future of the real estate market based on the change in Argentina's macroeconomic profile. The official argued that the country is moving toward an export-led growth model (energy, mining, and agriculture), which will strengthen the local currency.

Within his logic, a country that grows through its exports generates a greater flow of dollars. "If Argentina produces more dollars, the peso becomes stronger. And that pushes up the dollar prices of local assets, as is the case with real estate. Since there is no asset more representative in local currency than property," he explained.

The conclusion, according to the Minister, is that in a scenario of greater macroeconomic stability and export growth, property prices (measured in dollars) would tend to rise, with important profitability prospects.

Furthermore, the official added another key factor: the location of growth. The new scheme could direct demand toward today's less developed cities, opening a different map of opportunities for the sector, as is currently the case with Neuquén and Vaca Muerta.

## The Future of Mortgage Credit

Another strong topic of the day was the expected explosion of mortgage credit. The official projected significant expansion of mortgage financing over the next 10 years.

According to Sturzenegger's explanation, today banks operate with transactional money that represents barely between 12% and 15% of GDP, far from countries like Chile, where it exceeds 80%. That difference, he explained, responds to an incentive problem "because people do not deposit their savings in the financial system."

But not only that, but "they placed that money from short-term funds in instruments with high rates, driven by inflation." That model, he warned, ceases to be viable in a context of stability.

"With the drop in inflation, banks will have to change their business. They will go out to seek the savings of Argentines," he proposed.

That's where a key fact appears: Argentines have more than US$200 billion outside the local financial system. If part of those funds returns, the impact on credit could be significant since more long-term deposits allow more long-term loans.

In that scheme, UVA-adjusted credits will continue to be the central instrument, with real rates that could be at international levels. "The day banks offer attractive returns for savings, there will be much more funding. And that will turn into mortgage credit," he summarized.

Today, the stock of mortgages in Argentina is around US$5 billion. In his projection, "that number could multiply by 10 in the coming years if people bring their dollars," and he assured that the starting interest rate for loans will be UVA + 8%.

## "Construction Has Room to Lower Costs"

In parallel to the discussion on prices and credit, Sturzenegger focused on another key front of the real estate sector: "Today construction in Argentina is expensive due to distortions that could be corrected."
