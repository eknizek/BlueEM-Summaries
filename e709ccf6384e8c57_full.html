# They built a 200-meter skyscraper and forgot the elevator shaft

The city of Benidorm, located on the Costa Blanca in Alicante, Spain, is a case study. An area that began as a small fishing village on the Mediterranean, it became the "Spanish New York," also known as "Beniyork," for its 80 buildings over 100 meters tall. These figures position it as the second city in the world with the most skyscrapers per capita.

Benidorm became the "Spanish New York" also known as "Beniyork" for its large number of towers. This coastal area represents a major attraction for the Spanish and European population, especially retirees, as it established itself as an accessible city with beaches adapted for people with reduced mobility. Additionally, the 300 days of sunshine per year and mild climate mean its 74,000 permanent inhabitants quadruple during high season, when it hosts 400,000 travelers. Tourists can stay in one of the 142 hotels in the area, with over 90,000 available beds.

It is in this zone full of contradictions that an unthinkable fact occurred: the Intempo skyscraper, designed by Roberto Pérez Guerras—founder of the homonymous architecture studio specialized in large buildings—was missing the elevator shaft.

The building was originally intended to house 20 floors. However, a last-minute decision doubled this amount to 47. This expansion was not thought through in detail.

**How is the building?**

Intempo began construction in 2006, in the midst of Benidorm's fever for skyscrapers. In a context where higher was better, the developers of this building did not want to be outdone, which is why they made two 200-meter towers, connected at the top by a bridge.

Everything seemed to be going well. An ambitious idea in an ambitious town. But the 2008 Spanish crisis arrived, followed by financial problems that forced construction to halt. The structure, already standing and visible from all points in the territory, remained abandoned for years.

After the pause, construction resumed, but not as expected. Changes in construction companies, along with salary delays, produced serious errors and oversights. The Spanish newspaper El País lists some of the inefficiencies that occurred during this construction period. One of the most striking is that until the first 23 floors were built, they did not install lifts for workers going up and down stairs. They also did not have the plans for floor 47 when they already had concrete poured on floor 46.

These oversights also produced tragedies: a lift fell with 13 workers, causing them serious injuries. But since the vehicle entrance was not prepared, ambulances could not access the site.

However, the biggest surprise came in January 2012: they had not left space for the elevator in the upper floors. "The space was calculated for a 20-floor block," explained sources consulted by El País, who added that in May of that year the project's directing architects (the Pérez Guerra and Olcina office and Radúan Architects) resigned from the project.

Finally, in 2021—14 years after the start of the work—Intempo opened its doors to the public. With 198 meters, 47 floors, and apartments for sale from €200,000 to over €1,000,000, the building became an emblem of the city. And its complications (and oversights) remained part of the history of Benidorm's tallest skyscraper.
