# 'Unknown Territory': Airlines Raise Fares and Fees with Fuel Price Surge

Airlines around the world have raised prices for baggage and seats, cut profit projections, and have begun openly discussing possible partnerships with rivals, as fuel costs linked to the war become more rigid. Andrew Nocella, commercial director of United Airlines Holdings, declared that the sector finds itself in "unknown territory," while Alaska Air Group has dispelled any hope that consumers may enjoy lower fares again anytime soon.

On the more optimistic end of the spectrum, Emirates President Tim Clark said that most people, after all, move on quickly and things will return to normal soon.

Regardless of whether optimists or pessimists ultimately prevail, what is certain is that last week brought to light the many challenges facing the airline sector. This is forcing executives to make consequential decisions to stabilize their operations.

To complicate matters further, the U.S. President is openly tilting the competitive balance by promoting a possible $500 million bailout for struggling Spirit Aviation, while criticizing other potential transactions, including a possible merger between United and American Airlines. And not least, there is the uncertainty stemming from Donald Trump's wavering pronouncements about the state of the war with Iran. Taken together, the sector is heading toward months of insecurity in what was supposed to have been a year of strong demand, with initial projections of record $41 billion in gains and 5.2 billion passengers.

Airlines had invested heavily in their products, from cabin upgrades to lounges and onboard connectivity, betting that spending on the more luxurious air travel experience would persist. But now, the exuberance that propelled airlines in the early months of the year has given way to a sense that the sector has lost a clear sense of direction.

"It's not possible to know with certainty all the ways the sector can be affected," said Southwest Airlines CEO Bob Jordan, pointing to "significant economic and geopolitical uncertainty." "I cannot accurately predict the destination of fuel and therefore you cannot accurately predict the destination of prices and fares," he said.

In the immediate future, they will rise. Consumers are expected to absorb billions of dollars in extra aircraft fuel costs, so airlines are charging new surcharges and increasing fees for baggage dispatch and seat selection.

"Airlines never waste a good crisis," said William McGee, senior aviation and travel fellow at the nonprofit American Economic Liberties Project.

American Airlines said it is facing $4 billion in extra fuel-related costs by year-end, a burden it will try to pass on to consumers as much as possible. Airfares are already about 15% to 20% higher now and are unlikely to recede fully when wartime tensions diminish, said United CEO Scott Kirby on Wednesday. "The longer this lasts, the greater the likelihood that price increases will stick," Kirby said.

When the summer travel rush diminishes, carriers will likely take another look at capacity and eliminate more routes that have become unprofitable. United, based in Chicago, said it expects to be able to recover up to 100% of higher fuel costs by year-end by raising prices for customers. Delta Air Lines CEO Ed Bastian said in a recent earnings call that the company would assess the degree to which it could "maintain any pricing power," even after fuel costs decrease.

The saving grace at the moment is that bookings remain strong with the arrival of peak summer travel season — though cracks are appearing. United said current demand is resilient, but this is unlikely to hold as higher ticket prices discourage flights. Like American Airlines, days later, the airline cut its profit forecast for the entire year. American CEO Robert Isom said demand remains robust for now and that he expects double-digit revenue growth in the current quarter, even as the company reduces marginal flights. Still, the company cut its annual forecast and said it may end the year with a loss.

This turnaround occurs as American and Alaska Air pursue possible revenue-sharing agreements and other strategic partnerships, Bloomberg News reported. American has also been the subject of speculation about a possible merger with United. Kirby floated the idea during a meeting with Trump in February, according to people familiar with the matter. American and the President, generally business-friendly, rejected the idea, saying it would reduce competition. Days later, Trump threw another curveball at airline chiefs, saying he is evaluating a government purchase of Spirit rather than letting it fail.

"The current administration does not facilitate long-term planning, and airline CEOs are being forced to think on their feet," said Art Wheaton, director of labor studies at Cornell University's School of Industrial and Labor Relations.

This includes executives worldwide. There are signs that Asian countries are stockpiling aircraft fuel, and the International Energy Agency warned that Europe could run out of supplies in weeks. Deutsche Lufthansa AG, Europe's largest airline group, is cutting about 20,000 flights from its summer schedule and adopting low-cost airline pricing models, selling tickets that don't include baggage.

As in every crisis, there is always someone telling everyone to calm down. In aviation, that person is Clark, from Emirates, whose Dubai-based airline was devastated by the conflict. The world's largest airline is operating at 65% capacity after an near-total shutdown as travelers avoid the Persian Gulf region. However, once the Strait of Hormuz reopens, business should recover in just one or two months, he said.

"People have short memories," Clark said at a conference in Berlin on Thursday. When fighting ceases and there is a degree of stability, "things will return to normal."
