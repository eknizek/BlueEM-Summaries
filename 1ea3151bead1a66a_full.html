Colombia faces higher debt costs: interest rates at 13% boost market but trigger fiscal alarm

17 April 2026 · 11:44 PM

Colombia is paying dearly to finance itself, and the market is taking advantage. In the most recent debt auctions this week, the Government placed 1.5 trillion in TES with rates reaching up to 13.8 percent, plus another 900 billion in short-term securities at 13.3 percent, levels that would have been exceptional just a few years ago. This dual effect—financial appetite in the short term, fiscal pressure in the medium term—is redefining the debate on public debt. Because Colombia is not only borrowing more, but it is doing so at an increasingly higher cost, in a context where revenues continue to fall short of spending, warn analysts.

The discussion has moved beyond simply how much the country owes to asking how much that debt costs, who finances it, and how sustainable it will be in the coming years. Data from the Central Bank confirms the magnitude of the challenge. At the close of 2025, external debt reached $246.801 billion, equivalent to 53.8 percent of gross domestic product (GDP), with an annual increase of $25.848 billion. Of that total, 61.9 percent corresponds to the public sector, reflecting the Government's weight in the debt dynamics.

Beyond the size, the problem is the cost. Although the average rate of new public external debt stood at 6.1 percent in 2025, internal financing—key to covering fiscal needs—is being conducted at significantly higher rates, which increases the future interest burden. This impact is already felt in public accounts. The service of external debt in the public sector reached $19.505 billion in 2025, with a significant portion allocated to interest. And everything suggests that this pressure will continue to increase.

From the Fiscal Observatory at Javeriana University, the warning is that the country faces a "persistent imbalance between income and expenses that has not been corrected," which forces it to finance growing deficits under increasingly demanding conditions. The figures support this. With revenues of 16.3 percent of GDP against spending of 22.7 percent, the primary deficit stood at -3.5 percent of GDP. For 2027, budget growth would be explained almost entirely by debt service, which would increase by 43 trillion pesos, displacing other items such as investment. "The increase in spending does not reflect fiscal expansion, but rather pressure resulting from higher borrowing costs," warns the Observatory.

The Office of the Comptroller General of the Nation also raised the alarm. According to its estimates, debt service would go from 100.4 trillion pesos in 2026 to 149.9 trillion in 2027, an increase of 49.3 percent. "Market conditions...resulted in higher interest rates that will have an impact on debt service in subsequent years," noted the entity, warning of greater budget rigidity.

However, market behavior tells another part of the story. A report from Banco de Bogotá highlights that in April 2026, Colombian public debt has shown significant gains, driven by factors such as high oil prices, monetary policy, and an improvement in country risk perception. "TES returns to levels more in line with the country's fundamentals," the report states, reflecting renewed investor interest.

Bancolombia, for its part, offers a more cautious reading. In its monthly report, it warns that the TES curve has faced significant pressures, especially in the short and medium segments, with rate increases of up to 95 basis points in the short term.
