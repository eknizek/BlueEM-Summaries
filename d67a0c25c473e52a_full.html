# Medical Centers, Convenience Stores and Cafés: The New Map of Former Bank Branches in Santiago

Where there were once lines to cash checks, sign documents, or request banking services, there are now waiting rooms, bakery displays, or convenience store shelves. Traditional bank branches are changing face: spaces that for decades concentrated financial services are now medical centers, cafeterias, or small retail formats, in a silent but increasingly visible transformation in Santiago.

This change responds to a sustained trend of branch closures across the country. According to data from the Financial Market Commission (CMF), by February 2026 there were 1,426 branches in Chile, compared to 1,459 offices registered in the same month of 2025. A decade earlier, in 2016—when the adjustment began to accelerate—the system had approximately 2,295 service points.

## Reconversion

According to Colliers data, in the Metropolitan Region alone, between 2024 and 2025, approximately 76 bank branches closed, while in 2026 so far, 12 offices have shuttered. Over the last 18 months, Banco de Chile leads the adjustment with 15 closures, followed by Bci (10), while Santander has opted for hybrid formats. In contrast, BancoEstado remains the only player with openings.

Branch closures have led to the reuse of these spaces, especially in "AAA" locations with high traffic and visibility. The primary destination for former branches are outpatient health centers: for example, a former Banco Santander branch on Avenida Macul and Quilín was transformed into a UC Christus medical center, while on Pedro de Valdivia in Providencia, former Banco de Chile locations are now occupied by networks like Bupa or Integramédica.

There is also notable reconversion toward convenience retail and gastronomy, such as the former Bci office at Vitacura 8157, now a Bretti store, or old Scotiabank locations in Las Condes that have been subdivided to make way for formats like Oxxo or Ok Market. Meanwhile, in central Santiago, several former branches have been occupied by public services or, conversely, face prolonged vacancy.

"We see that generally, if a bank closes in a middle-to-upper-income neighborhood, it will most likely transform into a sample collection center or premium café within six months. If it closes in the historic center, the most likely destination is a public office or, unfortunately, prolonged vacancy," said Colliers vice president Reinaldo Gleisner.

## Reasons for Closures

According to the firm, closures in 2025 and 2026 respond to two clear factors. First, the expiration of rental contracts signed a decade ago that are not being renewed: "Upon expiration, banks prefer not to renew and return 500-square-meter spaces to operate with 50-square-meter digital islands in malls," Gleisner said.

Second, the increase in operating costs, with "cashier zones that have raised expenses between 25% and 30% due to new security requirements... Therefore, it is cheaper to close cashier service and redirect customers to ATMs or digital transfers," the executive added.

Although branches are still used for procedures like mortgage loan applications or cashier service, the trend of office reduction continues. Currently, the Metropolitan Region has approximately 525 active branches, and Colliers projects that figure will drop to around 500 by the end of 2026.
