# InterCement's Plans After Exiting Crisis

InterCement has exited intensive care. After years of suffering from debt that drained its cash and paralyzed investments, the company has entered a new phase: it changed control, restructured billions in liabilities, and after a long time, is talking about expansion again. "Previously, the main concern was cash, debt, and interest. Today the conversation is different: productivity, investment, and growth," CEO Sergio Faifman told Brazil Journal.

The turnaround happened this month after InterCement completed the restructuring planned when it began in December 2024. The company, which accumulated R$ 14.3 billion in debt, managed to convert part of that amount into shares and extended its liabilities until 2031 at a cost of 6.5% in dollars. Additionally, it won't need to make significant payments until 2031. Result: leverage, which exceeded 5x EBITDA, fell to 3.2x. This didn't happen without casualties: Mover (formerly Camargo Corrêa) exited the company's capital. Now InterCement is controlled by LATCEM, owned by Argentine businessman Marcelo Mindlin with 39%, and fund managers Redwood and Moneda Patria with 25% and 23% respectively. The remaining 13% is scattered among various bondholders.

With Mindlin as the new chairman and a completely reformed board, InterCement is trying to make up for lost time. According to the CEO, the company had to postpone investments and reduce maintenance of its plants in recent years, which directly impacted productivity. Of the 14 plants InterCement operates in Brazil, four are "hibernated" in industry jargon. Now, the plan is to make a quick catch-up.

The company projects capex between R$ 400 million and R$ 500 million for 2027, focusing on maintenance and efficiency. Faifman said these investments will come from the company's cash, which received a US$ 110 million injection from new shareholders. InterCement – owner of the Cauê, Goiás, and Zebu brands – closed last year with sales of 8.5 million tons and wants to reach 9 million this year. According to the CEO, with productivity gains in current plants there is room to grow approximately 10% without significant new investments. Within a horizon of three to four years, he says the potential is even greater: the company estimates it could expand production by 20% to 30%.

"We are evaluating whether it makes more sense to reactivate factories or invest in new expansions, always looking at productivity and profitability in the long term," said Faifman. Everything will depend on how the market evolves. Despite civil construction being in good shape (albeit below the nirvana of 2014), the cement industry remains pressured by rising energy and freight costs. Coke, a petroleum derivative that is one of the main inputs for cement, rose from US$ 70 to US$ 100 in recent years, while increased diesel costs directly impact freight, one of the industry's largest expenses. According to CFO Ricardo Hatschbach, a former ABN Amro banker who worked at Cosan group companies, 70% of the cement company's costs are variable, which increases management challenges.

Furthermore, the sector will have to deal with a relevant regulatory change: the replacement of 50 kg bags with 25 kg packaging by 2032 – a government initiative to reduce weight on construction workers' backs. The change will require additional investments of up to R$ 400 million for InterCement alone. Another near-term challenge is the sale of Loma Negra, the leader in the Argentine cement market that InterCement controls with 52% of capital. Faifman, who is also CEO of Loma Negra, said the expectation is that the asset will be sold in the short term. The company's restructuring plan provides that 100% of sale proceeds will go toward paying debt linked to the operation. One of the main candidates for purchase is Mindlin himself, through LATCEM.

"It's very likely the sale will happen quickly, whether Mindlin is the buyer or a third party," said the CEO. Even with debt resolved, the CEO is not thinking about making acquisitions – much less of CSN Cimentos, which Benjamin Steinbruch put up for sale. "It's an acquisition that would double our current capacity and we have many challenges within InterCement before thinking about such an important challenge outside here," said the CEO. Today CSN Cimentos is the second largest in the sector with 21%, behind only Votorantim with 35%. InterCement closes the podium with 14%.

The CFO is not concerned about a possible purchase of CSN assets by the Chinese, who could come with more aggressive pricing policies to gain market share. "You see China today looking very much inward and with more rigorous international capital allocation discipline," said Hatschbach. Faifman, who already has 20 years of career between Loma Negra and InterCement, said his work routine is even more intense than at any other time, as "controllers want to make a quick catch-up of the operation."

"But it's much better to be thinking about where to invest than where to get cash to pay debt."
