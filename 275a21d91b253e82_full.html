Offshore trusts: ruling protects large fortunes from automatic IRS taxation

04 May 2026 · 08:22 PM

# Offshore Trusts: Ruling Protects Large Fortunes from Automatic IRS Taxation

The Administrative Council for Tax Appeals (CARF) has just imposed a limit on the Brazilian Federal Revenue Service's attempt to automatically tax as income the amounts received by Brazilians from trusts abroad. In a unanimous decision handed down in April, the court's 1st Ordinary Panel of the 4th Chamber of the 2nd Section canceled a tax assessment of R$ 25.8 million against a taxpayer who had received $30 million from a trust established in the Cayman Islands. The Federal Revenue Service treated the entire amount as income received from abroad, subject to carnê-leão (provisional tax) and the progressive Personal Income Tax table, up to 27.5%.

CARF understood that the charge was based on a mistaken premise: that every amount distributed by a trust should automatically be considered income. The decision changes the game for Brazilian families with assets abroad by requiring the tax authority to separate what is income, what is capital gains, and what is simply a return of assets, which should not be taxed.

The discussion has a direct impact on a body of operations conducted before the Law of Offshores (Law 14.754) came into effect, which began to apply to events occurring from 2024 onwards. Until then, taxpayers and the Federal Revenue Service coexisted with different interpretations regarding the treatment of structures such as trusts, foundations, and patrimonial entities abroad.

According to Lucas Babo, a tax attorney specializing in wealth and succession planning at Cescon Barrieu, the decision places a limit on an aggressive stance by the tax authority. "The Revenue Service had been relying on administrative understandings that threw all trust distributions into the same basket," says Babo. "This decision is important because it shows that a trust cannot be automatically treated as a source of income."

The decision comes at a time when offices specializing in wealth planning report a wave of audits on facts from 2021 and 2022. Since the statute of limitations is five years, the Federal Revenue Service can still assess operations related to 2023 until 2028. After that, these cases may take several more years in administrative litigation.

"Today, what's on the table is the past. We've been receiving a flood of audits from 2021 and 2022, and notices of infraction are happening," says Rodrigo Massud, partner at Choaib, Paiva and Justo. "This ruling is a relief for families."

For those who have already been charged or opted to pay the tax given a more aggressive interpretation by the Federal Revenue Service, the decision may also open space for legal discussion and possible restitution requests. "If it becomes settled that there was improper payment, the taxpayer can request a refund, within five years from the payment," says Massud. "But this becomes a matter of recovering an undue payment."

The decision also increases predictability for those who intend to use the structure in international succession planning going forward.

## The Tax Bill

The case involves a taxpayer who had regularized assets under the Special Exchange and Tax Regularization Regime (RERCT), the so-called amnesty for foreign assets. Subsequently, she declared deposits and withdrawals linked to the trust. For CARF, this history was relevant because it showed that it was not possible to presume that the entire amount received in 2021 was new income.

The Federal Revenue Service, however, classified the entire withdrawal as income received from a foreign source. The court understood that the assessment lacked certainty and clarity because it did not discriminate the nature of each portion. In other words: it was not enough to say that the money came from a trust. It was necessary to demonstrate what that value represented.

"It was a very good decision because the rapporteur analyzed the matter and made the consideration that the tax officer should have made," says Alamy Candido, partner at Candido Martins Cukier.

In practice, the decision dismantles the thesis that the Federal Revenue Service could treat all money distributed by a trust as income subject to the maximum rate of 27.5%. In this case, the charge was canceled entirely because the tax authority did not make the correct separation.

This interpretation confronts understandings that the Federal Revenue Service had been using to support assessments. One is Cosit Ruling No. 41 of 2020, which treated amounts received from trusts as income from foreign sources. Another point cited by tax lawyers is Cosit No. 1678, which reignited discussions about capital reductions in structures outside Brazil. According to Massud, the tax authority began to argue that in certain cases of capital returns in cash, the amounts should be treated as income subject to carnê-leão, not as capital or capital gains.

"In this understanding, the money returned never ceased to belong to the partner. The tax authority began to push this further and distort historical concepts," says Massud. "To our fortune, this ruling is a relief. These assessments have been very unfair."

In the decision, CARF makes clear that the taxations are distinct. If the distribution is income, there may be taxation as foreign income. If it results from the sale, liquidation, or redemption of an asset with appreciation, there may be capital gains, subject to rates of 15% to 22.5%. If it is merely a return of assets already declared, without sale or increase in assets, that portion should not be taxed.

There is a large body of pre-Law of Offshores disputes, with assessments now arriving concerning facts prior to 2024. But the precedent is not binding: other CARF judges are not obligated to follow the same understanding. Still, this April ruling becomes an important piece for administrative defenses, ongoing audits, and possible appeals to the Superior Chamber.

"The decision helps a lot in discussions, because it requires looking at the nature of what is being distributed," says Candido. "From a legal standpoint, another judge has no obligation to follow. But from a reasonableness standpoint, it certainly helps to build theses."

## How Do New Trusts Stand?

Although the Law of Offshores created a specific regime for trusts from 2024 onwards, lawyers see in CARF's reasoning a point of support for future discussions. The reason is simple: the ruling anchors taxation in the concept of income under article 43 of the National Tax Code, requiring asset increase and identification of the origin of the amount received.
