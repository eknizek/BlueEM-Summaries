Trust no exterior: decision protects large fortunes from automatic tax collection by the IRS

04 May 2026 · 08:22 PM

# Trust no exterior: decisão protege grandes fortunas de tributação automática do Fisco

The Administrative Council for Tax Appeals (CARF) has just imposed a limit on the Federal Revenue Service's attempt to automatically tax as income the amounts received by Brazilians from trusts abroad. In a unanimous decision handed down in April, the 1st Ordinary Panel of the 4th Chamber of the 2nd Section of the court canceled a tax assessment of R$ 25.8 million against a taxpayer who had received US$ 30 million from a trust established in the Cayman Islands. The Revenue Service treated the entire amount as income received from abroad, subject to carnê-leão and the progressive Personal Income Tax table, up to 27.5%.

CARF understood that the charge was based on a flawed premise: that every amount distributed by a trust should automatically be considered income. The decision changes the game for Brazilian families with assets abroad by requiring the tax authority to separate what is income, what is capital gain, and what is merely a return of assets, which should not be taxed.

The discussion has a direct impact on a stock of operations carried out before the entry into force of Law 14,754, the Offshore Law, which became effective for taxable events from 2024 onwards. Until then, taxpayers and the Revenue Service coexisted with different interpretations regarding the treatment of structures such as trusts, foundations, and patrimonial entities abroad.

For Lucas Babo, tax lawyer in the area of patrimonial and succession planning at Cescon Barrieu, the decision places a limit on an aggressive stance by the tax authority. "The Revenue Service had been relying on administrative understandings that threw all trust distributions into the same basket," says Babo. "This decision is important because it shows that a trust cannot be automatically treated as a source of income."

The decision comes at a time when offices specializing in wealth planning report a wave of audits on facts from 2021 and 2022. Since the statute of limitations is five years, the Revenue Service can still assess operations related to 2023 until 2028. After that, these cases may take a few more years in administrative litigation.

"Today, what's on the table is the past. We've been receiving a flood of audits from 2021 and 2022, and notices of infraction are happening," says Rodrigo Massud, partner at Choaib, Paiva e Justo. "This judgment is a relief for families."

For those already charged or who chose to pay the tax in light of a more aggressive interpretation by the Revenue Service, the decision may also open space for legal discussion and possible request for reimbursement. "If it later becomes settled that there was undue payment, the taxpayer can request reimbursement, observing the five-year deadline from payment," says Massud. "But this becomes a discussion of recovery of undue payment."

The decision also increases predictability for those intending to use the structure in international succession planning going forward.

## The tax bill

The case involved a taxpayer who had regularized assets under the Special Currency and Tax Regularization Regime (RERCT), the so-called amnesty for foreign assets. Afterwards, she declared contributions and withdrawals linked to the trust. For CARF, this history was relevant because it showed that it was not possible to presume that all the amount received in 2021 was new income.

The Revenue Service, however, classified the entire withdrawal as income received from a foreign source. The court understood that the assessment lacked certainty and clarity because it did not discriminate the nature of each portion. In other words: it was not enough to say that the money came from a trust. It was necessary to demonstrate what that amount represented.

"It was a very good decision because the rapporteur analyzed the matter and made the consideration that the tax inspector should have made," says Alamy Candido, partner at Candido Martins Cukier.

In practice, the decision dismantles the thesis that the Revenue Service could treat all money distributed by a trust as income subject to the maximum rate of 27.5%. In the specific case, the charge was canceled entirely because the tax authority did not make the correct separation.

This reading confronts understandings that had been used by the Revenue Service to support assessments. One of them is Cosit Ruling No. 41, of 2020, which treated amounts received from trust as income from a foreign source. Another point cited by tax lawyers is Cosit No. 1678, which reignited discussions about capital reductions in structures outside Brazil. According to Massud, the tax authority began to argue that in certain cases of capital return in cash, amounts should be treated as income subject to carnê-leão, rather than capital or capital gain.

"In this understanding, money returned never ceased to be the partner's. The tax authority began to push this point and distort historical concepts," says Massud. "Fortunately for us, this judgment is a relief. These assessments have been very damaging."

In the decision, CARF makes clear that the tax treatments are distinct. If the distribution is income, there may be taxation as foreign income. If it results from the sale, liquidation, or redemption of an asset with appreciation, there may be capital gain, subject to rates of 15% to 22.5%. If it is mere return of already-declared assets, without sale or increase in assets, the portion should not be taxed.

There is a large stock of pre-Offshore Law litigation, with assessments now arriving on facts prior to 2024. But the precedent is not binding: other CARF judges are not obliged to follow the same understanding. Still, this April judgment becomes an important piece for administrative defenses, ongoing audits, and possible appeals to the Superior Chamber.

"The decision helps a lot in discussions, because it forces you to look at the nature of what is being distributed," says Candido. "From a legal standpoint, another judge has no obligation to follow it. But, from a reasonableness standpoint, it certainly helps build theses."

## How do new trusts stand?

Although the Offshore Law created a specific regime for trusts from 2024 onwards, lawyers see in the CARF's reasoning a point of support for future discussions. The reason is simple: the ruling anchors taxation in the concept of income from article 43 of the National Tax Code, requiring asset increase and identification of the origin of the amount received. For Babo, the Offshore Law solved part of the problem by reducing the space to treat all trust distributions as income in the same way, but uncertainty persists, especially regarding the definition of beneficiaries.
