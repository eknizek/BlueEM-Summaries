# War in Iran Shifts Stagflation Risks to Latin America, Which Still Resists the Impact: Citi

Citi warned about stagflation risks in Latin America due to the war in the Middle East, which is impacting global growth prospects and inflation. "We are already beginning to notice the stagflationary wind that Iran brings us, less growth and more inflation," said Ernesto Revilla, chief economist at Citi for Latin America, during a virtual presentation of projections.

He explained that Latin American countries have limited room for maneuver in the face of stagflationary shocks. Revilla noted that some analysts consider this could be "the most important shock in the crude oil market since the 1970s," given the region's relevance for production and the role of the Strait of Hormuz in oil transit.

He added that even if flow is restored through the Strait of Hormuz, market adjustment would not be immediate and could take weeks or months to normalize due to effects throughout the entire supply chain.

Regarding possible responses, Revilla indicated to Bloomberg Línea that Latin American countries must resort to their monetary and fiscal tools to prevent inflation expectation increases from becoming permanent. In this regard, he warned that central banks must act cautiously to prevent second-round effects and unanchoring of inflation expectations. He highlighted the prudence of institutions such as Brazil's Central Bank, Colombia's Banco de la República, and Peru's Central Bank as examples of proper management.

On fiscal policy, he specifically suggests "trying to counteract the blow to economic activity and especially to the most vulnerable population," depending on each country's space and flexibility. "It's a shock that needs to be managed and macroeconomic flexibility helps a lot," he noted.

Despite the challenges, the U.S. bank highlighted the region's resilience to resist external shocks in its virtual Economic Outlook presentation, while uncertainty persists due to Strait of Hormuz disruptions. "Latin America has done relatively well in recent weeks despite the conflict for several reasons," the Citi economist indicated.

First, he explained that investors perceive the region is well-positioned amid a global geopolitical and commercial reconfiguration. He details that Latin America is relatively distant from major global tensions. The region remains largely free of relevant internal conflicts, which contributes to its stability. This is added to relatively solid macroeconomic fundamentals and economic policy responses that have generally been adequate, in his opinion.

He also emphasizes that Latin America is a dynamic region, supplier of manufactures and essential raw materials for present and future global economies. The Citi economist referred to the combination of a weak dollar alongside relatively high commodity prices. Since Latin America is a raw material exporting region, this combination has particularly favored its recent economic performance.

Citi's projections point to the region as a whole growing 2.1% in 2026, dragged down by a group of decelerating countries. Citi reduced its global GDP growth forecast to 2.7% for 2026. "At the beginning of the year we had something similar to last year's 2025 growth, which was 2.9%," Revilla noted.

The reduction in prospects is associated "with the shock caused by higher oil prices due to the Iran war."

Among regional countries affected by deceleration in 2026 are economies such as Brazil with a growth projection of 1.8%, Uruguay with 2%, Peru with 2.8%, and Costa Rica and Argentina with 3.2%.

Meanwhile, the group of countries with greater acceleration in 2026 is led by the Dominican Republic (4.5%) and Panama (4.3%). For other large economies in the region like Mexico (1.4%), Chile (2.4%), and Colombia (2.7%), they also project moderate growth in economic activity for 2026, though improving performance from 2025.

"In our region, countries that remain extremely resilient and have done well despite everything are in Central America and the Caribbean, especially Panama and the Dominican Republic leading economic activity," said Revilla.

In Panama, he explained that the economy is supported mainly by logistics, construction, and services linked to the Panama Canal. In the Dominican Republic, the main driver has been tourism, which has significantly boosted economic activity. In general, he says that in Central America and the Caribbean, U.S. economic strength has helped the region remain relatively stable, thanks to solid remittances and dynamic tourism.

"And one would naturally be concerned about these countries, because those that have suffered the most in the world from the Iran conflict are the smallest, poorest, and those that depend on oil imports, as is the case with many in Central America and the Caribbean," said Revilla.

"But U.S. economic resilience, these flows of capital, tourism, and remittances have counteracted the blow for now, and we see relatively benign inflation, but with risks obviously growing going forward," he noted.

Looking ahead, he said there are important risks such as the possibility that crude remains high and the El Niño phenomenon materializes, which could especially impact these fragile economies by generating "upward pressure on inflation and downward pressure on economic growth."

The El Niño phenomenon "is another stagflationary shock," because it increases inflation through impact on crops and reduction in agricultural goods supply. Additionally, "it reduces the potential for cheap electricity generation and, therefore, increases food prices and fuel prices," he noted.
