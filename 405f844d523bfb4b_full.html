# Iran conflict shifts stagflation risks to Latin America, which still resists impact: Citi

Citi warned about stagflation risks in Latin America due to the war in the Middle East, which is impacting global growth and inflation prospects. "We are already beginning to see the stagflationary winds that Iran is bringing us — less growth and more inflation," said Ernesto Revilla, Citi's chief economist for Latin America, during a virtual presentation of economic projections.

He explained that Latin American countries have limited room to maneuver in response to the stagflationary shock. Revilla noted that some analysts consider this could be "the most important shock in the crude oil market since the 1970s," given the region's relevance to oil production and the role of the Strait of Hormuz in petroleum transit.

He added that even if flows through the Strait of Hormuz are restored, the market adjustment would not be immediate and could take weeks or months to normalize due to effects on the entire supply chain.

Regarding possible responses, Revilla indicated to Bloomberg Línea that Latin American countries must use their monetary and fiscal tools to prevent inflation expectations from becoming permanently elevated. In this regard, he warned that central banks must act cautiously to prevent second-round effects and anchoring of inflation expectations. He highlighted the prudence of institutions like Brazil's Central Bank, Colombia's Banco de la República, and Peru's Central Bank as examples of appropriate management.

On fiscal policy, he specifically suggests "trying to counteract the blow to economic activity and especially to the most vulnerable population," depending on the space and flexibility of each country. "This is a shock that must be managed and macroeconomic flexibility helps a lot," he noted.

Despite the challenges, the U.S. bank highlighted the region's resilience to weather external shocks during the virtual presentation of the Economic Outlook, while uncertainty persists due to disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz.

"Latin America has performed relatively well in recent weeks despite the conflict for several reasons," said Citi's economist.

First, he explained that investors perceive the region is well-positioned amid a reconfiguration of global geopolitics and trade. Latin America is relatively distant from the main sources of global tension. The region remains largely free from significant internal conflicts, which contributes to its stability. This is combined with relatively solid macroeconomic fundamentals and economic policy responses that have generally been adequate, in his opinion.

He also emphasizes that Latin America is a dynamic region, supplier of manufactures and essential raw materials for the present and future global economy. Citi's economist cited the combination of a weak dollar together with relatively high commodity prices. Since Latin America is a raw materials exporting region, this combination has particularly favored its recent economic performance.

## Citi Projections

Citi's projections indicate that the region as a whole will grow 2.1% in 2026, dragged down by a group of decelerating countries. Citi reduced its global GDP growth forecast to 2.7% for 2026. "At the beginning of the year we had something similar to last year's 2025 growth, which was 2.9%," noted Revilla.

The reduction in outlook is associated "with the shock of higher oil prices due to the Iran war."

Among regional countries affected by the slowdown in 2026 are economies like Brazil with a growth projection of 1.8%, Uruguay with 2%, Peru with 2.8%, and Costa Rica and Argentina with 3.2%.

Meanwhile, the group of countries with greater acceleration in 2026 is led by the Dominican Republic (4.5%) and Panama (4.3%).

For other major regional economies like Mexico (1.4%), Chile (2.4%), and Colombia (2.7%), they also project moderate growth in economic activity for 2026, though they would improve their performance versus 2025.

"In our region, countries that remain extremely resilient and have done well despite everything are in Central America and the Caribbean, especially Panama and the Dominican Republic leading economic activity," said Revilla.

In Panama, he explained that the economy is supported mainly by logistics, construction, and services linked to the Panama Canal. And in the Dominican Republic, the main driver has been tourism, which has significantly boosted economic activity.

In general, he says that in Central America and the Caribbean, the strength of the U.S. economy has helped the region remain relatively stable, thanks to solid remittances and dynamic tourism.

"And one would naturally be concerned about these countries, because the smallest, poorest ones that depend on oil imports have suffered most in the world from the Iran conflict, as is the case with many in Central America and the Caribbean," said Revilla.

"But the resilience of the U.S. economy, these flows of capital, tourism, and remittances have so far counteracted the blow and we see relatively benign inflation, but with obviously growing risks going forward," he noted.

Looking ahead, he said there are important risks such as the possibility of crude remaining high and the materialization of the El Niño phenomenon, which could especially impact these fragile economies by generating "upward pressure on inflation and downward pressure on economic growth."

The El Niño phenomenon "is another stagflationary shock," because it increases inflation through the impact on harvests and reduction in agricultural supply. Also, "it reduces the potential for cheap electricity generation and, therefore, increases food prices and fuel prices," he noted.
