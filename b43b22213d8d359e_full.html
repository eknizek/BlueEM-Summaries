# Brazil's Tax Reform Impacts Real Estate Investment; Investors Reassess Portfolios

Real estate investment has always held great appeal for Brazilian families due to the possibility of recurring income, being a "real" asset, and having relatively simple succession planning. However, tax reform has brought new costs and, for many, a portfolio review.

The change begins with the system mechanics. The reform replaces taxes such as ISS/ICMS and PIS/Cofins with a dual VAT: IBS (Tax on Goods and Services) and CBS (Contribution on Goods and Services). In other words, it adds taxes to this market. The schedule is staggered. In 2026, IBS debuts as a "test year" with 0.1%, and only gains weight gradually from 2029 onwards, while ICMS and ISS are gradually reduced until the transition ends in 2033. The CBS, in turn, takes effect in 2027 with a rate of around 9%.

The final combined tax rate is still unknown, but the market works with a reference around 28% — and this is where specific real estate sector reducers come in. "When we look at leasing, it will have a 70% reduction of the base rate. And when we are looking at the real estate buying and selling sector, this reduction is 50%," says Camila Bacellar, tax partner at Cescon Barrieu, on Wealth Point, NeoFeed's program.

The reform now affects those who operate real estate as an activity, and this applies to both legal entities and, in some cases, individuals. On the individual side, there is a relevant dividing line: the "cutoff" to differentiate occasional income from habitual activity. An investor can become an IBS/CBS taxpayer if they have R$ 240,000 or more in rental income in the previous year, or more than three commercially operated properties. The income tax rate of 27.5% remains on earnings.

For real estate holdings, real estate revenue enters IBS/CBS under the sector's specific regime, with reducers and VAT credit logic. This means the calculation can vary depending on the type of operation and the credits involved.
