# Tax Reform Reaches Real Estate: Investors Return to Spreadsheets

Real estate investment has always held great appeal for Brazilian families due to the possibility of recurring income, being a "real" asset, and having relatively simple succession planning. However, tax reform has brought new costs and, for many, a portfolio review.

The change begins in the mechanics of the system. The reform replaces taxes like ISS/ICMS and PIS/Cofins with a dual IVA: IBS (Tax on Goods and Services) and CBS (Contribution on Goods and Services). In other words, it adds tax to this market. The schedule is staggered. In 2026, the IBS debuts as a "test year" with 0.1%, and only gradually gains weight starting in 2029, while ICMS and ISS are reduced until the transition ends in 2033. CBS, in turn, takes effect in 2027, with a rate around 9%. The final size of the combined rate is still uncertain, but the market works with a reference around 28% — and this is where specific real estate sector reductions come in.

"When we look at rentals, it will have a 70% reduction of the base rate. And when we're looking at the real estate buying and selling sector, this reduction is 50%," says Camila Bacellar, tax partner at Cescon Barrieu, on Wealth Point, a NeoFeed program.

The reform now affects those who operate real estate as an activity, and this applies to both legal entities and, in some cases, individuals. On the individual side, there is a relevant dividing line: the "cutoff line" to differentiate occasional income from habitual activity. An investor can become an IBS/CBS taxpayer if they have R$ 240,000 or more in rental income in the previous year, or more than three commercially operated properties. The income tax rate of 27.5% on earnings remains.

On the side of real estate holdings, real estate income falls within IBS/CBS under the specific sector regime, with reductions and the logic of VAT credits. This means the calculation can vary depending on the type of operation and the credits involved.
