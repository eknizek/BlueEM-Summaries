# Exports soaring for grain with 111 open markets, now reaching China for first time

Argentine corn is experiencing an exceptional start to the export season. According to a report from the Rosario Stock Exchange (BCR), the shipping pace in March and April—when the 2026/27 commercial cycle begins—is reaching historic levels: 10.3 million tons are projected, 54% more than in the same period of the previous cycle and 3.7 million above the five-year average. This is significant: never before have 5 million tons been exceeded in a single month.

According to a report from the Argentine Agroindustrial Council (CAA), in March, grain shipments increased 50.4% compared to the same month in 2025, with $989.9 million exported as the new harvest entered the commercial circuit. The harvest volume would increase by 30%: the government estimates 67 million tons.

This performance is driven by a substantial improvement in supply. According to the latest report from Argentina's Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock and Fisheries, the planted area grew nearly 7%, reaching 11.2 million hectares, with upward adjustments in Buenos Aires, Córdoba, and Santa Fe.

In parallel, harvesting is progressing with some delays. Early plantings show 32% progress, while late plantings are still in critical stages. Frequent rains complicate threshing and prioritize soybean harvesting during favorable weather windows. Nationally, the average yield stands at 71 quintals per hectare, raising production to 67.6 million tons nationally, a 30% jump from last season.

This volume, combined with initial stock of 3.7 million tons, allows the agriculture ministry to project exports of 44 million tons in the current commercial cycle. Of total production, 18 million tons are estimated for animal feed and 3.3 million for industry, while final stock would be around 6 million tons.

Currently, Argentina has 111 corn export markets, making the grain the country's most commercially diversified product, according to Gustavo Idígoras, president of the Argentine Oil Industry Chamber and the Grain Exporters Center (CEC). Southeast Asia leads demand, with Vietnam as the main buyer, followed by other regional countries, while North Africa shows signs of recovery and South America remains a traditional destination.

One of the campaign's milestones is the opening of the Chinese market. After more than seven years of negotiations, Argentina completed its first corn shipment to the Asian giant, a 34,000-ton shipment by COFCO. Idígoras highlighted the technical work carried out with Senasa to meet China's phytosanitary requirements, considered the world's most rigorous market for border controls. He also warned that the challenge now is to sustain quality and avoid errors that could affect trade continuity.
