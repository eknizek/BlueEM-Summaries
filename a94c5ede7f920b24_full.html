# Argentina Poised to Exceed 40 Million Tonnes in Cereal Exports with China Market Entry

Argentine corn is experiencing an exceptional start to the export season. According to a report from the Rosario Stock Exchange (BCR), the pace of shipments in March and April—when the 2026/27 commercial cycle begins—is reaching historic levels: 10.3 million tonnes are projected, 54% more than in the same period of the previous cycle and 3.7 million above the five-year average. This figure is significant: never before have more than 5 million tonnes been exceeded in a single month.

According to a report from the Argentine Agro-Industrial Council (CAA), March corn shipments increased 50.4% compared to the same month in 2025, with US$989.9 million exported as the new harvest entered commercial circulation.

This performance is supported by a substantial improvement in supply. According to the latest report from Argentina's Secretary of Agriculture, Livestock and Fisheries, planted area grew nearly 7%, reaching 11.2 million hectares, with upward adjustments in Buenos Aires, Córdoba, and Santa Fe.

At the national level, the average yield stands at 71 quintals per hectare, bringing production to 67.6 million tonnes nationally—a 30% jump from the previous season. This volume, combined with initial stock of 3.7 million tonnes, allows the agricultural portfolio to project exports of 44 million tonnes for the current commercial cycle.

Of the total produced, 18 million tonnes are estimated for animal feed and 3.3 million for industry, while final stock would be around 6 million tonnes.

Currently, according to the president of the Chamber of the Oil Industry of the Argentine Republic and the Cereal Exporters Center (CEC), Gustavo Idígoras, Argentina has 111 corn export markets, making the cereal the country's most commercially diversified product. Southeast Asia leads demand, with Vietnam as the primary buyer, followed by other regional countries, while North Africa shows signs of recovery and South America remains a traditional destination.

One of the campaign's highlights is the opening of the Chinese market. After more than seven years of negotiations, Argentina achieved its first corn shipment to the Asian giant—a 34,000-tonne shipment by COFCO. Idígoras highlighted the technical work carried out with Senasa to meet China's phytosanitary requirements, considered the world's most rigorous border control market. He also warned that the challenge now is to maintain quality and avoid errors that could affect trade continuity.
