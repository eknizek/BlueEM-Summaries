The Three "Spoilsports" That Jamie Dimon Sees in the Path of Global Economy

# The Three "Spoilsports" That Jamie Dimon Sees in the Path of Global Economy

Jamie Dimon used his traditional annual letter to JP Morgan Chase shareholders to issue a warning that goes beyond the typical balance sheet on banks, credit, and the economy. In the view of the CEO of the largest bank in the United States, the war in Iran could transform into a new inflation shock, raise interest rates, and cause a sharper correction in financial markets.

In the document released this Monday, April 6, Dimon states that the conflict adds pressure to an environment already marked by war in Ukraine, tensions in the Middle East, and growing frictions between major powers. The risk, according to him, is of new shocks in oil and other commodities in the coming months, which could make inflation more persistent and, ultimately, push interest rates upward.

For him, the risk that the market still treats with complacency is a resurgence of inflation in 2026. In the letter, he sums up this threat in the expression "the skunk at the party": a kind of spoilsport capable, on its own, of pushing interest rates up and bringing down asset prices.

Dimon even resorts to history to support the argument. He recalls that rapid spikes in oil helped trigger major recessions in the 1970s and 1980s — although he emphasizes that today's United States is less vulnerable to this type of shock than in that period. Still, the parallel serves to show that, for him, war is not merely an abstract geopolitical risk, but an event with concrete potential to contaminate activity, prices, and markets.

The strategic role of the Strait of Hormuz, one of the main global oil routes, helps explain why energy appears at the center of the alert.

The letter also widens its focus beyond the economy. Dimon argues that the outcome of conflicts between major powers, including the wars in Ukraine and Iran, is more important than their immediate financial effects. In another passage, he hardens his tone by saying that one should not ignore the role of the Iranian regime in fostering terrorism and the death of thousands of people, including Americans and Iranian citizens, arguing that this threat should be confronted appropriately. This passage marks a more political than merely economic inflection in the document.

At the same time, the banker does not describe an American economy in collapse. On the contrary. According to him, the United States remains resilient, with consumers still employed and companies in relatively healthy positions, although some recent signs of weakening are beginning to appear. This resilience, however, coexists with a more fragile backdrop, sustained by years of stimulus, high deficits, and a growing need for infrastructure investments.

He also lists factors that could sustain activity in 2026. Such as additional fiscal stimulus, a potentially more lenient regulatory environment, and the expansion of investments in artificial intelligence. Bank economists calculate that the fiscal package advocated by Donald Trump could inject around $300 billion into the American economy, equivalent to 1% of GDP, while hyperscalers' spending on AI is expected to jump from $450 billion in 2025 to $725 billion in 2026.

The problem, Dimon observes, is that part of these vectors could also fuel inflation in the short term.

Although the tone of the letter is one of caution, Dimon does not ignore the engines that could still sustain the American economy in 2026. Among them, he cites the additional fiscal stimulus, the prospect of less regulatory pressure, and the multi-billion dollar race for artificial intelligence.

According to JP Morgan's calculations, hyperscalers' investments in Artificial Intelligence (AI) are expected to advance from $450 billion in 2025 to $725 billion in the following year. Nevertheless, the diagnosis does not become more benign: for the executive, these same vectors could end up reinforcing the inflationary pressures that the market insists on treating as temporary.

The document also devotes considerable space to private markets, where Dimon sees growing fragilities. Regarding private credit, he states that the segment probably does not represent a systemic risk to the financial system, but warns that various modalities of higher-risk credit could suffer greater losses than expected in a slowdown, at a time when lending standards have been deteriorating among various lenders. For him, the sale of private credit products to retail investors requires "greater transparency" and "higher standards" than currently observed.

In another passage, he states that not all agents currently offering credit are necessarily good at it, and that many entered this market late — which makes it expected that some will perform much worse than others when the cycle turns.

The criticism did not stop there. Dimon also took a jab at private equity, another recurring target of his letters. According to him, with stock exchanges at recent highs, it is noteworthy that private equity firms — which control something close to 13,000 companies — have not taken greater advantage of the good moment to list these assets in the market. For JP Morgan's CEO, the hesitation raises doubts about what would happen to these portfolios in a prolonged bear market.

The banker also renewed his attacks on parts of American banking regulation. He classified certain aspects of capital rules as deeply flawed and renewed criticism of the surcharge applied to systemically important banks, the so-called GSIB surcharge. Even after recent adjustments, Dimon argues, the requirement still remains excessive in JP Morgan's case.
